## Scene Twelve — “The Ledger” (Celeste POV, full prose draft)

Mara didn’t *announce* the new system.

She simply put it on the table one morning as if it had always existed.

A ledger. Thick. Hard cover. The kind you could drop and have it land with authority.

It sat between the pincushion and the shears, beside the tin of chalk. On the first page, in Mara’s angular hand, were headings and lines, already ruled.

**GARMENT:**  
**DATE ISSUED:**  
**WEAR-TESTER:**  
**NOTES (MOVEMENT / STRESS):**  
**FAILURE POINTS:**  
**FIX APPLIED:**  
**RE-TEST:**  
**SIGNED (MARA):**  
**SIGNED (CELESTE):**

I ran my finger down the columns and felt, absurdly, the relief of it. The whole room would be calmer now. Fewer frantic, vague sentences. Fewer people saying *it just tore* as though fabric did things for fun.

Mara watched my face without asking what I thought.

“What’s the rule?” I said.

She tilted her head toward the far curtain rail—newly fixed, properly anchored now, no longer sagging like an apology.

“Rule is,” Mara said, “we stop pretending guesses are data.”

And then she looked past me.

Charlie came in quietly, as always, like he’d learned how to make his presence small without making himself invisible. A bundle of twill under one arm, a roll of paper under the other, hair still damp at the edges as if he’d left the house in a hurry.

He saw the ledger and stopped.

Not because he disliked it. Because he understood what it meant.

Mara slid it toward him with two fingers, like a judge pushing forward an exhibit.

“Write,” she said.

Charlie set his bundle down with careful hands. He didn’t touch the book immediately—he glanced at the headings first, eyes moving fast, absorbing structure like it was a language he was fluent in.

“You want me to log the failures?” he asked.

“I want you to log the truth,” Mara said. “And I want your name on the page when you’re satisfied it’s repeatable.”

His throat moved once. A swallow, not panic, but the faint tension he got when something was about to be formal. When he’d be held to the work—*and* allowed to own it.

He picked up the pen.

“Start with the stays,” Mara said. “The working set. The one you ‘delivered last time.’”

I watched him write. His handwriting wasn’t pretty, but it was exact. Like stitching that might not be decorative, but it would hold through a season and then another.

**GARMENT:** Working stays, linen canvas, whalebone substitute (reed), size test 2  
**WEAR-TESTER:** C. Rossignol  
**MOVEMENT / STRESS:** bending, reaching overhead, lifting tray, stair ascent / descent  
**FAILURE POINTS:** seam stress at left side-back, binding roll at top edge, grommet pull at waist tie point

Mara leaned over his shoulder, close enough to read without hovering like a teacher.

“How did it feel?” she asked.

Charlie paused with the pen just above the paper.

Not *how did it feel*, in the way people said it when they wanted a story. Mara meant the thing itself: pressure, pinch, drag, creep.

He answered like he was reading off a diagram.

“Too much load goes to the left tie point,” he said. “That’s why the grommet starts to oval. If you redistribute tension—either move the tie or add a secondary anchor—then the binding won’t try to roll, because it won’t be fighting the torque.”

Mara’s mouth did a small, satisfied curve that wasn’t a smile so much as a verdict.

“And?” she said.

“And the seam at side-back is… it’s underdesigned for repeated bend,” Charlie added. He tapped the page lightly. “Not wrong for… standing. But if you want a working garment, you need the seam to expect work.”

He didn’t say *women do work.* He didn’t say *I understand women’s bodies.* He didn’t say any of the embarrassing sentences people tried to force into the air when gender was nearby.

He just described load paths.

Mara straightened.

“Celeste,” she said, without looking at me, “this is why we don’t trial on staff first.”

“I know,” I said. My voice came out lighter than I intended, because it was almost funny now—how obvious it was in hindsight. “We’d been doing it backwards.”

She finally looked at me then, eyes sharp.

“We weren’t doing it. We were letting it happen.”

That was Mara. She could turn a whole week of chaos into a single sentence and make you feel embarrassed you’d ever accepted it.

Charlie kept writing, pen scratching.

**FIX APPLIED:** move tie point 12mm; add secondary anchor tape; reinforce side-back with felled seam + narrow twill tape; adjust binding cut on bias

He stopped again. His fingers tightened slightly around the pen, and I recognised the moment: the place where competence collided with the other thing—the thing he didn’t speak about.

He didn’t want to be dramatic. That was his discipline. But the discipline had seams, too.

Mara waited. She didn’t rush him. She didn’t soften.

Finally, he said, very carefully, “We’re still doing… the fitting under the curtain, yeah?”

Mara’s gaze didn’t flicker.

“Yes.”

“And the door stays locked,” he added, quickly, as if he regretted asking at all.

“Yes,” Mara said again. “And the log stays factual. No one discusses it like it’s entertainment.”

Charlie nodded once.

He wrote **RE-TEST** and then stopped, as if the word itself asked for a calendar.

I watched him, and I felt something in my chest shift into place. Not pity. Not romance. Something more utilitarian and more dangerous: the sense of seeing an interface, and realising it could scale.

If we could keep him safe from being turned into a spectacle, if we could keep him inside the logic of the work, then the whole atelier could expand without losing its centre.

Lauren arrived not long after. She didn’t knock timidly; she rapped once and came in. Practical. No theatrics. She carried a box of notions and a roll of interfacing like she was delivering supplies to a site office.

Her eyes landed on the ledger immediately.

“Ah,” she said. “You’ve made it official.”

Mara didn’t bother with pleasantries.

“We’ve made it measurable,” she corrected.

Lauren looked at Charlie, then at the curtain rail, then back to Mara, reading the room the way an adult reads a room—fast, with the parts that mattered.

“And he’s signing off?” she asked.

“He is,” Mara said.

Lauren’s gaze sharpened with something that might have been approval, might have been alarm, might have been both.

Charlie, still seated, still holding the pen, looked up at Lauren as if he expected a reprimand. Or a warning. Or the old script where adults spoke *about* him.

Lauren surprised him. She put the box down, opened it, and slid a handful of reinforced grommets onto the table.

“Use those,” she said, as if it was obvious. “If you’re doing working garments, stop pretending decorative hardware can take load.”

Charlie stared at the grommets. Then—so small I almost missed it—his shoulders loosened.

Not because his mother had rescued him.

Because she’d spoken the language of the work.

Mara watched the exchange like she was watching two subcontractors finally agree on a specification.

“Good,” she said. “We’re past guessing.”

Lauren glanced at me. A quick look—adult to young adult. Not unkind. Just exact.

“And you,” she said, “keep it professional.”

I held her gaze.

“I am,” I said. “That’s the point.”

She nodded once. Like she’d ticked a box.

Charlie put his head down again and wrote.

**SIGNED (C. ROSSIGNOL)**

Mara took the pen from him when he was done. She signed her name with a thick, decisive stroke.

Then she pushed the ledger toward me.

My turn.

I signed, and the ink looked oddly serious on the page, as if the act itself had weight.

And just like that, the atelier took one step away from being a clever little pocket of women doing miracles in private, and one step toward being a system that could withstand daylight.

## Why stays are the perfect “try, try again” garment (in-story logic)

Even with skill, stays are notorious for re-dos because:

1. **Fit is geometry + body variability.** Tiny errors cascade: angle of the tabs, placement of the waistline, the “spring” over the hips, how the front sits when you bend.  
2. **Boning changes behaviour.** The same pattern with different boning density/stiffness behaves differently. One channel too close, one too wide, one bone too short, and the garment “kinks” or bruises.  
3. **Tension is non-intuitive.** Where the stress goes under movement is hard to predict until someone actually *works* in it: reaching, bending, lifting, stairs.  
4. **Historical accuracy fights modern expectations.** People expect comfort like modern undergarments; stays are engineered support. Mara won’t let “modern comfort myths” distort the silhouette.  
5. **Eyelets / lacing failures are common.** Placement, reinforcement, and hardware matter; otherwise they oval out, tear, or shift.  
6. **Binding and top edge behaviour is fussy.** Rolling, gaping, cutting, or collapsing at the neckline happens even when the seams are “correct.”

So a first-time success is rare, especially in *working* stays (not display/costume stays). That’s very believable, and it’s a goldmine for showing competence.

### Scene Twelve-and-a-Half — **“First Attempt”** (Celeste POV, full prose)

Mara didn’t bring the stays out with ceremony.

She laid the materials on the cutting table the way a surgeon lays out instruments: in plain sight, deliberately spaced, nothing decorative about the order. Canvas folded into a clean rectangle. A length of linen tape, already pressed. A small bundle of reed boning tied with string. A tin of grommets that looked comically minor for the amount of authority they were about to carry.

Charlie arrived a minute late and tried to apologise with his body—small shoulders, quick glance, a quietness that wanted to pay for the inconvenience in advance.

Mara didn’t accept payment in the form of shrinking.

“Don’t do that,” she said, without looking up from the table.

Charlie froze mid-breath. “Do… what?”

“Arrive like you’re already wrong.” Mara lifted the canvas and shook it once. The sound was flat and honest. “You’re here. That’s the point. Now, pay attention.”

He stepped closer, careful not to crowd. I watched his gaze take inventory without touching. He was good at that: reading surfaces, anticipating what mattered, trying to solve problems before anyone gave him the chance.

Mara didn’t reward pre-emptive heroics.

She nodded toward the ledger—still on the table, still heavy with its quiet insistence. “Open it.”

Charlie did, flipping to a clean page as if the paper might bruise. His pen hovered, waiting for permission.

“Title,” Mara said.

He wrote: **STAYS — ATTEMPT 1**. Underneath, he added the date without being asked.

Mara approved it only by continuing.

“These aren’t costume stays,” she said, tapping the canvas with two fingers. “They’re working stays. People keep confusing the two. Costume stays hold a silhouette for a photograph. Working stays hold a person for a day.”

Charlie’s pen moved, quick, neat enough to read.

I leaned on the table’s edge and watched his hand. When he wrote, he looked calmer. It was as if structure allowed him to be present without having to perform.

Mara laid out the pattern pieces. Nothing flamboyant—clean shapes that looked simple until you imagined them curved, tightened, forced to behave over bones and breath and movement.

“Your first attempt will fail,” Mara said. She said it the way she might say *it will rain on Thursday.* Not cruel. Not kind. Just factual.

Charlie blinked. “Is that… normal?”

Mara’s eyes flicked to him. “If someone tells you their first stays were perfect, they’re either lying or they don’t move in them.”

He swallowed, and for a moment I saw that old instinct—the urge to vanish so nobody could watch him be imperfect.

Mara caught it immediately.

“Charlie.” She said his name like a hand on a shoulder—firm pressure, not comfort. “The garment fails. You document it. We fix it. That’s the work.”

He nodded once. A small, stiff motion. But it was a yes.

Mara slid the chalk toward him. “Mark your seam allowances. And don’t be stingy. The first mock-up gets room to tell the truth.”

Charlie’s fingers closed around the chalk, and he began.

The workshop was quiet in the good way—scissors snipping, chalk whispering, the soft drag of canvas against the grain of the table. Outside, somewhere beyond the walls, the faire was waking: faint voices, the clink of something metal being unloaded, a distant laugh that didn’t belong to anyone in our room.

In here, it was trade.

Mara moved around him, watching without hovering. Every now and then she corrected a hand position with two taps of her knuckles against the table. Once, she stopped him entirely.

“No. Your stitch length is too eager.”

Charlie looked up, confused.

“You’re trying to impress the seam,” Mara said. “The seam doesn’t care about your feelings. It cares whether it holds.”

His ears coloured. He adjusted, shortened his stitch, slowed down. I filed the sentence away because it was Mara in a nutshell—brutally useful.

When the pieces were cut and aligned, Mara indicated the curtain rail with a tilt of her head. The newly fixed track sat straight and solid above the fitting nook, the curtain hanging like a clean boundary rather than a flimsy suggestion.

“Same conditions,” she said.

Charlie’s eyes flicked there instantly—relief and tension arriving together.

“Yes,” he said. “Same conditions.”

Mara’s gaze held him. “Say it properly.”

Charlie hesitated, then forced the words out as if they were heavier than any bolt of fabric. “Door locked. Curtain closed. Nobody enters. Nobody talks about it.”

“Good,” Mara said. “That includes you, Celeste.”

“I wasn’t planning on narrating it,” I replied.

Mara gave me a look that suggested she wasn’t interested in my tone. “I’m not accusing you. I’m setting standards.”

I let my mouth shut. She was right, and she liked people who could accept that without turning it into a drama.

Charlie disappeared behind the curtain with the mock-up folded over his arm. The lock clicked a moment later—sharp, decisive.

The room waited in the way a room waits when a task has moved into a private phase. Not awkward. Just… contained.

Mara didn’t fill the time with chatter. She began sorting the reed lengths by stiffness, bending each one with her fingers, assessing it the way you assess timber.

“Do you think he’ll manage?” I asked quietly.

Mara didn’t look up. “He’ll manage. He’ll also want to be perfect. That’s the part that will trip him.”

A faint rustle came from behind the curtain, then the sound of a breath let out slowly—controlled, not panicked.

Mara added, almost as an afterthought, “And if anyone in this place ever makes him into a story, I’ll have them out on their ear.”

It wasn’t a threat. It was policy.

I felt my shoulders loosen a fraction. It was strange, the way a boundary could calm a whole room.

Charlie emerged after a few minutes, mock-up on, laced loosely enough that it didn’t demand a performance. Over his clothes, still clearly a work garment rather than a transformation. Mara had been exact about that, too: *no theatre.*

Charlie stood stiffly at first, chin slightly raised as though he were bracing for judgement.

Mara didn’t give him judgement. She gave him tasks.

“Arms up,” she said.

Charlie lifted his arms.

The top edge of the mock-up shifted, the canvas flattening in some places, fighting in others. I watched the material as if it were a diagram drawn in real time. Near the left side-back, the seam line pulled, the stress gathering like a storm behind a ridge.

Mara’s eyes sharpened.

“Now reach forward. Like you’re taking something off a high shelf.”

Charlie did.

The left tie point dragged. You could *see* the load path trying to relocate, looking for the easiest way out. The lacing tugged, the fabric creased into a diagonal that wasn’t elegant—it was warning.

Mara stepped closer, not touching him, but close enough to see the truth of it.

“Do you feel that?” she asked.

# Attempt Two — “The Block” (Celeste POV, full prose)

Mara didn’t call it Attempt Two until it had already begun.

She had the failed mock-up on the table again, flattened like evidence. Chalk marks still faint on the canvas where the garment had confessed under movement: diagonal strain lines, a crease that had formed with embarrassing consistency, the small oval ghost of where the grommet had started to surrender.

“It’s because you’re a *baseline*,” she said. “And baselines are useful precisely because they are not everyone.”

She leaned in, voice lowering into something like a vow.

“This,” she said, tapping the ledger, “is how we get the grail. Not by hoping. By mapping.”

Charlie stared at the page for a long second.

Then he nodded, once, slow.

“Okay,” he said, and this time it wasn’t compliance.

It was commitment.

## The most believable mechanism

You basically want: **Celeste has the ambition + the aptitude, but not the capital. Charlie has capacity to help, but he must not gain ownership of her.**

The cleanest mechanism is **not** “a date confession and a swoony offer.” It’s too romance-coded and too easy to read as transactional.

Instead, make it a **three-way, competence-driven solution** where Charlie’s support is formalised as:

### Option A: Apprenticeship-to-Operations Salary + Household Ledger
Mara promotes Charlie into a paid operations role (inventory, vendor management, QA logs, scheduling, prototyping protocols). His pay rises because the business can now scale. He *chooses* to contribute to shared living costs later, which indirectly frees Celeste to study.

- Pros: totally grounded; keeps Celeste independent; avoids “kept woman” vibe but inverted.
- Story juice: Charlie thinks the money makes him “provider.” Celeste treats it as *logistics*. Mara treats it as *earned wage*.

### Option B: A Formal Sponsorship Contract (Mara as sponsor, Charlie as conduit)
Mara wants an MBA-trained operator but can’t fund Celeste alone. She offers: “If we sponsor you, you commit to bring your skills back here for X years.” Charlie adds a top-up contribution as a *personal gift*, but under a written agreement drafted/checked by Lauren (or a solicitor friend).

- Pros: makes women the architects; Charlie contributes without becoming the owner.
- Story juice: Celeste accepts because it’s a strategic partnership, not romance. Charlie’s fantasy scrambles.

### Option C: Profit-share / Scholarship Pool (the “Education Sinking Fund”)
They create an internal “education fund” from a fixed percentage of atelier profits—transparent, audited, agreed. Celeste earns it by measurable milestones. Charlie can contribute, but he can’t decide anything about it. He’s just one contributor to a system.

- Pros: perfect “women-led infrastructure” vibe.
- Story juice: Charlie realises the only way to be respected here is to *submit to process*, not insist on sentiment.

If you want maximum believability and maximum thematic alignment, I’d blend A + B:

- Charlie’s pay rise comes from genuine business growth (he earned it).
- Mara offers sponsorship with obligations (women set terms).
- Charlie contributes, but only after Celeste defines boundaries and Lauren sanity-checks the ethics.

That lets the support feel earned, adult, and not contrived.

## Scene One — **The Numbers** (Celeste POV)

Mara didn’t summon people for speeches.

If she called you to the cutting table, it meant she had something that would either save the shop or cut it open to see what was wrong.

That morning she had the ledger, three printouts, and a pencil sharpened to a point that looked faintly accusatory.

“Sit,” she said.

Charlie sat first, too quickly—eager the way he’d become eager in the last month, which was to say: not in a needy way, but in the way a person gets when the work finally fits. He brought his own notebook as if it were a reflex. The boy used to arrive like he was expecting to be told off for breathing; now he arrived like someone who had tasks waiting.

Mara pinned a printout to the table with her fingertip.

“Throughput,” she said.

Numbers. Rows. Columns. A simple graph that sloped upward like a verdict.

I leaned in and read the headings: repairs completed, prototypes tested, defects returned, defects caught before deployment.

Mara tapped one column.

“See that?” she said. “Defects caught before deployment. That’s the difference between a clever shop and a professional one.”

Charlie’s pen hovered.

Mara didn’t look at him, but she spoke as if she knew exactly how fast he was writing.

“That jump,” she said, “happens the week you started logging wear-tests properly.”

Charlie’s ears went slightly pink. He kept writing anyway.

Mara slid the next sheet forward.

“Money,” she said, blunt.

She didn’t say it like money was shameful or romantic. She said it like money was thread: you either had enough to hold the seam, or you didn’t.

“This,” she said, tapping the bottom line, “is surplus. Actual surplus. Not ‘we hope the bills are paid’ surplus.”

I looked at the figure and felt my mind do what it always did: convert it into possibilities. Better equipment. More fabric options. Paid research time instead of stolen hours. The sort of structural improvements that turn a talented room into a durable system.

Charlie, meanwhile, had gone very still.

It took me a second to understand why.

He was looking at the surplus not like a strategist.

He was looking at it like a promise.

Not about the atelier. About himself.

The change in him was subtle—he didn’t puff up or grin—but I could see the internal arithmetic running: *If there’s surplus, there’s wages. If there are wages, there’s stability. If there’s stability, I can…*

He didn’t finish the thought out loud. He never would. Charlie’s fantasies lived behind his eyes, where no one could laugh at them.

Mara pushed the third sheet forward.

“Hours,” she said. “Paid hours.”

Charlie’s pen stopped entirely at that.

Mara glanced at him at last. Not warmly. Not coldly. With professional focus.

“You’ve been doing an operations role without being paid for an operations role,” she said. “That’s not noble. It’s stupid.”

Charlie blinked. “I— I didn’t mind.”

“I didn’t ask if you minded,” Mara snapped. “I asked if it’s sustainable.”

He swallowed. “No.”

“Correct,” Mara said. “So this changes.”

She drew a line on the paper with her pencil.

“Here’s your new rate,” she said. “Here are your hours. Here are the conditions: you keep the logs clean, you keep the testing protocol, you keep the stock inventory accurate. If you want to work in this place, you work as a professional.”

Charlie stared at the line as if it were a doorway he hadn’t realised he was allowed to walk through.

Then he nodded once. Carefully. Like he was afraid the room might snatch it back if he moved too fast.

Mara went on, as if this were the simplest thing in the world.

“And before you get sentimental,” she said, “this is not charity. This is because you made my shop better.”

Charlie’s jaw tightened with something that wasn’t sadness. It was… pressure. The pressure of being seen accurately.

He wrote the number down anyway, as if anchoring it on paper would stop it from being a dream.

Mara’s gaze flicked to me.

“This shop is growing,” she said.

I didn’t pretend surprise. We both knew it. Clients were returning. Word was spreading. The work had that particular gravity: people could feel, even without knowing why, that our garments didn’t collapse under reality.

Mara continued.

“Growth means structure,” she said. “Structure means decisions. Decisions mean someone who can do more than sew.”

Her eyes held mine.

The implication wasn’t flattering. It was practical.

Charlie shifted beside me, and I felt it again—that faint, private surge in him. His shoulders squared a fraction, as if he’d heard Mara say *provider* when she hadn’t.

He was still imagining, I realised, that his usefulness would eventually translate into a role that mattered socially. A role with leverage. A role that would make him worthy in the way boys were taught worthiness worked.

Let him imagine. For now.

Charlie watched me. Not openly. Not hungrily. Just… attentive. Like he wanted to understand what I was thinking, and also like he enjoyed watching me think.

He didn’t.

*If I’m useful enough…*

It was almost sweet.

Almost.

## Scene Two — **Mara’s Proposal** (Celeste POV)

Mara didn’t let the numbers sit.

She let them sharpen.

That afternoon she pulled me aside while Charlie was testing a bodice under the curtain—movement list, chalk marks, tape measures, the whole ritual. The lock clicked; the atelier resumed its familiar hum.

Mara led me to the back table where the light was harsher and the conversations were always shorter.

She didn’t sit.

She placed her hands flat on the tabletop and looked straight at me.

“You need training,” she said.

I blinked, because it wasn’t what I expected. “I have training.”

Mara’s mouth tightened.

“You have taste,” she corrected. “You have research instincts. You have a good eye and a spine. You do not have business training.”

I felt irritation flare—not because she was wrong, but because she’d said it aloud, like a fact in public.

I kept my voice even.

“You think I can’t run it,” I said.

Mara’s eyes didn’t soften.

“I think you could run it into the ground,” she said. “Not from stupidity. From ignorance.”

That was Mara’s particular tenderness: refusing to let you comfort yourself with the idea that talent was enough.

I folded my arms. “So what do you suggest?”

Mara didn’t hesitate.

“MBA,” she said.

The word sat between us like a door I’d wanted and pretended I didn’t.

I held her gaze. “Those cost money.”

“Yes,” Mara said.

I felt my jaw tighten. “I don’t have money.”

“I know,” Mara replied, and her tone made it clear she wasn’t interested in my pride. “And I don’t have money to waste on hiring some glossy consultant who’s never held a needle and thinks women’s work is a hobby.”

I could feel my heartbeat in my throat, not from sentiment, from ambition. The thought of leaving this place for lectures and coursework and frameworks, then returning with tools sharp enough to protect the atelier from stupidity—mine included—made my mind run hot.

But money was still money.

I didn’t “pour my heart out.” I didn’t sigh. I didn’t romanticise.

I said the truth.

“Even part-time, I can’t fund it,” I said. “Not without draining everything I’ve already got.”

Mara nodded once.

“Good,” she said. “Then we don’t drain you. We do it properly.”

My eyes narrowed. “Meaning?”

Mara reached into the folder she carried—the one that made her look like she’d stepped out of a courtroom and into a workroom—and pulled out a single sheet of paper.

She slid it toward me.

It was not a love letter.

It was terms.

At the top she’d written:

**SPONSORSHIP PROPOSAL — TRAINING INVESTMENT (CELESTE)**

Underneath: bullet points. Clean. Cold. Perfect.

- Atelier contributes tuition up to a capped amount per semester  
- Celeste enrols part-time while maintaining agreed hours at atelier  
- Celeste commits to return as Operations / Business Lead for X years post-completion  
- Performance milestones: semester completion, systems implemented, measurable outcomes  
- Exit clause for both parties

I stared at it, reading it twice because my brain couldn’t decide whether to be offended or grateful.

“You wrote a contract,” I said.

“I wrote structure,” Mara corrected.

A ridiculous warmth flickered in my chest—annoying, unwanted, and not the kind that made you weak, more the kind that made you *seen.* She was investing not because she liked me, but because she believed I could protect her life’s work.

It was better than affection.

“And where do you get the money?” I asked, because I didn’t believe in miracles.

Mara tapped the paper. “From growth,” she said. “From what the shop is earning. From not running this place like a nice little cottage industry.”

I thought of Charlie’s new rate. The surplus. The way the shop was beginning to produce enough stability to allow planning rather than scrambling.

Then I thought, inevitably, of Charlie.

He was earning more now. He was already imagining things.

Mara watched my expression change, the way she always did—like she could hear gears turning.

“No,” she said instantly.

I blinked. “No what?”

Mara leaned forward.

“No leaning on him,” she said. “Not informally. Not with hints. Not with gratitude that turns into expectation.”

I held her gaze. “I wasn’t planning on manipulating him.”

Mara’s face stayed stern.

“I’m not accusing you of cruelty,” she said. “I’m warning you against laziness. He’s devoted. Devotion is easy to use by accident.”

Her words hit cleanly.

I exhaled once. “So what happens if he offers?”

Mara’s answer was immediate.

“Then we handle it like adults,” she said. “If he wants to contribute, it goes through written terms that protect him *and* you. But the sponsorship is between you and me. Between you and the atelier.”

I stared at the proposal again.

It was the most Mara thing I’d ever seen: a future drawn with straight lines and consequences.

A part of me wanted to snatch it up and sign it immediately.

Another part of me—more cautious, more political—wanted to understand every lever.

“You’re asking me to bind myself to the shop,” I said.

Mara nodded. “Yes.”

“And you’re binding the shop to me,” I replied.

Mara’s mouth tightened.

“That’s the point,” she said. “I don’t want your loyalty. I want your competence. Loyalty goes soft. Competence holds.”

A sound came from the other side of the room—the curtain rustling, Charlie stepping out, ledger in hand. He moved like he belonged there now, like the space had stopped being something he tiptoed through and started being something he maintained.

He noticed us.

### 2) The Chore Chart That Saves Us

When my coursework started, my brain became a furnace.

I didn’t have time for vague domestic arrangements. I didn’t have time for a man-shaped sulk because I hadn’t guessed what he wanted. I didn’t have time for romance-as-telepathy.

I systematised it.

- Study hours are sacred  
- Meals are scheduled or not assumed  
- Laundry is a system, not a favour  
- Visitors are agreed, not sprung  
- Chores are assigned; no martyrdom

**If you’re tired, say so. No tests.**

### 3) The Moment He Thinks Being Support Means Being Smaller

It happened on a Saturday.

### 4) Provider and Support Are the Same Tasks, Different Power

Over time, the patterns emerged.

And the system running kept *us* running.

The work stayed the same.

The meaning changed.

### 6) The Startling Truth (Celeste’s)

The truth doesn’t strike like lightning. Not for people like me.

It arrives like a spreadsheet you can’t unsee.

It came during a group project, of all things. I was paired with two people who talked in circles and used confidence like currency. They kept trying to “lead” by speaking over others.

I watched them, mildly disgusted, and then I watched myself in the reflection of a dark laptop screen: my posture, my voice, the way I cut through noise with a sentence that left no room for argument.

I wasn’t like them.

But I was not innocent, either.

Because later, when Charlie texted me—**How’s it going?**—I felt irritation flare, sharp and unreasonable.

Not at him.

At the idea of him needing reassurance.

At the idea of anyone interrupting my momentum.

I typed: **Busy. Talk later.**

Then, without thinking, I added: **Eat. Sleep. Don’t wait up.**

And when I hit send, it landed in my stomach like a stone.

*Don’t wait up.*

It wasn’t cruel.

It was… directive.

A command disguised as care.

The startling truth wasn’t that I liked being in charge.

Everyone likes being in charge when they’re competent.

The startling truth was worse and cleaner:

I liked shaping people.

Not violently. Not with threats. Not with melodrama.

With systems.

With rules.

With small, rational decisions that made someone’s life fit inside my plan.

I could tell myself it was for the good of the atelier. For the good of the business. For the good of Charlie.

And it *was.*

But that wasn’t the whole truth.

The whole truth was: I felt a quiet satisfaction when he adjusted.

When he stopped guessing and started obeying the structure I set.

When his fantasies dulled and his usefulness sharpened.

I sat in my car for a full minute after class, phone in my hand, and felt something that might have been fear.

Not fear of being found out.

Fear of recognising myself.

Because if I was capable of shaping him, then I was also capable of going too far.

And I knew, with sudden clarity, that nobody would stop me unless I chose to stop myself.

Not wanting him.

Expecting him.

His shoulders loosened a fraction.

Charlie blinked.

I went to the sink and washed my hands slowly, as if the act of washing could rinse off the day’s noise.

He stood behind me, not touching, not hovering.

“Hard day?” he asked.

“Yes,” I said.

He waited, the way he’d learned to wait—present without prying.

I dried my hands. I turned.

He looked tired too. Not in a dramatic way. In the quiet depletion of someone who had spent the day being steady.

And I saw, very suddenly, what he’d done in the last few months.

Not one grand sacrifice.

A hundred small attentions.

He’d made my life run smoother without acting like it entitled him to anything.

He’d been careful with me in a way people were rarely careful with driven women.

Most people either competed or clung.

Charlie… maintained.

I felt something in my chest—an uncomfortable warmth that tried to turn itself into a thought I could manage.

*This is nice.*

I hated how small that sentence sounded compared to what it meant.

“Are you okay?” he asked.

I could have dismissed it.

I could have said, “Yes, fine,” and moved on.

Instead, I chose honesty, the same way I chose good stitching: because it held.

“I’m realising something,” I said.

Charlie’s eyes widened slightly. He didn’t speak.

I leaned back against the counter so I wouldn’t pace. Pacing was what I did when I was trying not to feel.

“I like rules,” he said. “They make things safe.”

I felt my mouth twitch, despite myself.

“Yes,” I said. “They do.”

He hesitated. “So… what do we do?”

The question was so earnest it could have been childish. But it wasn’t. It was partnership.

I looked at him and felt that warmth again—this time sharper, like a pinprick.

Gratitude.

Not as a performance.

As a fact.

Charlie blinked. “From you?”

I held his gaze, unapologetic.

He was quiet for a long second.

Then he nodded once.

“Okay,” he said. “What safeguard?”

I reached for the paper on the fridge—the **HOUSE PROTOCOL**—and pulled it down.

Charlie watched me, wary.

I wrote a new line at the bottom, in neat block letters.

**Charlie can say ‘not tonight’ with no explanation and no consequences.**

Then I handed him the pen.

His fingers closed around the pen like it was a delicate instrument.

I stared at the line.

It was so simple.

And yet it acknowledged me as something more than a machine.

It treated my needs as real without turning them into drama.

I felt the warmth rise again, and this time I didn’t try to crush it into a manageable shape.

“Thank you,” I said.

He stood there, pen in hand, as if he didn’t know what to do with gratitude that wasn’t flirtation.

Dinner simmered. My notes waited. The flat held steady around us.

And I realised, with a kind of quiet shock, that the thing I was feeling wasn’t a swoon.

It was a recognition.

That being cared for—without entitlement attached—was not something I wanted to lose.

Whether I called it love or not.

## Why shifts are perfect for “debug → deploy”

Shifts are deceptively “flat.” The pattern pieces are simple, but the *behaviour* isn’t.

**What real wear reveals (especially overnight):**
- **Underarm gusset placement**: too high = pinches; too low = sleeve pulls; wrong angle = binds when you roll over.
- **Sleeve twist**: a flat sleeve can rotate and torque seams into the armpit.
- **Neckline creep**: the front can climb when you lie on your side, or the back can slide and expose shoulders; ruffles can itch.
- **Seam allowances**: fine standing seams can become sandpaper under pressure.
- **Length + hem drift**: too long bunches; too short rides up and tangles.
- **Grain + fabric choice**: linen that’s too coarse feels “fine” standing up and becomes intolerable in bed.
- **Ease distribution**: you can have enough circumference but the wrong ease placement and it still feels tight.

All of those are *discoverable*, measurable, and fixable — exactly the kind of learning arc you want for Charlie.

I froze.

It had steadied him.

Less likely to be weaponised by someone else.

Not performing.

Not apologising.

## Scene — “Public Talent, Private Time” (Celeste POV)

“It’s the angle,” he replied. “If it’s even a few degrees off, it collapses under movement.”

Jade’s friend—Nina, older, more poised—leaned in closer, eyes following Charlie’s hands.

“Can you do one for me?” Nina asked. “Same design, but… you know. For a real body.”

Charlie paused.

The phrase hung there.

Charlie blinked once, then recovered.

“It’s not about ‘real’,” he said, carefully. “It’s about measurements.”

“We’d grade it,” he said. “We’d adjust the lacing panel—”

Our.

The word slid under my skin.

Charlie, still not clocking the boundary being pressed, continued doing what he always did: he made space.

“I can draft a graded set,” he said. “But it would have to go through the log. Mara decides what gets made first.”

Nina leaned closer.

“But if you *wanted* to,” she said, voice softer now, “you could do it after hours. Just for me. I’d buy you coffee.”

Admiration was turning into access-seeking.

Outside, the world could interpret him however it wanted.

Inside our kitchen, inside Mara’s atelier, inside the structure we were building, the rules were clear:

admire the work  
respect the process  
and nobody—nobody—gets private access just because they want it.
