[Emily]  
Yes. This is *exactly* the kind of quiet control mechanism that makes a reader feel the power shift without you ever having to announce it.

What you’re proposing does three things at once:

1. it locks Celeste in as the narrative authority (“what Celeste calls you is what you are”),
2. it lets Lauren’s “Charles” read as a *structural* label rather than a competing truth, and
3. it gives you a slow-burn dial you can turn over time, so the relationship and the atelier’s culture visibly evolves.

Here’s how I’d make it land cleanly, with maximum psychological effect and minimum reader confusion.

---

### The naming hierarchy

---
1) **Charles**

This belongs to *institutions and origin stories*:

* family paperwork, school rolls, a mother’s framing,
* the old life, the prior claim on him.

Let “Charles” feel like a label Lauren uses to keep him anchored to *her* interpretation of reality. Not cruel — just consequential. It reads as: *I named you first. I still can.*

So when Lauren says “Charles,” it doesn’t threaten Celeste’s authority; it reveals Lauren’s.


---
2) **Charlie**

This is Celeste’s first capture: friendly, manageable, modern, plausible.

* It’s the bridge name.
* It’s what the atelier can adopt *without admitting they’re adopting anything*.

It also has an important tactical benefit: “Charlie” is socially legible; it’s not flamboyant. Celeste can enforce it without looking like she’s performing ideology. She’s simply being… accurate.


---
3) **Charl**

This is the “you belong here now” name.

It’s slightly odd in print (good—memorable), but in speech it can be *intimate* or *professional* depending on who says it and how. It’s also modular: you can let it shift from “Charrrl” (hard, clipped, almost a shop nickname) to “Sharl” (soft, French-tinged, absorbed into the women’s mouths).

That phonetic migration is the point: the reader hears acceptance happen.


---

### How to make
“Charl → Sharl” work without clunky exposition


---
* Stage A: **Hard “Charl” as a worksite nickname**

Let this appear first from Mara (and maybe Emma, if she’s in this arc later), because Mara’s authority makes nicknames feel like policy.

Example effect: Mara doesn’t *ask* if it’s okay. She tries it once, as if it’s already been decided.

* “Charl. Hold still.”
* “Charl, bring me the tape. The *good* one.”

Hard “Charl” reads like: *you are a tool in a system, and you will be named accordingly.* It’s not unkind. It’s efficient. Which is Mara’s love language.


---
* Stage B: **Celeste deploys “Charl” strategically**

Celeste shouldn’t adopt it too early. When she does, it should be *selective*, like she’s choosing when he gets that closeness.

Use it in moments where she is granting him status:

* when he performs well under pressure,
* when he follows standards without being handheld,
* when he doesn’t reach for praise.

You want the reader to feel: *he earned this syllable.*


---
* Stage C: **Soft “Sharl” arrives as a cultural slip**

This is the delicious part: nobody announces it. It just… happens.

The “Sh” sound should come from someone whose speech naturally makes it plausible:

* Pauline (French influence, second-language rounding),
* Sarah (UK accent with its own reshaping),
* Miss Kathy (French extraction; even if bilingual, she can soften it deliberately),
* even Celeste, if she’s “warming” without admitting warmth.

The first time it happens, don’t explain it. Just let Charlie notice, and let it hook into his nervous system.

Something like:

* Mara: “Charl—”
* Pauline, without thinking: “Sharl, viens…”
* And Charlie freezes for half a beat because it landed somewhere *different* in him.

No lecture. Just physiology.


---
* Stage D: **Standardization**

Once “Sharl” appears, let it spread the way culture spreads:

* first among the women who spend the most time near him,
* then among those who are most invested in the atelier’s identity as a women-led space,
* finally among outsiders *trying to fit in*.

This is how you show the atelier functioning as a social organism: people adapt to the dominant language to avoid friction.

---

### Who gets to say what
(power through permission)

A good rule of thumb:

* **Lauren** earns no right to “Charl.” She stays on “Charles.”
  If she ever says “Charlie,” it’s either a concession or a tactic.

* **Mara** gets to use any name she wants because she’s the standards authority.

* **Celeste** is the gatekeeper. When she uses “Charl,” it’s a gift; when she uses “Charlie,” it’s neutral governance; if she ever uses “Charles,” it’s a *punishment*.

That last one is potent. You can make “Charles” a disciplinary tool in Celeste’s hands:

> “Don’t do that again, Charles.”

And the air changes. Because he understands instantly: he just got demoted back toward his mother’s jurisdiction.

That’s not melodrama. That’s control.

---

### Keep the reader oriented
(so it stays elegant, not confusing)

1) Use “Charles” *rarely* and *with intent*

Every time “Charles” appears, it should clang like a key turning in a lock.

If it starts appearing casually, it loses its power and becomes a continuity hazard.

2) Let the narrative voice follow Celeste’s naming, always

Even if we’re in Lauren’s POV, *the narration* can still keep Celeste’s authority by reserving “Charles” for Lauren’s direct speech only.

So the text can read:

…and then immediately, narration returns to “Charlie” the moment we’re out of her mouth.

That preserves your “Celeste leads the reader” rule at the sentence level.


---
3) When “Sharl” arrives, make it sensory

Don’t phonetic-explain; *show impact*.

That’s your whole story’s engine in miniature.

No need to apologise — and thank you for pasting it. This beat is already doing a lot right: it’s brisk, it’s unilateral (Celeste *acts*), and the humour is exactly the solvent that keeps it from reading as cruel.

What I’d tune is mostly **precision of dominance** and **how the humour lands**.

Right now:

* “It is now” is great, but it’s slightly *banner-like* (a touch “I’m making a point”).
* “It suits you” is good, but it’s not yet *Celeste-specific*. Celeste doesn’t compliment; she *classifies*.

You want her to name him the way she names anything: as a decision that’s already been filed, stamped, and moved along.

Below are three variants. All keep your structure and your tone, but each leans into a slightly different Celeste-energy.

---

* Variant A — The most “Celeste-as-authority,” minimal theatre

*Go on.*

He swallowed and opened the door.

“Charlie,” I said, lightly, as he stepped through.

He froze. “My name’s not—”

“It is,” I said. Not unkindly. Not negotiable. “Charlie.”

A flicker crossed his face, like I’d put something in his hands without telling him what it was for. Then he was gone—swallowed by the tide of students.

**Why this works:**
It removes the “now,” which can read like a dramatic gavel, and replaces it with *administrative certainty*. Celeste isn’t crowning him; she’s updating the record.

---

* Variant B — Warmer, but still controlled (the humour is a hook, not a cushion)

*Go on.*

He swallowed and opened the door.

“Charlie,” I said, as if I’d always known him by it.

He froze. “My name’s not—”

“It is when you’re with me,” I said, and let the smile show—just enough to make it land. “Charlie.”

He looked at me as if I’d handed him something small and dangerous. Then he was gone, swallowed by the tide of students.

**Why this works:**
That “when you’re with me” plants your title *Celeste’s Girl* without turning it into a speech. It frames naming as **relational jurisdiction**—not identity coercion. He can still be “Charles” elsewhere; Celeste simply establishes what *her* world calls him.

---

* Variant C — Cleanest setup for the future “Charl” shift

*Go on.*

He swallowed and opened the door.

“Charlie,” I said, lightly, as he stepped through.

He froze. “My name’s not—”

“It is,” I said. “Charlie. You’ll answer to it.”

A beat. His eyes held mine, uncertain—like he was deciding whether the ground had just moved or whether he’d only noticed it. Then he was gone—swallowed by the tide of students.

**Why this works:**
“You’ll answer to it” quietly introduces **obedience to naming** as a skill he learns in Celeste’s orbit. Later, when she shortens it to “Charl,” the reader understands that the shift isn’t random; it’s a *promotion of closeness* within an established rule: Celeste names, Charlie adapts.

---

Celeste doesn’t say, *“Your name is Charlie because you’re actually a girl.”*
She says, *“In my world, this is what you’re called.”*

That distinction keeps agency intact while still making Celeste the prime force shaping his self-discovery. The environment is hers; the choice to belong remains his.

— *The First True Test* (Celeste POV)

We found it the way we found most good things in Wardrobe: not through inspiration, but through paperwork.

Mara slid a thin archival printout across the cutting table without ceremony. It landed beside my notebook like a challenge.

“Look.”

The image was a plate from an old catalogue: eighteenth century, late enough that it carried a Georgian neatness, early enough that it still remembered softness. A working woman’s garment, not court finery: fitted through the back, generous through the skirt, closures placed for hands that were busy. It had intelligence in it. It had been designed by necessity, not ego.

My pulse tightened, that familiar feeling when history stops being “interesting” and becomes *possible*.

“Well, it’s not a costume,” I said automatically, more to myself than anyone else. “It’s equipment.”

Mara’s mouth twitched. Approval. She liked that phrasing.

“It’s also clever,” she said. “See the reinforcement here? And here.”

I leaned in, tracing the lines with my fingertip without touching the paper. The sketch suggested a hidden strength at stress points: underarm, waist, the place where movement always found the weak seam. It wasn’t decorative. It was structural.

“We can draft this,” I said. “We can actually draft this.”

Mara already had a pencil in hand.

“Then draft it,” she replied.

That was Mara: no ceremony for the moment a dream became work. The moment you spoke it, you owned it.

We split the labour without speaking. I took the research: proportions, plausible fabric weight, seam placement, what could be original and what had to be translated for a modern body in a modern job. Mara took the pattern: chalk, ruler, sharp decisions. Charlie hovered nearby, the kind of quiet orbit of someone who listened for when he was needed. He didn’t volunteer. He didn’t insert himself. That was one of his strengths. He didn’t presume he belonged at the centre.

We moved fast. Paper became pattern. Pattern became cloth. Cloth became the first prototype under Mara’s hands. The room filled with that particular concentration that only happens when a thing becomes real: pins tapping into the pincushion, the soft rasp of shears, the hiss of the iron. By mid-afternoon the garment hung from the mannequin, half-finished but already legible. Even unfinished, it had a line. It made sense. 

It didn’t scream “pretty.” It whispered “capable.”

Mara stepped back, eyes narrowed.

“It’s got spine,” she said.

“It has purpose,” I replied.

Charlie said nothing. He simply reached in and adjusted a seam allowance that had curled under itself, as if the fabric had misbehaved in a way the eye might miss.

Mara noticed. Mara always noticed.

“You’ve got a problem with that, Rossignol?” she asked, not looking at him.

Charlie paused with his fingers on the fabric.

“No,” he said quietly. “Just... making it honest.”

Mara grunted. That was as close to praise as she came without a contract.

We didn’t have time to admire it. Wardrobe had learned that excitement was a luxury you enjoyed after delivery. So we did what we always did next: we tested. Not with a photo shoot. Not with a “try it on and twirl.” With a shift.

We put it on Annie — one of our most reliable staff, who didn’t treat clothing as costume theatre. Annie did front-of-house, lifted baskets, crouched for children, ran for late arrivals. She was the kind of wearer who revealed the truth.

She came back near closing time, cheeks flushed, hair escaping pins.

“It looks brilliant,” she announced, breezy. “But — ”

There’s always a *but*. Clothing is always honest in the end. She turned slightly and tugged at the underarm.

“Here,” she said. “When I lift my arms. It’s not tearing yet, but it’s... fighting.”

Mara’s eyes flicked to the seam line and her whole brain shifted into assessment.

“And,” Annie added, touching the waist closure, “this. It held. But it’s been tugged a lot. People grab. You know.”

Yes. We knew.

“It’s not failure,” Mara said, and I realised she wasn’t talking to Annie. She was talking to me. “It’s information.”

“It’s reality,” I replied.

Mara’s gaze sharpened. “Reality is violent.”

Mara’s mouth twitched again, like she found my idealism irritating but useful.

“Fine,” she said. “Demanding. The point is, it needs a tester who understands what it’s telling us.”

“Charlie.”

“You,” she said, “are going to wear it.”

Annie blinked. “*He* is?”

I didn’t flinch, because this had been forming in my mind all day. We couldn’t test garments properly if our tester didn’t understand construction. And Charlie was the only person in the room whose mind automatically translated *feeling* into *fixing*.

But Charlie did something small—so small most people wouldn’t catch it. His eyes flicked down, not to the garment, but to his own torso. A brief recalibration. *I’m about to put on attire meant for women.*

He inhaled once, measured. Mara didn’t rush him. Mara didn’t soothe. Mara simply waited, giving him the dignity of his own decision. Charlie’s voice, when it came, was quiet and precise.

“For the record,” he said, not looking at Annie, not looking at the women, looking at Mara as if Mara was the authority who mattered: “I understand why. It’s... the fastest way to know what fails.”

Mara nodded. “Yes.”

Charlie swallowed. The tiniest pause.

“Still,” he added, evenly, “it’s not my first preference.”

There. Acknowledged. Named. No wallowing. No performance. Mara’s eyes didn’t soften. But they did become cleaner, as if she respected him more for stating it plainly.

“Noted,” she said. “And irrelevant.”

Charlie’s mouth twitched, almost a smile and almost not. Mara had a way of stripping the emotion off a thing without stripping the person out of it. I chose my moment carefully.

“It’s equipment,” I said, calm. “Not identity. We’re not asking you to become anything. We’re asking you to report accurately.”

Charlie’s gaze flicked to me — quick, startled. Then away again, as if eye contact was too loud for what he was doing internally.

“Yes,” he said. “Accurately.”

Annie looked between us, a little uncertain, then shrugged with the easy pragmatism of someone who’d worked with Wardrobe long enough to trust the women running it.

“If anyone asks,” Annie said lightly, “you’re a mannequin with opinions.”

Mara snorted.

“Don’t be daft,” Mara said. “He’s not a mannequin. He’s a stress map.”

That made Charlie blink — because being called *useful* in Mara’s language was a kind of privilege. We moved to the fitting area. Mara drew the curtain and held the garment up.

“Arms up,” she said.

Charlie complied, efficient, as if his body were a coat stand. That was how he survived discomfort: by treating himself as part of the process. Not numbing out: simply focusing.

Mara didn’t fuss. She worked quickly, checking line, checking pull, checking where the fabric resisted movement. She wasn’t dressing him; she was assessing the garment’s behaviour on a frame. I stood just outside the curtain, notebook in hand, listening to the sound of pins and Mara’s clipped instructions.

“Turn. Now lift your arms. Higher. Good. Twist. Again.”

Charlie’s responses were quiet, obedient, but not meek. He did not apologise for existing. He followed instructions like a professional. Then Mara’s voice snapped — irritated, but satisfied.

“There,” she said. “Feel that?”

A beat.

Charlie’s voice came through the curtain, measured.

“Yes,” he said. “It’s not the seam itself. It’s the direction of strain. When I raise my arms, the tension line runs across the tape and stops the fabric doing its job.”

Mara exhaled sharply. “Say it again.”

Charlie repeated it, clearer the second time, because Mara demanded clarity like a tool. “And the closure,” he continued without being prompted, “holds. But if someone grabs here—” there was a faint sound of fabric being tugged — “it transfers force to the waistband. You need the reinforcement to stop before the pivot point, or it becomes a lever. It will eventually tear *next* to the reinforcement.”

Mara’s silence was almost reverent. Not warm. Reverent in the way a professional respects a correct diagnosis. I wrote fast, my mind already mapping the fix. Stop the tape at the pivot. Shift the ease. Strengthen without bulk. Preserve silhouette. Mara drew the curtain back.

Charlie stepped out, still in the garment, looking slightly flushed — not with embarrassment but with the faint heat of having been under scrutiny. He kept his eyes on the floor for a beat, then lifted them to the table like a person returning to work. Mara grabbed chalk and marked a line on the garment where his finger had indicated strain.

“Good,” she said.

Charlie stood still, letting her mark him up like he was a draft. I watched his face — how controlled it was, how determined. There was a kind of bravery in being willing to do a thing you disliked because it was necessary, without demanding anyone comfort you for it.

“That’s why,” Mara said to me, curt, as if she’d just proved a point, “we don’t test with people who only wear.”

I nodded. “We test with people who understand.”

Mara’s eyes flicked to Charlie.

“We test with Charlie,” she corrected.

Charlie’s ears went slightly pink. Not flattery — more like the discomfort of being singled out as important.

I kept my voice neutral, because tone mattered. “We log everything,” I said, already flipping to a clean page in my notebook. “Every deviation from the original design. Every reinforcement. Every reason.”

Mara nodded. “Good. Make it defensible.”

Then, without ceremony, she pointed at Charlie.

“Take it off,” she said. “And write me a report.”

Charlie blinked. “A report.”

“Yes,” Mara said. “What you felt. Where it pulled. What caused it. What you propose. In plain language. No poetry.”

“Right.”

He moved towards the fitting curtain again, and this time the moment of misgiving didn’t follow him like a shadow. The task had overtaken the discomfort. Work had swallowed the awkwardness, the way it always did for him. As he disappeared behind the curtain, I realised something with a cold, clean satisfaction:

We hadn’t asked him to be brave in front of a classroom. We’d asked him to be precise in a room that respected precision. And he’d answered the way he always answered when the world stopped trying to eat him:

By becoming indispensable.

Mara looked at my notebook.

“Title it,” she said.

I wrote at the top of the page, in neat block letters:

**DESIGN REALISATION — PROTOTYPE 1 — STRESS TEST LOG**

Then underneath, because it mattered, because it named what we were building:

**Tester:** Charlie Rossignol
**Purpose:** durability + mobility without silhouette compromise
**Notes:** *equipment, not theatre*

Mara’s gaze flicked over my shoulder, and her mouth twitched again.

“Good,” she said. “Now we do it properly.”

And in that moment, with chalk on fabric and a plan on paper, Wardrobe stopped being a place that repaired old worlds.

It became a place that made new ones.

Linen is… honestly pretty brilliant for sleeping in **if you like a crisp, cool bed** and you don’t mind a bit of texture.

### What it feels like

* **Cool to the touch at first**, then it warms just enough without getting clammy.
* The hand-feel can be **slightly rough / nubbly** when it’s new (especially cheaper or lower GSM linen). After a handful of washes it softens a lot, but it never becomes “silky” like sateen cotton.

### Temperature and sweat

* Linen is famous for being **very breathable** and **good at wicking moisture**. If you run hot at night, it’s one of the best natural fabrics for that.
* It also tends to feel **less sticky** than cotton when you perspire.

### Noise + wrinkles
(the real trade-offs)

* Linen **wrinkles** like it’s its job. If you need your bed to look smooth, linen will not cooperate.
* It can be **a bit noisier** (a dry “crisp” rustle) than soft cotton percale, especially at first.

### Skin sensitivity

* If you’ve got very sensitive skin, some linen can feel **scratchy** until it’s broken in. Garment-washed linen and higher-quality long-fibre flax helps. Blends (linen/cotton) can be a gentler entry point.

### Practical buying hints
(if you’re choosing for sleep)

* **Garment-washed / stonewashed linen** = softer from day one.
* **Midweight** linen sheets tend to be more durable; lightweight can feel airy but may wear faster.
* If you like the cooling but want “softer,” look at **linen-cotton blends** or a very soft **cotton percale** as the alternative.

### Is linen

In practical use, **linen tends to outlast cotton**, and one detail matters hugely for underclothes:

* **Linen (a bast fibre) has very good tensile strength and its strength increases when wet**, which is exactly the condition your shift spends a lot of its life in: soaking, boiling, scrubbing, wringing. ([sff.arts.ac.uk][1])

### Why linen was

Think of the shift as *body-linen*: not fashion, but a washable interface between skin and expensive outer layers.

**1) It was the fabric that could take the laundering regime**
Undergarments were among the few items washed frequently, and they were washed aggressively (hot water / boiling, strong soaps, lots of mechanical abuse). Linen’s wet-strength and toughness made it a sensible choice. ([Victorian Web][3])

**2) Hygiene culture: “clean linen” stood in for bathing**
In a lot of early modern and 18th-century practice, cleanliness was strongly tied to **clean linens against the skin** (and visibly white/bright linens as a marker of respectability). **3) Availability and economics**
Flax/linen production was deeply established across Europe (and colonial contexts), whereas many cottons—especially fine ones—were tied to long-distance trade and could be relatively **costly/novel earlier in the 18th century**. Cotton absolutely exists in the 1700s, but linen remains the workhorse for “against the skin” basics. ([American Duchess Blog][5])

**4) Cotton doesn’t really “win” until the 19th century**
Once cotton becomes mass-produced and cheaper, it starts displacing linen in a lot of everyday uses (including underclothes), and you see the shift/chemise story tilt toward cotton more often. ([Victoria and Albert Museum][6])

In that login box, **“Domain” is basically the SMB workgroup / Windows domain field**. If you’re *not* on an AD domain (and you almost certainly aren’t), you have three safe options:

### What to put in “Domain”

* **Leave it as `WORKGROUP`** (the default) ✅
  This is the most common and usually correct.
* **Blank it out** ✅
  Most clients treat blank as “no domain / use default workgroup.”
* **Use the server’s workgroup name** (whatever is set in `/etc/samba/smb.conf`) ✅
  Often still `WORKGROUP`, unless you changed it.

So: **yes, you can blank it**, but if it already says **Workgroup/WORKGROUP**, just leave it.

### The more important
bit: username format

Depending on the client (macOS especially), you may need to enter the username as one of these:

* `robyn`  (most common)
* `WORKGROUP\robyn`
* `robyn-amd\robyn`  (sometimes works, sometimes not)

Password is the **Samba password**, which may or may not be the same as your Linux login password.

on the AMD box (to make it just work)

1. Confirm Samba’s workgroup and share:

```bash
testparm -s | egrep -i 'workgroup|security|map to guest'

sudo smbpasswd -a robyn
sudo smbpasswd -e robyn
```

3. Make sure the share path is readable/writable as intended.
