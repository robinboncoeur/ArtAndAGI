## Scene 42
### Unsafe

Night didn’t fall in their apartment so much as settle—quietly, with the same unshowy thoroughness that characterised everything Celeste insisted on running properly.

Charlie lay on his back in the dark, eyes open, listening to the house. The refrigerator’s low hum. A distant car. The occasional creak of the building cooling. Each sound seemed to arrive with a question attached, and none of them had answers.

The toiletry bag was gone. Not confiscated like contraband—simply removed, the way Celeste removed hazards from a workroom. The blister packs and box had vanished into whatever controlled place she kept things that couldn’t be allowed to float around freely.

He should have felt relief.

Instead, his mind kept returning to the moment his sentence had escaped him—*It’s my body. I want to control this*—and the way Celeste had looked at him, as if she’d discovered something she hadn’t expected.

He’d wanted her to understand. He’d wanted her to see it wasn’t vanity, wasn’t fussiness, wasn’t a boy trying to be special.

But now that she *had* understood—at least a little—fear rose up in him like a tide.

What if that understanding changed how she saw him?

What if “wife” had been safe because it was a functional joke they both pretended wasn’t serious, and now he’d made it serious? What if he’d taken something she’d been giving him—tenderness, structure, belonging—and turned it into a demand?

He pressed his fingertips lightly to his upper lip in the dark and then stopped himself, ashamed even of the gesture. The hair wasn’t much. It was almost nothing. But it felt loud inside his skin, like a warning siren only he could hear.

He turned his head toward the door. The line of light from the hallway was thin, barely there, but it made him aware of the distance between rooms. He imagined Celeste in the kitchen, still upright, still working, still the one who didn’t lie down until the world had been made orderly.

The thought hurt, because it was why he loved her.

He didn’t want to be a problem she had to solve.

He wanted to be the kind of person she could rest against.

His throat tightened. He blinked hard, once, then stared at the ceiling again, willing himself to be quiet enough not to disturb the life he’d been allowed to have.

In the next room, Celeste sat at the table with her laptop closed, phone in her hand. The kitchen light above the stove was on, not because she needed it, but because she disliked shadows when she was thinking.

She had already done the first part: remove the unsafe variable. She had put the medication away in the locked drawer she used for spare keys, important documents, and anything else that needed to be kept from casual hands.

Now came the second part: widen the container.

She opened her messages, thumb hovering for a beat over Lauren’s name.

A year ago, she might have hesitated. Not because she feared Lauren, but because she disliked inviting other people’s opinions into her domain. She believed in leadership, not committees.

But Lauren wasn’t a meddler. Lauren was infrastructure. Guardrails. Legitimacy. A woman who understood that safety had to be built, not begged for.

Celeste wrote, erased, wrote again. Not panicked. Not melodramatic. Controlled. Accurate.

Finally she sent:

> **Lauren —**
> I found medication tonight. Anti-androgens, taken privately, no doctor. He did it to slow hair growth. It isn’t vanity — it’s identity distress tied to the “wife” role and belonging. I removed it and told him we’ll handle it properly (medical, supervised).
> He’s scared he’ll lose belonging. I’m not angry, but I am concerned.
> When you can, tell me what you heard at the station. And if you’ve seen anything similar before.
> — Celeste

She read it twice, made sure it didn’t accuse, didn’t soften into helplessness, didn’t invite chaos.

Then she put the phone down and sat still for a moment, listening to the house the way Charlie was listening.

Her thoughts moved with their usual cold clarity, but beneath that clarity was something she didn’t entirely like admitting: a tenderness that felt almost… protective.

Not patronising. Not “poor Charlie.”

Protective in the way you protect something you’ve invested in—something you believe in. The relationship, yes, but also the person he was becoming. That new spine. That adult sentence: *I want control.*

She understood, now, why he’d hidden it.

He hadn’t hidden it because he didn’t trust her. He’d hidden it because he *did* trust her—trusting that she would stop him, and he couldn’t bear to be stopped.

Celeste exhaled slowly. She could see the shape of the conflict clearly:

* If she cracked down too hard, he would retreat into secrecy again, quieter next time.
* If she indulged him, she would become complicit in harm.

So the solution was the one she always chose: structure.

She would give him control that was real and safe, not control that burned him in the dark.

She stood, walked to the hallway, and paused outside his room. She didn’t open the door. She didn’t announce herself. She listened, just long enough to confirm he was awake—she could tell, somehow, by the quality of the silence.

Then she knocked once, soft.

“Charlie,” she said through the door, voice low. “I’m not coming in. I just want you to hear me.”

A beat. The bed creaked. He didn’t answer, but he didn’t tell her to go away.

Celeste kept her tone even, almost clinical—because that was how she made tenderness safe.

“You did something dangerous,” she said. “Not because you’re bad. Because you were scared. I understand that now.”

Another beat.

“This is not changing how I see you,” she added, and if there was any warmth in her voice, it was contained, like a flame behind glass. “But it is changing how we handle it. We do it in the light.”

Silence, then a small sound—Charlie’s breath catching, a near-sob swallowed.

Celeste waited, giving him room to choose words.

When he finally spoke, his voice was hoarse. “Are you… angry?”

Celeste answered without hesitation.

“No,” she said. “I’m serious.”

There was a difference, and Charlie would learn it, and it would steady him.

“I’ll book the appointment,” she continued. “You’ll come with me. We’ll ask questions. We’ll make a plan. And we’ll keep you healthy.”

A pause.

“And Charlie?”

“Yes,” came the whisper.

Celeste’s gaze shifted to the closed door as if she could see him through it. Her voice softened one degree—enough to nourish, not enough to flood.

“You don’t lose belonging because your body does something you didn’t ask for,” she said. “That isn’t how this works.”

She didn’t say *wife*. She didn’t need to. She left the word untouched on purpose, because she could feel it was both a comfort and a live wire tonight.

She stepped back from the door.

“Good night,” she said.

“Good night,” Charlie replied, so small it almost didn’t reach her.

Celeste walked back to the kitchen and turned off the stove light. The apartment folded into darkness properly then, as if the act of naming things had allowed the night to settle.

In bed, Charlie lay very still. The fear hadn’t vanished. It hadn’t resolved into certainty.

But it had been met.

And somewhere in the quiet, under the unresolved questions, a different sensation began to take root—faint as the first green after snowmelt:

not relief, exactly.

Something closer to trust.
