A young woman who wants personal info without being rude usually does one (or a blend) of these:

And importantly: she’ll do it **while her hands are busy** (threading a needle, smoothing seam tape, setting up the phone for photos). When people ask intimate questions, they often *avoid direct eye contact* — not from shame, but to reduce pressure.

“Oh, okay,” she said quietly. "Cool.”

“I’m not judging,” she added quickly, and then, with a little huff, “I was a tomboy for years. Board-shorts, surfing Bondi, black eyes. Wore my hair so short Mum used to think I liked girls.”

I let out a breath I didn’t know I’d been holding. Brittany smiled, but my cheeks burned anyway—not from shame, but from being seen.

Brittany nodded. “True," she said, her eyes bright. "Girls like Celeste?”

“Right,” she said, brisk. “You wanted something to demonstrate with.”

Brittany didn’t look at Fiona. She didn’t look at the costume.

She looked at me. 

Her eyes said, *I’m glad for that, for you*.

## Scene Four (Celeste POV, 1st person, past tense)

Carl’s boots made the hallway sound like a different house.

Not loud. Not heavy. Just… male. A wider rhythm. The boards had been quiet all morning, and now they answered him with small, practical creaks as he moved from the switchboard to the kitchen and back again.

Mum had opened every window even though it was muggy. She said it was “for the fumes,” as if electricity had fumes. Really it was for the feeling of control — curtains breathing, air moving, noise from the street to prove we weren’t sealed in.

I stood at the bench with a dishcloth in my hands and watched her in the way I always did when she was in one of her capable moods: not frantic, not brittle — just… organised. She’d cleared the counter for him. She’d moved Charli’s little bowl of hairpins out of reach, like it was fragile. She’d offered tea twice. She’d already learned his name.

Carl had a screwdriver tucked behind one ear. He was in his early thirties, sun-browned in the way tradies got from being outside even when they weren’t, and he smiled like he had time. It was irritating and oddly reassuring at the same time.

“So,” Mum said, leaning in the doorway with her arms folded, “tell me — do you do this because you like it, or because you’re good at it?”

Carl looked up from the open plate near the kettle. “Bit of both,” he said. His voice had that easy Queensland flatness to it. “Mostly because I like fixing things people have given up on.”

Mum’s mouth curved. Of course it did. She loved a line like that.

I was deciding whether to be amused or worried when my phone buzzed on the bench.

**CHARLI**.

My thumb moved before my mind finished its first sentence.

“Hey,” I said, softening my voice as I picked up. “You okay?”

There was a fraction of a pause on her end — not silence, exactly. Air moving. A breath being decided on.

“I’m okay,” she said. And then she added, too quickly, “Are you okay?”

That was the thing about Charli. She could be holding a whole storm in her chest and still worry about my weather.

“I’m fine,” I said. “What’s happened?”

“I just—” She stopped. I could hear traffic faintly, or maybe it was a fan. “I wanted to tell you about today.”

Behind me, Carl shifted his weight and said, to Mum, “Could you flick that switch for me, please?”

It was nothing. A normal sentence. A man’s normal request.

Charli went still on the line as if someone had put a hand over her mouth.

My shoulders tightened. Not because of Carl. Because I knew Charli. I could feel her fold in on herself from two states away.

I turned my back to the kitchen without thinking, stepping into the narrow strip of hallway like it was a shield.

“That was…?” Charli asked. Her voice went smaller. Careful. Trying to sound casual and failing.

“Sparkie,” I said. “Carl. He’s fixing the lights Mum broke.”

“I didn’t break them,” Mum called, offended, from the kitchen.

Carl laughed, low and polite, and said, “I’m not taking sides.”

Charli’s breath hitched. She heard the laugh. Of course she did.

“And you’re… at home?” she asked.

“Yes,” I said.

Another pause. The air between us thickened with all the things she wasn’t saying. The picture forming in her mind. The old reflexes. The stupid, cruel maths of it: man in our flat, Charli not there, Charli being… away.

I could feel irritation spark, quick as static. Not at her fear. At the way fear still managed to make her shrink.

“Charli,” I said, and kept my voice steady, not sharp. “Mum’s here.”

It shouldn’t have mattered.

But it did.

I heard her exhale like she’d been bracing. “Oh.”

And then Mum’s voice floated down the hallway anyway, bright and warm.

“Celeste, love — do you want sugar in yours or are you still pretending you don’t?”

I closed my eyes for a second. Relief and anger braided together in me: relief because the tension eased; anger because it took my mother’s presence to do it.

Charli gave a small sound that might have been a laugh, might have been a sigh.

“Hi, Lisbeth,” she said, suddenly more audible. More herself.

“Hello, poppet,” Mum called, using the word like she’d invented it. “You’re still alive up there? Good. We were just saying your girl has the domestic skills of a feral cat.”

“Excuse me,” I said.

Mum’s voice turned smug. “Am I wrong?”

Carl’s laugh came again, and there was a pause afterwards where I could almost hear Charli trying to decide what her face was doing. Relief first. Then the second wave: guilt. I could feel it in the way her speech started to trip.

“I’m sorry,” Charli blurted. “I— I didn’t mean— I just heard a man’s voice and—”

“I know what you meant,” I said. I kept my tone gentle, because if I went hard she’d dissolve. “And I’m not upset.”

That was true.

It was also not the whole truth.

I leaned my shoulder against the wall and stared at the framed print that hung there — something Mum had bought when she first moved us here, a bit of England nailed to an Australian wall. My chest felt tight in that familiar place between my collarbones.

“You’re safe,” I added. “I’m safe. Our home is not… porous.”

Her breath went out again, slower this time. “Okay.”

Mum called, “Charli, are you eating? Because if you’re living on coffee and anxiety, I’m coming up there myself.”

Charli’s laugh sounded real now. “I ate,” she said. “And I had a coffee.”

“Course you did,” Mum said. “Right. Put her on speaker, Celeste.”

“No,” I said immediately.

Carl’s boots shifted. I could hear the faint scrape of metal on metal. A tool being set down. He wasn’t listening — he was working — but the fact of him in the space changed the shape of the conversation. Charli felt it. I felt it.

“Tell me,” I said quietly. “What happened today?”

Charli paused. Then she started, and I listened to her voice move through the day: the late start, the dead phone, the construction noise at Wardrobe, Fiona sending them out for coffee like she was swatting flies away from a wound. Montville. Poets. Natalie and Ellie. Brittany’s nice car. The green hills. The fan. The alternative woman in the caftan.

Charli told it the way she always did: as if she were confessing to being present.

Then she said Brittany asked her how she got the job.

My hand tightened on the phone.

“And?” I asked.

“I… answered,” Charli said. “Sort of.”

That was Charli’s tell: the word *sort of* meant *I tried to be honest and got trapped.*

“She asked about corsets,” Charli said quickly, as if speed could keep her safe. “And stays. And I explained the difference. And then I said you drafted patterns from museum images and—”

Her voice snagged.

I knew what was coming before she said it.

“I said your name,” Charli admitted.

I leaned my head back against the wall. A slow exhale. Not annoyed. Not exactly. Something soft and aching.

“And?” I asked again, because Charli would run away if I didn’t keep a steady hand on the thread.

“I got a bit… misty,” she said, small. “It was stupid. Brittany noticed. I blamed it on being homesick.”

That earned a flare in me — sharp, quick. Not because she missed home. Because she thought loving me was something she had to hide behind a lie.

“That’s not stupid,” I said. “That’s human.”

There was a pause where she didn’t answer. The silence had weight.

Then she said, even smaller, “And later… Brittany and I were alone. She was meant to train with me. And I said ‘maintenance’ by accident.”

My stomach dropped a fraction.

“You said what?”

“Maintenance,” she repeated. “Like… my old job. I didn’t mean to. It just came out and then I—” She stopped. I heard her swallow.

“And Brittany heard it.”

“Yes.”

“What did she do?” My voice stayed calm, but my fingers had gone cold.

“She didn’t pounce,” Charli said. “She just… named it. And then she went ‘Oh. Cool.’ Like it didn’t matter.”

Good girl, Brittany. I filed that away.

“And then,” Charli said, “she told me she was a tomboy. Like— like she was giving me somewhere to land.”

I let out a breath I hadn’t noticed I was holding. I could almost see it: Brittany’s social competence, the way women sometimes made bridges out of nothing but tone and timing.

“And then,” Charli continued, slower now, “I said liking girls was cool.”

I blinked once.

“You said that?” I asked, not because I disapproved — because it was bold for Charli. It was a spine.

“Yes,” she said, and the word carried both pride and fear. “And then Brittany said… ‘Girls like Celeste?’”

Heat flared in my chest. Annoyance, yes — but not at Brittany.

At the way Charli still measured herself against men, as if their presence was a scale she might fail.

At the way she felt safe only when she could locate my mother as a chaperone.

At the way she believed my orbit could be altered by a random male voice in the background.

“She asked that,” I said carefully.

“Yes.” Charli’s breath hitched. “And then Fiona walked in with a costume and everyone was there and it was— it was fine.”

Fine, I thought. The word Charli used when something had scraped her raw.

I looked down the hallway toward the kitchen. Mum was talking to Carl about something ridiculous — the difference between English and Australian plugs, probably — and Carl was smiling like he’d wandered into a play and didn’t mind his role.

“Charli,” I said, keeping my voice low, “listen to me.”

“Okay.”

“You are allowed to feel whatever you feel,” I said. “But you don’t get to turn yourself into a ghost because a man exists in the same room as me.”

A beat.

“I didn’t,” she said, too quickly.

I closed my eyes. “You did,” I said, not cruelly. Just truth.

Her silence told me I’d hit something tender.

I softened my tone, but not my point. “This is our home. Mum is here because she’s visiting. Carl is here because the lights were sparking and Mum would rather flirt with a fire hazard than admit she needs help.”

From the kitchen, Mum called, “I heard that!”

Carl laughed again, and said, “I’m staying neutral.”

Charli’s breath shifted into something like a laugh — a real one, this time.

“I’m not—” she began.

“I know,” I cut in gently. “You’re not accusing me. You’re not jealous in a nasty way. You’re… scared you’re easy to replace.”

The words landed. I heard it in her inhale.

“You’re not,” I said immediately, voice firm now. “You’re not a placeholder. You’re not a guest. You are my person. And you don’t need Mum in the room to make that true.”

Charli didn’t speak for a moment. When she did, her voice was rougher.

“Okay,” she whispered.

“And,” I added, because I couldn’t help myself, “charge your phone.”

A tiny laugh. “Yes.”

“Purse,” I said.

“Yes.”

“And if Brittany asks if I’m the kind of girl you like,” I said, the corner of my mouth lifting despite myself, “you can tell her I’m the kind of girl who will drive up there and take her eyebrows off if she makes you cry.”

Charli made a startled sound that turned into a laugh she tried to swallow. “Celeste—”

“I’m joking,” I said. “Mostly.”

From the kitchen, Mum called again, “Are you threatening people, darling?”

“No,” I said.

Carl’s voice came, amused: “Sounds like yes.”

Charli heard it — I felt her stiffen, then let go again, because Mum was right there, filling the space with her loud, impossible warmth.

And there it was: the guilt. The ballooning. I could hear it in Charli’s sudden softness.

“I’m sorry,” she murmured. “About earlier. About… hearing him and—”

“Stop,” I said, not harsh. “You don’t need to apologise for a reflex. You do need to retrain it.”

A beat.

“I’m trying,” she said.

“I know,” I answered. And I meant it. “And I’m proud of you.”

Her breath came out shaky on the other end. “I miss you,” she said, almost inaudible.

My throat tightened.

“I miss you too,” I said. “Now tell me about Brittany. Properly. What’s she like?”

Charli’s voice warmed as she spoke — the way it always did when she talked about women who were kind without being soft.

And as she spoke, I listened for the seams. The weak points. The places she might split — not in cloth, but in herself — and I marked them down the way Wardrobe had taught us to: calmly, without panic, with the assumption that everything could be reinforced.

## Yes: it’s cadence. Here’s what cadence actually *is* on the page

Cadence isn’t “pretty writing.” It’s three mechanical things you can learn:

### A) Breath units

Sentences should match **human breath and emotion**.

* Calm = longer sentences, smoother clauses.
* Alarm/shame = short sentences. Fragments. Hard stops.

If everything is medium-long, the reader’s nervous system stays flat.

### B) Contrast

You want a pattern like:

* long → short → medium
* descriptive → punchline
* external fact → internal sting

Contrast is what makes prose feel “alive.”

### C) Placement of weight

The most important word in a sentence is often the **last one**.
So you can improve a line by moving the *payload* to the end.

Example (generic):

* “I realised I was afraid” (flat)
* “I realised I was afraid **of being seen**.” (weight lands)

---

## A mini “cadence gym” you can do on any paragraph (takes 2 minutes)

Take one paragraph and do **three micro-variants**:

1. **Cut 20%** (remove extras, keep meaning)
2. **Add one body tell** (only one)
3. **Flip the last word** (move the emotional payload to the end)

Then choose the best *two* and blend.

This is exactly like food tracking: you don’t need perfection, you need repetition until your instincts change. 🙂

## A small craft tool you’ll love: the abstraction ladder 🪜

When you’re deciding whether you’re “allowed” to clarify, think in rungs:

1. **Sensation** (air-con too loud)
2. **Body impulse** (jaw set, hands open)
3. **Name** (annoyance, relief)
4. **Reason** (“because men…”, “because she feels temporary…”)
5. **Thesis** (a paragraph of psychology)

Great prose often lives on **rungs 1–3**, with an occasional quick step to **4** for clarity. Your “At the way…” stack was living on **4–5** for several lines in a row.

So: keep your clarity, just drop the altitude. 😊
