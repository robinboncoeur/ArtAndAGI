Straight.

Steady.

Yes — you’re getting it exactly right.

Present tense isn’t “cool style” here; it’s a *mechanical choice* that matches what the scene is doing.

In past tense, Charli can accidentally sound like she’s already processed it:

* *I froze. I realised. I understood.*
  That can read like: **the decision has been digested and narrated from a safe distance**.

But this is your first time crawling into her head, and it’s the hinge where the self stops being theoretical and becomes bodily. So you want the reader to feel:

* **the chalk hovering**
* **the breath catching**
* **the exact instant the nervous system answers before language does**
* **the moment the choice becomes irreversible**

Present tense delivers that because the narration can’t “get ahead of itself.” It has to live alongside her, beat by beat, without hindsight smoothing it into something tidy.

A second (smaller) advantage: present tense helps prevent the scene from sounding like a retrospective “identity essay.” It stays *felt* and procedural: stimulus → body response → thought → choice.

### Will it create dissonance?

Potentially a little — but you can make it feel intentional (and not jarring) if you treat it as a **one-time hinge technique**:

* A **single present-tense first-person** “threshold scene” for Charli
* Then return to your usual tense/POV afterwards

Readers accept that kind of formal shift when it’s clearly motivated by importance. It feels like the story leaning in.

### If you want to keep past tense overall

You can still get immediacy by using **past tense with “free indirect” pressure**, but it won’t hit quite as hard as present. If you’re curious, the trick is to keep sentences short, sensory, and avoid “I realised/I understood” language.

But for *this* scene — first time in Charli’s head, first time the door swings — present tense is the cleanest way to make it land as an experience rather than a report.
