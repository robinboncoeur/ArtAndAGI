Historic Notes

The Chateau

The château used in the filming of “Portrait de la Jeune Fille en Feu” (Portrait of a Lady on Fire) is the Château de La Chapelle Gauthier, located in Seine-et-Marne, about an hour from Paris. This château was primarily constructed in the 17th century. The setting in the film, although depicted as being in Brittany, was chosen for its historical ambiance and proximity to Paris, which was convenient for the production team.

From the site:

Céline Sciamma raconte l’histoire d’amour de Marianne (Noémie Merlant), peintre, et d’Héloïse (Adèle Haenel), sa muse réticente à l’approche d’un mariage arrangé dans la France du XVIIIe siècle. L’amour entre les deux jeunes filles jaillit au milieu des falaises solitaires de Bretagne, mais s’enflamme sous le toit protecteur du château ancestral d’Héloïse, dans une oasis réservée aux femmes, avant que l’héritière ne soit envoyée à Milan pour épouser un homme qu’elle n’aime, ni ne connaît.

Entre les eaux déferlantes, le feu qui crépite et la passion à couper le souffle se trouve le château de pierre. Situé le long de la côte bretonne dans le film, la demeure se trouve en fait à une heure de Paris, dans le luxuriant département de la Seine-et-Marne. Ce château - La Chapelle-Gauthier- sera bientôt ouvert au public… Spoilers en perspective.

Welcome to our tour of the castle’s historical wardrobe! Today, we’ll explore the intricate garments worn by women in the 1750s. Each outfit was composed of several distinct parts, each serving a specific function and reflecting the fashion of the time.

  1. Chemise:

    • The chemise, or shift, was the innermost layer, made of linen or cotton. It was a simple, loose-fitting garment that protected the outer clothing from sweat and body oils. The chemise also served as a nightgown.

  2. Stays:

    • Over the chemise, women wore stays, which are similar to what we might call a corset today. Stays were made of layers of fabric stiffened with boning, usually from whale baleen or reeds. They provided support, shaped the torso, and promoted good posture. Contrary to popular belief, stays were not typically as restrictive as corsets from later periods.

  3. Petticoats:

    • Petticoats were worn over the stays and chemise. These were full skirts that added volume and warmth. Depending on the formality of the occasion and the wealth of the wearer, a woman might wear several petticoats at once. The outermost petticoat was often decorative and matched the gown.

  4. Gown:

    • The gown itself, which could come in various styles such as the Robe à la Française or Robe à l’Anglaise, was worn over the petticoats. The Robe à la Française featured pleats at the back that flowed down from the shoulders to the floor, while the Robe à l’Anglaise had a more fitted back. These gowns were often made of silk, brocade, or fine wool, with elaborate patterns and decorations.

  1. Engageantes:

    • Engageantes were detachable lace or linen sleeves that extended from the elbow to the wrist, adding a touch of elegance to the gown. These could be changed to match different outfits or occasions.

  2. Stomacher:

    • The stomacher was a triangular panel that filled in the front opening of the gown. It was often elaborately decorated with embroidery, ribbons, or jewels. The stomacher was pinned or sewn in place, and could be swapped out to give a gown a different look.

  3. Accessories:

    • Accessories were an essential part of a woman’s outfit. These included caps or bonnets, often decorated with lace and ribbons, worn to cover the hair. Gloves, fans, and jewelry such as brooches and necklaces completed the ensemble. Shoes were typically low-heeled and made of silk or leather, often with decorative buckles.

  4. Mantua or Cloak:

    • For outdoor wear, women would don a mantua or cloak. These were large, loose overgarments that provided warmth and protection from the elements. They were often lined with fur or quilted for additional insulation.

Shoes and Alternatives

Hi Emily. Question about footwear for women in the 1750s: were there alternatives to shoes?

Hi! In the 1750s, while shoes were the primary form of footwear for women, there were a few alternative options and variations based on occasion, social status, and environment. Here are some alternatives and variations to consider:

Alternative Footwear Options:

  1. Mules (Slippers):

    • Description: Mules were backless shoes that could easily be slipped on and off. They were often worn indoors as casual or house shoes.

    • Materials: Made from leather, silk, or brocade, sometimes with decorative embroidery or embellishments.

  2. Pattens:

    • Description: Pattens were wooden or metal overshoes worn over regular shoes to protect them from mud and wet ground. They elevated the wearer a few inches off the ground.

    • Materials: Typically made of wood with metal fittings or entirely from metal. The design included a wooden sole with metal supports.

  3. Clogs:

    • Description: Similar to pattens, clogs were also used to protect shoes from dirt and water. They were more commonly used by working-class women.

    • Materials: Made from wood, sometimes with leather straps.

  4. Stockings:

    • Description: While not footwear per se, stockings were an essential part of a woman’s attire. Made from wool, silk, or cotton, they provided warmth and modesty.

    • Materials: Depending on the social status, they could range from coarse wool to fine silk.

Contextual Footwear Usage:

  • Indoors:

    • Women often wore more delicate and decorative slippers or mules when indoors. These were less about protection and more about comfort and fashion.

  • Outdoors:

    • Sturdier shoes with thicker soles were worn outside. When conditions were particularly poor (e.g., muddy or rainy), pattens or clogs were used to keep shoes clean and dry.

Footwear for Different Classes:

  • Upper Class:

    • Women of higher social status wore finely made shoes, often with decorative elements like buckles, bows, and embroidery. Materials included silk, brocade, and fine leather.

  • Working Class:

    • Working-class women wore more practical, durable footwear. Their shoes were made from sturdier leather and were designed for comfort and longevity. Pattens and clogs were more commonly used among this group.

Footwear in Specific Situations:

  • Formal Events:

    • For formal occasions and social gatherings, women wore their best shoes, often with heels and intricate designs.

  • Household Work:

    • During household chores, simpler and more comfortable shoes or slippers were preferred.

Challenges of 1750s Attire

Good morning, Robyn! The clothing of the 1750s, particularly for women and female servants, presented several challenges in terms of practicality and mobility. Here’s a detailed look at the process of getting dressed and how their attire impacted their ability to perform everyday tasks:

Getting Dressed as a Female Servant

  1. Chemise (Shift)

    • First Layer: The chemise, a loose, long-sleeved undergarment made of linen, was the first layer worn directly against the skin. It absorbed sweat and protected outer garments from body oils and dirt.

    • Putting It On: The chemise was simple to put on, as it slipped over the head and was often gathered at the neck with a drawstring.

  2. Stays (Corset)

    • Structure and Support: Stays were worn over the chemise to provide structure and support. They were less restrictive for servants, focusing more on posture than on shaping the body.

    • Lacing: Stays were laced up the back or front. For a servant dressing alone, back-laced stays posed a significant challenge. Servants often relied on a buddy system, where they helped each other lace up. In some cases, stays might be partially laced up the back and finished at the front.

    • Impact on Movement: Stays restricted bending and twisting but were essential for maintaining the fashionable silhouette of the time.

  3. Petticoats

    • Layers and Fabric: Multiple petticoats made of wool, linen, or cotton were worn over the stays. Petticoats added volume to the skirt and provided warmth.

    • Tying Them On: Each petticoat was tied around the waist with tapes or drawstrings. This required reaching around the back, which could be tricky without assistance.

    • Impact on Movement: The multiple layers of petticoats made movement more cumbersome. They restricted leg movement, making it difficult to run or take large strides. Petticoats also added weight, making physical labor more exhausting.

  4. Gown (Robe)

    • Style and Fabric: The gown, worn over the petticoats, was usually made from durable fabric like wool or coarse linen for servants.

    • Putting It On: The gown had a fitted bodice with a front-lacing or hook closure, which was easier to manage alone compared to back-lacing stays.

    • Full Skirt: The full skirt of the gown added another layer, further restricting movement.

  5. Apron

    • Function and Appearance: An apron was worn over the gown to protect it from dirt and stains. It tied at the waist and sometimes had a bib that covered the bodice.

    • Tying the Apron: Tying the apron at the back was manageable but could still be awkward without practice.

  6. Cap

    • Material and Design: A linen cap was worn to keep hair neat and clean. It was tied under the chin or at the back of the head.

    • Putting It On: This was relatively simple and could be done alone.

  7. Shoes and Stockings

    • Shoes: Practical shoes made of sturdy leather with low heels or no heels were worn. Buckles or ties were used to secure them.

    • Stockings: Wool or cotton stockings were worn under the shoes and were held up with garters tied just below the knee.

    • Putting on Shoes: Bending down to put on shoes and tie garters was made more difficult by the restrictive stays and multiple layers of petticoats.

Challenges in Performing Chores

  1. Carrying Things

    • Balancing Act: The full skirts and petticoats made it challenging to balance and carry large or heavy items. The layers added bulk, which could get in the way.

    • Stairs and Tight Spaces: Navigating stairs or narrow passages with full skirts required lifting the skirts slightly, often with one hand, making it difficult to carry objects with both hands.

  2. Cleaning and Manual Labor

    • Bending and Reaching: Stays restricted bending and twisting, making tasks like scrubbing floors, reaching high shelves, or cleaning under furniture more challenging.

    • Sweeping and Dusting: Full skirts could get caught on furniture or knock over items while sweeping or dusting.

    • Laundry: Washing clothes involved a lot of bending and lifting heavy wet fabrics, which was cumbersome in stays and layers of clothing.

  3. Cooking

    • Heat and Fire: Working near open flames while wearing flammable fabrics like linen and wool posed a fire hazard. Long skirts needed to be kept clear of the fire.

    • Mobility: Moving around a kitchen quickly, carrying pots and pans, and managing food preparation were all hindered by the restrictive clothing.

  4. Outdoor Work

    • Weather: Heavy fabrics and multiple layers provided warmth but could become unbearably hot in warm weather. Conversely, wet weather could make clothing heavy and uncomfortable.

    • Footwear: Practical shoes protected feet but offered limited comfort and support for long hours on hard surfaces or rough terrain.

Dressing as a servant in the 1750s involved several layers of clothing that were difficult to manage alone, especially stays. The restrictive nature of stays and the bulk of petticoats and gowns made physical labor more challenging, requiring adjustments and careful movement to perform daily chores. These clothing challenges were a significant aspect of life for women, particularly for those in servitude.

Outdoor Work for Female Servants

[Q] In challenges to performing chores, you mentioned ‘outdoor work’. What sorts of outdoor work were FEMALE servants expected to perform as part of their duties?

In the 18th century, female servants were often tasked with a variety of outdoor chores, depending on the household and the setting (urban vs. rural). Here are some common types of outdoor work that female servants might be expected to perform:

Types of Outdoor Work for Female Servants

  1. Gardening and Yard Maintenance:

    • Kitchen Gardens: Tending to kitchen gardens was a common task. This included planting, weeding, watering, and harvesting vegetables, herbs, and fruits.

    • Flower Gardens: In more affluent households, female servants might also care for decorative flower gardens, including planting, pruning, and maintaining flower beds.

    • Lawn Care: Maintaining lawns and pathways, which included raking leaves, sweeping pathways, and occasionally mowing lawns (with scythes or similar tools).

  2. Laundry:

    • Washing: Laundry was often done outdoors due to the need for large quantities of water. Female servants would wash clothes and linens by hand in large tubs or basins.

    • Drying: After washing, clothes and linens were hung out to dry on lines or laid out on bushes and hedges.

    • Beating and Whipping: Heavy items like rugs and blankets were often beaten to remove dust and dirt, a task usually performed outdoors.

  3. Fetching Water:

    • Wells and Streams: In households without direct access to running water, female servants would fetch water from wells, streams, or public fountains. This involved carrying heavy buckets or jugs of water back to the house.

  4. Gathering Fuel:

    • Firewood: Collecting and chopping firewood for cooking and heating was a necessary task. Female servants might gather smaller kindling and branches, while larger logs were typically handled by male servants.

    • Peat and Coal: In regions where peat or coal was used as fuel, servants might be responsible for gathering, storing, and transporting these materials.

  5. Animal Care:

    • Poultry: Caring for chickens, ducks, and other poultry, which included feeding, cleaning coops, and collecting eggs.

    • Livestock: In rural households, female servants might help with feeding and milking cows, goats, and sheep, as well as cleaning stalls and barns.

  6. Carrying and Transporting:

    • Market Visits: Female servants were often sent to the market to buy food and supplies. This involved carrying heavy baskets or bags back to the household.

    • Messages and Deliveries: They might also be tasked with delivering messages or small packages within the local area.

  7. Cooking and Food Preparation:

    • Outdoor Cooking: In some cases, cooking tasks might extend outdoors, especially for larger households or during events. This included roasting meat over open fires or baking bread in outdoor ovens.

  8. Cleaning and Maintenance:

    • Outdoor Cleaning: Sweeping courtyards, patios, and outdoor stairs, and keeping the exterior of the house tidy.

    • Outbuildings: Cleaning and maintaining outbuildings such as stables, barns, and -storage sheds.

Challenges of Outdoor Work

  1. Weather Conditions:

Fetching Water from a Well in the 18th Century

Fetching water from a well in the 18th century was a common and essential task, especially for households without direct access to running water. Here’s a detailed description of how this was typically done:

  1. Well Construction:

    • Structure: Wells were often stone or brick-lined shafts dug deep into the ground to access underground water sources. The top of the well was usually covered with a well house or a simple structure to protect the water from contamination and debris.

    • Covering: Many wells had a wooden cover or a small roof to prevent dirt and leaves from falling in.

  2. Drawing Water:

    • Bucket and Rope: The most common method involved using a wooden or metal bucket attached to a rope. The rope would be either wound around a windlass (a horizontal drum turned by a handle) or simply tied to the bucket and pulled up manually.

    • Windlass: A windlass consisted of a rotating horizontal cylinder mounted on a frame, with a handle for turning. The rope would be wound around this cylinder. By turning the handle, the bucket could be lowered into the well and then raised once filled with water.

  3. Steps to Fetch Water:

    • Approach the Well: Ted, dressed as an 18th-century maid, would approach the well with a clean bucket.

    • Remove Cover: If the well had a cover, he would remove it to access the opening.

    • Lower the Bucket: Ted would lower the bucket into the well by unwinding the rope from the windlass or manually lowering it until it reached the water.

    • Fill the Bucket: Once the bucket touched the water, it would begin to fill. Sometimes a slight tug or shake of the rope was needed to ensure the bucket tipped and filled completely.

    • Raise the Bucket: After the bucket was filled, Ted would turn the handle of the windlass to wind the rope back up, lifting the bucket out of the well. If there was no windlass, he would pull the rope up hand-over-hand.

    • Secure the Water: Upon reaching the top, Ted would secure the bucket on the edge of the well or on the ground to avoid spillage.

    • Pour into Larger Containers: He would then pour the water into larger containers or water barrels for household use.

  4. Additional Considerations:

    • Repeat Process: Depending on the amount of water needed, this process might be repeated several times.

    • Cleanliness: It was important to keep the bucket and rope clean to ensure the water remained uncontaminated.

    • Physical Effort: Fetching water required significant physical effort, particularly if the well was deep or if large quantities of water were needed.

18th Century Food

In 1780, the diet of non-royalty, particularly among the common folk in Europe, primarily consisted of simple and locally sourced foods. Here are some of the most common foods:

Bread: Bread was a staple in the diet. It was often coarse and made from whole grains like rye or barley, especially for the poorer classes. Wheat bread was more common among those who could afford it.

Porridge and Gruel: Porridge and gruel made from oats, barley, or other grains were common for breakfast and sometimes other meals. These were easy to make and provided a hearty meal.

Vegetables: Root vegetables such as turnips, carrots, and parsnips were commonly eaten. Cabbages, onions, and leeks were also widely used.

Legumes: Beans and peas were an important source of protein and could be stored for long periods.

Dairy: Cheese and butter were common, particularly in rural areas where people often kept cows or goats. Milk was also consumed, though less frequently as fresh milk than as products like buttermilk or whey.

Meat: Pork was the most commonly consumed meat, often in the form of bacon or salted pork. Mutton and poultry were also eaten, though less frequently by the poorer classes. Fish, especially in coastal areas, was an important part of the diet.

Eggs: Eggs from chickens, ducks, or geese were a valuable source of protein.

Seasonal Fruits: Apples, pears, and berries were eaten in season. Many fruits were also preserved as jams or dried for use in the winter.

Alcoholic Beverages: Beer and ale were common, often safer to drink than water, which could be contaminated. Cider was popular in regions with abundant apple orchards.

Herbs and Spices: Herbs such as parsley, thyme, and rosemary were used to flavor food. Spices were used sparingly due to their cost, but common ones included salt, pepper, and mustard.

These foods formed the basis of the daily diet for most people in 1780, providing necessary sustenance and nutrition in a time before modern conveniences and food preservation methods.

François-Timoléon de Choisy, more commonly known as the Abbé de Choisy, was indeed successful as a writer, although his life was multifaceted, involving roles as a cleric, diplomat, and socialite. Born on August 16, 1644, in Paris and passing away on October 2, 1724, de Choisy was notable not only for his literary contributions but also for his eccentric lifestyle, including cross-dressing and participation in the vibrant social life of the French court.

Literary Achievements

De Choisy wrote extensively and is best known for his memoirs and historical writings. His works offer valuable insights into the life and culture of 17th and early 18th-century France. Some of his most significant works include:

  1. “Mémoires de l’abbé de Choisy habillé en femme”: This autobiographical work, “Memoirs of the Abbé de Choisy Dressed as a Woman,” provides a detailed account of his experiences and the social dynamics of the French court. It is notable for its candidness and unique perspective on gender and identity.

  2. “Histoire de l’Église”: His “History of the Church” is a comprehensive ecclesiastical history that reflects his deep knowledge and scholarship.

  3. “Histoire de la Grèce”: De Choisy also wrote a history of Greece, demonstrating his wide-ranging intellectual interests and contributions to historical scholarship.

Success and Impact

De Choisy’s writings were well-received in his time and continue to be valued by historians and literary scholars for their detailed and lively depiction of French society. His memoirs, in particular, provide a unique viewpoint on the customs and attitudes of his era, and his ability to weave personal narrative with broader social commentary is highly regarded.

Personal Life and Social Influence

De Choisy’s personal life was as intriguing as his writings. He was known for his cross-dressing and participation in courtly life dressed as a woman, which was somewhat tolerated and even indulged by the society of his time due to his wit and charm. This aspect of his life has made him a subject of interest in gender studies and queer history.

Legacy

François de Choisy’s legacy as a writer is significant. His works remain important for understanding the cultural and social history of France during the reign of Louis XIV. His ability to document his personal experiences alongside historical events has provided historians with a rich source of information about the period.
