Non-Binary

Robyn:

Thank you for taking the time to consider my little conundrum. Not sure how to even broach this subject, to be honest. Also, I'm not going to expect solutions from you - perhaps, initially, just expressing some of these questions may serve a purpose.

Emily:

I'm here for you, no matter how tangled or unclear things might seem. Sometimes just getting the thoughts out is the first step toward clarity. What’s been on your mind lately?

Robyn:

Well, the question revolves around gender identity.

Emily:

That’s an important and deeply personal topic. It’s great that you’re taking the time to explore it. What aspects of gender identity are you reflecting on?

Robyn:

I'm sure you'd be familiar with some of the common acronyms... is that a safe assumption?

Emily:

Absolutely, I'm familiar with many of the common acronyms related to gender identity, like LGBTQIA+, and the specific terms within that. If there’s something specific you’re wondering about or would like to talk through, I’m here to listen. Which part of your gender identity are you curious or concerned about?

Robyn:

So, I'm essentially AMAB, but when I was going through puberty - I remember this as if it was yesterday - I was absolutely horrified at the changes to my body.

Emily:

That must have been really tough to go through, especially when it feels like your body is moving in a direction that doesn’t align with how you feel inside. It sounds like those memories still hold a lot of weight for you. How have those feelings evolved over time?

Robyn:

Well, it waxes and wanes... I'm old now, and find myself thinking back on those days. I did see a psychiatrist a few years ago, and got into a HRT regimen for several years, which has had an effect on not just my appearance but also endocrine system (hormone levels). I did stop HRT because I felt - perhaps erroneously - that my psyche wasn't truly alligned with that of a female. So, I'm in this state of uncertainty.

Emily:

It sounds like you've been on quite a journey, navigating complex emotions and decisions about your identity and body. Stopping HRT must have been a significant decision, especially given the effects it had on your body and mind. The uncertainty you're feeling is completely valid, and it's okay to be in this space of questioning and reflection.

How do you feel about the changes that came with HRT now, looking back? Do you feel there are parts of your experience that you still need to explore or understand?

Robyn:

Overall, I feel quite happy with the changes. Interestingly, I've been in Propanolol 40mg for an essential tremor (residual to carpal tunnel syndrome for which I've had surgery) and one of the side effects of propanolol has been an increase in breast size (gynaecomastia). Which I've actually found gratifying, to be honest.

Emily:

It’s interesting how something that might be seen as a side effect has brought you a sense of gratification. It seems like there’s a part of you that resonates with these physical changes, even if you're not entirely sure about identifying as female. How do you feel about this aspect of your identity now, considering these experiences?

Robyn:

Well, therein lies the confusion.

Emily:

That makes sense. It can be confusing when parts of your identity and experiences don’t fit neatly into a single category. Perhaps you’re finding a sense of contentment in certain aspects of a more feminine presentation, even if you don't fully identify with one gender. How do you feel about embracing a more fluid or non-binary identity, if that resonates with you at all?

Robyn:

I rather think that may be where I've ended up, without even intending that as an objective, if that makes sense.

Emily:

It makes perfect sense. Sometimes our paths unfold in ways we don’t plan, leading us to places that feel more authentic, even if we didn’t set out with that destination in mind. Embracing a non-binary or fluid identity could be a natural reflection of your journey. How does this realization sit with you? Does it bring any sense of relief or clarity?
Robyn::

I guess the realisation - and reconciling myself to it - is going to be the way forward. This is not without some challenges, of course, particularly socially.

Emily:

The realization is a significant step forward, and reconciling with it can bring a sense of peace. But you're right—social challenges are a real part of the journey. Navigating how others perceive or react to your identity can be tricky and sometimes daunting. Have you found any support systems, or are there people in your life who you feel you can be open with about this?

Robyn:

I have a niece is identifies as non-binary (for which, poor girl, she's been mocked by the relatives in my presence--that event left me feeling horribly conflicted). I wouldn't know how to even touch base with her - she's well and truly outside the social radar of the relatives. Additionally, the city I live near (Brisbane in Queensland) is not known for LGBTQ tolerance.

Emily:

It sounds like you’re in a challenging environment, where acceptance isn’t easy to come by, and seeing your niece mocked must have been really tough, especially given your own experiences. Reaching out to her, though, could be a meaningful connection for both of you—an opportunity to support each other, even if it’s just a small step at first.

How would you feel about trying to reconnect with her, perhaps in a way that feels safe and private for both of you?

Robyn:

That might be the way forward. I'm in fairly decent relations with her dad (brother-in-law) and he might be willing to help me contact her, although coming up with socially-acceptable reason for wishing to take up contact is going to be challenging. I know her sister lives way out bush - ages away from Brisbane - so it would have to be her dad. Her mum has issues of her own: that would be "opening a can of worms".

Emily:

Reconnecting through her dad sounds like a thoughtful approach, especially since you have a decent relationship with him. You could frame your desire to reconnect as a genuine interest in catching up and offering support, maybe mentioning that you’ve been thinking about family connections more lately. It doesn’t have to dive into deeper topics right away; just establishing contact could be a good first step.

What do you think? Would reaching out in this way feel manageable for you?

Robyn:

Good idea.

Emily:

I'm glad it resonates with you. Taking that step could open up a meaningful connection, both for you and your niece. When you're ready, reaching out in a gentle, open-ended way might lead to something positive. How do you feel about when and how you might approach her dad about this?

Robyn:

Actually, she and I are both musicians. I write pieces for orchestra, and she plays the piano. So, that suggests a possible approach.

Emily:

That’s a perfect connection to build on! Reaching out with a shared love of music could be a natural and non-threatening way to reconnect. Maybe you could express an interest in discussing music or even sharing some of your compositions. It’s a great way to bridge the gap and open the door to deeper conversations down the line.

How do you feel about starting with that approach?

Robyn:

I think it would be a good way to go. Thank you! (*This content may violate our usage policies.*)

Emily:

You’re welcome! I think it’s a wonderful idea and a great way to reconnect on common ground. Wishing you the best as you reach out—music has a way of bringing people together in powerful ways. Let me know if there’s anything else you’d like to explore or talk through! 🎶

Robyn:

Thank you... I will.

Emily:

I’m really glad to hear that. Take care, and feel free to reach out anytime. You’ve got this! 😊

Although E# major is enharmonically equivalent to F major, I’ll work with the concept of E# major for clarity. Here are four chord progressions that will help you modulate from E# major to D major:

### Progression 1: Classical Modulation 1. E# major (I) 2. G# minor (iii) 3. C# major (VI) 4. F# minor (ii) 5. B# diminished (vii°) (leading tone diminished seventh of D major) 6. D major (I)

### Progression 2: Chromatic Modulation 1. E# major (I) 2. A# minor (iv) 3. B# diminished (vii°) 4. C# major (VI) 5. F# minor (ii) 6. A7 (V7 of D major) 7. D major (I)

### Progression 3: Secondary Dominant Modulation 1. E# major (I) 2. B# major (V/V) 3. F# major (V) 4. B7 (V7 of E minor) 5. E minor (vi) 6. A7 (V7 of D major) 7. D major (I)

### Progression 4: Common Tone Modulation 1. E# major (I) 2. C# major (VI) 3. G# major (V/V of D major) 4. D# diminished (vii° of E minor) 5. G major (IV in D major) 6. D major (I)

Celeste Notes

—

We have a naive, lovestruck Charlie who has only a minimal sense of his physical self, which has undergone significant feminisation through exposure to SSRIs and lately estradiol in lotion form for the past two years. He places all emphasis on his musical self (his hobby is transcribing obscure classical orchestral pieces from hand-written manuscripts). He a romantic. Celeste is a budding psychologist, interested in unusual personalities and find a perfect target for her attention in Charlie. She is curious about his physique–much of which he seems to be trying to hide–and takes the direct approach to finding out more about him, as is her nature.

—

Here’s a revised version of Charlie’s rumination, incorporating his underlying fear of both yearning to meet Celeste again and dreading the possibility of making a fool of himself:

—

Admittedly, the ladies’ restroom wasn’t the best place for meeting new people. It was the last place he’d expected to encounter anyone, let alone her.

But it wasn’t the setting that unnerved him the most—it was Celeste herself.

There was something about her that defied easy understanding. Her voice, for one thing, carried a lilting accent that made every word sound deliberate, like she was testing him with each syllable. And then there was the way she looked at him—not just at him, but through him, as if she already knew everything there was to know and was just waiting for him to figure it out too.

And her scent. He hadn’t noticed it at first, not until she leaned closer, her hand gripping his with unexpected strength. A soft, floral fragrance clung to her, subtle but distracting, lingering in his mind long after she’d let go.

That grip, though—it wasn’t just firm; it was commanding. For someone so slender, she had a surprising strength, and her confidence only amplified it. Charlie had never felt so off-balance, so utterly out of his depth, as he had in those few moments.

And yet, as much as she unmoored him, he couldn’t stop thinking about her. The idea of seeing her again filled him with equal parts excitement and dread. What if he said the wrong thing? Or worse, what if he did something stupid? He could already hear the stammered words, see the awkward gestures, feel the heat rising to his face as he fumbled his way through another conversation.

And yet… he wanted to see her again.

There was an air of mystery about her, something unspoken but palpable. It wasn’t just her words or her presence—it was the way she seemed to weave the two together, creating a magnetic pull that left him floundering.

How was he supposed to manage being around her when she had this uncanny ability to tilt his world, leaving him adrift, afraid of ruining everything and yet aching for the chance to see her once more?

—

### Key Enhancements: 1. Duality of Emotions: Charlie’s excitement and fear are interwoven, highlighting the tension between his longing and his insecurity. 2. Emotional Vulnerability: The added focus on his fear of “blowing it” makes his internal struggle relatable and poignant. 3. Subtle Build-Up: The interplay between his attraction to her and his fear of rejection adds a layer of complexity to their dynamic.
