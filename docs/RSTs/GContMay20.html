  2. Stays:

    • Over the chemise, women wore stays, which are similar to what we might call a corset today. Stays were made of layers of fabric stiffened with boning, usually from whale baleen or reeds. They provided support, shaped the torso, and promoted good posture. Contrary to popular belief, stays were not typically as restrictive as corsets from later periods.

  3. Petticoats:

    • Petticoats were worn over the stays and chemise. These were full skirts that added volume and warmth. Depending on the formality of the occasion and the wealth of the wearer, a woman might wear several petticoats at once. The outermost petticoat was often decorative and matched the gown.

  4. Gown:

    • The gown itself, which could come in various styles such as the Robe à la Française or Robe à l’Anglaise, was worn over the petticoats. The Robe à la Française featured pleats at the back that flowed down from the shoulders to the floor, while the Robe à l’Anglaise had a more fitted back. These gowns were often made of silk, brocade, or fine wool, with elaborate patterns and decorations.

  1. Engageantes:

  3. Accessories:

    • Accessories were an essential part of a woman’s outfit. These included caps or bonnets, often decorated with lace and ribbons, worn to cover the hair.
