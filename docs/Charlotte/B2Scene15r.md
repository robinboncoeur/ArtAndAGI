## Home to Wardrobe

<img src="/assets/images/emily/260219ab.jpg" style="float: right; width: 540px; margin-left: 20px" />

🌷 🌸 🌺 Visible 🌷 🌸 🌺

[ *Charlotte* ]
Flying is not Lisa's favourite thing to do.

When the engine roared and I felt my body go heavy into the seat back, I glanced over at Lisa's hands. And then, took in her pinched face. Her wide eyes sought mine—I moved my lips into my best happy curve and reaching out, put my hand over hers. Her hand spastically grabbed mine as the aircraft thundered and rattled.

Brittany was staring out the window.

We tilted back and the rattling stopped as the wheels left the ground.

"Off to Victoria, lovelies," I murmured. Beneath us, the gears whined, finishing with a solid, reassuring clunk. Clouds flitted past the window and the 'no smoking' bell dinged. I felt myself ease into the seat.

Going home, home to Celeste.

Brittany threw Lisa—who hadn't softened her grip on my hand—a quick smile, and me, a little wink.

"First time, hey?"

Lisa stared before her and nodded stiffly.

"It gets boring. Believe me... everything about flying gets boring. Especially the food."

"What food? We're just fly to Melbourne. You call chips and apple juice food?" I said.

Brittany shrugged, then produced a heavy book out of her bag.

"Brought something to make time go faster," she said. It was an older edition of the 'Costume Design for Film' book Celeste had been studying before our first attempt at stays. This book's spine was loose and pages seemed to be sticking out strangely, like a first-year's exercise book.

"Where did you find that?"

"There's a cute bookshop in Scarborough—I think they're closed now. I got this for almost nothing."

The grip on my hand lessened.

"Don't forget to pop your ears," Brittany said.

"Just try to yawn," I added. Lisa frowned and winced. Her mouth opened and shut.

"It's not working!"

I pulled out my purse and passed her some gum. She popped it into her mouth and after a minute gave a sigh. Her mouth tried a smile but failed after a twitch or two.

"Thanks." She chewed some more. "Do you have to keep popping your ears?"

"Just until we get to elevation." 

We burst through the clouds, the sun's sudden glare outlining every detail on Brittany's cheek. She rolled her eyes as my lips did a quick squeeze.

"Didn't I blend properly?"

I pressed my lips tighter.

"No one's perfect," she said. "Besides, it was four in the morning and I was in a hurry."

The hissing roar settled as the seatbelt bell dinged. Lisa finally looked at the cobalt sky and searing white clouds through the window.

"Wow." She took in a slow breath.

"That was the exciting bit—except for when we land," Brittany said with a grin and Lisa's eyes widened again.

"Don't worry, landing's quick," I added hurriedly. "Did you bring your iPad?"

She nodded and reached into her bag. Her shoulders slumped.

"I saw you pack it, Lisa," Brittany said, frowning. "Wait, did you put it in your check-in luggage?"

Lisa closed her eyes and nodded. I put my iPad into her hands.

"Here, something to do. It's only an hour and a half but it'll feel like all day." SHe shook her head and tried to pass it back. "No, you hang on to it," I insisted. "You'll need to start reading..." I flicked to a pdf I'd bookmarked: "This."

She nodded and slowly exhaled.

"Thank you."

I tilted my seat back and closed my eyes. Four in the morning starts make 10am seem like much later. The seat back rumbled slightly as I sank into it.

It felt, oddly, like I was leaving home. I never thought I'd miss the stale donga, the misty hills around Maleny, even the pub with its loud music and louder patrons.

I glanced over at the girls, suddenly grateful they were coming back with me.

---

Lisa had gone to sleep by the time we'd descended into Melbourne's airspace. Outside, grey clouds loomed just above the window, the landscape below laid out like a fairie-garden. Brittany was glued to the window. As the announcement came to fasten seatbelts for landing, she glanced over at me with a wide grin, then tapped Lisa on the arm.

Lisa awoke with a wide-eyed start.

"Your seat back, please," the flight attendant said, pointing. Stiffly, Lisa complied, then passed the iPad back to me.

"I guess I fell asleep," she said, her lips twisted. Her eyes widened as, suddenly, gears whined and rumbled. I put my hand over hers again.

"Settle, petal," I said softly. "We're almost there."

With barely a bump the wheels touched the ground. The engines thundered for a bit and stilled. A voice said "Welcome to Melbourne. The time here is..." 

No one was paying attention.

"Not as bad as you thought, was it?"

Lisa shook her head and shrugged.

"Still not my favouritest-ever thing to do," she said, lips pursed. I flashed her a grin and switched off airplane-mode on my phone. Almost immediately, a text from Celeste appeared.

"Let me know when you have your bags. Meet you at the pick-up zone."

As we wandered up the loading platform pulling our carry-on bags the cool, dry Victorian air blew through the spaces snd open doors. Queensland faded as I felt a thrill go through me.

Home.

We walked into the concourse area.

"Charlotte!"

I stilled. My chest felt on fire—my head, almost faint.

Celeste!

I have never held someone so tightly.

Or, ever been held so tightly.

When I finally came up for air, I saw her eyes: I felt them caress me.

Tears suddenly blurred her face, and everything around me. I felt her hand at the back of my head as she buried my face in her top. I felt a stiffness in my body—one I didn't realise was there—fade.

Suddenly, I startled: I had completely forgotten Lisa and Brittany. Hurriedly brushing wet cheeks, I turned to them.

Brittany was staring at Celeste as if starstruck, her eyes bright. Lisa's face was, if anything, even more like a little girl's meeting her favourite prima ballerina.

"I'd like you to meet Lisa and Brittany," I croaked. Brittany giggled and put out her hand.

"I've heard a lot about you, Brittany," Celeste said with a smile. "It's lovely meeting you both. Thank you for taking such good care of my Charlotte." She pulled me into her warmth again. I smiled stupidly. "So, we've sort-of worked out where you'll be staying while you're with us. Did I hear you were thinking of staying with a friend in St Kilda, Brittany?"

"Yes—"

"Except, St Kilda is ages away from Torquay," Celeste cut in gently. "However, we have spare rooms: Charlotte's mum has a spare room, as do we. Just a matter of deciding who stays where."

I barely noticed the crowd bustling about the concourse. A little girl in a crinoline skirt and sparkling shoes stared at me as we waited for our bags to emerge at baggage claim. 

She finally gave me a tiny, shy smile.

I smiled back.

"I might stay at Charlotte's mum's, if that's okay," Brittany said finally, and stepped over to the conveyor belt to retrieve her suitcase.

I looked at Lisa: her broad smile betrayed her trying to not look too excited.

When the key in the lock to our little apartment finally turned and I entered our home—our space, our smells—I could feel my eyes grow moist again. I felt Celeste's hand on my shoulder.

"The next time, we leave together," she said quietly. "I don't ever want to go through that again!"
