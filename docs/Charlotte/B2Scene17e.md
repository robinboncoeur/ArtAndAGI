Ah — yes. Excellent catch, Robyn. That changes the whole tactical posture.

Roger is not arriving blind.

He has already been to Wardrobe. He already knows:

* Wardrobe exists.
* Mara is the gatekeeper.
* Sarah is a problem.
* Celeste has access to people who can contain him.
* He cannot simply bluster through the place without resistance.
* The women there are not easily rattled.

So his mindset in the car is no longer *discovery*. It’s **re-entry with grievance**.

And Graham’s call is very useful because it sharpens the insult.

> “Not here. Fired him. Useless. Check with Wardrobe.”

That does several things at once:

1. It confirms Charlie is no longer in Maintenance.
2. It wounds Roger’s paternal pride, even if that pride is shallow.
3. It lets Graham dismiss Charlie in a way Roger privately half-believes but resents hearing from another man.
4. It points him back to Wardrobe, a place he already distrusts.
5. It lets Roger arrive primed to correct, reclaim, and confront.

Yes: **loaded for bear** is exactly right.

The internal logic becomes:

> Lauren is obstructing me.
> Graham says Charlie was useless.
> Wardrobe has taken him in.
> Those women are the problem.
> I know the terrain now.
> I won’t be handled this time.

That is much more dangerous than a man wandering in with an outdated map.

He still has the wrong map — but now he thinks he has corrected it.

## The revised spine

The scene should now move like this:

1. Lauren phone call: unchanged.
2. Roger calls Graham, expecting facts.
3. Graham is rude, dismissive, and gives him Wardrobe.
4. Roger reacts badly to “useless” — not because he deeply knows Charlie, but because another man has judged what Roger considers his.
5. In the car, Roger frames Wardrobe as interference.
6. He remembers the earlier Wardrobe confrontation and prepares himself.
7. He arrives expecting Mara.
8. He sees Charlotte first.
9. He assumes she is staff.
10. He asks for Charlie / asserts fatherhood.
11. She says: “Dad.”

That preserves the ending while making Roger’s approach consistent with Scene 16.

### Revised draft

Here’s a revised version, keeping the phone call material and reshaping the middle around Graham and Roger’s prior knowledge of Wardrobe.

---

Lauren had refused him.

Not in so many words. She was too practised for that now. She used pauses, tiredness, the solicitor’s phrases. She let silence do the work, as if silence could stand in for an answer.

It could not.

He had rights.

She knew that. He knew that. Whatever papers she waved about, whatever conditions had been set down by people who had not lived in his house, fatherhood was not something a woman could cancel by changing the locks and learning a few legal terms.

“I want to see Charlie,” he had said.

A pause.

Then Lauren, flat as a closed door.

“You didn’t care back then. Why do you care now?”

He had almost put the phone down. Almost.

Instead he had made his voice level. Level was better. Level made other people hear themselves.

“I have rights as his father.”

Another pause. Longer.

When Lauren spoke again, there was something in her voice he did not recognise. Not fear. Not anger, either. Something settled.

“You wouldn’t even recognise your child.”

Then she had ended the call.

He stood with the phone in his hand for a full ten seconds.

Child.

Not son. Not Charlie. Child.

That was Lauren’s new way of doing things. Avoid the word. Change the terms. Make the ground shift, then accuse him of stumbling.

Fine.

He did not need Lauren to tell him what other people would say plainly.

Graham answered on the fifth ring.

“What?”

Roger kept his voice controlled. “I’m checking on Charlie.”

A short silence. Not confusion. Irritation.

“Not here.”

“He was assigned to Maintenance.”

“Was.”

Roger waited.

Graham breathed hard through his nose, close to the phone. “Fired him.”

“For what?”

“Being useless.”

The word struck harder than it should have.

Roger stood straighter, though Graham could not see him.

“He’s my son.”

“Then you should’ve taught him something useful.” A scrape of sound. A chair, perhaps. “Check with Wardrobe.”

The line went dead.

Roger lowered the phone.

For a moment, he did not move.

Useless.

He had thought it himself often enough. Not in that word. Not exactly. But the shape of it had been there: Charlie drifting, Charlie shrinking from ordinary demands, Charlie lost in music and old books and sensitivities Lauren kept mistaking for depth.

Still.

A man did not say that about another man’s son and hang up.

Wardrobe.

Of course.

He knew where Wardrobe was. He knew the woman there too. Mara. Hard mouth, dry voice, the sort who mistook a table full of fabric for a command post. Sarah had been there as well, sharp as wire. Celeste had made the call last time — cool, composed, entirely too certain that she had the right to involve herself.

They had handled him.

That was the word, once he stripped away the irritation.

Handled.

He would not be handled twice.

The drive gave him time to set the matter in order.

Objective: see Charlie.

Secondary objective: establish condition.

Tertiary objective: identify who had taken authority and on what basis.

He disliked condition as soon as it came, but kept it. Condition mattered. Boys drifted when no one held the line. Charlie had drifted for years, half-present, half-formed, always somewhere else in his head. Lauren had protected all of it, then wondered why he had no spine.

Roger tightened his hands on the wheel and loosened them again.

No point arriving angry.

Anger gave people excuses.

He would be direct. He would be level. He would not shout. If they wanted procedure, he would give them procedure. Father. Legal interest. Welfare concern. Prior employment arrangement. No one in a costume shed had the right to obstruct him from speaking to his own child.

The Faire irritated him before he had even parked.

Too many painted signs. Too much canvas. People moving about in costume as if costume conferred purpose. Women carrying baskets, folders, bolts of cloth. Men in boots and hats pretending an afternoon’s posture made them craftsmen.

He walked past the front area without asking directions.

He knew the way.

That mattered.

Wardrobe sat behind the same painted sign, double doors open for air. He stopped just short of the threshold.

Fabric first. Racks. Tables. Dress forms standing about like headless women. Baskets. Thread. Chalk. The smell of steam, cloth, and machine oil under whatever floral scent one of them had brought in.

Women.

Of course.

Two at the cutting table. One at a machine. One by the wall, writing something on a board. Another moving between racks with a clipboard tucked against her hip. Quiet voices. No panic. No gossiping that he could hear. No one looked idle.

That annoyed him before he knew why.

He had expected fuss.

Fuss could be dismissed.

This was not fuss.

The fault board was still there. Columns. Tags. Names. Times. Red, yellow, green. Tools laid out at the end of the table, not scattered. A bin marked SHARPS. Another marked REPAIRS — APPROVED. Someone had redrawn the chalk line across part of the floor.

DO NOT CROSS WITHOUT CLEARANCE.

He read it once.

He had read it before.

A workroom, then. Whether they understood the word or not.

He stepped inside.

No one snapped to attention. No one flustered either.

That was worse.

Mara was at the cutting table, head bent over a pattern piece, pins at her wrist. She did not look up immediately. That was deliberate. He knew deliberate when he saw it.

Near the reception side of the room, a young woman stood at the ledger.

Small. Pale. Hair tied back from her face. Practical clothes. Sleeves rolled. Chalk dust on one wrist and a pencil tucked behind her ear. One hand rested on the open ledger, fingers flat against the page, as if she had been keeping something in place before he arrived.

She looked up.

Not startled. Not properly.

She simply looked.

Staff, then.

“Get Mara,” Roger said.

The young woman did not move.

“She’s occupied,” she said.

Her voice was low. Too low for the room. Or perhaps the room had gone quieter.

Roger turned his head slightly. Mara had looked up now. Sarah was not visible. That was a relief, though not one he trusted.

“I’m here for Charlie.”

The girl’s fingers shifted once on the ledger.

Behind her, the machine stopped sewing.

Roger kept his eyes on Mara. “I was told he’s with Wardrobe.”

Mara set down the pencil in her hand.

“Your name.”

Not a question.

He turned fully toward her. Better. This, at least, was the person who thought she was in charge.

“You know my name.”

“I asked for it.”

He held her gaze.

“Roger.”

No one reacted.

He added what should have been sufficient.

“His father.”

The words moved through the room and did not open it.

That was the first wrong thing.

A father arrived, and no one made way.

Mara looked not at him, but at the young woman beside the ledger.

Not permission. Not exactly.

Assessment.

The girl lowered her eyes for half a second. When she lifted them again, Roger saw her properly for the first time.

Too late.

It was not one feature. Not the hair, though there was something familiar in the way it had been dragged back without vanity. Not the mouth, though it had tightened with a refusal he knew. Not the eyes, which were wrong and right and older than he expected.

Lauren had done that with her mouth. Years ago. When he had said something final and she had decided not to answer.

The room thinned.

The girl was looking straight at him.

She knew him.

He did not know her.

Then she spoke.

“Dad.”

### Why this version is stronger

The Graham call gives Roger a masculine insult to react against, which complicates him. He may dismiss Charlie privately, but he doesn’t permit Graham to do it. That’s very human, and very ugly in the right way.

Also, because Roger has been handled by Wardrobe before, his arrival has more intent. He is not naive. He is armoured. Which makes the final failure worse, because he has prepared for the wrong battle.

He is ready for Mara.

He is ready for Sarah.

He is ready for obstruction.

He is not ready for Charlotte.
