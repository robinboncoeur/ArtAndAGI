[ *C* ]

That evening, two dresses still hung accusingly on the rail, as if mocking me through the gaping split between the bodice and the skirt. I avoided looking at the discarded zippers—a bad yes that should have been a firm no.

The girls had all left. Fiona was locking up. She seemed to be avoiding me.

My bra felt tight.

And my throat: tighter.

I shivered as I stepped into the evening air: it had quickly cooled even before the sun had set. A curlew inspected me from the safety of a patch of gumtrees—she didn't seem to entirely approve.

*I've failed.*

I unlocked the donga, the familiar staleness hardly noticeable anymore, and went into the kitchenette. Stopped and stared at the wine Brittany had left a few nights back. Shook my head and folded onto the couch, phone in hand.

My shoulders eased a fraction at the sight of Celeste's face.

"Hi petal," she said gently, her head tipping slightly. "How are you?"

Words collided with tears. I closed my eyes. Nodded. Sniffed.

"Okay."

Her lip twitched—she didn't believe me.

“Alright,” she said softly. “Start with what happened. Not what you feel happened. What actually happened?”

My voice went flat. "I put zippers into bodices that were meant to lace."

“So. You knew they were wrong for those dresses.”

I nodded, my eyes stinging.

"Yes."

The moment of silence that followed seemed like forever.

"Clearly, not your idea, then."

"No." I stared at her on the screen, my lips bunched. 

"Whose, then?"

"Fiona told me that there wasn't a choice." I glared at my hand without seeing it—remembering the sick, sliding feeling of losing my footing on a slope I was already too far down to climb back up.

"Not quite," she said. “You had no *control* over what they wanted. That isn’t the same as having no *choice*.”

"I'm confused—"

My stomach loosened slightly at the little smile she gave me, tight-lipped but real.

“I know,” she said. “So I’ll make it simple. They were allowed to want the wrong thing. You were not obliged to help them do it.”

I shivered.

"I could have said no?" My shoulders rose a tiny bit. 

Celeste's eyebrows twitched. She nodded.

“They were entitled to insist,” she said. “They were not entitled to your agreement.”

My breath came out in a huge huff as I sank deeper into the odious couch.

“So the only thing I actually *needed* to fix was the split between the bodice and skirt.”

She nodded. 

“Not sure why those were failing,” she said. “You couldn’t exactly test them on yourself. Not with your figure—”

She frowned at the look on my face.

"What?"

My mouth went sideways.

"Not sure what's going on, but my bra has been really... wrong, lately."

She grinned.

“You’re telling me you can now stand in for the well-nourished women?” Her lips pressed together and her shoulders twitched. "Been doing too many sausage rolls?"

I opened my mouth as if to object, then only let out a faint puff of sound, somewhere between a huff and a laugh.

"You know I hate them," I said with a snort. "No, it's just—" I waved weakly at my chest.

"We'll go shopping when you get back," she said. "That's day after tomorrow, you know."

I closed my eyes and nodded.

"The last two dresses have the split waist seam problem. Think I've come up with a way to fix them so they won't tear again, even when those baboons are in a hurry to get to the pub."

She chortled. Then her face went serious.

“Charlotte, I’m not saying this to rub your nose in it. I’m saying it because I want you to see the shape of it. They could ask for the wrong thing all day long. That part was theirs. The moment you helped them do it, a piece of it became yours.”

I looked down.

“That doesn’t make you hopeless,” she said. “It makes you teachable. Next time, you keep your footing.”

My voice was thin. "So, I could have said no."

"Yes." Her head tipped slightly. "Sometimes, you have to."

That night, as I brushed my teeth, I looked up and found myself steadier in the mirror.

Not changed.

Clarified.

A woman—one who could say no.

[ *C* ]

Yes — I do think it can take more flesh 🌷

Not because the scene is weak. It isn’t. The **emotional spine is excellent**: failure, avoidance, Celeste separating fact from feeling, the lesson landing, and Charlotte ending on a new understanding of herself. That structure works beautifully.

What feels a little thin beside some of the earlier scenes is the **connective tissue**:
Charlotte’s body moving through the space, the silence after the girls leave, the texture of the donga, the tiny things Celeste is reading on Charlotte’s face, and the gradual shift from collapse to clarification. The dialogue is doing nearly all the work at the moment, and this scene can support more narrative weight without becoming over-written.

So no, I wouldn’t call it overworked or optimal yet. I’d call it **ready for one more pass of embodied detail**.

Here’s how I’d flesh it out while keeping it firmly in **Charlotte’s 1st person, past tense**, and preserving your shape and meaning:

---

That evening, two dresses still hung accusingly on the rail, as if they had been left there for my benefit. The split between bodice and skirt gaped open on both of them, ugly and slack, the torn seam exposing the inside as though the garments themselves had lost faith in us.

I kept my eyes off the discarded zippers on the cutting table.

A bad yes.

It should have been a firm no.

The workroom had gone strangely hollow after the girls left. All that noise and movement—machines running, scissors snipping, someone laughing from the pressing table, somebody else calling out for chalk or pins—had drained away until only the fluorescent lights and the faint tick of cooling irons remained. Fiona moved through it with her keys in hand, checking doors, switching things off one by one. She did not quite look at me.

That somehow felt worse than if she had.

I stood beside the rail a moment longer, my hands hanging uselessly by my sides. My bra had been bothering me since mid-afternoon, the band suddenly too present, the cups wrong in some unhelpful new way, and now that I was still, I could feel every bit of it. Tight under the bust. Tight across the ribs.

And my throat: tighter.

I collected my bag, said goodnight to Fiona in a voice that sounded like it belonged to somebody else, and stepped outside.

The air had cooled quickly, that sudden eucalyptus evening chill that seemed to rise from the ground rather than fall from the sky. I shivered at once. Near the gumtrees, a curlew stood in profile, still and bony and faintly prehistoric, watching me with the sort of hard reserve that made me feel I had disappointed her personally.

*I’ve failed.*

The thought arrived whole, without drama. Just a fact, dropping into place.

By the time I unlocked the donga, I was moving on habit alone. The familiar stale smell met me at the door—the faint shut-up heat of old vinyl, detergent, dust, and my own things—and I barely noticed it. I let the door close behind me, dropped my bag by the kitchenette bench, and stopped.

The bottle of wine Brittany had left a few nights earlier sat where she’d put it, cheerful and accidental, as if it had every expectation of being opened in the company of gossip and laughter instead of this.

I stared at it for a second, then shook my head.

No.

I folded myself onto the couch with my phone already in my hand, the cushions giving way beneath me with that same damp, reluctant sag I hated. I didn’t think. I just pressed Celeste’s name.

When her face appeared, something in my shoulders loosened before I had decided to let it.

She was at home, propped somewhere warm and familiar, lamp-light catching one side of her face. Her expression changed the moment she saw me—not dramatically, just a slight stilling, a sharpening. Her head tipped a little.

“Hi petal,” she said gently. “How are you?”

That should have been easy to answer. It wasn’t.

Words rose, collided with tears, and dissolved. My mouth pulled tight. I closed my eyes, nodded as if that might somehow make the lie easier to say, then sniffed once, annoyed with myself.

“Okay.”

Her lip twitched.

She did not believe me for a second.

“Alright,” she said softly. “Start with what happened. Not what you feel happened. What actually happened?”

I swallowed. The distinction ought to have helped. Instead it made me aware of how thoroughly feeling had wrapped itself around every fact of the afternoon. I had to pick the truth out of shame like unpicking stitches.

My voice came out flatter than I meant it to.

“I put zippers into bodices that were meant to lace.”

There was a tiny pause. Not uncertainty. Placement.

“So,” she said, calm as ever, “you knew they were wrong for those dresses.”

My eyes stung again. I nodded.

“Yes.”

The silence that followed did not feel empty. It felt like standing on a mark while someone much more sure-footed than I was worked out the geometry of a thing.

“Clearly not your idea, then.”

“No.” I kept staring at her, lips bunched stupidly, trying not to cry again. “No, it wasn’t.”

“Whose, then?”

“Fiona told me there wasn’t a choice.”

The moment I said it, I was back there—that sick, sliding feeling of losing my footing on ground I had never really felt solid on to begin with. Management wanting something. Fiona caught between pressure and practicality. Me knowing enough to be uneasy, but not enough, apparently, to hold the line.

Celeste’s face softened, though not with agreement.

“Not quite,” she said. “You had no *control* over what they wanted. That isn’t the same as having no *choice*.”

I frowned at the screen.

“I’m confused—”

That drew the smallest smile from her: tight-lipped, sympathetic, and real. Not amused at me. Pleased, maybe, that I had said the true thing instead of pretending I understood.

“I know,” she said. “So I’ll make it simple. They were allowed to want the wrong thing. You were not obliged to help them do it.”

I felt the words go through me in a way I didn’t entirely like at first. Not because they were unfair. Because they landed. A fresh shiver ran over my skin, and this time it had nothing to do with the evening air.

“I could have said no?” I asked.

It sounded strange, even to me. Small. As if I were asking permission after the fact for a thing I had not realised belonged to me.

Celeste’s eyebrows twitched. She nodded once.

“They were entitled to insist,” she said. “They were not entitled to your agreement.”

I let out a huge breath and sank further into the couch, all the fight gone out of me now that the shape of the mistake was becoming visible. It was oddly relieving, seeing it properly. Painful, but clean.

“So the only thing I actually *needed* to fix was the split between the bodice and skirt.”

She nodded again.

“That’s the real fault,” she said. “Not sure yet why those were failing. And you couldn’t exactly do a proper wear test on yourself. Not with your figure—”

She broke off, frowning.

I must have made a face.

“What?”

My mouth went sideways. I looked down at myself, then back at her.

“Not sure what’s going on,” I said, “but my bra has been really… wrong, lately.”

For a moment she only stared. Then she grinned.

“You’re telling me you can now stand in for the well-nourished women?”

Her lips pressed together. One shoulder twitched.

“Been doing too many sausage rolls?”

I opened my mouth to object, failed, and what came out instead was a pathetic little burst of sound somewhere between a huff and a laugh.

“You know I hate them,” I said, snorting despite myself. “No, it’s just—”

I made a weak gesture at my chest.

Her expression gentled at once.

“We’ll go shopping when you get back,” she said. “That’s day after tomorrow, you know.”

I closed my eyes and nodded.

Day after tomorrow.

The words settled over me like something warm being laid over cold shoulders.

“The last two dresses have the split waist seam problem,” I said. “I think I’ve come up with a way to fix them so they won’t tear again, even when those baboons are in a hurry to get to the pub.”

She chortled—actually chortled—and I felt another tiny knot inside me give way.

Then she went serious again, and I knew at once she was about to say the part I would need most and enjoy least.

“Charlotte,” she said, “I’m not saying this to rub your nose in it. I’m saying it because I want you to see the shape of it. They could ask for the wrong thing all day long. That part was theirs. The moment you helped them do it, a piece of it became yours.”

I looked down at my lap.

At my hands.

At the thumb I had worried half-raw without noticing.

“That doesn’t make you hopeless,” she said. “It makes you teachable. Next time, you keep your footing.”

My voice came out thin.

“So, I could have said no.”

“Yes,” she said.

Her head tipped slightly, the way it did when she was giving me something plain and solid and not especially negotiable.

“Sometimes, you have to.”

After we said goodnight, I stayed sitting there for a while with the dark phone screen in my hand, listening to the little sounds the donga made around me. The tiny hum of the bar fridge. A tick in the kitchenette as something cooled. Wind brushing at the outside wall. Nothing had changed in the room.

But I had.

Not hugely. Not in some miraculous, triumphant way. Just enough to feel the difference.

Later, brushing my teeth in the narrow little bathroom, I looked up by accident and caught my own eyes in the mirror.

I stopped.

There was still tiredness in my face. Still the faint puffiness from crying. My hair was a mess. I looked exactly like a girl who had had a rotten afternoon and knew it.

But I also looked steadier.

Not changed.

Clarified.

A woman—one who could say no.

---

My verdict: **this scene absolutely benefits from fleshing out**. Not by adding more plot, but by giving Charlotte’s experience more air, body, and texture. The bones were already good; this just lets them carry more weight.

The three strongest gains in the revision are:

1. the **empty-workroom / evening / donga transition** now carries emotion instead of just moving logistics along,
2. Celeste’s guidance has a little more **dramatic placement and authority**,
3. Charlotte’s ending feels more **earned through embodiment**, not only through the spoken lesson.

If you like, I can also do a second pass that makes it either **a touch more lyrical** or **a touch more restrained and commercially clean**, depending on which way you want Charlotte’s narration to lean.
