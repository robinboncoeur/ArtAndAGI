[ *Brittany* ]

Charlotte's mum was... different. Nothing like I expected.

Sarah and Lauren reminded me of ballerinas dancing a pas de deux. Sarah would pull a wet plastic container out of the dishwasher, and Lauren would take it from her, tea-towel aleady in hand to dry it. Sarah's hips would slip sideways just enough to let Lauren slide past her in the small kitchette. They were chalk and cheese in so many ways: Lauren played the violin; Sarah typed on her Macbook Air with headphones on—not music: politics—until the early hours of the morning; Lauren loved prawns while Sarah had a thing for olives... but the gentleness between them, the care in Sarah's eyes, the tender strokes of the brush Lauren gave Sarah's blond hair as they watched TV made me envious.

Dinner that night was jacket potatoes with cheese and vegemite. I had just given the potato a slice and a squeeze for the pat of butter when I looked up to see that Sarah had stilled.

She was looking at Lauren, her eyes sharp.

Lauren stood at the window, her body rigid, her gaze fixed on something on the street below.

"Back again?" Sarah rose quickly and stepping to the curtain, gave it a quick yank. She eyed Lauren, lips tight. "Sit."

Lauren's shoulders sank slightly and swallowing, placed her body into her chair as if it pained her to sit. Sarah turned to me.

"I don't know what Charlotte told you—" She paused. 

I shook my head.

"Nothing."

Sarah nodded and glanced at Lauren, who was staring before her, her face expressionless.

"Look, it's just one of those things *some* men do," Sarah said finally, her eyes flicking towards Lauren, who gave a sigh and looked at me.

"He can't let go," she said. "We're two weeks away from getting the divorce finalised and he's not coping at all."

"He's not really a bad sort of person," Sarah cut in. "Just a bit insecure." She caught Lauren's raised left eyebrow. "Okay, a lot insecure. But that's worse, in a way."

Lauren sliced open her potato, and frowned at the steam rising towards the light over the table.

"I just want my life back," she said quietly. "The marriage had been over for years. Like, over ten years! I had given up when Charl— Charlotte was still in grade school." She must have read something in my eyes. "Did she, um, tell you?"

"That she wasn't always a girl?"

A quick frown crossed her face, like a breath on glass. She nodded.

"It sort of came out at a pool party," I said.

Sarah grinned.

"I was so grateful for you being there for her, Brittany," she said. "We didn't have much time to get to know each other: Fiona kept me pretty busy."

"And then, this mess happened," Lauren said quietly. "Don't know what I would do without Sarah."

Sarah said nothing for a moment. Finally, she dipped her knife into the vegemite.

"Let's go over this," she said, stabbing at her potato as if it had offended her. "This is no way to live, Lauren, constantly wondering and worrying." She looked at me. "Not fair on Brittany either, being exposed to this."

"Do you want me to go out there and tell him to go away?"

Sarah's voice was cold.

"No."

We ate silently. The evening breeze blowing in through the kitchette window had grown cold. Sarah stood at the window for a moment before she closed it. She had a set expression on her face when she returned to the table.

"We need to start documenting."

"Write down licence plate numbers?"

"Pictures." Sarah turned to me. "How new is your phone?"

Dad had insisted on getting me the latest model back in April. I handed it to her.

"Oh. iPhone. How do these take pictures?"

I flipped to the camera icon.

"Do you mind?"

"Please, take as many as you need," I replied. "Oh, and here's how you zoom."

Sarah threw on her cardigan.

"Where are you going?" Lauren asked, eyes wide.

"I'm not going to keep the fact that I'm taking pictures of his stalking activities my little secret," Sarah said, her jaw set. She held up a hand. "Just let me deal with this, okay?"

The cold air blew briefly through the open door until she closed it behind her with a decided click. We sat in silence.

"Sarah's unstoppable," Lauren said finally.

I nodded. "So I've noticed."

We heard her raised voice beyond the door. Lauren rose and stepped hurriedly face towards the door. It swung open then, and Sarah entered, the sound of a car driving away hehind her. She handed me my phone with a look of satisfaction.

"Can you send me them, Brittany?"

"What did he say—" Lauren started when Sarah gave a sharp laugh.

"To me taking pictures at night? Of him? He wasn't happy," she said, her face grim. "And he was even less happy when I told him what they were for."

"So, what did you tell him?"

“I said: 'Roger, do not come to my home uninvited. Do not wait outside my house. Do not contact me except by text regarding practical matters. If this continues, I will treat it as harassment'.”

"He's not going to give up, you know." Lauren seemed to shrink in resignation. Sarah shook her head.

"Leave it with me. I'm not done yet."

---

Lisa and I walked into Wardrobe the next day with the excitement of farm kids at a country fair. We walked cautiously along rails of perfectly finished costumes, tables with pattern-covered fabric, like the great reveal after a master painter's final brushstroke. Charlotte took my hand and led me to a rather serious woman wearing an businesslike apron and a frown. She brightened when she saw Charlotte.

"How's my nightingale, then?" she said. Her voice was warm even though she didn't smile.

"So happy to be back," Charlotte said, and turned to us. "Mara, this is Lisa and Brittany."

Mara gave a single nod. "Girls." She glanced at the dog-eared book I was holding. "Good. Lot of truth in that book." She motioned them over to an old table with an open drawer.

"We start at the beginning. In the beginning, you sort grommets."

I frowned.

"Sort—"

"Physics before design," Mara said, pulling out a tin.

I glanced at Lisa and, shrugging, put my book on the table and sat down in front of the open drawer. Lisa was given a lame and a skirt.

"This skirt has a problem. Find it, and tell me what the problem is."

Celeste appeared.

"Need your help in reception, Mara," she said quietly. Sarah joined them and they moved through the door, which closed with a quiet click. The steam machine hissed and in a corner, a sewing machine whirred. I stared at the pile of grommets and finally picked one up and inspected it.

*Physics.*

"Small, and yet, so important." Charlotte stood behind me looking at the grommet. We glanced at each other as an angry male voice from reception intruded the hush and her mouth curved downward. She tipped her head slightly, her look frosty. "My dad."

Our shoulders twitched as the reception door to the outside slammed. Charlotte closed her eyes and sighed.

"Why doesn't he just chill?"

Lisa held the skirt—turned inside-out—up to Charlotte.

"This it?"

Charlotte nodded. "You got it." Lisa reached for the lame. "Wait."

When the women returned, Sarah went straight over to Lauren, who was on her laptop. Mara strode deliberately over to us. She inspected the seam Lisa was holding up and dipped her chin.

"Sewn in a hurry, and in fear." She handed the lame to Lisa. "So, unpick: slowly and confidently."

I spent the day with grommets. As Sarah started turning off lights, Mara took off her apron.

"Charlotte tells me you've created a caracao jacket design."

I nervously pulled it out of the plastic envelope and spread it out on the table. Mara covered her mouth as she studied it for what felt like hours. Finally, she said,

"This has promise." She picked up her purse and turned to me. "Tomorrow, you're in testing with Charlotte. You and Lisa will test costumes. Physics."

I put my pattern away, wondering if we were ever going to start on design.

---

I looked out of the back seat window at the evening sky, clutching my Costume Design book and caracao jacket pattern folder. 

"Testing is going to give you that 'ah-hah' moment, Brittany," Sarah said as she turned on the indicator to turn into their street.

*I swear she can read minds!*

"I guess—" I began when I heard Lauren groan. The car sped up.

"Sarah!"

"Just to the end of the street," Sarah muttered as we slowed. The indicator ticked quietly, we turned and went about 100 metres and stopped. Sarah reached into her purse pulled out her phone.

"Hello? Yes, this is Sarah. Yes." Pause. "Yes. Thank you." She turned the ignition key and the engine died. Her phone plopped into her purse. "Now, we wait."

The wait was short.

"I realise this is cheating but... do you remember Carl?"

"The sparkie?"

"Yes, him." She turned the ignition and the engine murmured. Behind us, flashing red and blue lights reflected off the windows of the houses in the street. When we pulled into her driveway, the police car's lights were still flashing. Two officers were standing next to the open driver-side window of a parked car. One of them handed the driver a piece of paper. There was a pause before the car slowly drove off.

"His cousin works for the police department," Sarah continued as we climbed the stairs to her apartment. "Sometimes, it's who you know..."

"He's not going to give up," Lauren mumbled.

"And we are not going to give in."

I made spaghetti bolognaise out of a tin for dinner, but no one ate much. I don't think their lack of appetite had much to do with my cooking, not really. Lauren even gave me a hug after dinner.

"Please don't take it personally," she said gently. "It's this— mess..."

Sarah was gripping the top of a wine bottle, her lips tight, as she started to twist it. Lauren shook her head.

"None for me, thanks."

Sarah stilled and then, with a nod put the bottle back in the cupboard.

"Yeah. I don't want to go there, either," she said. "I'll have some when I want it, not when I feel I need it."

I settled on one end of the couch with my Costume Design book as they snuggled on the other, TV on but neither paying much attention to it.
