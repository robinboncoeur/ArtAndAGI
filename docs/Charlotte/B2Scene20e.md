[Emily]

On **The Deep End**: yes. I think you’re circling something very strong here.

Brittany is actually beautifully positioned to do something the others can’t quite do, because she has three kinds of authority:

1. **She saw Charlotte before Charlotte was socially “settled.”**
   Brittany didn’t come to Charlotte through theory, ideology, or Wardrobe hierarchy. She came through need, admiration, and naming.

2. **She carries emotional evidence.**
   The dog dying. Pool Party. Charlotte comforting her. These are not arguments; they are remembered proofs.

3. **She is young enough to say the obvious without laundering it through adult caution.**
   Brittany can say something Lauren, Sarah, and even Celeste might be too tangled to say cleanly.

The key, I think, is that Brittany should not “explain Charlotte” to Lauren. That would feel too neat. Instead, she should **give Lauren a new way to see her daughter**.

Something like:

> “She didn’t do it to hurt him. She did it because he was hurting you.”

That is Brittany’s contribution in miniature.

Not: Charlotte confronted Roger because she needed recognition.
Not: Charlotte wanted to prove she was brave.
But: Charlotte saw Lauren being diminished by Roger’s imagined grievance, and she stepped between them.

That makes Charlotte’s act filial, loyal, and womanly in the best sense: not performative courage, but protective courage.

And it lets Lauren realise something devastating and beautiful:

**She had been grieving the child she thought she failed to see, while that very child was quietly defending her.**

That’s potent.

### Brittany’s best function here

I’d make Brittany the one who reframes Charlotte’s confrontation with Roger from “risk” or “recklessness” into **love**.

Lauren might initially see it as dangerous:

> “She shouldn’t have had to do that.”

Sarah might see it as evidence:

> “But she did.”

Celeste might feel implicated:

> “I taught her to stand there.”

But Brittany can cut across all of that:

> “No. She stood there because she loves her mum.”

That’s very Brittany. Simple. Direct. A little devastating.

And because Brittany was the one who named Charlotte, she has a kind of innocent priestess-role in the story. Not religious, not mystical — but narratively, yes. She recognised Charlotte before the adults were done arguing over her.

### The Pool Party allusion

Yes, absolutely bring it back — but softly.

The Pool Party can become Brittany’s private evidence that Charlotte’s courage didn’t begin with Roger. Sarah may think Queensland was the proving ground, but Brittany knows better.

Charlotte had already been brave in quieter ways.

Not brave like Sarah recognises: not barbed, not commanding, not loud. Brave in the way of someone who is frightened and still gentle. Someone exposed, humiliated, observed, and yet still attentive to another person’s hurt.

That matters because it pushes Sarah’s growth too.

Sarah’s earlier TERF-ish resistance can soften not because she has been “educated,” but because Charlotte keeps contradicting Sarah’s categories. Sarah respects behaviour. Standards. Nerve. Reliability. And now Charlotte has met her on all three fronts.

So Brittany could say something that causes Sarah to realise she missed something:

> “She was brave at the pool, too. You just didn’t call it that.”

Oof. That has teeth.

Sarah would hate how right that is. Which means it’s useful. 😄

### The dog-death memory

This might be the emotional hinge.

When Brittany’s dog died, Charlotte comforted her. That gives Brittany a deeply personal claim:

> “She knew what to do when I was sad. Not what to say. What to do.”

That distinction is important. Charlotte’s loyalty is embodied. Practical. Present.

Lauren needs to hear that, because Lauren’s guilt is partly about not seeing Charlotte clearly. Brittany can offer her a Charlotte Lauren never fully witnessed: not fragile, not merely endangered, not someone who must be protected from everything, but someone who already knows how to protect others emotionally.

Possible Brittany line:

> “When Banjo died, everyone kept telling me he’d had a good life. Charlotte didn’t. She just sat with me until I could breathe again.”

That’s the kind of detail that would undo Lauren.

And then Brittany might connect it, not too neatly, but enough:

> “That’s what she did for you, I think. With Roger. She sat in the hard place so you didn’t have to keep standing there.”

That is *lovely* territory.

### Brittany and Sarah

Brittany could also be the one who challenges Sarah without directly challenging her.

Sarah has always been sharp enough to hurt and honest enough to matter. If Brittany says something guileless, Sarah can’t easily swat it away.

For example:

> “You like her now because she was brave in a way you understand.”

Sarah:

> “Careful.”

Brittany:

> “I am being careful.”

That would land. Not as insolence, but as a young woman discovering she can name adult hypocrisy.

Sarah might not apologise in a sentimental way. More likely she’d do something Sarah-like:

> “Little menace.”

But then later, she treats Charlotte with visibly altered respect.

Maybe she stops calling her “Charlie” altogether without ceremony. Or uses “Charlotte” in front of someone who matters. Or corrects someone else.

That would be more powerful than Sarah giving a speech.

### Brittany and Celeste

Brittany could also help Celeste’s arc, but indirectly.

Celeste is coming to terms with influence: being right was not innocent. Brittany can show her the other side: influence can be dangerous, yes, but recognition can also save someone.

Brittany named Charlotte. That was influence too.

But unlike Celeste’s influence, Brittany’s naming was not strategic. It wasn’t framing. It wasn’t management. It was recognition.

That contrast could be very useful.

Celeste might be sitting with the awful awareness that she shaped Charlotte too much. Brittany could say, plainly:

> “You didn’t make her Charlotte. You made room for her.”

Celeste might reject that as too kind. But Brittany can hold the line:

> “No. I named her. I know the difference.”

That is *excellent*, I think.

Because Brittany has naming-authority in the story. She can distinguish between invention and recognition. She can say: Charlotte was not manufactured by Celeste, Mara, Wardrobe, Sarah, or Lauren. They all contributed conditions. But Charlotte was already there.

That lets Celeste breathe without being absolved.

Perfectly aligned with where you are:

**Celeste was not wrong. But she was not innocent.**

Brittany can add:

**Recognition is not creation. But it still carries responsibility.**

### Possible scene shape

I can see a strong Brittany POV scene after the Roger confrontation, perhaps not immediately after, but once the adults have gone quiet and everyone is behaving as if the crisis is over when it clearly isn’t.

Brittany observes:

* Lauren hovering around Charlotte, over-careful.
* Sarah quieter than usual, watching Charlotte with new calculation.
* Celeste trying not to manage the aftermath and failing slightly.
* Charlotte doing something ordinary — folding, cleaning, making tea, resetting a room — as though the ordinary task is the only safe thing.

Then Brittany steps into the emotional gap.

Not with a grand intervention. More likely she says one simple thing to Lauren while Charlotte is out of earshot.

Something like:

> “You know she did that for you.”

Lauren might say:

> “I know.”

Brittany:

> “No. I mean, I think she did it because he kept making you smaller.”

That’s the line. That’s the pivot. Lauren may *know* Charlotte acted bravely. But Brittany names the motive: Charlotte defended Lauren’s dignity.

Lauren then realises she has not lost a son and gained a daughter in some abstract identity sense. She has discovered the daughter who was already loyal to her — perhaps more loyal than Lauren had earned, or more loyal than Lauren knew how to receive.

### How Sarah’s respect changes

Sarah’s new respect should stay Sarah-shaped.

She doesn’t become soft overnight. She doesn’t start making speeches about inclusion. Instead, her standards change because Charlotte has met them.

Before: Sarah saw Charlotte as someone being accommodated.
After: Sarah sees Charlotte as someone who has carried cost.

That’s a major difference.

She might say to Lauren:

> “She held the room.”

Lauren:

> “Charlotte?”

Sarah:

> “Yes. Charlotte.”

That “yes” matters. Sarah choosing the name without being prompted is the apology.

Or later, to Charlotte:

> “Next time you decide to stare down a ghost, warn someone.”

Charlotte:

> “I didn’t know I was going to.”

Sarah:

> “That’s usually how bravery works.”

Dry. Exact. Sarah.

### Brittany’s deeper contribution

Brittany can make the women realise that Charlotte’s courage has always been relational.

She is not becoming assertive in a masculine, dominance-coded way. She is becoming assertive because women around her need truth held firmly. That is a beautiful way to keep the theme intact.

Charlotte’s assertiveness should not be “I finally stand up for myself” in the familiar individualist sense.

It’s better than that.

It becomes:

> “I can stand where I’m needed.”

That fits Charlotte. It fits Wardrobe. It fits the women-led structure. It also lets her retain gentleness without seeming passive.

### A lovely possible Brittany closing beat

Brittany watching Charlotte from across the room after everything, realising that the name she gave her has become truer than Brittany understood at the time:

> I had named her before I understood what naming cost.
> Not because I was clever. I wasn’t.
> I had simply seen her when everyone else was still arguing with the light.

That might be too lyrical for Brittany depending on her voice, but the underlying idea is strong.

A more Brittany-ish version:

> I used to think I’d named her because I’d noticed first.
> I didn’t think that anymore.
> I thought maybe I’d only said out loud what the room had been waiting to admit.

That’s closer. Still reflective, but not overripe.

### My instinct

Yes: let Celeste and Charlotte sort themselves out somewhat, but don’t solve everything through them. Let Brittany become the witness who widens the meaning of what Charlotte did.

The adult women are all compromised in different ways:

* **Celeste** by influence.
* **Lauren** by guilt.
* **Sarah** by prior prejudice.
* **Mara** by standards and structural certainty.
* **Charlotte** by vulnerability and need.

Brittany is not uncompromised, exactly, but she is emotionally clean in this context. She doesn’t need Charlotte to be a theory. She already needed Charlotte to be Charlotte.

So her contribution could be:

**She reminds the adults that Charlotte’s womanhood is not proven by suffering, presentation, or usefulness — but her loyalty and courage have made it impossible for them to keep treating her as provisional.**

That, to me, feels like the next rung. 🌺
