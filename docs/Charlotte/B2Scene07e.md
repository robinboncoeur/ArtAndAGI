## Over a Croissant

Brittany sat opposite me with both hands wrapped around her coffee as though she meant to warm herself back into working order. The colour had returned to her face, mostly. Not all. Her hair was tied up anyhow, and there was something almost brave about the way she was attacking the morning with sunglasses still perched on her head and half a croissant in hand.

The café windows were open to the street. Somewhere nearby, crockery clinked. Milk hissed. The smell of coffee and warm butter kept folding over the table between us.

I turned my cup slowly on its saucer.

“How could you tell?” I asked.

She looked up at once. No pretending not to know what I meant. That was one thing I liked about Brittany already.

“That you were—” She stopped herself. Not out of fear. Out of care. “That there was more going on than just you being shy?”

I nodded.

Brittany tore a neat piece from the croissant and chewed it while she thought. She didn’t rush to fill the silence. I appreciated that too.

“It wasn’t one thing,” she said at last. “It was a pile of little things that didn’t sit together unless I looked at them sideways.”

I felt my shoulders inch up.

She noticed. “That’s not me saying there’s something wrong with you, Charli.”

“I know.”

“Do you?”

I gave a small, embarrassed shrug. “I’m trying to.”

That made one corner of her mouth lift.

“Well. First off, you’re not like a bloke. Not remotely. That part was obvious nearly straight away.”

Heat touched my face, though not from shame exactly. It was stranger than that. Relief, perhaps, when someone said aloud a thing I had felt for so long in private.

Brittany went on, matter-of-fact. “You move like you’re used to making room. You listen properly. You notice what women mean, not just what they say. You don’t push yourself to the centre of anything. None of that read male to me.”

I stared at the table.

“But,” she said, and tapped one finger lightly near my cup, “there were little gaps. Tiny ones. Places where you felt more like a girl who’d missed a chunk of the briefing than anything else.”

That made me look up.

Her expression softened when she saw mine. “That’s the truth of it, from where I’m sitting.”

Without thinking, I reached into my bag for the little notebook I carried around for measurements, reminders, lists, anything I was afraid of losing. Brittany saw it and nearly laughed.

“Are you taking notes?”

“I might be.”

“Charli.”

“What?” I opened it anyway. “This seems useful.”

She sat back, squinting at me as if I were both absurd and dear. “You are such a little dag.”

I found a pen. “Go on.”

Her smile stayed, but only faintly. “All right. You asked.”

I poised the pen.

“You already had the instincts,” she said. “That was the first thing. But every now and then you’d miss the social wrapper. Like, you’d understand the feeling underneath something, but not the expected girl-way round it.”

I wrote: **instinct there / wrapper missing**

Brittany watched me write that and gave a tiny shake of her head.

“For example,” she said, “the way you reacted to the idea of the pool party.”

I looked up sharply. “That was pure terror.”

“Exactly. Too much of it.”

I frowned. “I’m not sure that helps.”

“It does,” she said. “A girl who just didn’t feel like swimming would’ve made an excuse or rolled her eyes or negotiated. You looked like you’d been shown the scaffold.”

To my horror, I let out a laugh.

Brittany pointed at me. “That. Exactly that. You’re funny, by the way. You just forget sometimes.”

I bent my head over the notebook to hide a smile that had no business appearing in the middle of such a conversation.

She sipped her coffee, then said, quieter, “And then there was the way you handled little awkward moments. You weren’t thick. You just went earnest.”

I winced.

“Mm.” She nodded. “Like that.”

“I go earnest when I’m nervous.”

“I know. And earnest is lovely, up to a point. But with girls, sometimes earnest is too much information all at once. We don’t always explain ourselves in full. Sometimes we just let each other cover for us.”

That landed. Deeply.

I wrote that down too.

**do not explain in full
let girls cover for you**

Brittany leaned over and read it upside down.

“Crikey,” she murmured. “You really are taking minutes.”

“It helps.”

“Right. Then write this properly.” She waited until I did. “You do not need to become somebody else. You only need the bits the rest of us got by osmosis.”

My pen stopped for a second before finishing the line.

I looked at it there on the page.

That was it, really. The shape of it.

Not a fraud. Not broken. Not even exactly behind. Just... under-tutored in certain things everyone else had absorbed standing around change rooms and sleepovers and bathroom mirrors and whispered conversations I’d never been invited to have.

My throat tightened unexpectedly.

Brittany saw that too, of course.

“Oh, Charli,” she said, her voice gentling. “Don’t do that.”

“Do what?”

“Look like I’ve just told you the world ended.”

“It hasn’t ended,” I said, though my voice had gone small. “It’s just—”

I shut the notebook.

The butter smell from the croissants suddenly seemed too rich. I looked past Brittany to the street, where a woman in activewear was walking a little white dog with ridiculous conviction.

“It’s just that I didn’t know if anyone would ever be able to see the difference,” I said. “Between me being... me... and me simply getting it wrong.”

Brittany was still for a moment.

Then she said, very plainly, “I can.”

I looked back at her.

“I can see it,” she said. “And the girls will too, once they know what they’re looking at. Not that they need the whole story. They don’t. But they’ll understand the practical bits. Women are actually very good at practical mercy.”

That phrase sat with me.

“Practical mercy,” I repeated.

“Yep.”

I wrote it down before I could lose it.

Brittany huffed a laugh through her nose. “I’m feeding a machine.”

“You’re helping.”

She sobered at once. “Yes. I am.”

There was no flirtation in it. No funny edge. Just clean intent. Protection, offered without ownership.

I trusted that.

After a moment I said, “Sarah asked you to watch out for me.”

Brittany’s mouth tipped slightly. “Sarah didn’t exactly hand me a badge and sash, but yes. I got the message.”

I traced the rim of my cup with one finger. “And now you know why.”

“Now I know how important it was.”

We sat with that.

A trampling little silence, but not a bad one.

Then Brittany reached for her croissant again and said, with deliberate brightness, “So. Pool party.”

I stared at her. “No.”

“Yes.”
 
“Brittany.”

“Charli.”

I felt a helpless laugh trying to break through and resisted it on principle. “I thought you’d just explained why that was an appalling idea.”

“I explained why dropping you into the deep end with no briefing was an appalling idea. Different thing.”

She took another bite, chewed, swallowed. “You should go.”

My whole body objected before my mouth did.

“No.”

“You should.”

“I absolutely should not.”

“Why?”

I gave her a look.

She lifted her brows, waiting.

I lowered my voice. “Because there is no universe in which I am appearing at a pool party in swimmers.”

“I didn’t say in swimmers.”

“People swim at pool parties, Brittany. That’s rather the point.”

“People also sit around pool parties eating chips and complaining about sunscreen and pretending they’re not looking at each other’s outfits.”

I opened my mouth.

She raised a finger. “And before you get all earnest at me again, listen. I’m not suggesting anything stupid. I’m saying we put you there safely, with cover.”

I frowned. “What kind of cover?”

Her eyes flicked sideways for just a second, then back to me. “The kind girls don’t argue with.”

It took me a moment.

Then I said, softly horrified, “Oh.”

She nodded once.

“Oh, no.”

“Oh, yes.”

I put a hand over my face.

Brittany laughed then, properly this time, though still a little croaky from the night before. “Charli, half the population has used it at some point, either truthfully or strategically.”

“I can’t say that.”

“You won’t have to. I will.”

I lowered my hand. “You would?”

“Of course I would.”

“For me.”

“Yes, for you.” She frowned. “What do you think taking someone under your wing actually means?”

Something in my chest shifted at that. Not dramatically. Just enough to hurt a little.

I looked down at the closed notebook.

“No exposure?” I asked.

“None.”

“No one trying to chuck me in?”

“I’ll make sure of it.”

“No teasing?”

“Oh, there might be teasing,” she said. “But not about that. Girls respect ‘Martha Monthly.’ It’s practically industrial law.”

That dragged a laugh out of me despite myself.

“Industrial law?”

“Absolutely.”

I shook my head.

Brittany pointed lightly at the notebook. “Write that down.”

I did not, though I smiled.

Then, because she had been so direct with me, I made myself be direct back.

“I do need help,” I said.

Her expression changed at once. Not softer exactly. More attentive.

“I know.”

“No, I mean it. Not just for that day. In general.” I looked at the table, then forced myself to meet her eyes again. “There are things I don’t know how to do because I never got taught them when everyone else did. I can feel the holes, even when I can’t name them. And I don’t want to keep blundering into them and making people do extra work around me.”

Brittany listened without interrupting.

“I don’t want a performance,” I said. “And I don’t want pity. But if you see me about to put my foot wrong, or if there are things I ought to know and just... don’t...” I swallowed. “Would you tell me?”

Brittany didn’t answer immediately. She set down her coffee first.

Then she said, “Yes. But not like a schoolteacher, and not like you’re some project.”

I nodded once.

“It’ll be one thing at a time,” she went on. “As needed. Quietly. Humanely. Same as any girl helping another girl catch up.”

Another girl.

She said it so simply.

Not with drama. Not with a speech. As though the matter had already settled itself and was no longer up for debate.

My eyes dropped to the notebook again because I couldn’t quite manage her face for a second.

I opened it.

On a blank line, I wrote:

**one thing at a time**

Brittany watched me do it and gave a small, satisfied nod.

“Good,” she said. “First lesson, then.”

I looked up warily. “That quickly?”

“That quickly.”

“All right.”

She leaned in over the table.

“When a girl gives you a graceful excuse, take it.”

I blinked. “What?”

“At the pool party. Or anywhere. If someone opens a door for you socially, don’t stand there explaining the architecture. Walk through it.”

I stared at her.

Then I wrote that down too.

**when a girl gives you a graceful excuse, take it**

Brittany sat back. “There. You’re already improving.”

I closed the notebook with more care than it strictly deserved.

Outside, a ute went past too fast. Inside, the coffee machine burst into another snort of steam. Brittany reached for the last piece of croissant and pushed the plate towards me first.

I took it.

For a moment neither of us spoke.

Then I said, “Thank you.”

She shrugged, but not dismissively. More like she didn’t want thanks made too grand.

“You’d do the same,” she said.

I thought of the sofa, the panadol, the black coffee, walking her slowly into the morning without making her feel foolish.

“Yes,” I said. “I would.”

“I know.”

That was the thing with Brittany. Once she saw something true, she didn’t fuss over it. She just put it where it belonged and went on.

I broke the last piece of croissant in two.

“So,” I said. “What exactly is one meant to wear to a pool party one has no intention whatsoever of entering?”

Brittany smiled over the rim of her cup.

“Now you’re asking the right girl.”

Yes — very much yes 🌿

That is an excellent working method, and more importantly, it suits **you**. It is structured without being rigid, which is exactly what you need while you’re training instinct into muscle memory.

I’d only refine it slightly, like this:

**1. Write the bones**
What happens. Who says what. What shifts.
Not pretty. Not polished. Just true.

At this stage, keep asking:

* What is the scene really *doing*?
* Where does the power move?
* What does each person want from the exchange?

**2. Add bodily response**
Not all of them. Just the meaningful ones.
What does Charli physically feel when something lands? Heat in her face, fingers on the cup, throat tightening, shoulders inching up, breath catching.

This is where the prose starts living in a body instead of a summary.

**3. Add atmosphere**
Now give the scene a room to happen in.
A smell, a sound, a texture, a bit of movement outside the window. Not wallpaper — only details that help mood or pacing.

A very good question here is:

* What would a camera miss but a body would notice?

**4. Read for inference**
This is the grown-up pass.
Take out anything the reader already understands from action, tone, or context. If Brittany’s kindness is already visible, you do not need to explain that she is kind. If Charli’s embarrassment is already on the page physically, you may not need to name it.

This is where the scene stops sounding “written” and starts sounding lived.

**5. Put it away, then read for intent**
This is such a strong instinct of yours. Keep it.
When you come back, ask:

* Is this the scene I meant to write?
* Does anyone feel too knowing, too polished, too explained?
* Is the emotional emphasis in the right place?
* Have I accidentally made the prose prettier than the truth?

That last one matters. Sometimes a line is lovely but wrong.

So yes — your structure is sound. Very sound. I’d just phrase it as:

**bones → body → room → inference → intent**

That is a beautifully practical ladder.

One more tiny refinement: on the first pass, don’t even worry about “who sort-of says what” in polished dialogue terms. Sometimes it is enough to write:

> Brit explains how she knew.
> Charli asks two sincere questions.
> Brit gives practical examples.
> Charli writes things down.
> Pool party comes up.
> Brit proposes cover.
> Charli resists, then asks for help properly.

That is already a real scene skeleton. Then you clothe it.

**Scene pass method**

1. What happens
2. What the body feels
3. What the room feels like
4. What can be cut because the reader already knows
5. Does this now say what I meant it to say?

That’s not a beginner’s method, by the way. That’s a serious writer’s method.

* **The door closed with a solid thunk, and the crows dropped to a faint racket outside.**

“Rule two,” she went on, “if someone offers you a drink, you take one.”

“What if I don’t want one?”

“You take one anyway. Water, soft drink, whatever. Then you have something in your hand and no one tries to manage you for the first ten minutes.”

I blinked. “Is that a real thing?”

“Yes.” She sounded almost offended that I had to ask. “An empty-handed girl attracts admin.”

I let out a startled laugh.

Brittany’s mouth twitched very slightly.

“You are terrible.”

“I’m right.”

That, annoyingly, seemed possible.

She went on in the same brisk tone, as though reciting a list she had built years earlier by trial, error, and watching other girls get things wrong.

“Rule three: if someone asks whether you’re swimming, you say, ‘Not today,’ and that’s the end of it.”

I turned that over in my head. “Just that?”

“Yes. Just that.” Her fingers tapped once on the steering wheel. “Not ‘I didn’t bring anything,’ not ‘I’m not much of a swimmer,’ not a whole family history of why. Just: not today.”

“Right.”

“Rule four: you do not say, ‘I don’t usually wear dresses.’”

I looked down sharply at the bag.

Brittany made a small sound of satisfaction. “Exactly. Because the second you say that, everyone starts looking at the dress instead of listening to you.”

I frowned. “I suppose.”

“No suppose about it. The whole point of today is that you wear the thing and then forget about it.”

I looked out the passenger window again. A girl in running shorts and a singlet crossed in front of a bakery with an iced coffee in one hand and her phone in the other, sun on her bare shoulders, careless as a cat. It struck me, suddenly and painfully, how much of womanhood looked effortless from the outside because someone had already done all the thinking.

“Rule five,” Brittany said, “don’t hover at the edge like you’re waiting to be told where to stand.”

I looked back at her. “What am I meant to do instead?”

“Pick a spot and occupy it.” She shrugged one shoulder. “Near me to begin with, if you like. Near the drinks table. Wherever. Just don’t do that thing.”

“What thing?”

She hunched her shoulders a fraction, tucked her elbows in, and put on an expression of strained politeness so accurate I burst out laughing.

“Oh no.”

“Oh yes,” she said dryly. “‘I’m just so grateful to be included in the vicinity of everyone else.’ That thing.”

“I’m not that bad.”

She gave me a long side-eye.

“Am I?”

“A bit.”

I slumped in my seat. “This is dreadful.”

“No, it isn’t. It’s useful.”

There it was again—her way of making things bearable by dragging them back into practicality.

We stopped at lights. The bag shifted against my shoes as the car settled.

Brittany drummed her thumb once on the wheel. “Also, if someone compliments your dress, you say thank you.”

I stared ahead at the red light. “That feels horribly self-involved.”

“It isn’t self-involved. It’s called not fighting people when they’re being nice.” She looked across at me. “No arguing. No shrugging. No ‘oh, this old thing,’ and definitely no telling them what it cost.”

I gave a reluctant little nod.

“Thank you,” I said.

“Good girl.”

The words hit me with a small, strange warmth, not patronising in her mouth so much as approving. I did not look at her.

The light changed. We moved on.

“Rule six,” Brittany said, as though neither of us had noticed anything in particular, “eat something if food appears.”

I laughed under my breath. “That sounds less like a social rule and more like general life advice.”

“It is both.” She shifted gears. “Girls who don’t eat at parties because they’re nervous get silly faster. Especially if somebody starts handing round drinks.”

“I’m not drinking much.”

“You’re drinking none if you’re still this wound up.”

“I am not wound up.”

“You are sitting like you’ve been strapped into place for medical transport.”

I looked down. My knees were together, ankles tucked back, both hands folded primly atop the shopping bag.

“Oh.”

“Yes, oh.”

I made myself relax by degrees, stretching my legs slightly. The new sandals box nudged against the side of the bag. I could still feel the shape of the dress in there, folded and waiting, as though I had acquired not merely an item of clothing but an obligation to become the sort of person who wore it without fuss.

Brittany must have read something of that off me, because her voice changed a little.

“Charlotte.”

I turned.

“It’s just a dress.”

I let out a breath through my nose. “I know.”

“No, you know that in theory.” She took one hand off the wheel and made a brief, dismissive little motion in the air. “I’m telling you in practice. It’s not an exam. It’s not a test of whether you’re a girl properly. It’s a dress you can sit in and walk in and wear again. That’s all.”

Something tightened and eased in me at once.

“All right,” I said quietly.

She nodded as if that were settled.

Then, because she was Brittany and could not leave seriousness alive for too long without skewering it, she added, “Also, if you fall in the pool, I will laugh first and rescue your dignity second.”

I groaned. “That is not reassuring.”

“It’s realistic.”

I leaned my head back against the seat and laughed despite myself. The road curved. Green lifted on either side, thick and summer-bright. Somewhere ahead of us there would be a house, a pool, girls in sunlight, voices, drinks sweating in hands, all the ordinary female social weather I had spent half my life watching from outside like someone peering through glass.

The bag rustled as I touched it with my fingertips.

“One more thing,” Brittany said.

I looked over.

“If you get overwhelmed, don’t vanish.”

That sobered me.

She kept her eyes on the road.

“You come and find me. Or you text me from the loo if you have to. But don’t just disappear off somewhere and make yourself miserable on your own.”

I swallowed. “Okay.”

“I mean it.”

“I know.”

For a little while after that we drove in companionable silence. Not empty silence. Settled silence. The kind that came when one girl had done the necessary thinking and the other, for once, was allowed simply to absorb.

At the next set of lights, Brittany glanced across again, her expression measuring without making a show of it.

“You’re quieter,” she said.

I looked down at the bag. “I think I understand the outfit now.”

“That wasn’t the hard part.”

“No,” I said. “I know.”

She smiled faintly. “You’ll be fine.”

I should have found that hard to believe. An hour ago I probably would have. But the bag at my feet was real. The sandals were real. The rules were real. The whole thing had been broken down into pieces small enough to carry.

You say hello.
You take a drink.
You say not today.
You say thank you.
You don’t hover.
You don’t apologise.
You don’t vanish.

Simple enough to say. Complex enough to learn.

I rested my hand lightly on the top of the shopping bag and looked out at the sunlit road unspooling before us.

For the first time since Brittany had announced the words *pool party*, I no longer felt as though I was being taken somewhere to be judged.

I felt as though I was being taken somewhere with instructions.

And for now, that was enough.

It is not about clothes.

It is about Brittany creating a lane for Charlotte.

Shopping gives girls a reason to stand side by side rather than face to face, and that matters. That is a major threshold.

---

## Beat 3 — Brittany offers survival advice about the pool party

This is where Brittany can reframe the party.

Not:

> “Now let’s talk about your swimsuit trauma.”

More:

> “Pool party doesn’t mean you have to become a pool person.”

Useful Brittany truths:

* plenty of people don’t swim
* turning up matters more than performing
* standing around with a drink and talking is acceptable
* leaving early is acceptable
* staying near the girls is normal
* you don’t owe anyone a “fun” version of yourself

That last one is very female wisdom.

---

## Beat 4 — Brittany lets slip a little protectiveness

This is where Brittany’s investment in Charlotte becomes clear.

Not by saying “I care about you.”
By assuming responsibility.

For example:

> “Just stick with me.”

Or:

> “If anyone gets annoying, you come and stand next to me.”

Or:

> “You don’t have to handle random idiots alone.”

That is very strong. It gives Charlotte cover.

And it is exactly how competent girls often protect one another: not theatrically, just operationally.

---

## Beat 5 — Charlotte absorbs what this means

Charlotte need not articulate it fully. She can just feel:

* safer
* seen
* included
* slightly stunned

This is where her internality can shine.

Not thesis. Something sensory, small, precise:

* the iced glass damp in her hand
* Brittany saying things as though they were already true
* the strange ease of being expected, not questioned

That last notion is beautiful for Charlotte.

---

# 7. The kinds of things Brittany might say

Here is a bank of Brittany-style lines you can draw from. Not all in one scene, obviously.

## On practical shopping

> “No. That’s hanger-pretty. Real-life ugly.”

> “That fabric will betray you in heat.”

> “If you have to keep adjusting it, it’s not yours.”

> “You want something you can forget you’re wearing.”

> “Cute is cheap. Useful is harder.”

> “We are not paying that for three teaspoons of fabric.”

> “You need one thing that works, not five things that almost do.”

## On money

> “Nobody our age is living some glamorous full-price life.”

> “Kmart basics are fine. Ignore class snobbery.”

> “Spend where it counts. Save where it doesn’t.”

> “Expensive and good are not the same thing.”

## On female logistics

> “Always carry a layer. Air-conditioning was designed by lunatics.”

> “Shoes need to survive the walk back to the car.”

> “Tiny bags are propaganda.”

> “Pretty things that can’t cope with sitting down are a scam.”

## On parties

> “Pool party just means water is present. It does not mean you have to become aquatic.”

> “Half of social confidence is leaving before the mood turns stupid.”

> “Stay near the girls and you’ll be fine.”

> “You can always say you’re getting another drink and relocate.”

> “You never have to explain leaving.”

## On Charlotte specifically

> “You’re very easy to shepherd.”

> “You don’t resist in an annoying way.”

> “You listen.”

> “You’re odd, but not exhausting.”

> “You don’t do that thing where people perform helplessness.”

> “You make more sense than half the population.”

That last line, delivered dryly, could be gorgeous.

---

# 8. What Charlotte might notice about Brittany

These details will make Brittany feel real.

Charlotte might notice:

* Brittany rarely hesitates once she’s made a decision
* she touches fabric with the confidence of someone used to judging quality quickly
* she looks at price tags without shame
* she doesn’t apologise for having standards
* she rejects things cleanly
* she talks while scanning, comparing, calculating
* she knows when salesgirls are being helpful and when they are being pushy
* she walks like she belongs wherever she is
* she is not intimidated by shops
* she is not seduced by shops either

That is a nice combination:
Brittany is feminine, but not prey to femininity as marketing theatre.

Charlotte may quietly admire that enormously.

---

# 9. What makes it feel female without becoming tropey

This is crucial.

You do **not** need:

* makeover language
* mirror gasps
* “you look amazing”
* montage energy
* endless trying-on
* body fascination
* lingerie weirdness
* girlish squealing

What you *do* want is:

## Competence

Women often carry huge amounts of embodied, practical competence around clothes, safety, movement, weather, cost, timing, presentation.

## Calibration

Girls often adjust themselves to context constantly:

* what shoes for this place
* what layer for that temperature
* how late to stay
* who to avoid
* what bag to bring
* how to phrase a no

## Sideways intimacy

They may say something personal while looking at a rack of dresses or stirring a drink rather than sitting in direct emotional confrontation.

## Mild correction without drama

A girl may say:

> “No, not that one.”
> “Take the bigger size.”
> “Don’t call me Brit.”
> “Bring your jacket.”

And none of it feels hostile. It feels relational.

That is a very useful female dynamic for you to trust.

---

# 10. What Brittany may be thinking, but not saying aloud

This is the subtext engine.

Brittany may be privately thinking:

* Charlotte is more comfortable around women than most people realise.
* She’s inexperienced, not false.
* She needs structure, not rescuing.
* She has no idea how exposed she is socially.
* She’s safer than most boys and more vulnerable than most girls.
* Someone should have taught her half this earlier.
* I can do that, at least a bit.
* She takes direction well.
* She does not feel like trouble.

That last one is a very powerful female judgement.

A lot of social trust between women turns on:
**Does this person feel like trouble?**

Brittany deciding Charlotte does **not** is foundational.

---

# 11. A plausible micro-arc for the outing

To make the scene emotionally satisfying, you can let it move through this arc:

### Start:

Charlotte feels uncertain and somewhat juvenile

### Middle:

Charlotte sees Brittany’s competence and realises how much female life is managed through quiet practical intelligence

### Late middle:

Brittany shares enough of herself that Charlotte no longer feels “handled,” but included

### End:

Charlotte leaves with more than clothes — she leaves with a place beside Brittany

That is the true gain.

---

# 12. A mini-scene skeleton

Here is a loose scaffold. Not polished prose, just scene bones you can hang your own language on.

---

Brittany pulled up with one hand on the wheel and sunglasses in her hair, already looking as though the afternoon belonged to her.

“Come on,” she said.

I leaned in. “Just like that?”

She looked me over. “Yes, just like that. Shoes. Bag. A proper one.”

“I have a bag.”

“You have an object with straps. Go get an actual bag.”

---

In the car:

“Also,” she said, indicating and turning, “it’s Brittany. Not Brit.”

“Sorry.”

“That’s fine. Just Brittany.”

A beat later, as though the matter had naturally led to the next:

“And you’re Charlotte.”

I looked at her. “That was decided, was it?”

“Just now.”

“By whom?”

She glanced at me, deadpan. “By the only competent person currently in this car.”

---

In the first shop:

She lifted a sundress from the rack, rubbed the fabric between finger and thumb, and put it straight back.

“No.”

“What was wrong with it?”

“Hanger-pretty.”

I frowned.

“Looks nice standing still,” she said. “Would be annoying within seven minutes of actual life.”

“You can tell that?”

She looked at me like I’d asked whether the sky could be observed.

“Yes.”

---

Later:

“I always thought girls just sort of… knew,” I said.

Brittany snorted softly and kept flicking through hangers.

“No. We buy nonsense, regret it, and eventually develop standards.”

---

At coffee:

“You were very calm in there,” she said.

“I was terrified.”

“That’s fine. Calm is what counts.”

I smiled into my drink.

She leaned back in her chair.

“You’re easy to take places, Charlotte.”

I looked up.

“That sounds like something you say about a Labrador.”

“It is,” she said. “High praise.”

---

On the pool party:

I made a face.

She noticed immediately.

“Oh, stop it,” she said. “Pool party doesn’t mean you have to fling yourself bodily into public view.”

“Doesn’t it?”

“No. It means you show up, talk to people, hold a drink for a while, and leave before the boys become anthropological.”

I laughed despite myself.

“I’m serious.”

“I know.”

“And you stay near me.”

That made me go still for half a second.

She took a sip of her drink like she hadn’t said anything unusual.

---

That little “stay near me” is the kind of line that does a tremendous amount of work.

---

# 13. Where you can deepen it even further

A few subtle additions can make the whole thing sing.

## A. Let Brittany occasionally notice Charlotte noticing

For example, Charlotte may be watching her judge fabrics or prices.

Brittany catches it:

> “What?”

> “Nothing. You’re just very fast.”

> “That’s what years of female civilisation are for.”

That kind of line gives Brittany wit and poise.

## B. Let Charlotte be genuinely surprised by ordinary female competence

Not in a patronising way. In a respectful way.

This is fertile territory for Charlotte because she is observant and humble enough to learn.

## C. Let Brittany’s standards reveal her values

She may dislike:

* impracticality
* waste
* social stupidity
* girls playing helpless
* boys demanding emotional labour
* overpriced rubbish

That tells us who she is without speeches.

## D. Let Brittany’s kindness stay structured

She is not cooing over Charlotte. She is giving her a system.

That is much stronger.

---

# 14. What Brittany might share after shopping, once the mood softens

This is probably what you were reaching for most when you asked for more context.

Here are the kinds of personal things Brittany might naturally reveal once the practical mission is done.

## About her family

* her parents are supportive but expect self-sufficiency
* they helped with the car but not with endless extras
* they want her to stand on her own feet
* she resents and respects that in equal measure

This gives her backbone and context.

## About being twenty

* she doesn’t actually feel grown-up all the time
* she is better at appearing sorted than feeling sorted
* she is tired of people assuming pretty girls have easy lives
* she has learned to get on with things rather than announce distress

## About friendship

* she values girls who are steady
* she has no patience for competitive nonsense
* she likes girls who don’t make her do all the emotional carrying
* she has had friendships where she ended up acting like the only adult in the room

That last one is excellent because it would explain why Charlotte feels refreshing to her.

Charlotte is inexperienced, yes, but not performative.

## About Charlotte herself

Brittany may eventually say, in some form:

> “You’re not hard work.”

That is the sort of sentence women often understand instantly. It sounds small. It isn’t.

It means:

* you don’t drain me
* you don’t force me to decode bullshit
* your vulnerability is real, not manipulative
* I can relax around you

For Charlotte, that would matter deeply.

---

# 15. The most important emotional truth of the scene

Let me distil the whole thing.

The deepest thing Brittany gives Charlotte on this outing is not advice, not clothes, not even the name.

It is this:

**Brittany behaves as though there is already a place beside her for Charlotte.**

No debate.
No inspection.
No ceremony.

Just:

> Come on.
> Bring a proper bag.
> It’s Brittany.
> You’re Charlotte.
> Stay near me.

That sequence alone tells a story.

It is how female society can sometimes work at its best:
firm, practical, unsentimental, inclusive through action.

And I think that is exactly why this scene has so much life in it.

---

# 16. My recommendation for tone

For Brittany, I would keep:

* dry
* confident
* lightly teasing
* observant
* not overtalkative
* not emotionally gushy
* occasionally unexpectedly kind

For Charlotte, I would keep:

* attentive
* earnest
* slightly overwhelmed
* quietly funny
* humbled by Brittany’s ease
* increasingly warmed by it

That pairing will work beautifully.

---

# 17. A few especially strong little lines worth saving

These are the ones I think have real legs:

> “Pretty is not enough. It has to behave.”

> “You want something you can forget you’re wearing.”

> “Nobody our age is living some glamorous full-price life.”

> “People think girls just know this stuff. We don’t.”

> “You’re easy to take places.”

> “Pool party doesn’t mean you have to become aquatic.”

> “Stay near me.”

Those all feel very usable to me.

**Option B:** give you a **scene blueprint in beats only**, very detailed, so you can write it yourself with full ownership.
