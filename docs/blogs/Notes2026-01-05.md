Yes. Very good direction — and, more importantly, it’s a *clean* way to let the truth surface without you (or Celeste) having to “declare” anything too early.

Hair-brushing is almost unfairly useful here, because it carries three meanings at once, all of them daylight-proof:

1. **Aftercare / caretaking** (non-sexual, practical intimacy)
2. **Claiming-by-competence** (a woman does a quiet, competent act that subtly redefines the relationship)
3. **A private recognition** (Celeste finally admits to herself what she clocked in the restroom: not “a boy in the wrong place,” but *someone she felt safe with* — because there was a girl-shaped steadiness in him she could sense, even before she could explain it)

And because Wardrobe is systems-led and ethics-forward, you can stage it so it never reads as coercion, fetish, or “test.” It reads as: **I saw you. I’m not sloppy with people. I’m choosing you.**

### How to make the “my girl” realisation land without saying it

You want the thought to arrive the way real thoughts arrive: sideways.

Celeste doesn’t think, *He is my girl*. That’s too slogan-y. She thinks things like:

* *This isn’t flirting. This is… sorting. Putting something back in order.*
* *He lets me.* (and then the follow-up: *He doesn’t let everyone.*)
* *He goes still the way girls go still when someone’s finally gentle with them.*
* *I trust him in a way I don’t trust boys. That’s it. That’s the difference.*

Let her be almost irritated by the clarity of it — because it means she has to behave accordingly. Celeste doesn’t get to pretend neutrality anymore.

### The scene that fits your current block perfectly

Set it **after** a wear-test (chemise, shift, whatever), when Sharl’s hair is a *problem* in the purely logistical sense: sweat, tangles, pins, friction at the nape from costume pieces. Something that would tempt a lesser story into “ooh intimate,” but yours stays in the lane of **work + care**.

**Beat sequence (Celeste POV):**

1. **Context anchored in competence:**
   Mara’s notes, returns pile, production chatter outside the room — this is Wardrobe, not a boudoir.

2. **Sharl’s small fracture:**
   He tries to deal with his hair alone. It’s awkward. He gets it half-right. He’s quietly annoyed with himself.

3. **Celeste’s decision arrives as an order:**
   Not romantic. Not coy. A calm, firm instruction, because that’s her authority style.

   * “Sit.”
   * “You can’t do that properly with your shoulders tense like that.”
   * “Stop fighting it. Let me.”

4. **Consent, cleanly done:**
   He hesitates. She doesn’t pounce. She simply holds the brush out like a tool, not a proposition.

   * “Yes or no, Sharl. I’m not guessing.”
     He nods. Or: “If you want.”
     And she corrects *that*, too:
   * “Not ‘if I want.’ If *you* want.”

5. **The brushing itself becomes the realisation engine:**
   The first strokes are purely functional. Then she notices his whole nervous system *downshifts*. Not arousal — relief. The kind of relief Celeste recognises from girls around other girls: *being handled without being evaluated.*

6. **Her internal hinge:**
   This is where the restroom memory recontextualises. She remembers feeling safe *immediately* — not because he was harmless, but because he wasn’t performing maleness at her.

7. **A small, decisive line that “claims” without theatrics:**
   Not “mine.” Something cleaner, sharper, more Celeste.

   * “You don’t have to earn care here.”
     or
   * “I’m not indifferent. I’m disciplined.”
     or, my favourite:
   * “I’m not letting the room misread you.”

8. **Exit back into daylight:**
   She ties his hair off, checks the nape like a tailor checks a seam, and stands back like she’s finished a job.

   * “There. Now you can move.”
     And then:
   * “Back to work.”

That last beat is crucial. It tells the reader: *this is intimate, but it isn’t indulgent.*

Celeste didn’t think of it as intimacy.

That was the sort of word people used when they wanted to excuse something sloppy.

In here, everything had a name and a purpose. Mara had trained the room that way: labels on drawers, headings on ledgers, procedures taped to the wall in plain sight. If you couldn’t describe what you were doing without blushing, you weren’t doing it at Wardrobe.

The shipment had gone out. Two hundred units, boxed and pallet-wrapped and hauled away with a driver who looked faintly scared of Mara’s clipboard. The workroom still smelled like cardboard and steam and that flat metallic tang that clung to needles when you’d been at it too long.

It was quieter now. Not calm—quiet the way a machine goes quiet between cycles.

Celeste stood by the cutting table, flipping through the post-mortem sheet with one hand and pinching the bridge of her nose with the other. Columns: *Failure points. Fix applied. Re-test.* A neat list of what had held and what had not.

Behind her, Sharl moved with that careful, economical impatience he got when he’d tried to do something alone too long.

He was wear-testing again. Not because anyone had “asked”—because the revised chemise needed a second run, and because he’d logged the first run without theatrics, and because he never lied about what pulled or rode up or chafed.

His job was to be honest when the fabric wasn’t.

The chemise itself wasn’t the problem anymore. The neckline sat properly now. The sleeve cuffs had enough ease. The seams didn’t bite when he lifted his arms above his head. The notes he’d given were, as usual, surgical: *two inches too tight across upper back under sustained reach; stitch tension at side seam causes micro-rolling; adjust seam allowance; retest.*

What was getting him, now, was his hair.

He’d grown it out for the 1770s work—at first as a practical concession to period silhouette, then because it stopped being a novelty and became just… his. It was long enough to catch in things. Long enough to hold sweat and pins. Long enough to make him look, at a glance, like someone who belonged on the far side of the room in the fitting mirror with the girls, not in the doorway holding a box cutter.

At the moment, it hung down his back in a thick dark fall, slightly damp at the nape. He’d tried to gather it up one-handed while still moving, still testing, still being useful. He’d gotten it twisted once, then lost it again, then caught a pin between his fingers and hissed when it stabbed him.

The sound was small, but it went through Celeste like a snapped thread.

She didn’t turn right away. She watched, in peripheral vision, as he did the thing he always did when he hit a minor limit: he tightened. Not dramatically. Just enough that his shoulders rose a fraction and his breath became shallow and contained.

He was trying to muscle his way through a problem that did not respond to force.

Celeste put the post-mortem sheet down, aligned it square with the edge of the table, and let her palm rest there for a beat—an unnecessary gesture of control she did when she needed to shift gears.

Then she turned.

Sharl had the brush in one hand now. It was one of Mara’s utilitarian things: wood handle, stiff bristles, no pretty curve, no indulgence. He’d tried two strokes. The brush caught immediately in a knot near the nape, tugged at the skin, and his whole body flinched in reflex.

He made an annoyed sound under his breath. Not a curse. He was careful even when he was frustrated, as if language could injure a room.

Celeste crossed to him and stopped just outside his space.

“Sit,” she said.

Sharl blinked. “I’m fine.”

It wasn’t defiance. It was reflex—his default insistence that nothing was wrong, that nothing required anyone’s time.

Celeste tilted her head, not unkindly, and let the pause do the work.

“You’re not ‘fine.’ You’re trying to win an argument with hair.”

His mouth twitched, almost a smile, then vanished. He looked down at the brush like it had betrayed him.

“I can do it,” he said, quieter. Determined. The same tone he used when he thought the only way to be worthy was to provide.

Celeste’s voice stayed level. “You can also sit.”

He hesitated.

Celeste didn’t reach for him. She didn’t touch his shoulder. She didn’t take the brush out of his hand. She simply held her own hand out—palm up—like a technician asking for a tool.

Sharl stared at that open hand for a second, like the room had shifted subtly under his feet.

“Is this… okay?” he asked. It came out awkward, because he wasn’t used to being asked for permission and then being the one who had to answer.

Celeste’s chest tightened and warmed at once. Not because it was cute. Because it was *clean.*

“Good question,” she said. “Yes or no. I’m not guessing.”

His throat moved.

“Yes,” he said.

And then, because he couldn’t help himself, he added softly, “If you want.”

Celeste’s eyes narrowed just a fraction. She took the brush from his fingers with steady care, but she didn’t let that line pass.

“Not ‘if I want.’” Her tone wasn’t harsh; it was exact. “If you want.”

Sharl’s gaze lifted to her, briefly. He looked like someone who had been corrected in a way that made him safer, not smaller.

“I want,” he said.

“Good.” Celeste nodded once. “Chair.”

He moved to the simple wooden chair by the sewing station and sat. His posture remained held, as if sitting was a kind of failure. His hands stayed in his lap, fingers laced together, knuckles pale.

Celeste stood behind him and gathered the heavy fall of his hair carefully, assessing with the same attention she used on fabric. Where it was damp. Where it was tangled. Where the strain points were.

She lifted it away from his neck, and he exhaled as if he hadn’t realised he was holding his breath.

That, more than anything, made her still.

Not arousal. Not embarrassment. Relief.

The first brush stroke was purely procedural. Start at the ends, work upward. Detangle without yanking. Don’t punish the scalp for a knot.

The second stroke smoothed a section and caught again, gentler. Sharl flinched reflexively, then stopped himself—like a person unlearning the expectation of pain.

Celeste adjusted her angle without comment and went in again, slower, patient as a seam ripper.

After half a dozen strokes, his shoulders lowered a fraction.

He didn’t melt. He didn’t lean back. He didn’t make a sound.

He simply… allowed it.

And that allowance rang in Celeste’s body like an answer she hadn’t realised she’d been asking since the first day she’d seen him.

The restroom flash came uninvited, sharp as a photograph.

Not the scandal of it. Not the narrative people would slap onto it if they knew. The *feeling*—the immediate quiet certainty she’d had in her chest when she’d looked at him and her brain had said, with strange calm: *This is not danger. This is not entitlement. This is someone trying to get through something without taking anything from anyone.*

She’d thought, then, that it was simply decency.

She knew better now.

Decency was common enough. Boys could perform it for applause. Men could weaponise it for access.

This wasn’t performance.

This was… alignment.

He went still in the way girls went still when another girl helped them—when the help was not a prelude, not a hook, not a claim. When the help was just help, competent and unremarked.

Celeste felt her own breath shift. She slowed without meaning to.

The brush moved steadily, making that soft dry whisper through the strands. The workroom was quiet enough that the sound became a kind of metronome.

She realised, with a small jolt that felt almost like irritation, that she had been restrained for too long.

Not from shame. From discipline. From the fear of becoming entitled.

She’d kept herself “correct.” She’d corrected culture, corrected language, corrected drift. She’d kept her stake invisible because she didn’t believe in staking people.

And in doing so, she’d let the room misread her.

She’d let Tilly drift into the edges. She’d let the girls treat him like a novelty. She’d let outsiders think he was available for their curiosity, their jokes, their soft little social experiments.

Not because Celeste hadn’t cared.

Because she’d refused to behave like a person who cared.

Sharl’s shoulders dropped another fraction, as if his body could feel her thought moving.

Celeste paused with the brush held midair and looked at him—his profile, the line of his jaw, the slight tension at the corner of his mouth as he tried not to take up space.

“You didn’t do anything wrong,” she said.

Sharl blinked, startled by words that didn’t match any question he’d asked.

“I—what?”

Celeste resumed brushing, gentler. “The hair. You’re treating it like you have to win.”

His mouth opened, then shut. He looked down at his hands.

Celeste’s voice stayed calm, but there was steel under it now.

“You don’t have to earn care here.”

His fingers tightened together.

“I wasn’t—” he started, and stopped, as if he couldn’t find a sentence that didn’t sound like apology.

Celeste didn’t let him spiral into self-correction.

“I know what you do,” she said. “You log. You fix. You carry things quietly. You think that makes you safe to keep.”

Sharl’s head dipped, the smallest movement.

Celeste brushed through a long section, smooth now, and felt something in her chest ease in a way that was almost frightening.

“That isn’t the deal,” she said.

Silence.

She continued, because she needed this said, and if she waited until she was emotional she’d say it wrong.

“I’m not indifferent,” Celeste said. “I’ve been disciplined.”

Sharl’s breath caught. He didn’t turn. He didn’t ask for more. He simply listened, as if he didn’t trust his own right to.

Celeste gathered his hair in her hand and began to twist it into a tie at the nape—neat, functional, secure. The way you secured a garment before a test. The way you secured a person inside a system so they couldn’t be casually mishandled.

“I corrected Tilly because she was two inches too close,” Celeste said, matter-of-fact. “That was the rule. But the reason I felt it so sharply is… I’m not neutral.”

Sharl’s shoulders went very still, as if his body had decided not to move until it knew the terms.

Celeste finished the tie, tugged it gently to check it would hold, and let her hand rest at the back of his neck for exactly one second—contact as brief and deliberate as a signature at the bottom of a contract.

Then she stepped away.

“I saw you,” she said, quietly. Not romantic. Not dramatic. Like a verdict.

Sharl turned his head slightly, enough that she could see the corner of his eye. His expression wasn’t triumphant. It was stunned. Like someone who’d been told, for the first time, that the thing he’d been hiding wasn’t a liability.

Celeste kept her face composed. If she let herself soften too much, the room would swallow the moment and turn it into sentiment. That was how people lost their standards.

“Are we clear?” she asked.

Sharl swallowed. “Yes.”

“Good.” Celeste nodded once. “Now you can move.”

He stood, cautiously, testing the tie without touching it. The way he tested everything—by moving inside it and noting what held.

Celeste picked up the post-mortem sheet again and slid it into place on the table, anchoring herself back in daylight.

“The revised seam allowance needs another run,” she said, businesslike. “You have fifteen minutes in that chemise. Then we log. Then you eat something that isn’t caffeine.”

Sharl’s mouth twitched again, that almost-smile.

“Yes, boss,” he murmured, and the words were affectionate only in the sense that compliance could be affection when it was chosen.

Celeste looked up. “Don’t call me that.”

His eyes flicked to hers, startled again.

Celeste’s gaze held, steady.

“I’m not your boss,” she said. “I’m your lead. There’s a difference.”

Sharl nodded once, solemn as if he’d just been handed a rule he could live inside.

“Okay,” he said.

Celeste let herself exhale.

“Keep it clean,” she said. “Then back to work.”

And it wasn’t a dismissal.

It was a promise.

Lauren noticed the hair first.

Not because it was *long*—she’d made her peace, long ago, with the fact that her son contained multitudes and that some of them refused to be marched into neat little masculinity. Not because it was *tied*—that, if anything, was an improvement.

She noticed it because it was tied the way you tied something when you had hands that knew what they were doing. Secure. Balanced. No fussy bow. Nothing performative. The knot sat clean at the nape, exactly where it wouldn’t rub, exactly where it wouldn’t pull.

Lauren had done that knot on a thousand mornings for a child who’d never asked for it, and had secretly loved her for doing it anyway.

Her gaze slid down and took in the rest with the clinical affection of a mother who had learned to look without flinching at truth.

Sharl’s shirt was ironed—someone else’s iron, she suspected, because the sleeves sat too neatly for him to have done it with his usual “good enough” impatience. His jeans were clean. His shoes were… acceptable. His hands, when he lifted them to take a roll of tape from the bench, were scrubbed in the way you scrub when you’ve been made accountable to a standard.

He looked better.

Not pretty. Not polished. Just *better.*

And the thing that hit Lauren hardest was that he looked better in the quiet way a person looks better when someone has finally expected something of them and meant it.

It wasn’t effort for attention.

It was effort for belonging.

She watched him for a minute without interrupting—watched the way he moved through the room like a support beam that had learned to stop apologising for holding up the ceiling. He was checking a hem, logging a note, sliding a finished piece into a labelled tote with care that wasn’t sentimental. No flourish. No “look at me.”

Infrastructure. Always infrastructure.

Lauren had spent years trying to explain to well-meaning teachers and smug relatives that her son wasn’t lazy. He wasn’t defiant. He just didn’t do “performance.” He did *systems.* He did the boring work that made other people’s shining possible.

The world loved the shining and treated the boring as if it were optional.

Wardrobe, for all its strangeness, looked like it had finally given him the one thing he’d never had: a place where boring was sacred.

She turned and found Celeste at the cutting table, head bent over paper, pencil in hand. The girl’s posture was all decision. All control. Not harsh, not cruel—simply uninterested in chaos. Celeste looked up at Lauren as if she’d already clocked her presence and had been allowing the room to keep moving while she waited to engage.

That alone made Lauren like her more.

No social scrambling. No insecurity acting as politeness. Just—*present.*

Lauren’s eyes flicked, almost without permission, to Sharl’s hair again and then back to Celeste’s face.

Ah.

So that was it.

Lauren didn’t *smile* at the thought. She kept her expression neutral, the way you did when you’d discovered a key piece of information and didn’t want the room to see you pocket it.

But internally: **good.**

Not because she wanted her son “matched up” like a doll. Not because she wanted romance as a distraction prize.

Because Lauren had seen what happened to boys like Sharl when no one claimed their goodness properly. They drifted. They got hired for their hands and ignored for their minds. They got taken for granted by louder people. They ended up with women who wanted a doormat or men who wanted a servant.

Celeste didn’t want a doormat.

Celeste wanted competence.

And Celeste, Lauren could see even from here, had a very specific way of looking at Sharl—an attention that wasn’t hungry, wasn’t mocking, wasn’t “isn’t this fascinating.”

It was recognitional.

Lauren’s throat tightened, unexpectedly.

She waited until the rush of the room eased—the girls moving to another station, Mara disappearing into the back with a ledger and that expression that said *no one is going to die on my watch, but some of you may suffer.*

Then Lauren drifted toward her son.

She didn’t call his name from across the room. That was for people who needed to be noticed.

She walked up beside him and spoke in a voice only he would hear.

“Hi,” she said.

Sharl startled, because he always startled when affection arrived quietly. He turned, and his face did that immediate shift—from composed worker to boy, for half a second—and then he steadied.

“Mum,” he said, warmth and caution braided together. “You didn’t say you were coming.”

“I didn’t know I was,” Lauren replied. “I had a minute. I used it.”

He nodded, as if that logic made sense in his world. “Okay.”

Lauren looked at the garment in his hands, then at the notes clipped to it. She didn’t pretend she understood the technicalities. She understood the posture of someone who mattered.

“You look… more like yourself,” she said.

Sharl blinked. “Do I?”

“Yes,” she said simply. “More solid.”

He didn’t know what to do with that. Praise made him awkward. He’d been trained by life to suspect compliments were an entry fee, that you’d have to pay them back with performance.

Lauren let the moment sit. Then she tipped her chin toward the tie at his nape.

“Your hair,” she said.

Sharl lifted a hand toward it, reflexive. “Oh. Yeah. It was… getting in the way.”

Lauren kept her tone light. “I can see that.”

He hesitated. “Celeste—” he started, and then stopped, as if saying her name out loud might be too revealing.

Lauren waited. She didn’t pounce. She didn’t tease. She’d learned that jokes were knives when your child was sensitive.

“She… helped,” he finished, quietly.

Lauren nodded once, as if that was merely a practical detail. Internally, she filed it under **confirmed.**

“That was kind,” Lauren said aloud.
