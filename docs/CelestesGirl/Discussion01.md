### To the Story

Now, on the story evolution: you’ve got a really potent premise, but there’s a delicate line to walk so it doesn’t read as “a man explaining women to women.” The way to keep it respectful—and actually insightful—is to make the instrument of awareness *the mismatch between his assumptions and the social reality*, not “he learns the truth about women.”

---

**Keep him clear about himself — but not entitled to interpret women**

He can remain “I’m a bloke” while realizing:

* the world reacts to him differently,
* he can’t predict what will feel unsafe,

That’s compelling, and it avoids the “conversion story” vibe.

**Make the women characters the authorities**

* right more often than not,

**Use “micro-costs,” not melodrama**

The lived experience is often death by paper cuts:

* being interrupted,
* being evaluated for appearance before competence,
* being “helped” in ways that reduce agency,
* being treated as public property (stares, comments, entitlement),
* safety calculations done automatically.

One or two of those per scene is enough. It’ll feel true without becoming a sermon.

The most interesting emotional arc isn’t “I’m sorry.” It’s:

* “Wait… that wasn’t about me at all.”
* “Why did I assume that was harmless?”
* “I used to move through the world like it owed me neutrality.”

Those realizations are *gold* on the page.

**A practical structure that works brilliantly**

You can alternate:

* **period/costume/craft scenes** (beauty, fabric, ritual, social choreography)

That rhythm keeps it from becoming heavy—and keeps the “period” content serving theme rather than just being pretty.

Yes — you’re getting it exactly right. The “rescued” version keeps him centred: *he’s the problem to be solved.*

And that’s where the lived-experience lens becomes potent: he isn’t simply learning “what women go through” in some abstract way. He’s experiencing what it feels like when:

* your **options are shaped by someone else’s decisions**, even benevolent ones,
* your **social role is defined publicly** before you’ve privately caught up,

## Zygote

[Q]

If it’s present in zygote form from the restroom—tiny, deniable, almost accidental—then every later escalation feels like the natural growth of something that was *always there*.

The trick is to seed it in a way that reads as **social intelligence + decisiveness**, not instant domination. Think: Celeste doesn’t “take him” on day one. She **names him**, **frames him**, and **moves him**—just slightly—before he’s even sure what happened.

Here are clean ways to do that, with waxing/waning built in.

<hr style="height:2px;border-width:0;color:blue;background-color:blue">


**The zygote moment in the ladies’ restroom**

You want three micro-beats:

---
1) She controls the *frame* (what this scene means)

Charlie is panicking internally. Celeste is calm. She chooses the interpretation.

* She doesn’t ask, “Why are you here?” (interrogation).
* She says something like, “You’re lost,” or “You’re in the wrong place,” as a statement—not a question.

That tiny move makes her the authority in the room.

---
2) She gives him an exit—but on *her* terms

This is key: “claiming” begins when the escape route is routed through her.

Examples of subtle “terms”:

* “Look at me. Breathe. Now—follow my lead.”
* “Don’t speak. Let me handle it.”
* “Hands down. Chin up. You’ll be fine.”

It’s protective, but it also installs her as the pilot.

---
3) She leaves a small hook behind

A line that keeps the connection alive without melodrama:

* “If anyone asks, you were with me.”
* “I’ll see you around. You owe me a coffee for the inconvenience.”
* “Don’t make a habit of panicking. It’s loud.”

That “if anyone asks” line is pure seed. It’s a social claim disguised as a kindness.

<hr style="height:2px;border-width:0;color:blue;background-color:blue">


**Waxing and waning, so it feels real**

Yes—let it fluctuate. Real relationships aren’t a smooth slope; they oscillate around power, especially when one person is quietly steering.

Here are the *two* patterns that keep it believable:

---
Pattern A: She advances → he pushes back → she reframes

* Celeste makes a move (a suggestion, a decision, a “come with me”).
* Charlie resists, not heroically, but sincerely.
* Celeste doesn’t argue; she redefines the resistance as unnecessary.

That keeps her powerful without turning him into a doormat on page one.

---
Pattern B: She withdraws occasionally (on purpose or irritation)

Nothing makes a “claimed” dynamic feel real like the fear of losing the shelter.

Let there be moments where she:

* is busy, doesn’t text back,
* chooses to sit with others,
* says, “Handle it yourself.”

Charlie feels the drop in oxygen. That’s when the reader sees how dependent his stability has become on her orbit. And when she returns, it feels like relief—dangerously so.

<hr style="height:2px;border-width:0;color:blue;background-color:blue">


**How to show the growth without shouting it**

Use a repeating motif that evolves. (You already love these.)

For example, a three-stage refrain:

1. Early: **“Look at me. Match my pace.”** (restroom; immediate control)
2. Middle: **“Don’t guess. Ask me.”** (she becomes his social interpreter)
3. Late: **“You know what I expect.”** (he internalises her standards)

That’s the arc in three sentences.

<hr style="height:2px;border-width:0;color:blue;background-color:blue">


**One scene seed you can drop right into the restroom**

Celeste notices something (his posture, hands, breath) and corrects it like it’s normal.

> “Stop doing that,” she says softly.
> “Doing what?”
> “Apologising with your whole body. Stand up properly.”

That is *so* zygote. It’s not sexual, not humiliating — it’s social coaching with authority.

<hr style="height:2px;border-width:0;color:blue;background-color:blue">


**What to avoid in the early seed**

To keep your new subtle tone and avoid summoning DA’s wrong aisle, avoid:

* “I’m going to make you…” language
* underwear/skirt exposure beats
* “you’re a girl” declarations
* any “caught” humiliation

Keep it about **composure, optics, and choice**—and it will read as powerful and woman-centred.

So yes: seed it early, let it ebb and flow, and let the reader slowly realise (often before Charlie does) that Celeste has been shaping the frame from the first minute.

Not because I’d saved him.

Because I’d placed him.

I felt the smile return, sharp and private.

“Yes,” I said. “Exactly.”

Mara’s eyes narrowed, but she didn’t argue with that. Instead she leaned on the doorframe, arms folded. “What’s the catch?”

“The catch,” I said pleasantly, “is that I’m short-staffed, not stupid.”

Mara huffed a laugh through her nose, then nodded toward Charlie. “If he ruins that shirt—”

“He won’t,” I said.

Charlie’s needle moved again, small and determined. It was the right kind of pressure: being seen without being mocked.

Mara drifted away, still unconvinced but no longer obstructing. She respected results. Most people did, if you forced them.

I returned to the table and placed a pair of boots beside Charlie’s elbow. Coachman’s boots. Thick leather. Mud-caked. One heel beginning to separate where the glue had started to fail.

Charlie glanced at them, startled—like I’d placed a dead animal on the table.

“These,” I said, “need cleaning and polishing. And this heel needs attention. You’ll bring them back looking like they’ve never known the yard.”

His brow furrowed. “How?”

I let the silence stretch long enough for him to feel the shape of the problem. Then I opened another drawer and laid out supplies: brush, cloth, polish, glue, a small clamp.

“You’ll work it out,” I said. “If you need one instruction, you ask it once.”

He swallowed. “Okay.”

It wasn’t confidence. It was compliance with a spine.

Good.

As he finished the cuff repair, he didn’t smooth the fabric with a careless palm. He pressed it gently, flattening the seam like he was calming something living. Then he held it up and looked at it critically, turning it as though the light itself might reveal his errors.

He wasn’t trying to impress me.

He was trying to be correct.

That difference matters.

“You’re frowning,” I observed.

“I… I can still see it,” he admitted, pointing to the tiniest irregularity in the stitch line.

“You can see it because you’re the one who did it,” I said. “The public will not. What they’ll see is a cuff that doesn’t unravel when a tray tilts.”

He blinked at that framing, as if no one had ever taught him that “good” was sometimes practical rather than perfect.

Then, because I wanted to anchor the basso continuo now—early, cleanly—I said, “Listen.”

He lifted his eyes to me, immediate.

“This room isn’t about clothes,” I said. “It’s about bodies. Clothes are what the world allows bodies to be.”

His face went blank in the way it did when something too large brushed past him. Not offended. Not excited. Just… trying to understand.

I continued, because the message mattered.

“If you handle garments with disrespect, you’re disrespecting the person who has to wear them,” I said. “Not in some abstract moral way. In a physical way. Because a seam that scratches, a stocking that slips, a boot that pinches—those things become someone’s whole day.”

Charlie’s gaze dropped to the shirt in his hands, and I watched the idea settle into him with a kind of relief.

Not fascination.

Purpose.

“I understand,” he said quietly.

“I think you do,” I replied. “Which is why you’re here.”

He swallowed. “Thank you.”

His reflex wanted to add *sorry* again, to apologise for existing in my space, for taking my time, for being a problem.

I lifted my eyebrows.

He caught himself mid-breath and corrected, carefully: “I mean… okay.”

I almost smiled.

Almost.

“Good,” I said instead, because *good* is cleaner than affection when you’re building a system.

I stood, took the repaired shirt from him, and inspected it with the same seriousness I’d give a wound dressing. The stitch was neat. The tear was gone. The cuff lay flat.

I nodded once.

That was all the reward he needed. His shoulders loosened like a knot being untied.

I placed the shirt on the “Ready” rail and turned back to him.

“Now,” I said, “boots.”

He looked at the muddy leather, then at the brush, then at me—puzzled, still. As if he couldn’t quite believe that this, too, was a kind of permission.

“You’re… sure?” he asked.

“Yes,” I said. “And Charlie—”

He stilled at his name.

I made my voice brisk, to keep it from feeling like an embrace. “If you ruin something, you tell me immediately. You don’t hide it. You don’t cry over it. You don’t try to fix it in secret.”

He flinched slightly at *cry*—not because I’d accused him, but because it hit close to a fear.

“You tell me,” I repeated. “Truth is always cheaper than damage.”

He nodded. “Truth,” he echoed, as if committing it to memory.

“That’s right,” I said. “You’ll learn our values quickly.”

*Our.*

The word was a small claim, slipped in like thread through fabric. Not ownership. Not yet.

Membership.

He didn’t react outwardly, but I saw the way it struck him—the way the room became, for him, less like a strange country and more like a shelter with rules.

He picked up the brush and began on the boots with slow, methodical strokes. Mud flaked away. Leather emerged. His hands moved with the same careful respect he’d used on the shirt, as if he were uncovering something rather than merely cleaning it.

And the strangest thing was: he looked calmer.

Not happy. Not free. But calmer—because the work made sense. Because the rules were clear. Because I was here, and my presence had a shape he could lean against without falling.

I watched him for a moment longer than necessary.

He felt it and glanced up, startled, like a bird sensing a shadow.

“Yes?” he asked, immediate—waiting for instruction.

I let my gaze hold him, steady and unreadable.

“No,” I said. “Nothing. Carry on.”

He looked down again, and the brush kept moving.

That was the start of it: not obedience, not romance, not salvation.

Just a boy doing careful work in a room where carefulness was finally useful—and a girl quietly measuring exactly how much trust he could carry before he noticed he was carrying it.

## Celeste — Trust Test One

By midmorning, Wardrobe had turned from a room into a machine.

People drifted in and out in half-dressed waves: a footman needing his cuffs re-starched, a girl with a loose cap ribbon, Mara complaining about missing pins as if pins were a personal affront. I answered without urgency, because urgency is contagious and I refused to spread it.

Charlie stayed where I put him, brush moving in steady strokes over the coachman’s boots. Mud came off in obedient flakes. Leather emerged. His shoulders were no longer up around his ears.

Good.

He’d needed a task with rules. He’d needed a way to be useful without being tested on confidence.

Which was, unfortunately, the next thing he needed.

I watched him without making it obvious. He glanced up once, startled, and I let my face remain neutral.

No reward for checking in. He had to learn to work without constantly seeking permission.

When the boots finally shone and the heel sat clamped in place like a problem that had agreed to behave, I reached for them and set them on the Ready rail.

“Nice,” I said. One syllable. Enough.

His throat moved. He nodded. He looked pleased in a way that was almost painful to witness—like someone who’d been starving and had forgotten what bread felt like.

I turned back to my clipboard. “Charlie.”

He straightened instantly. Too instantly.

“Yes?”

“There’s a missing waistcoat,” I said, flicking my pen to the inventory list. “Black. Brass buttons. Size medium. It should be on rack C with the footmen’s set.”

He frowned as if picturing the rack. “I didn’t see—”

“I know,” I said. “That’s why we’re fixing it.”

He waited, hands hovering. Always ready to be told what reality was.

I picked up a note card and wrote quickly. *Wardrobe requires waistcoat — black, brass buttons — for Footman 3. Signed: C. Shelley (Wardrobe).* Then I slid it across the table.

“Take this to Graham.”

His eyes widened. “Maintenance Graham?”

“Yes.”

He didn’t move.

The old reflex rose in him like a tide. I could almost see it: *He’ll be angry. I’ll get in trouble. I’ll say sorry. I’ll make it worse.*

This wasn’t cruelty. This was calibration.

Because if Charlie was going to work with me, he couldn’t be a ghost. He had to be able to carry my words into other rooms.

I leaned slightly forward. “Listen.”

He looked at me, immediate.

“You are not going to him as a nuisance,” I said. “You are going to him as my runner. You are carrying a request from Wardrobe. That is normal. That is allowed.”

His lips parted.

“And,” I added, “you are not going to apologise. Not once.”

He swallowed. “What if he—”

I lifted my eyebrows.

He caught himself. “Okay.”

“Good,” I said. “You’ll say exactly this.” I tapped the note card with my pen. “*Graham, Celeste needs the black waistcoat for Footman Three. Have you seen it, or should we pull a spare?*”

He repeated it silently, eyes tracking my mouth as if words were physical objects he could hold.

“Again,” I said.

He repeated it, slightly more steady.

“Again,” I said.

His jaw tightened. He said it a third time, clearer. Almost firm.

There was the line. There was the spine.

I nodded once. “Excellent. Now—go.”

He stood, then hesitated, hovering on the edge of motion like a bird at the edge of flight.

“Charlie,” I said, gently, because the gentleness mattered *precisely because* I didn’t use it often. “Look at me.”

He looked.

“Match my pace,” I said. “In your head. Walk like you’re doing a job.”

He blinked, as if walking could be reframed.

Then he nodded, turned, and left.

I watched him go. Not with sentiment. With attention.

This was the first test: could he carry a simple sentence into a room full of men and tools without collapsing into apology?

I returned to the table and began sorting lace trims, because you don’t announce that you’re waiting for a result. You behave as though the outcome is inevitable.

Mara wandered in again, eyeing the open door. “Where’s your boy gone?”

I didn’t look up. “Errand.”

Mara made a sound between amusement and disdain. “You’re brave.”

“I’m correct,” I said.

A few minutes passed. Five, perhaps. Long enough for the mind to invent catastrophes.

I pictured Charlie in the yard, shoulders hunched, saying *sorry* before he even spoke, Graham’s patience snapping. I pictured Charlie shrinking back into himself and deciding he didn’t belong anywhere.

Then I heard footsteps—too light, too careful—returning.

Charlie appeared in the doorway, cheeks flushed, eyes bright with the strange feverish sheen of someone who has just survived something they’d been certain would kill them.

He held the waistcoat in both hands as if it were a fragile animal.

Behind him, Graham’s voice carried down the corridor. “Tell her it was in the prop crate like I bloody said. And tell her to label her racks properly.”

Charlie flinched at the shouted words, but he didn’t crumple. He walked in, shut the door softly, and approached the table.

He set the waistcoat down with reverence.

I inspected it quickly: correct garment, correct buttons, faint smell of dust. It would do.

I looked up at him.

He was waiting—tense, braced—like he expected punishment for existing.

“Well?” I said, mildly.

He blinked. “I… I said the sentence.”

“Did you?” I asked, as if it were a genuine question, not an invitation to confess.

He nodded, then swallowed. “I said it exactly. He… stared at me.”

“And you did what?” I prompted.

Charlie’s throat bobbed. “I… looked back.”

That was the moment.

Not the waistcoat. Not the errand. The looking back.

He didn’t even realise he’d done it. He said it like a fact, not a victory. But I heard the victory anyway.

“And did you apologise?” I asked.

He hesitated. His mouth opened—habit reaching for the old word—

Then he stopped himself, eyes widening, as if he’d just caught his own hand reaching toward a hot stove.

“No,” he said, softly. “I didn’t.”

I let my expression remain calm, because if I rewarded him too warmly he’d begin performing for warmth. I wanted him performing for competence.

“Good,” I said. “That is what you do now.”

His shoulders loosened slightly, as if someone had undone a strap that was cutting into him.

Mara, who had been hovering by the shelves pretending not to listen, gave a reluctant snort. “He actually did it?”

I didn’t glance at her. “Of course he did.”

Charlie’s eyes flicked toward Mara, uncertainty spiking again—social danger.

I cut it off.

“Charlie,” I said, turning back to him. “You’re not here to be liked. You’re here to be useful.”

His face flushed. He nodded. “Okay.”

“And,” I added, because this part mattered, “you’re not here to be humiliated either. If anyone tries it, you tell me.”

He looked at me quickly—startled, almost disbelieving. “You… want me to tell you?”

“Yes,” I said, as if it were obvious. “That’s part of the job.”

His gaze held mine for a beat too long. That struck quality again—relief with teeth in it.

I broke eye contact first, because I could feel the hook setting and I didn’t intend to let it set too quickly.

I turned to the waistcoat, lifted it, and hung it on the Ready rail.

Then I slid another item across the table: a bundle of linen ties.

“Next,” I said, briskly. “Sort these. Count them. Tell me exactly how many. And if you don’t know, you ask once.”

Charlie took the bundle with both hands.

He didn’t smile.

But he stood a little straighter.

And somewhere in the quiet machinery of his mind, I could feel it clicking into place: when I gave him words, he could walk through rooms he’d been afraid of.

That was trust.

Not blind.

Not yet.

But alive enough to test again.

## When Celeste starts seeing “constant companion” value

Not as romance. Not even as “I need someone nearby.” It evolves as a *systems insight*.

Here’s a clean progression that will feel completely organic:

### Stage 1: Asset (today)

**Label in her head:** *runner / hands / labour redeployed*
**What she values:** obedience to process, careful touch, low chaos.

### Stage 2: Trusted implement (after discretion test)

**Label:** *safe with the private*
**What she values:** he doesn’t turn bodies into jokes, doesn’t repeat things, doesn’t get weird about women’s garments.

This is the first moment she relaxes around him — not emotionally, operationally.

### Stage 3: Extension of her attention (after initiative test)

**Label:** *he notices what I notice*
**What she values:** anticipation. He starts reducing her cognitive load. That’s addictive for a high-functioning girl who’s always carrying the room.

This is where “keep him near” becomes rational.

### Stage 4: Social shield (first public-facing day / VIP day)

**Label:** *portable authority*
Celeste discovers something delicious: Charlie can move through the Faire without provoking the same reactions as a confident man, and without triggering the same policing reactions as a confident woman. He can be invisible when needed — and *visible* when instructed.

That’s power, if you know how to use it.

### Stage 5: Identity reinforcement (later)

**Label:** *my standard made flesh*
He begins to internalise her phrasing, her tempo, her “truth is cheaper than damage.”
He starts representing her without her being present.

That’s when “constant companion” stops being a convenience and becomes a *choice*.

### Stage 6: The dangerous stage (only when you’re ready)

**Label:** *dependence loop*
He feels safest near her. She functions better with him near.
Neither calls it what it is. She tests it. He endures it. The reader sees the shape.

This is where your “tested and tested and tested again” arc becomes emotionally potent rather than mechanical.

## Celeste — Trust Test Two (Discretion)

Wardrobe has its own weather.

Outside, the Faire was waking properly now—voices lifting, hammer taps, the first laugh of someone who’d already decided the day would go their way. Inside, we lived in a quieter climate: fabric whisper, drawers sliding, the soft puncture of pins finding their place. The kind of work that looks like nothing until it fails.

Charlie moved through it like someone learning a new language without daring to mispronounce. He kept his hands clean without being reminded. He didn’t apologise. Not once since the waistcoat errand. I’d heard him swallow it twice—felt it in the tiny pause before he chose silence instead. That was progress.

Mara, unfortunately, noticed progress the way some people notice a new smell: with suspicion.

She’d stationed herself at the cutting table with a pile of stockings and an expression that suggested stockings were beneath her. Another woman—Jodie, one of the performers—had come in for a fit check and then stayed too long, leaning in the doorway as if Wardrobe were a café.

I was at the central table, inventory open, making the day’s problems into lists. Charlie was sorting linen ties by length, methodical, lips moving slightly as he counted. If he kept this up, he’d become dangerous—competent in a room where competence is rare.

Jodie’s voice drifted, casual and unkind in that “girl talk” way that pretends it isn’t a weapon. “I’m telling you, she can’t keep wearing the stays from last season. They’re… not happening.”

Mara made a sound of agreement. “Oh, I know. I said to her, darling, it’s not the garment, it’s the *geometry.* But you can’t say that, can you.”

Jodie laughed. “She’s going to pop a seam in front of the patrons. I’d pay to see it.”

Charlie’s counting slowed. Not much. Just a half-beat. But I saw it.

He kept his eyes on the ties as if they were suddenly fascinating.

I didn’t look up. I didn’t give the conversation oxygen by reacting. This, too, was part of my test. People behave differently when they think you’re listening.

Mara continued, voice dropping into that syrupy intimacy that always means cruelty. “And she’s got the nerve to complain her bodice scratches. I’m like—of course it scratches, love. It’s trying to escape.”

Another laugh.

I watched Charlie’s hands.

He didn’t stiffen. He didn’t smile. He didn’t glance up to see if I’d heard. He simply… tightened his grip on the linen ties for one moment, then deliberately loosened it, as if reminding his own fingers to be gentle.

Good.

A boy with the wrong instincts would have leaned into this. Would have used it to purchase belonging—shared mockery as currency. Even a sweet boy might have tried to be “helpful” in the worst way: offering sympathy to Jodie, making the performer’s body into a story.

Charlie did neither.

He went still inside himself. He became careful.

That was worth more than charm.

Jodie drifted away eventually, satisfied she’d deposited her poison. Mara returned to her stockings with an expression that said she’d improved the world.

Charlie finished his count and looked up, uncertain, as if he’d been holding his breath.

I kept my face neutral. Then I said, lightly, “How many?”

He blinked at the normal question, relief flashing across his eyes. “Forty-six ties. Twelve short, twenty-two medium, twelve long.”

“Good,” I said. “Put the long ones in drawer B, label the bundle. Don’t guess on the missing count—write it down.”

He nodded and stood, moving to the drawers.

Mara watched him go, then turned her gaze on me as if I’d just brought a stray animal into her kitchen. “You’re making him your pet project.”

“I’m making him useful,” I corrected, without looking up from the inventory.

Mara’s mouth twisted. “He’s a boy. They get weird.”

“They get weird when you teach them to,” I said, and then finally looked at her. “We’re not doing that here.”

Her eyes narrowed. “Who’s ‘we’?”

I let my voice stay calm. “We is Wardrobe. We is the line between bodies and spectacle. We don’t make someone’s laces into entertainment.”

Mara snorted softly. “You’re so serious.”

“Yes,” I agreed. “That’s why the patrons don’t see our failures.”

She held my gaze for a beat, then looked away first, annoyed at herself.

Good.

Charlie returned to the table and sat down again, posture straighter than before. The ties were sorted. The label was neat. He’d done exactly what I asked.

I waited until the room was quiet again—the door closed, Mara busy, no witnesses who would mistake this moment for softness.

Then I said, “Charlie.”

He looked up immediately.

“Did you hear what they were saying earlier?” I asked, mild.

His eyes widened just enough to betray him. Then he lowered his gaze, careful. “Yes.”

“And?” I prompted.

He swallowed. I could see his old reflex trying to please, to answer in a way that would make me approve. He could have condemned Mara. He could have joined her. He could have tried to be noble.

Instead, he said something much better.

“It wasn’t… about the clothes,” he said quietly.

I paused. “Go on.”

He hesitated. “It was about… her. About making her… a joke.”

The words came slowly, as if he was testing each one for correctness.

“And what did you do?” I asked.

Charlie’s mouth opened, then shut. He seemed genuinely puzzled by the question. “Nothing.”

“Exactly,” I said.

He looked up, startled, as if the word had struck him.

I leaned back slightly, watching him. “That was the test.”

His face flushed. Not with pride. With the sudden awareness of having been seen.

“I—” His mouth shaped itself toward apology again. He caught it, eyes widening, and rerouted. “…Okay.”

“Good,” I said, because good is clean. “Now listen carefully.”

He did.

“This room doesn’t punish people for having bodies,” I said. “It supports bodies so people can do their jobs. That’s all. When someone tries to turn a body into gossip, you don’t participate. You don’t comfort them. You don’t lecture them. You simply don’t make it your story.”

Charlie nodded, and I watched the idea settle into him like a weight he was relieved to carry. Not moral grandstanding. Practical rule.

“Understood,” he said.

I held his gaze for one beat longer than necessary.

Then I stood.

The next part mattered: upgrading him without ceremony. Treating respect as normal, because that was the frame I wanted to set.

I crossed to the women’s rail.

Charlie’s eyes flicked away automatically, as if he’d been told, somewhere in life, that he was not permitted to look.

I didn’t correct him. Not yet. This wasn’t about forcing him into anything. This was about teaching him that objects aren’t shameful.

I pulled out a petticoat—plain cotton, not lace, not a fetish object, nothing theatrical. Just a garment that had done its work and needed care. A seam along the hem had started to loosen, and there was a small tear where someone’s shoe had caught it.

I brought it back to the table and placed it down gently. Not dramatically. Like a towel.

Charlie looked at it and went still.

I said, briskly, “Wash your hands again.”

He blinked. “I just did.”

“I know,” I said. “Do it anyway.”

He stood and did it immediately, no questions, no sulking. When he returned, he held his hands out for a moment, then lowered them, remembering I didn’t want performances.

I pointed to the petticoat. “This needs a hem repair. Small stitch. Flat. Strong. The wearer has to walk all day in it.”

Charlie’s eyes stayed on the fabric. He looked as if he expected his own thoughts to betray him.

“Celeste,” he said, low, and then stopped, as if he wasn’t sure he was allowed to say my name in that tone.

“What?” I asked.

He swallowed. “I don’t… want to mess it up.”

That wasn’t fear of women’s clothing. That was respect. The right kind.

“Then you won’t,” I said. “Because you’re going to treat it like it’s alive.”

He nodded.

And then, because I wanted to make the basso continuo explicit without turning it into a sermon, I added one line—quiet and final.

“This isn’t intimate,” I said. “It’s important.”

Charlie looked up at me, and something in his expression loosened—like a knot untied. He nodded once, deeply. “Okay.”

He picked up the petticoat with both hands and laid it flat. He didn’t pinch it. He didn’t fumble. He handled it like fabric that mattered.

Mara, of course, chose that moment to glance over.

Her eyes widened slightly. Her mouth tightened in anticipation of commentary. I didn’t look at her. I didn’t give her the stage.

Charlie threaded the needle with the same concentrated care as before. His hands trembled less now. He began the hem repair slowly, stitch by stitch, building a line that would hold.

I watched him for a minute.

He wasn’t fascinated.

He was focused.

That distinction mattered so much it felt like relief in my own chest.

When the stitch line reached the tear, he paused and adjusted the fabric so the repair would distribute strain rather than create a new weak point. That wasn’t something you did by instinct. That was someone who understood pressure and failure and how to prevent both.

He looked up. “Is… this right?”

I leaned in and examined it closely. The work was neat. The hem lay flat. The tension was correct.

“Yes,” I said. “Carry on.”

He did.

Mara muttered, barely audible, “Well, I’ll be.”

I didn’t respond. I didn’t need to. Results do their own speaking if you let them.

Charlie finished the repair and smoothed the fabric gently, like calming a living thing. He held it up to the light, checking for puckers, for unevenness, for any sign he’d been careless.

Then he looked at me, waiting.

I gave him what I always gave when something mattered: the smallest possible acknowledgement.

A nod.

His shoulders loosened as if someone had slipped a strap off his chest. He exhaled—quietly, as though even breathing was something he didn’t want to do wrong.

And in that exhale I heard the shape of the new trust forming: not blind obedience, not romance, not worship.

A practical belief.

When I said something was important, he treated it that way.

When I said something was normal, he stopped being afraid of it.

That kind of trust can be tested. It can be strengthened. It can be used.

I turned back to my clipboard and made one small mark beside his name—nothing dramatic, just a private notation in the system I was building.

**Upgrade.**

Then, without looking up, I slid the next task toward him: a bundle of linen and a small tin of buttons.

“Next,” I said. “We’re going to stop things failing before they fail.”

Charlie’s fingers closed around the bundle.

And this time, he didn’t glance toward the door.

He didn’t need an exit.

He had a role. He had rules.

And he had me—steady, purposeful, unreadable—setting the tempo he’d begun to match without realising he’d started.
