## Wardrobe, First Assignment

Wardrobe is where romance goes to die.

People imagine velvet and ribbons. They imagine a girl turning prettily in front of a mirror while someone murmurs, *perfect.* They imagine lace like it grew on the body.

What it is, in practice, is triage.

It’s a room full of fabric with histories: sweat that won’t come out, seams that have carried too much weight, tiny tears where someone laughed too hard, buttons missing like teeth. It’s the mild panic of knowing the public will be here soon to pay money for an illusion—and the illusion must not fail in front of them.

Charlie stepped in as if the air changed at the threshold. His gaze moved, careful and wide, taking in rails of garments, labelled drawers, the old wooden mannequins that stood like pale witnesses. He kept his hands half-lifted, unsure what to do with them. Not because he was fascinated. Because he was trying not to do harm.

That pleased me.

“Shut the door,” I said, because doors are boundaries and boundaries matter.

He did it quietly.

The room settled into its own sound: the soft clink of hangers, the faint hum of a fan, the distant bass of the yard outside where Maintenance still shouted at weight and weather.

Charlie’s eyes snagged on a row of women’s garments—stays hanging like ribcages, petticoats folded in neat stacks, chemises white as paper. I watched him look, then look away, as if he didn’t have the right. As if he might be accused simply for noticing.

That, too, was information.

I set my clipboard on the central table and turned to him.

“Right,” I said. “Rule one you’ve already heard: if you touch a garment, you touch it like it’s alive.”

He nodded once, solemn.

“Rule two,” I continued, “is that wardrobe is not a confessional. You don’t tell me your life story, you don’t tell me your feelings, and you do *not* apologise at me like it’s your job.”

His mouth opened reflexively—*sorry* living behind his teeth—and I lifted a hand.

“No,” I said, without heat. “Not here.”

He closed his mouth, eyes flicking down, as if he’d been caught doing something rude.

Good. He could learn.

I crossed to the sink in the corner, turned the tap, and let the water run until it warmed. “Wash your hands.”

He blinked. “Now?”

“Now,” I said. “And properly.”

He moved toward the sink, still cautious, and rolled up his sleeves without being told. That, at least, was promising—an instinct for prep. He lathered the soap, fingers interlacing, nails scrubbed against palm. His hands were long and fine-boned, not labour hands. Hands made for detail.

He rinsed, dried carefully on a towel I’d hung there specifically for this purpose.

When he turned back, he held his hands out for a second, as if he expected me to inspect them.

I did. Not theatrically. Just a glance. Clean.

“Good,” I said.

The word landed on him like a small weight. He straightened a fraction, as if praise were something he didn’t want to drop.

I gestured to the shelves. “Those drawers are labelled. Don’t improvise. Don’t ‘probably.’ Don’t guess. If you don’t know, you ask once—clearly—and then you do what I say.”

His eyes widened a little at *ask once.* “Just once?”

“If you ask me the same question twice,” I said, “it means you weren’t listening the first time. And that’s dangerous here.”

He nodded again, more quickly.

I opened my clipboard. “We’re starting you on men’s wardrobe. Shirts, waistcoats, boots. Durable things. Public things.”

Charlie’s gaze flicked back toward the women’s rail and then away again, almost guiltily, as if relieved that I’d named a safe category.

I didn’t comment. Strategists don’t waste moves.

I walked to the far rack and pulled down a footman’s blouse-shirt: white linen, soft from use, collar still stiff enough to hold a line. A tear the size of a fingernail near the cuff. Easy, if you knew what you were doing. Catastrophic, if you didn’t—because the cuff is what guests see when the boy lifts a tray.

I held it out.

Charlie approached as if the shirt might bruise.

He didn’t grab. He took it with both hands, supporting the fabric rather than pinching it. His touch was… respectful. Not reverent in a religious way, not precious—just careful, as if the object had a purpose beyond being cloth.

That was the basso continuo, right there, without him knowing it.

“This needs a cuff repair,” I said. “Small stitch. Tight. Invisible.”

He looked down at it, eyes narrowing in concentration. “I… I can do that?”

“You can learn,” I corrected. “Show me how you hold a needle.”

He hesitated, then glanced around for a sewing kit as if it might leap into his hands. He looked absurdly lost for a second—like someone asked to fly.

I reached into the drawer and placed a needle and thread on the table. I did it slowly, deliberately, so he could watch my hands.

“Sit,” I said.

He sat.

I didn’t hover behind him like a teacher. I sat opposite, elbows on the table, the way you sit when you’re negotiating rather than instructing.

He picked up the needle like it was a fragile instrument. He threaded it after two tries, lips pressed in concentration. No showmanship, no jokes, no complaint. He simply persisted.

That persistence—quiet, humourless—was something most people mistook for weakness.

They were wrong.

“Alright,” I said. “Do one stitch.”

He brought the needle to the fabric, paused. His hands trembled slightly. Not from fear of me. From fear of doing harm.

“I’m not… good at this,” he said, voice barely above a whisper.

I didn’t answer the insecurity. I answered the reality.

“Then you’ll do it slowly,” I said. “Slow and correct beats fast and ruined.”

His shoulders lowered a fraction, as if that permission to be careful unclenched him.

He made the first stitch. It wasn’t perfect. But it was neat. The thread lay where it should. The fabric didn’t pucker.

I watched his face as he worked—how the tension in it shifted from shame to focus. Focus was a safer home for him than confidence.

While he stitched, I moved around the room, doing what I always did: scanning, counting, anticipating.

Two waistcoats missing from rack C. One pair of stockings unpaired. A bodice with a loose eyelet. A smear of something suspicious on a petticoat hem. The kind of details the public never sees and yet always feels when they’re wrong.

A woman leaned into the doorway then, hair in a messy bun, pencil stuck behind one ear. Mara. She’d been here three seasons and treated her knowledge like personal property.

Her eyes flicked to Charlie, then to me. “Who’s that?”

“Mine,” I said, without looking up from the list.

Mara’s mouth tightened. “Wardrobe’s not a daycare, Celeste.”

“It’s not,” I replied, still calm. “That’s why I removed him from Maintenance before he injured himself.”

Mara snorted. “Maintenance isn’t exactly gentle, is it.”

“No,” I said. “And neither am I.”

Her gaze sharpened at that, measuring. She didn’t like my tone, which meant it was the right tone.

“What does he do?” she asked, as if Charlie weren’t in the room.

Charlie’s hands paused over the cuff, needle suspended.

I glanced at him once. He’d gone still again. The old instinct: become invisible when people discuss you.

I decided against letting it happen.

“He hears instructions,” I said. “He handles fabric properly. And he doesn’t treat the room like a joke.”

Mara’s eyes narrowed, but she didn’t argue with that. Instead she leaned on the doorframe, arms folded. “What’s the catch?”

“The catch,” I said pleasantly, “is that I’m short-staffed, not stupid.”

Mara huffed a laugh through her nose, then nodded toward Charlie. “If he ruins that shirt—”

“He won’t,” I said.

Charlie’s needle moved again, small and determined. It was the right kind of pressure: being seen without being mocked.

Mara drifted away, still unconvinced but no longer obstructing. She respected results. Most people did, if you forced them.

I returned to the table and placed a pair of boots beside Charlie’s elbow. Coachman’s boots. Thick leather. Mud-caked. One heel beginning to separate where the glue had started to fail.

Charlie glanced at them, startled—like I’d placed a dead animal on the table.

“These,” I said, “need cleaning and polishing. And this heel needs attention. You’ll bring them back looking like they’ve never known the yard.”

His brow furrowed. “How?”

I let the silence stretch long enough for him to feel the shape of the problem. Then I opened another drawer and laid out supplies: brush, cloth, polish, glue, a small clamp.

“You’ll work it out,” I said. “If you need one instruction, you ask it once.”

He swallowed. “Okay.”

It wasn’t confidence. It was compliance with a spine.

Good.

As he finished the cuff repair, he didn’t smooth the fabric with a careless palm. He pressed it gently, flattening the seam like he was calming something living. Then he held it up and looked at it critically, turning it as though the light itself might reveal his errors.

He wasn’t trying to impress me.

He was trying to be correct.

That difference matters.

“You’re frowning,” I observed.

“I… I can still see it,” he admitted, pointing to the tiniest irregularity in the stitch line.

“You can see it because you’re the one who did it,” I said. “The public will not. What they’ll see is a cuff that doesn’t unravel when a tray tilts.”

He blinked at that framing, as if no one had ever taught him that “good” was sometimes practical rather than perfect.

Then, because I wanted to anchor the basso continuo now—early, cleanly—I said, “Listen.”

He lifted his eyes to me, immediate.

“This room isn’t about clothes,” I said. “It’s about bodies. Clothes are what the world allows bodies to be.”

His face went blank in the way it did when something too large brushed past him. Not offended. Not excited. Just… trying to understand.

I continued, because the message mattered.

“If you handle garments with disrespect, you’re disrespecting the person who has to wear them,” I said. “Not in some abstract moral way. In a physical way. Because a seam that scratches, a stocking that slips, a boot that pinches—those things become someone’s whole day.”

Charlie’s gaze dropped to the shirt in his hands, and I watched the idea settle into him with a kind of relief.

Not fascination.

Purpose.

“I understand,” he said quietly.

“I think you do,” I replied. “Which is why you’re here.”

He swallowed. “Thank you.”

His reflex wanted to add *sorry* again, to apologise for existing in my space, for taking my time, for being a problem.

I lifted my eyebrows.

He caught himself mid-breath and corrected, carefully: “I mean… okay.”

I almost smiled.

Almost.

“Good,” I said instead, because *good* is cleaner than affection when you’re building a system.

I stood, took the repaired shirt from him, and inspected it with the same seriousness I’d give a wound dressing. The stitch was neat. The tear was gone. The cuff lay flat.

I nodded once.

That was all the reward he needed. His shoulders loosened like a knot being untied.

I placed the shirt on the “Ready” rail and turned back to him.

“Now,” I said, “boots.”

He looked at the muddy leather, then at the brush, then at me—puzzled, still. As if he couldn’t quite believe that this, too, was a kind of permission.

“You’re… sure?” he asked.

“Yes,” I said. “And Charlie—”

He stilled at his name.

I made my voice brisk, to keep it from feeling like an embrace. “If you ruin something, you tell me immediately. You don’t hide it. You don’t cry over it. You don’t try to fix it in secret.”

He flinched slightly at *cry*—not because I’d accused him, but because it hit close to a fear.

“You tell me,” I repeated. “Truth is always cheaper than damage.”

He nodded. “Truth,” he echoed, as if committing it to memory.

“That’s right,” I said. “You’ll learn our values quickly.”

*Our.*

The word was a small claim, slipped in like thread through fabric. Not ownership. Not yet.

Membership.

He didn’t react outwardly, but I saw the way it struck him—the way the room became, for him, less like a strange country and more like a shelter with rules.

He picked up the brush and began on the boots with slow, methodical strokes. Mud flaked away. Leather emerged. His hands moved with the same careful respect he’d used on the shirt, as if he were uncovering something rather than merely cleaning it.

And the strangest thing was: he looked calmer.

Not happy. Not free. But calmer—because the work made sense. Because the rules were clear. Because I was here, and my presence had a shape he could lean against without falling.

I watched him for a moment longer than necessary.

He felt it and glanced up, startled, like a bird sensing a shadow.

“Yes?” he asked, immediate—waiting for instruction.

I let my gaze hold him, steady and unreadable.

“No,” I said. “Nothing. Carry on.”

He looked down again, and the brush kept moving.

That was the start of it: not obedience, not romance, not salvation.

Just a boy doing careful work in a room where carefulness was finally useful—and a girl quietly measuring exactly how much trust he could carry before he noticed he was carrying it.

Good.

Which was, unfortunately, the next thing he needed.

No reward for checking in. He had to learn to work without constantly seeking permission.

When the boots finally shone and the heel sat clamped in place like a problem that had agreed to behave, I reached for them and set them on the Ready rail.

“Nice,” I said. One syllable. Enough.

His throat moved. He nodded. He looked pleased in a way that was almost painful to witness—like someone who’d been starving and had forgotten what bread felt like.

I turned back to my clipboard. “Charlie.”

He straightened instantly. Too instantly.

“Yes?”

“There’s a missing waistcoat,” I said, flicking my pen to the inventory list. “Black. Brass buttons. Size medium. It should be on rack C with the footmen’s set.”

He frowned as if picturing the rack. “I didn’t see—”

“I know,” I said. “That’s why we’re fixing it.”

He waited, hands hovering. Always ready to be told what reality was.

I picked up a note card and wrote quickly. *Wardrobe requires waistcoat — black, brass buttons — for Footman 3. Signed: C. Shelley (Wardrobe).* Then I slid it across the table.

“Take this to Graham.”

His eyes widened. “Maintenance Graham?”

“Yes.”

He didn’t move.

The old reflex rose in him like a tide. I could almost see it: *He’ll be angry. I’ll get in trouble. I’ll say sorry. I’ll make it worse.*

This wasn’t cruelty. This was calibration.

Because if Charlie was going to work with me, he couldn’t be a ghost. He had to be able to carry my words into other rooms.

I leaned slightly forward. “Listen.”

He looked at me, immediate.

“You are not going to him as a nuisance,” I said. “You are going to him as my runner. You are carrying a request from Wardrobe. That is normal. That is allowed.”

His lips parted.

“And,” I added, “you are not going to apologise. Not once.”

He swallowed. “What if he—”

I lifted my eyebrows.

He caught himself. “Okay.”

“Good,” I said. “You’ll say exactly this.” I tapped the note card with my pen. “*Graham, Celeste needs the black waistcoat for Footman Three. Have you seen it, or should we pull a spare?*”

He repeated it silently, eyes tracking my mouth as if words were physical objects he could hold.

“Again,” I said.

He repeated it, slightly more steady.

“Again,” I said.

His jaw tightened. He said it a third time, clearer. Almost firm.

There was the line. There was the spine.

I nodded once. “Excellent. Now—go.”

He stood, then hesitated, hovering on the edge of motion like a bird at the edge of flight.

“Charlie,” I said, gently, because the gentleness mattered *precisely because* I didn’t use it often. “Look at me.”

He looked.

“Match my pace,” I said. “In your head. Walk like you’re doing a job.”

He blinked, as if walking could be reframed.

Then he nodded, turned, and left.

I watched him go. Not with sentiment. With attention.

This was the first test: could he carry a simple sentence into a room full of men and tools without collapsing into apology?

I returned to the table and began sorting lace trims, because you don’t announce that you’re waiting for a result. You behave as though the outcome is inevitable.

Mara wandered in again, eyeing the open door. “Where’s your boy gone?”

I didn’t look up. “Errand.”

Mara made a sound between amusement and disdain. “You’re brave.”

“I’m correct,” I said.

A few minutes passed. Five, perhaps. Long enough for the mind to invent catastrophes.

I pictured Charlie in the yard, shoulders hunched, saying *sorry* before he even spoke, Graham’s patience snapping. I pictured Charlie shrinking back into himself and deciding he didn’t belong anywhere.

Then I heard footsteps—too light, too careful—returning.

Charlie appeared in the doorway, cheeks flushed, eyes bright with the strange feverish sheen of someone who has just survived something they’d been certain would kill them.

He held the waistcoat in both hands as if it were a fragile animal.

Behind him, Graham’s voice carried down the corridor. “Tell her it was in the prop crate like I bloody said. And tell her to label her racks properly.”

Charlie flinched at the shouted words, but he didn’t crumple. He walked in, shut the door softly, and approached the table.

He set the waistcoat down with reverence.

I inspected it quickly: correct garment, correct buttons, faint smell of dust. It would do.

I looked up at him.

He was waiting—tense, braced—like he expected punishment for existing.

“Well?” I said, mildly.

He blinked. “I… I said the sentence.”

“Did you?” I asked, as if it were a genuine question, not an invitation to confess.

He nodded, then swallowed. “I said it exactly. He… stared at me.”

“And you did what?” I prompted.

Charlie’s throat bobbed. “I… looked back.”

That was the moment.

Not the waistcoat. Not the errand. The looking back.

He didn’t even realise he’d done it. He said it like a fact, not a victory. But I heard the victory anyway.

“And did you apologise?” I asked.

He hesitated. His mouth opened—habit reaching for the old word—

Then he stopped himself, eyes widening, as if he’d just caught his own hand reaching toward a hot stove.

“No,” he said, softly. “I didn’t.”

I let my expression remain calm, because if I rewarded him too warmly he’d begin performing for warmth. I wanted him performing for competence.

“Good,” I said. “That is what you do now.”

His shoulders loosened slightly, as if someone had undone a strap that was cutting into him.

Mara, who had been hovering by the shelves pretending not to listen, gave a reluctant snort. “He actually did it?”

I didn’t glance at her. “Of course he did.”

Charlie’s eyes flicked toward Mara, uncertainty spiking again—social danger.

I cut it off.

“Charlie,” I said, turning back to him. “You’re not here to be liked. You’re here to be useful.”

His face flushed. He nodded. “Okay.”

“And,” I added, because this part mattered, “you’re not here to be humiliated either. If anyone tries it, you tell me.”

He looked at me quickly—startled, almost disbelieving. “You… want me to tell you?”

“Yes,” I said, as if it were obvious. “That’s part of the job.”

His gaze held mine for a beat too long. That struck quality again—relief with teeth in it.

I broke eye contact first, because I could feel the hook setting and I didn’t intend to let it set too quickly.

I turned to the waistcoat, lifted it, and hung it on the Ready rail.

Then I slid another item across the table: a bundle of linen ties.

“Next,” I said, briskly. “Sort these. Count them. Tell me exactly how many. And if you don’t know, you ask once.”

Charlie took the bundle with both hands.

He didn’t smile.

But he stood a little straighter.

And somewhere in the quiet machinery of his mind, I could feel it clicking into place: when I gave him words, he could walk through rooms he’d been afraid of.

That was trust.

Not blind.

Not yet.

But alive enough to test again.

Yeah — that dilemma is real, and it’s exactly the sort of thing that can either make the story feel painfully true or accidentally tip it into a familiar teen-attachment trope.

You don’t need to decide “is he possessive or not?” as a single on/off switch. The truest version is usually this:

**Charlie becomes *attached*, but his attachment expresses as restraint, vigilance, and self-erasure — not ownership.**
He doesn’t try to claim Celeste. He tries not to *lose* her. That difference matters.

And because we’re in Celeste’s POV (and she’s sharp), we can explore it with elegant distance: she reads him, calibrates, sets limits, and lets him grow into something healthier without ever becoming his emotional nurse.

### What’s psychologically natural for Charlie (given your setup)

If you’ve been unchosen for years, the first person who chooses you can trigger a cocktail like:

* **Relief:** someone sees me.
* **Awe:** how is she this… competent?
* **Fear:** this will be revoked.
* **Shame about wanting:** wanting feels dangerous.
* **Hyper-attunement:** reading her mood like weather.
* **Overcompliance:** “If I’m perfect, she’ll keep me.”

Possessiveness isn’t the only (or even most likely) outcome. Often it’s the opposite: **he becomes careful to the point of invisibility**, because invisibility has been his survival skill.

### How to let it “ring true” without steering into cliché

Here are three believable trajectories — you can blend them:

#### 1) The “quiet imprint” trajectory (my favourite for your lane)

Charlie’s feelings are intense, but he contains them. He becomes:

* punctual, precise, eager to please,
* protective of her time,
* almost allergic to being a burden.

Celeste notices his attachment through behaviours, not speeches:

* he watches her face for permission,
* he mirrors her pace,
* he brightens when she enters the room and dims when she leaves.

She doesn’t romanticise it. She sees it as **a nervous system learning safety.**

#### 2) The “crush with shame” trajectory

He has a crush, yes — because he’s 18 and she’s the first girl to choose him — but it’s undercut by:

* the belief he’s not “allowed” to want her,
* fear of being disgusting,
* fear of ruining the one good thing.

It’s tender because it’s self-policed. He’d rather suffer privately than risk touching the bond.

#### 3) The “misread closeness” trajectory (small, controlled)

He mistakes structured intimacy (working side-by-side, being addressed, being seen) for romantic intimacy, *briefly*. This can be brilliant if it’s handled cleanly:

* one small overstep (a text too late, waiting outside too often, a question that’s too personal),
* Celeste corrects him once, calmly and firmly,
* he absorbs it, adjusts, and the relationship strengthens.

That’s not melodrama. That’s education.

### The crucial question: what does *Celeste* want?

Because the story is really about *her*, Charlie’s feelings need to serve her arc, not derail it.

Celeste can want:

* a **trusted ally** (someone she can direct and rely on),
* a **mirror** that reflects her power back to her,
* a **project in competence** (not in identity),
* or, later, a **romantic partnership** that grows out of work and respect.

And she can also remain ambivalent — she’s 18, she’s wary after a bad athlete boyfriend, she’s not hunting romance. That ambivalence is *believable* and, importantly, it gives her the upper hand.

### A clean way to explore Charlie’s emotional state “as perceived by Celeste”

We don’t have to enter his head. Celeste can observe:

* **His eyes:** do they ask? do they take? do they wait?
* **His body:** does he retreat when she’s close? does he hover? does he follow?
* **His speech:** does he apologise too much? does he ask permission for everything?
* **His work:** does he sew with fear when she’s watching? does he loosen when she leaves?

That last one is especially strong: *emotion visible as stitch tension.* You’ve already planted it.

### My recommendation for your tone

Go with this blend:

* Charlie has a **strong crush/attachment** (natural),
* expressed as **hyper-responsibility and fear of revocation** (not possessive ownership),
* with one **small boundary-correction scene** from Celeste later to keep it honest and growthful.

It lets you show vulnerability without turning Charlie into a “clingy boy,” and it lets Celeste remain decisive without becoming his therapist.

## Scene — Wardrobe, mid-morning, trial day two

Mara didn’t test people the way teachers tested them.

Teachers announced the test, circled the date, watched you sweat, and then pretended your result said something noble about your soul. Mara tested you the way you tested a seam: under her fingers, quietly, looking for strain.

Charlie had been back for a second morning. Same early arrival. Same clean hands. Same careful stillness, as if he didn’t want the room to notice he’d come in.

Mara noticed everything.

She didn’t say hello. She pointed at a tin on the table.

“Pins,” she said. “Sort them. Then you’re on mending pile.”

Charlie nodded once and moved. No flinch. No performance. He poured the pins out onto a cloth and began arranging them by length with a kind of tidy focus that made the task look dignified.

I was at the rail, tagging garment bags, listening to the hiss of the steamer and the small scrape of hangers sliding. Wardrobe had its own rhythm — calm on the surface, precise underneath — and Charlie had already started matching it without being told.

That was his first tell: he could join a system without trying to dominate it.

Mara’s second tell came ten minutes later, when she “accidentally” left a pair of vintage shears too close to the edge of the table.

Not a dramatic setup. Just a temptation: a valuable tool sitting in the wrong place. A careless person would grab it without asking. A nervous person would ignore it and let it fall.

Charlie noticed. His eyes flicked to it, then to Mara. He didn’t touch the shears. He nudged the cloth closer, stabilised the table edge with his palm, and slid the shears back with two fingers—careful, respectful—like he was returning a bird to its perch.

Mara didn’t look up. But I saw the smallest change in her mouth: the line eased by a millimetre.

Ten minutes after that, she called across the room, voice neutral.

“Rossignol. Bring me the blue tape.”

Charlie paused. His gaze moved to the shelves. There were three blue tapes, different widths.

He didn’t panic. He didn’t guess. He looked once at Mara, then asked — quietly, once.

“Which width?”

Mara’s eyes lifted. She held his gaze for a beat, then said, “Quarter-inch.”

Charlie retrieved the correct roll and brought it to her. No apology for asking. No shame. Just accuracy.

Mara took it and said, “Good.”

Just that. One syllable. It landed like a stamp.

Charlie went faintly pink at the ears anyway, as if the word had surprised his body.

I kept my eyes on the tags, but my attention drifted. It wasn’t romantic interest. It was… data. I watched people the way you watched fabric: how it fell, where it pulled, what it revealed when it thought nobody was looking.

Charlie’s attention to Mara was respect. Charlie’s attention to the room was caution.

Charlie’s attention to me—

That was different.

It wasn’t the obvious stare you got from boys who thought you existed to be noticed. It wasn’t even the furtive kind. It was as if his eyes kept finding me on their own, the way a compass needle finds north, and each time he realised, he corrected himself like it was a breach of discipline.

He was trying not to.

Which made it almost endearing.

Almost.

Mara sent him to the mending pile: a basket of small catastrophes — popped seams, torn cuffs, fraying apron ties. She didn’t give him the easiest ones. She gave him the ones where haste would show.

Charlie sat, assessed each item the way he’d assessed the torn dress yesterday: calm, quiet, not fearful. He chose thread that matched without holding it up to the light like a show. He measured seam allowance with his eye, then confirmed with a tape. He stitched with even tension, no puckering, no desperate pulling.

The room stayed stable around him.

Half an hour later, Mara did another test.

She handed Charlie a garment bag and said, “Hang that.”

It was heavier than it looked — wool, boning, metal closures. Charlie took it with both hands. He carried it the way you carried something that mattered, and when he reached the rail he stopped.

He didn’t hang it immediately.

He looked at the rail, checking spacing, weight distribution, the hook’s position—like he was thinking not of this one garment but of the system as a whole. Then he hung it in a place that made sense, not in the first empty gap.

Mara watched him.

She didn’t praise him. She just didn’t correct him.

That was Mara’s version of warmth.

When she moved away, I stepped closer to Charlie’s table, because it was time to introduce the next lesson, and because I’d been told — explicitly — not to hover, but not told not to function.

“You’re stitching like you’ve done this for years,” I said.

Charlie’s hands paused for half a second, needle hovering. Then he kept going.

“My mum,” he said, voice low. “She… she showed me. If you make it neat, people pay.”

“That’s true,” I replied. “And if you make it neat, Mara doesn’t kill you.”

His mouth twitched. A small smile he didn’t quite permit to exist.

I watched his eyes flick up to my face, and then away again too fast. Like touching a hot surface.

“Thanks,” he said.

“For what?”

He swallowed. “For… yesterday. For… getting me in.”

I let a beat pass. I didn’t want to train him into gratitude. Gratitude can turn into dependence, and dependence can rot a person.

So I kept it clean.

“You got you in,” I said. “You turned up. You worked. Mara cares about that.”

His shoulders loosened slightly, like that was a relief and an insult in one.

He threaded another needle, hands steady. Then, without looking at me, he said, “I’m not… I’m not trying to be weird.”

I tilted my head. “Weird how?”

His ears went a deeper pink. He frowned at the fabric as if it had betrayed him.

“I just—” He stopped. The words clogged.

It was there, in the stall between his sentences: the thing he didn’t want to say because saying it would turn it into a liability.

I could have teased him. I could have pretended I didn’t understand. I could have made it soft.

But soft is how boys slip out of accountability.

So I did what I always did: I decided what it meant.

“You mean you don’t want to make me uncomfortable,” I said, evenly.

His head snapped up. Hazel eyes, startled. Then he looked down again, quick as shame.

“Yes,” he whispered.

“That’s good,” I said. “Keep it that way.”

He nodded once — sharp, immediate — like he’d been given a rule and was relieved.

Then, because his brain was honest even when his mouth wanted to be cautious, he added, “It’s just… you’re… you’re a lot.”

I blinked.

“A lot?”

He winced as if he’d just spilled ink. “Not— not bad. I mean… you’re… you make things… make sense.”

It was the crush, peeking out in its most Charlie form: not desire as entitlement, but admiration as gravity. It made me want to smile.

I didn’t. I let it sit between us, uninflated.

“That’s not romance,” I said quietly. “That’s structure.”

His eyes flicked to mine. Confused, searching.

I continued, calm. “You’ve been living in rooms where you can’t win. Wardrobe is a room where you can. If you’re feeling something… aim it at your work first. That will keep you safe.”

He stared at me, stunned by the fact I’d named it without making it dirty.

Then he nodded. Slowly.

“Okay,” he said.

“Good.” I glanced at the garment in his hands. “Now make that invisible. Mara hates visible.”

He almost smiled again.

I turned to go back to the rail, and that’s when he said it — the other confession, the one that mattered.

“I’m not going back next year,” he said, too quickly. “To school.”

I stopped, hand on a garment bag.

“What do you mean?”

He looked at his stitches. Not me. “I’m gonna wag it. I can’t… do it again. They just— they don’t—” He shook his head once, small and furious. “It’s a waste.”

I walked back to him and placed my finger lightly on the fabric near his seam, not touching him, just anchoring the moment.

“No,” I said, voice flat.

He blinked. “No?”

“You’re not wagging,” I replied. Simple. Not a debate.

His jaw tightened. “Why do you care?”

I held his gaze.

“Because Mara will train you,” I said. “Your mum will back you. I can point you at doors. But if you sabotage your own foundation, you’ll spend your whole life needing someone to catch you.”

His throat bobbed. He looked away.

“That’s not fair,” he muttered.

“I’m not here to be fair,” I said. “I’m here to be accurate.”

He stared at his hands as if they belonged to someone else.

I softened by a fraction—not into comfort, into clarity.

“If school is the wrong language,” I said, “we’ll find you translation. But you don’t get to disappear. That’s the old you talking.”

He swallowed. “I don’t know how to do it.”

“Then you learn,” I said, and let a small edge of humour in, because humour makes medicine swallowable. “You’re doing a whole new trade. You can learn Year Twelve.”

His mouth twitched.

He didn’t say yes.

He didn’t say no.

But he didn’t argue either.

That, for Charlie, was progress.

Across the room, Mara’s voice cut through the hiss of the steamer.

“Rossignol!”

Charlie’s head snapped up immediately. “Yes?”

Mara held up a sleeve with a tear near the cuff. “This one. If you stitch it tight, I’ll know. And you’ll unpick it in front of everyone.”

Charlie went very still.

Then he said, clear and calm, “Okay.”

He rose, took the sleeve, and walked to Mara’s table with the careful confidence of someone who had found a rule-set that didn’t hate him.

As he passed me, his eyes flicked to my face again — quick, warm, grateful, frightened — and then away.

Not taking.

Just… orbiting.

I watched him go and thought, not unkindly:

He’s going to have to learn that being chosen is not a miracle.

It’s a responsibility.

And I’m not letting him waste it.
