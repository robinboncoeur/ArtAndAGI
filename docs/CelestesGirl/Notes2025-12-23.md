[Q]

I don't even know how, as an immature 18 yo I would have reacted.. You can see my dilemma writing this.

[Emily]  
Yeah — that dilemma is real, and it’s exactly the sort of thing that can either make the story feel painfully true or accidentally tip it into a familiar teen-attachment trope.

You don’t need to decide “is he possessive or not?” as a single on/off switch. The truest version is usually this:

**Charlie becomes *attached*, but his attachment expresses as restraint, vigilance, and self-erasure — not ownership.**
He doesn’t try to claim Celeste. He tries not to *lose* her. That difference matters.

And because we’re in Celeste’s POV (and she’s sharp), we can explore it with elegant distance: she reads him, calibrates, sets limits, and lets him grow into something healthier without ever becoming his emotional nurse.

### What’s psychologically natural for Charlie (given your setup)

If you’ve been unchosen for years, the first person who chooses you can trigger a cocktail like:

* **Relief:** someone sees me.
* **Awe:** how is she this… competent?
* **Fear:** this will be revoked.
* **Shame about wanting:** wanting feels dangerous.
* **Hyper-attunement:** reading her mood like weather.
* **Overcompliance:** “If I’m perfect, she’ll keep me.”

Possessiveness isn’t the only (or even most likely) outcome. Often it’s the opposite: **he becomes careful to the point of invisibility**, because invisibility has been his survival skill.

### How to let it “ring true” without steering into cliché

Here are three believable trajectories — you can blend them:

#### 1) The “quiet imprint” trajectory (my favourite for your lane)

Charlie’s feelings are intense, but he contains them. He becomes:

* punctual, precise, eager to please,
* protective of her time,
* almost allergic to being a burden.

Celeste notices his attachment through behaviours, not speeches:

* he watches her face for permission,
* he mirrors her pace,
* he brightens when she enters the room and dims when she leaves.

She doesn’t romanticise it. She sees it as **a nervous system learning safety.**

#### 2) The “crush with shame” trajectory

He has a crush, yes — because he’s 18 and she’s the first girl to choose him — but it’s undercut by:

* the belief he’s not “allowed” to want her,
* fear of being disgusting,
* fear of ruining the one good thing.

It’s tender because it’s self-policed. He’d rather suffer privately than risk touching the bond.

#### 3) The “misread closeness” trajectory (small, controlled)

He mistakes structured intimacy (working side-by-side, being addressed, being seen) for romantic intimacy, *briefly*. This can be brilliant if it’s handled cleanly:

* one small overstep (a text too late, waiting outside too often, a question that’s too personal),
* Celeste corrects him once, calmly and firmly,
* he absorbs it, adjusts, and the relationship strengthens.

That’s not melodrama. That’s education.

### The crucial question: what does *Celeste* want?

Because the story is really about *her*, Charlie’s feelings need to serve her arc, not derail it.

Celeste can want:

* a **trusted ally** (someone she can direct and rely on),
* a **mirror** that reflects her power back to her,
* a **project in competence** (not in identity),
* or, later, a **romantic partnership** that grows out of work and respect.

And she can also remain ambivalent — she’s 18, she’s wary after a bad athlete boyfriend, she’s not hunting romance. That ambivalence is *believable* and, importantly, it gives her the upper hand.

### A clean way to explore Charlie’s emotional state “as perceived by Celeste”

We don’t have to enter his head. Celeste can observe:

* **His eyes:** do they ask? do they take? do they wait?
* **His body:** does he retreat when she’s close? does he hover? does he follow?
* **His speech:** does he apologise too much? does he ask permission for everything?
* **His work:** does he sew with fear when she’s watching? does he loosen when she leaves?

That last one is especially strong: *emotion visible as stitch tension.* You’ve already planted it.

### My recommendation for your tone

Go with this blend:

* Charlie has a **strong crush/attachment** (natural),
* expressed as **hyper-responsibility and fear of revocation** (not possessive ownership),
* with one **small boundary-correction scene** from Celeste later to keep it honest and growthful.

It lets you show vulnerability without turning Charlie into a “clingy boy,” and it lets Celeste remain decisive without becoming his therapist.

1. **Mara’s tests** are procedural and plausibly deniable (she’s not playing games; she’s protecting her room).
2. **Charlie’s crush** leaks in *behaviour*, not declarations. One small verbal slip is enough.
3. **Celeste’s correction about wagging school** is calm, firm, and *future-facing* — not a scold, a reallocation.

The overall feel: *the women keep the room safe; Charlie keeps earning his place; his feelings become fuel, not mess.*

## Scene — Wardrobe, mid-morning, trial day two

Mara didn’t test people the way teachers tested them.

Teachers announced the test, circled the date, watched you sweat, and then pretended your result said something noble about your soul. Mara tested you the way you tested a seam: under her fingers, quietly, looking for strain.

Charlie had been back for a second morning. Same early arrival. Same clean hands. Same careful stillness, as if he didn’t want the room to notice he’d come in.

Mara noticed everything.

She didn’t say hello. She pointed at a tin on the table.

“Pins,” she said. “Sort them. Then you’re on mending pile.”

Charlie nodded once and moved. No flinch. No performance. He poured the pins out onto a cloth and began arranging them by length with a kind of tidy focus that made the task look dignified.

I was at the rail, tagging garment bags, listening to the hiss of the steamer and the small scrape of hangers sliding. Wardrobe had its own rhythm — calm on the surface, precise underneath — and Charlie had already started matching it without being told.

That was his first tell: he could join a system without trying to dominate it.

Mara’s second tell came ten minutes later, when she “accidentally” left a pair of vintage shears too close to the edge of the table.

Not a dramatic setup. Just a temptation: a valuable tool sitting in the wrong place. A careless person would grab it without asking. A nervous person would ignore it and let it fall.

Charlie noticed. His eyes flicked to it, then to Mara. He didn’t touch the shears. He nudged the cloth closer, stabilised the table edge with his palm, and slid the shears back with two fingers—careful, respectful—like he was returning a bird to its perch.

Mara didn’t look up. But I saw the smallest change in her mouth: the line eased by a millimetre.

Ten minutes after that, she called across the room, voice neutral.

“Rossignol. Bring me the blue tape.”

Charlie paused. His gaze moved to the shelves. There were three blue tapes, different widths.

He didn’t panic. He didn’t guess. He looked once at Mara, then asked — quietly, once.

“Which width?”

Mara’s eyes lifted. She held his gaze for a beat, then said, “Quarter-inch.”

Charlie retrieved the correct roll and brought it to her. No apology for asking. No shame. Just accuracy.

Mara took it and said, “Good.”

Just that. One syllable. It landed like a stamp.

Charlie went faintly pink at the ears anyway, as if the word had surprised his body.

I kept my eyes on the tags, but my attention drifted. It wasn’t romantic interest. It was… data. I watched people the way you watched fabric: how it fell, where it pulled, what it revealed when it thought nobody was looking.

Charlie’s attention to Mara was respect. Charlie’s attention to the room was caution.

Charlie’s attention to me—

That was different.

It wasn’t the obvious stare you got from boys who thought you existed to be noticed. It wasn’t even the furtive kind. It was as if his eyes kept finding me on their own, the way a compass needle finds north, and each time he realised, he corrected himself like it was a breach of discipline.

He was trying not to.

Which made it almost endearing.

Almost.

Mara sent him to the mending pile: a basket of small catastrophes — popped seams, torn cuffs, fraying apron ties. She didn’t give him the easiest ones. She gave him the ones where haste would show.

Charlie sat, assessed each item the way he’d assessed the torn dress yesterday: calm, quiet, not fearful. He chose thread that matched without holding it up to the light like a show. He measured seam allowance with his eye, then confirmed with a tape. He stitched with even tension, no puckering, no desperate pulling.

The room stayed stable around him.

Half an hour later, Mara did another test.

She handed Charlie a garment bag and said, “Hang that.”

It was heavier than it looked — wool, boning, metal closures. Charlie took it with both hands. He carried it the way you carried something that mattered, and when he reached the rail he stopped.

He didn’t hang it immediately.

He looked at the rail, checking spacing, weight distribution, the hook’s position—like he was thinking not of this one garment but of the system as a whole. Then he hung it in a place that made sense, not in the first empty gap.

Mara watched him.

She didn’t praise him. She just didn’t correct him.

That was Mara’s version of warmth.

When she moved away, I stepped closer to Charlie’s table, because it was time to introduce the next lesson, and because I’d been told — explicitly — not to hover, but not told not to function.

“You’re stitching like you’ve done this for years,” I said.

Charlie’s hands paused for half a second, needle hovering. Then he kept going.

“My mum,” he said, voice low. “She… she showed me. If you make it neat, people pay.”

“That’s true,” I replied. “And if you make it neat, Mara doesn’t kill you.”

His mouth twitched. A small smile he didn’t quite permit to exist.

I watched his eyes flick up to my face, and then away again too fast. Like touching a hot surface.

“Thanks,” he said.

“For what?”

He swallowed. “For… yesterday. For… getting me in.”

I let a beat pass. I didn’t want to train him into gratitude. Gratitude can turn into dependence, and dependence can rot a person.

So I kept it clean.

“You got you in,” I said. “You turned up. You worked. Mara cares about that.”

His shoulders loosened slightly, like that was a relief and an insult in one.

He threaded another needle, hands steady. Then, without looking at me, he said, “I’m not… I’m not trying to be weird.”

I tilted my head. “Weird how?”

His ears went a deeper pink. He frowned at the fabric as if it had betrayed him.

“I just—” He stopped. The words clogged.

It was there, in the stall between his sentences: the thing he didn’t want to say because saying it would turn it into a liability.

I could have teased him. I could have pretended I didn’t understand. I could have made it soft.

But soft is how boys slip out of accountability.

So I did what I always did: I decided what it meant.

“You mean you don’t want to make me uncomfortable,” I said, evenly.

His head snapped up. Hazel eyes, startled. Then he looked down again, quick as shame.

“Yes,” he whispered.

“That’s good,” I said. “Keep it that way.”

He nodded once — sharp, immediate — like he’d been given a rule and was relieved.

Then, because his brain was honest even when his mouth wanted to be cautious, he added, “It’s just… you’re… you’re a lot.”

I blinked.

“A lot?”

He winced as if he’d just spilled ink. “Not— not bad. I mean… you’re… you make things… make sense.”

It was the crush, peeking out in its most Charlie form: not desire as entitlement, but admiration as gravity. It made me want to smile.

I didn’t. I let it sit between us, uninflated.

“That’s not romance,” I said quietly. “That’s structure.”

His eyes flicked to mine. Confused, searching.

I continued, calm. “You’ve been living in rooms where you can’t win. Wardrobe is a room where you can. If you’re feeling something… aim it at your work first. That will keep you safe.”

He stared at me, stunned by the fact I’d named it without making it dirty.

Then he nodded. Slowly.

“Okay,” he said.

“Good.” I glanced at the garment in his hands. “Now make that invisible. Mara hates visible.”

He almost smiled again.

I turned to go back to the rail, and that’s when he said it — the other confession, the one that mattered.

“I’m not going back next year,” he said, too quickly. “To school.”

I stopped, hand on a garment bag.

“What do you mean?”

He looked at his stitches. Not me. “I’m gonna wag it. I can’t… do it again. They just— they don’t—” He shook his head once, small and furious. “It’s a waste.”

I walked back to him and placed my finger lightly on the fabric near his seam, not touching him, just anchoring the moment.

“No,” I said, voice flat.

He blinked. “No?”

“You’re not wagging,” I replied. Simple. Not a debate.

His jaw tightened. “Why do you care?”

I held his gaze.

“Because Mara will train you,” I said. “Your mum will back you. I can point you at doors. But if you sabotage your own foundation, you’ll spend your whole life needing someone to catch you.”

His throat bobbed. He looked away.

“That’s not fair,” he muttered.

“I’m not here to be fair,” I said. “I’m here to be accurate.”

He stared at his hands as if they belonged to someone else.

I softened by a fraction—not into comfort, into clarity.

“If school is the wrong language,” I said, “we’ll find you translation. But you don’t get to disappear. That’s the old you talking.”

He swallowed. “I don’t know how to do it.”

“Then you learn,” I said, and let a small edge of humour in, because humour makes medicine swallowable. “You’re doing a whole new trade. You can learn Year Twelve.”

His mouth twitched.

He didn’t say yes.

He didn’t say no.

But he didn’t argue either.

That, for Charlie, was progress.

Across the room, Mara’s voice cut through the hiss of the steamer.

“Rossignol!”

Charlie’s head snapped up immediately. “Yes?”

Mara held up a sleeve with a tear near the cuff. “This one. If you stitch it tight, I’ll know. And you’ll unpick it in front of everyone.”

Charlie went very still.

Then he said, clear and calm, “Okay.”

He rose, took the sleeve, and walked to Mara’s table with the careful confidence of someone who had found a rule-set that didn’t hate him.

As he passed me, his eyes flicked to my face again — quick, warm, grateful, frightened — and then away.

Not taking.

Just… orbiting.

I watched him go and thought, not unkindly:

He’s going to have to learn that being chosen is not a miracle.

It’s a responsibility.

And I’m not letting him waste it.

---

## Scene 5 (Mislabeled)

### Scene Four — *School, mid–Year 12*

By Term Two, the classrooms had settled into their preferred lies.

The first lie was that Year Twelve was about learning. The second was that pressure made you better. The third was that everyone in the room was equally human.

I’d watched those lies take hold the way mould takes hold: quietly, then all at once, until you couldn’t remember what clean air felt like.

Charlie sat where he always sat — not because he liked the back left corner, but because it was where you went when you wanted the least surface area. He kept his shoulders narrow, his legs tucked in, his pencil aligned with the edge of his workbook like it mattered. The act of aligning things was the closest he got to control.

Mr Greeves had stopped treating him as a joke months ago. In fact, of all the teachers, Greeves was the one who almost tried. He didn’t patronise Charlie. He didn’t perform sympathy. He’d given him that one nod the first week — *Good diagram* — as if competence was a thing he could grant in small, careful doses.

Which is why what happened next landed like betrayal, not cruelty.

Greeves wrote *EXPLAIN YOUR WORKING* on the board in large block letters, underlined it twice, then turned and faced the room with chalk dust on his fingertips.

“Right,” he said. “You lot can do the practice questions. Most of you can even get the right answers when the wind’s behind you. But what you can’t do is explain it. And that’s where the marks live.”

There were a few soft laughs, the obedient kind. Laughing at the teacher is how teenagers pay rent.

Greeves nodded at the front row, a boy with enough confidence to be noisy about it. “Jackson. Come up. Show us Question Seven.”

Jackson swaggered up, wrote the first line, then stalled. He put on the usual performance: half-joking, half-shrugging, pretending not to care. He got one line wrong. Greeves corrected him briskly and sent him back to his seat with a “Sit down.”

No harm. No humiliation. Just the normal ritual: boys get to fail as entertainment.

Greeves scanned the room again, his gaze moving like a spotlight looking for someone it could burn without consequence. His eyes flicked past me. Past the safe faces. Past the kids who would argue and get away with it.

Then—inevitably—to the corner.

“Charlie,” he said.

The room’s attention shifted in that particular way it did when it scented blood: quick, eager, pretending to be casual.

Charlie looked up immediately, like a dog who’d learned the sound of his name meant something was about to be demanded. He didn’t pretend not to hear. He didn’t do the boy thing of staring at his desk and forcing the teacher to repeat themselves. He was too honest for that.

“Yes?” he said. His voice didn’t crack. But something in his throat tightened, and I saw his pencil hesitate above the page.

“Come up,” Mr Greeves said, tapping the board. “Show us Question Seven properly. You can do it.”

The words *you can do it* were meant as encouragement. I could see that. But the room heard something else: *Watch him try.*

Charlie didn’t move at first. Not refusal—processing. His eyes dropped to his workbook, as if he could find a trapdoor in the lines of his own handwriting. Then he stood, slowly, and walked to the front with the careful gait of someone stepping onto ice.

He didn’t look at anyone as he passed. He looked at the floor, at the desk edges, at the safe geometry of straight lines. Not fear exactly. Avoidance. The human version of pulling your hands inside your sleeves.

Mr Greeves handed him the chalk like it was nothing. Like it didn’t weigh half a classroom.

“Take your time,” he said, and then—because he couldn’t help himself, because teachers are addicted to moral framing—added with a faint smile, “Show them, hey. Some of the staff keep telling me you won’t make it through this year. Prove them wrong.”

It was tossed out lightly, like camaraderie.

It was a knife.

Charlie froze just enough for me to see it: the tiny slackening of his face, as if someone had pulled a pin from underneath his skin. His gaze flicked to Greeves’ eyes — quick, searching — and then away again.

*Some of the staff.*

So it wasn’t just the students who had decided he was defective. It lived in the staffroom too. It lived in adult mouths. It lived where he couldn’t argue back.

And Mr Greeves had just carried it into the open like a bag of rubbish and left it on Charlie’s desk.

Greeves stepped aside to give him the board. The room went quiet, not because they cared, but because they were waiting for the sound of a crack.

Charlie wrote the question number neatly: **7.** The chalk squeaked once. His handwriting on the board was the same as on paper: controlled, slanted slightly right, as if it had manners even when forced into public.

Then he stared at the question on the projector screen.

I watched his breathing. In. Hold. Out. Like he was trying to keep his ribcage from being noticed.

He wrote the first line of working—correctly. A good start. A small mercy.

Behind me, someone shifted in their chair. Someone else coughed into their hand, the fake-casual sound of impatience. The room’s hunger sharpened.

Charlie wrote the second line. His chalk hesitated, then went on. The algebra was fine. Not brilliant, but fine. The kind of working that would earn marks in an exam and no applause in a classroom.

Then he reached the step where the method required confidence. Not mathematical confidence — social confidence. The kind where you commit to a rearrangement and trust your own hands.

He paused.

The pause was a bell.

A boy two rows back let out a small, involuntary snort. It wasn’t even fully laughter — it was the sound of somebody enjoying themselves.

Charlie’s shoulders tightened. Not visibly; he contained it. But I saw the way his neck tendons stood out. He kept his eyes on the board.

Mr Greeves made the mistake that mattered. He didn’t shut it down immediately.

He turned his head towards the sound with a “Come on,” said with a teacher’s weary indulgence—*boys will be boys*—as if a gentle scolding was enough to stop a pack.

It wasn’t.

The laughter didn’t swell into a roar. It didn’t need to. It stayed in little clicks and snickers, like stones tapping inside a tin.

Charlie’s chalk hovered.

And then I saw it: the thing I’d been hoping not to see.

A phone.

Not held high. Not obvious. Just a hand half-hidden behind a workbook, the black rectangle angled towards the front, the screen glowing faintly. Recording. Capturing. Making it permanent.

Charlie’s eyes flicked sideways—just once—and landed on the screen. His face didn’t change much. But something behind his eyes did.

He wasn’t panicking.

He was calculating.

*If they keep this, I exist forever as the misfit on the board.*

Mr Greeves spoke again, too bright, trying to rescue the moment with positivity.

“You’re alright,” he said. “Keep going. This is good for you.”

Good for you.

As if humiliation was physiotherapy.

Charlie swallowed. He wrote the next symbol. It was the wrong symbol.

Not because he didn’t know the right one. Because the room had reached into his hands and nudged. Because pressure doesn’t sharpen everyone. For some people, it blurs.

A couple of students laughed more openly now, relieved by the error. The teacher corrected him quickly, pointing at the board.

“No, no. Not that. You want to take it to the other side first, then divide.”

Charlie stared at the chalk line as if it had become foreign.

He nodded once. Automatically.

He erased the wrong symbol, rewrote the correct one, and his hand trembled enough that the chalk made a ragged mark.

Mr Greeves glanced at the class again, and his tone sharpened—finally, too late.

“Enough,” he snapped.

Silence. But the silence was the kind that comes after damage has already been done.

Charlie stood with the chalk in his hand, facing the board. The equation was there. The method was there. He could finish it if he wanted.

But his body had already made a different decision.

He put the chalk down.

It was such a small action—no drama, no flinging, no snapping—but it felt like a door closing.

He turned to Mr Greeves and said, very softly, “I can’t.”

Mr Greeves blinked. For a second he looked genuinely surprised, as if he hadn’t considered that the boy might refuse to be used.

“Charlie—” he began, lowering his voice as if gentleness could patch it now. “It’s alright. Just—”

He simply walked back to his desk.

The room watched him as he went. Some faces wore the satisfaction of having been right about him. Others looked away quickly, because watching became ugly once he chose not to play along.

At his desk, Charlie picked up his bag.

Not hurriedly. Not theatrically.

He moved with the same deliberate care he used in Wardrobe: place the book in, zip the pocket, check the strap, stand.

A chair scraped behind him. Someone whispered my name, as if I had some role to play.

I didn’t move.

This wasn’t my stage. And if I rushed to him now, I’d turn his exit into a rescue scene.

Charlie walked to the door.

Mr Greeves stepped forward. “Charlie, sit down.”

Charlie paused with his hand on the handle. He didn’t look back at the class.

He looked at Mr Greeves. Only him.

And he said, not angry, not trembling, not pleading — just clear:

“No.”

It wasn’t insolent. It wasn’t loud. It was self-respect in its simplest form: refusal to be handled.

Then he opened the door and left.

No slam.

The door clicked shut with a quiet finality that made my stomach go tight.

For a moment, the room sat in the stunned hush that comes after a rule has been broken. A student leaving class without permission was supposed to be unthinkable. You weren’t meant to do it. Especially not someone like Charlie, who’d spent his whole life making himself small enough to be tolerated.

Mr Greeves stared at the door.

Then he exhaled, and whatever expression crossed his face—annoyance, regret, embarrassment—he swallowed it and turned back to the board as if he could rewind.

“Right,” he said, voice too brisk. “We’re moving on. Write down what he did before he—”

Before he what? Before he vanished? Before he embarrassed you? Before he refused to be your lesson?

He didn’t finish the sentence. He didn’t have to. The room filled it in for him.

As he spoke, the phone disappeared back under the desk. The recording, I knew, was already safe in someone’s camera roll. The school would “address it” later with a generic email. The clip would live anyway. That’s how places like this worked.

I picked up my pen. I wrote nothing.

I watched Mr Greeves’ hand shake slightly as he wrote the method on the board himself, as if doing it now might prove he hadn’t made a miscalculation.

And all I could think was:

He didn’t disappear because he’s weak.

He disappeared because he refused to be eaten.

When the bell went, the class rose in a loud scrape of chairs and zips and forced casualness. People talked too brightly, excited by the disruption. A few kids glanced at me, curious how I’d react, as if my reaction could grant the event its meaning.

I didn’t give them anything.

I left the room at a normal pace.

In the corridor, the noise of the school pressed in—shouting, laughter, lockers banging—but Charlie wasn’t in it. He was gone with a precision that felt deliberate.

A boy brushed past me and said, laughing, “He finally cracked.”

I stopped walking.

I turned my head just enough for him to feel my gaze like a hand on his collar.

He faltered. “What?”

“He didn’t crack,” I said. My voice was calm. Almost mild. “He left.”

The boy blinked, confused by the fact that words could be different things. Then he shrugged, already bored, and moved on.

I continued down the corridor.

Not rushing. Not chasing.

Because Charlie had chosen dignity.

And I wasn’t going to trample it by making it dramatic.

But in my head, the decision formed with the clean satisfaction of a pattern clicking into place:

School had declared what it was.

Charlie had heard it.

Now the only question was how quickly we could get him out of its mouth — and into a room where he would be required to exist, not hidden.

Not saved.

Placed.

“With everyone,” Mara replied. “Because people love the idea of a system until a person refuses to be ground down by it.”

Mara’s gaze stayed on him. “No. He made a decision.”

I didn’t soften. I didn’t reassure.

I said, simply, “You can do this. But you do it properly.”

Charlie swallowed, then nodded again.

“Yes,” he said. “Properly.”

And just like that, the story shifted.

Not into rescue.

Into work.

Into responsibility.

Into the next room.

## Scene Eight — *Two women, the same map*

“You’re protective,” Lauren said.

Lauren’s mouth twitched. “That’s what protective looks like when you’ve had enough.”

Mara glanced back down at the fabric. She didn’t deny it. She didn’t confirm it. She simply returned the notebook.

Lauren’s eyes flicked away for a moment, and when she spoke again her voice was quieter, almost wry.

“You learn them,” she said, “or you get eaten.”

Mara’s eyes lifted to hers, steady.

“Yes,” Mara said. “Exactly.”

In the background, Charlie shifted a garment on the mannequin and said, softly, “Mara, the seam’s pulling when you raise the arm.”

Mara turned immediately, focus snapping back to the work.

“Good,” Mara said. “Different is useful. Different is how you build better.”

“Humiliation is a tool,” Mara said. “Some people use it because they have nothing else. We don’t use it here.”

“And what do you use,” she asked, as if she needed the answer for herself too.

Lauren nodded slowly, as if those words were something she could live by.

Not refuge.

Infrastructure.

Women building something that would hold.

And a boy learning, quietly, how to live inside it.

## Scene Seven — *Lauren Rossignol arrives* (Celeste POV)

Lauren Rossignol didn’t enter Wardrobe like a mother storming into a school office.

She entered like a woman who’d had enough of other people’s chaos and had finally decided she would not add to it—not even for her own child.

She stopped just inside the doorway, canvas tote on her shoulder, keys in her hand as if the weight of them was something she could press against her palm to stay calm. Her hair was pulled back too tight to be for beauty. Her face was clean—no makeup, no performance. Her expression was not soft.

Mara looked up from the cutting table, pencil tucked behind her ear, pattern paper spread out like a land survey.

“Can I help you,” Mara said, the way she said it to everyone: flat, not unfriendly, not inviting.

“I’m Charlie’s mother,” the woman replied.

I didn’t look up immediately. I kept tagging garment bags at the rail and let Mara hold the room the way she always did. Wardrobe was hers. If you wanted to keep things clean, you didn’t wrestle the centre off her.

Mara’s eyes shifted, just slightly, like a lens tightening focus.

“Right,” Mara said. “Lauren.”

The woman blinked—only once. It wasn’t a flinch. It was surprise at being named before she’d offered it.

She looked around the room. Not with curiosity, but with assessment. Rails. Mannequin. Prototype jacket pinned into shape. Worktable cleared for pattern paper instead of emergency mending. The place had changed, and she could see it.

“My son says he isn’t going back to school,” she said. “He says he’s working here.”

Mara nodded once as if she’d been told the weather.

“He is.”

Lauren’s jaw tightened. “He’s eighteen. He’s leaving Year Twelve. That’s not… smart.”

Mara didn’t soften. She didn’t reassure.

“It’s not what you wanted,” she said.

Lauren exhaled through her nose, controlled. There was fatigue in it. The kind you got when you had carried too many people’s consequences and were tired of pretending you weren’t.

“No,” she said. “It isn’t.”

Mara flicked two fingers towards the chair by the table. Not an invitation. Permission.

Lauren sat carefully and kept her keys on her knee rather than putting them on the table, as if she didn’t want to claim space she hadn’t earned. Her tote stayed on her shoulder.

“You’re running a studio,” she observed, eyes still moving. “Not… a dressing-up shop.”

Mara’s mouth tightened.

“Correct.”

Lauren nodded once. Then she asked the only question worth asking—plain, adult, without theatrics.

“Is he hiding here?”

The women in the room didn’t stop moving, but the air shifted. Everyone heard it. No one pretended not to.

Mara’s gaze slid to Charlie at the inventory drawer. He was sorting hooks and tape with his usual precision, head down, hands clean, shoulders quiet. He didn’t look up to see if anyone was talking about him. He didn’t perform awareness. That alone told you he’d been in trouble before, and had learned the only safe way to survive it: work as if work is prayer.

“He’s not hiding,” Mara said. “Not the way you mean.”

Lauren’s fingers tightened around her keys. “And the way you mean?”

Mara’s eyes returned to her.

“The way I mean is he’s chosen a room that rewards him for being precise,” Mara said. “School doesn’t.”

“School is still school,” Lauren said, as if saying it made it unavoidable.

Mara nodded once—conceding the fact without granting it authority.

“And this is still work,” Mara replied. “With deadlines. With standards. With consequences.”

Lauren’s gaze flicked to Charlie again. Her voice stayed level, but I caught the fear under it: not the fear of him failing, the fear of him being swallowed.

“He’s not built for consequences,” she said.

Mara raised one eyebrow.

“He is,” she said, crisp. “He’s built for them more than most. He just doesn’t tolerate being mocked while he learns.”

Lauren’s throat moved. She swallowed it down.

“He’s always been… gentle,” she said, like the word might be misinterpreted if it left her mouth too clearly. “And school treats gentleness like weakness.”

“School treats anything it can’t classify as weakness,” Mara said. “That’s what institutions do.”

Lauren looked down at her keys for a beat. When she looked up again, the calm was still there, but it had a new edge: the edge of a mother who was no longer negotiating with herself.

“I’m disappointed,” she said. “I wanted him to finish. I wanted that paper. I wanted him to have choices.”

Mara let the sentence sit without decorating it.

Lauren continued, quieter now. “But I also don’t want him to be eaten alive.”

Mara’s gaze sharpened as if she’d heard the one honest thing in a room full of polite lies.

“I don’t run a sanctuary,” Mara said. “If he’s here, he works. He doesn’t drift. He doesn’t disappear mid-task because he can’t cope.”

Lauren’s eyes flicked to Charlie.

“He disappears when he’s ashamed,” she said.

Mara nodded once, filing it like a measurement.

“Then he learns not to,” Mara said. “Or he doesn’t stay.”

Lauren didn’t like it. I could see that. But she didn’t reject it either. Harshness was not the same as cruelty. It was sometimes the only honest form of care.

Her gaze moved—briefly—to the fitting curtains.

“And you’re comfortable,” she asked carefully, “having him here? Around… around all this?”

Mara’s eyes narrowed.

“You mean around women.”

Lauren didn’t flinch. She didn’t deny it.

“Yes.”

Mara leaned forward slightly, not threatening—simply making the boundary visible.

“This is a women’s space,” she said. “It stays that way because we keep it that way. He is not entitled to anything in this room. He’s here because he is useful and respectful. If that changes, he’s out.”

Lauren’s shoulders loosened a fraction, as if that sentence unknotted something she’d been carrying.

Mara sat back.

“Now,” she said, brisk, “what do you actually want.”

Lauren inhaled, then answered without wobble.

“I want to know if this is real,” she said. “Not a phase. Not him hiding because school is hard. Real training. Real work. Real future.”

Mara’s eyes flicked to the prototype jacket and back.

“It’s real,” she said. “Because I’m making it real.”

Lauren held her gaze. Then she brought my name into the room like she was testing it.

“And where does Celeste fit into this?”

Mara didn’t look at me when she answered. She didn’t need to. It wasn’t my authority that mattered.

“Celeste is the research,” Mara said. “The direction. The brain that won’t let the work get lazy.”

Lauren’s eyes came to me then—direct, sharp.

“And you,” she said. “Are you rescuing him?”

That was the moment you could ruin everything with softness.

I lifted my head.

“No,” I said. One clean syllable. “He’s working. Mara decides whether he stays.”

Mara’s mouth twitched—approval, minimal.

Lauren nodded once. As if she’d asked what she needed to ask and received the answer she needed to hear.

She stood and shifted her tote into her hand. From it she drew a folded bundle of fabric.

“This is his,” she said, placing it on the table without ceremony. “He left it at home. I washed it. I pressed it. Whatever it is.”

Mara unfolded it: a linen apron. Hemmed cleanly. Ties reinforced. Stitching invisible.

Mara’s gaze flicked to Lauren.

“You sewed this.”

“I fixed it,” Lauren said, like the difference mattered.

Mara ran a finger along the stitching.

“Good work,” she said.

Lauren blinked again. A small, involuntary surprise at praise that wasn’t wrapped in obligation.

Mara folded the apron and set it with the issued workwear as if it had been accepted into the system.

Lauren looked at Charlie.

“Charlie,” she said, louder now, not to shame him but to place truth where it belonged. “I’m disappointed. I wanted you to finish.”

Charlie’s hands paused. He didn’t turn around.

“I know,” he said quietly.

Lauren swallowed whatever wanted to rise in her throat. I respected her for that. Not because emotion is weakness—but because she knew this wasn’t the room for it.

Mara cut cleanly across the moment.

“Rossignol,” she called. “Continue.”

Charlie resumed immediately.

Lauren exhaled once, like she’d just watched her son survive something without disintegrating.

She picked up her keys again and looked at Mara.

“If he’s here,” she said, “he works.”

Mara nodded. “Correct.”

“And if the school comes sniffing,” Lauren added—her voice now containing something new: not panic, but readiness.

Mara’s gaze hardened. “They can sniff elsewhere.”

Lauren’s mouth twitched. Not a smile. Relief in disguise.

She turned to go, paused at the door, and looked back at Mara.

“I don’t do pity,” she said. “He won’t survive that.”

Mara answered immediately, without softness.

“Neither do I,” she said. “That’s why he might.”

Lauren left. The door clicked shut. The room returned to its rhythm.

Mara looked at me, pencil still behind her ear.

“Your friend,” she said.

“She’s not my friend,” I replied.

Mara’s mouth twitched again, faintly amused.

“Good,” she said. “Keep it clean.”

## Scene Eight — *Two women, the same map* (Celeste POV)

Lauren returned a week later, and she didn’t look like she’d slept badly this time.

She still looked tired—because mothers are always tired—but the braced edge had softened into something else: acceptance with boundaries. Like she’d stopped fighting the fact of what was happening and started watching how well it was being done.

She didn’t bring keys to squeeze. She brought a small paper bag that smelled like coffee and warm pastry.

Mara glanced up from the worktable.

“We don’t eat over fabric,” she said.

Lauren nodded as if she’d expected that.

“I didn’t bring it for you to eat over fabric,” she said. Dry humour, controlled. “I brought it because you look like the sort of woman who forgets food when she’s busy.”

Mara’s eyes narrowed.

“I don’t forget,” Mara said. “I postpone.”

Lauren’s mouth twitched. “Yes. That.”

I stayed near the rail, letting them speak. This wasn’t my meeting. This was infrastructure forming.

Lauren stood and watched the room—quietly, not intrusively.

The atelier had taken shape fast. The new garments were not prettier for the sake of it; they were *better*. Reinforced where stress hit. Forgiving where bodies moved. Designed for the real physics of the faire instead of the fantasy of it.

Charlie had become the engine beneath it. He didn’t talk much. He simply made things survive.

Lauren watched him now near the fitting curtains, holding a bodice steady while Mara adjusted a seam on the mannequin.

He didn’t look up for her approval. He didn’t seek her face like a boy asking permission.

He simply worked.

Lauren’s expression shifted. Pride, restrained so tightly it nearly looked like pain.

Mara noticed it without looking.

“You can watch,” Mara said. “Just don’t hover. He doesn’t need that.”

“I’m not hovering,” Lauren replied.

Mara’s mouth twitched. “You’re hovering in French.”

Lauren let out a short laugh that startled even her, as if humour had slipped out before she could stop it.

“You’re Australian,” she said. “You shouldn’t know that.”

Mara went back to her pins. “Women are women,” she said. “Different accents.”

Lauren stepped closer to the worktable and reached into her tote. She pulled out a small notebook. When she opened it, I saw fabric swatches taped in neat rows.

“I’ve got a supplier in Sydney,” Lauren said, voice turning practical—the way women often smuggled intimacy in under logistics. “Linen that doesn’t go transparent under light. Not cheap. But consistent. If you’re moving into design, you’ll want consistent.”

Mara’s fingers paused. For Mara, that was a reaction.

She held out her hand. “Let me see.”

Lauren passed the notebook across.

Mara examined the swatches the way she examined everything: with honesty. Thumb and forefinger, rubbing the weave lightly, testing body, strength, recovery.

“This one has spine,” Mara said. “It won’t collapse.”

Lauren nodded. “That’s why I used it.”

Mara’s eyes flicked up. “For what.”

Lauren didn’t answer immediately. She watched Charlie for a moment—his hands, his steadiness, the way he held fabric like it deserved respect.

Then she said, carefully, “For things that need to survive men.”

It was the first personal sentence she’d offered, and she didn’t dramatise it. She didn’t attach a story. She simply placed the truth on the table like a tool.

Mara’s face didn’t change much, but her eyes softened in a way only another woman would catch.

“Mm,” Mara said. “Yes.”

Lauren exhaled slowly, like she’d been holding her breath in her own life for too long.

“You’re protective,” Lauren said.

Mara snorted. “I’m professional.”

Lauren’s mouth twitched. “That’s what protective looks like when you’ve had enough.”

Mara didn’t deny it. She didn’t confirm it either. She returned the notebook and said instead, “You have standards.”

Lauren’s gaze flicked away briefly. When she spoke, her voice was quieter.

“You learn them,” she said, “or you get eaten.”

Mara looked at her then, steady.

“Yes,” Mara said. “Exactly.”

They held that look for a beat—no sentimentality, no softness—just recognition. Two women reading from the same map.

In the background, Charlie shifted fabric on the mannequin and said softly, “Mara—the seam’s pulling when you raise the arm.”

Mara turned immediately, attention snapping back to the work.

“Good catch,” she said, then added—because she couldn’t help herself—“Of course it is. It’s always there.”

Charlie didn’t smile. He didn’t preen. He just held the bodice steady while Mara repinned, the way he held everything: quietly, without demanding credit.

Lauren watched him again, and this time her pride showed slightly more openly.

“He’s… different,” she said.

“Good,” Mara replied. “Different is useful. Different is how you build better.”

Lauren’s throat moved. She swallowed.

“He wanted to quit school because he felt humiliated,” she said, and the word *humiliated* was controlled but bitter, like it didn’t belong in her mouth.

“Humiliation is a tool,” Mara said. “Some people use it because they have nothing else. We don’t use it here.”

Lauren nodded slowly, as if she needed to hear that from someone other than herself.

“And what do you use,” she asked, and I knew she wasn’t asking only for Charlie.

Mara tugged the fabric once, then twice, testing tension.

“We use standard,” she said. “We use consequence. We use work.”

Lauren stood still, as if those words were something she could finally rest on.

Then she surprised herself again by adding, lightly, “And coffee.”

Mara’s mouth twitched.

Lauren went to the side bench and opened the paper bag. Two coffees emerged—not delicate, not fancy. She set one near Mara’s elbow, away from fabric.

Mara stared at it like it might be a trap.

“Don’t make it a thing,” Lauren said, already turning away as if generosity had to be disguised to be tolerated.

Mara picked up the cup, took a sip, and said, grudgingly, “Fine.”

Lauren’s smile was brief and real.

In the fitting corner, Charlie glanced over—not at his mother exactly, but at the coffee, at the two women standing in quiet alliance. The look on his face wasn’t dramatic. It was the subtle weather-shift of someone realising the world might actually hold.

He didn’t ask what they’d talked about. He didn’t intrude.

He just returned to the seam and held the fabric steady while Mara corrected the line.

And for the first time since he’d walked out of school, I felt something settle into place—not refuge, not rescue.

Infrastructure.

Women building something that would hold.

And Charlie learning, quietly, how to live inside it.

[Q]  
I do have a question,Emily. For the sake of veracity, what actually happens in, say, Victoria Australia when a student decides mid-term to stop attending school? Are there repercussions? Is there a remedy?

---

[Emily]  
Yes — and it depends *a lot* on the student’s age and what (if anything) they transition into.

### 1) If they’re under 17

In Victoria, schooling is compulsory from age 6 until turning 17 (unless an exemption is granted). ([Department of Education Victoria][1])
So if a student under 17 just stops showing up mid-term:

* **The school will contact the parent/carer** and require an explanation for absences; parents/carers have a legal obligation to ensure attendance. ([Victoria Government][2])
* If it becomes a pattern, the school escalates through its attendance processes (meetings, documentation, support, re-engagement planning). ([Department of Education Victoria][1])
* **A “remedy” exists, but it’s formal**: the family can pursue an **attendance/enrolment exemption** (e.g., if the student will participate full-time in approved education/training and/or employment, or if leaving is in the student’s best interests). ([Department of Education Victoria][3])
* Another lawful pathway is **home education**, but in Victoria that requires **registration with the VRQA** (for ages 6–17) and an approved learning plan. ([www2.vrqa.vic.gov.au][4])

### 2) If they’re 17 (and not yet 18)

They’re no longer “compulsory school age,” but Victoria still frames this period as needing to be within permitted age/eligibility rules and/or a transition plan, and schools still push strongly toward **approved study/training/work pathways**. ([Department of Education Victoria][5])

### 3) If they’re 18 (Charlie’s case in your story)

This is the key detail for veracity.

In Victorian **government** schools, a person aged 18+ generally must not enrol/attend **unless an exception or exemption applies** — and one major exception is exactly what Charlie would be doing: turning 18 during the year while enrolled solely in an accredited senior secondary course (e.g., VCE / VCE VM / IB / VPC). ([Department of Education Victoria][5])

So: **Charlie being 18 in Year 12 is plausible** (especially if he turned 18 during the enrolment year and is doing VCE/VCE VM). ([Department of Education Victoria][5])

If an 18-year-old simply stops attending mid-term:

* It’s **less about “you’re breaking compulsory schooling law”** (that’s mainly the 6–17 obligation) and more about:

  * the school’s **attendance/welfare duty of care**, and
  * the practical reality that he’s now **not going to complete VCE** unless something changes. ([Department of Education Victoria][1])
* The school will still call home, request meetings, document non-attendance, and push re-engagement or a pathway shift.

### What “remedy” looks like in practice (and how you can dramatise it)

If you want it to feel real without becoming paperwork-porn, the most believable remedies are:

1. **A careers/pathways meeting** (school tries to keep the student in some recognised track). ([Victoria Government][6])
2. **A formal exemption process** if leaving school is judged in the student’s best interests or they’ll do approved work/training. ([Department of Education Victoria][7])
3. **Shift to VCE VM / VET / TAFE / apprenticeship/traineeship** type pathways (often framed as “still engaged” rather than “dropped out”). ([Youth Central][8])
4. **Home education** only if under 17; for Charlie at 18 it’s not the standard lever, but you *can* still use “adult education / alternative settings” as a pivot (or keep it simple: he’s 18, he walks, school can’t legally *force* attendance the same way). ([www2.vrqa.vic.gov.au][4])

### The story-friendly punchline

* If Charlie were **under 17**, the system has sharper “teeth” and the parent is on the hook to either fix attendance or formalise an alternative. ([Victoria Government][2])
* Because Charlie is **18**, the system’s resistance is more likely to be **bureaucratic + reputational + moral pressure**:

  * meetings,
  * “you’re wasting your future,”
  * “you’ll have no options,”
  * perhaps a wellbeing check,
  * and a push to re-enrol or transfer into a recognised pathway.

If you tell me whether Charlie is in a **government school or a private school**, I can tune the exact flavour of “system resistance” (language, formality, who calls whom). But even without that, your current direction is very defensible for Victoria: the school tries to pull him back *quietly*, and he refuses *quietly*, and the real battle becomes paperwork, stigma, and adult pressure — not handcuffs.

No fuss. No jokes. No eyebrow raises.

Just… order.

Charlie, at the bench, lifted his head.

“I can do it now,” he said.

“Good,” she said. “Ten minutes. Break it on purpose.”

As the curtain fell closed behind him, I felt a small, quiet satisfaction.

School had made him visible in the worst way—public, exposed, used as a lesson.

Wardrobe had made him visible in the only way that mattered: as a person whose judgement the room trusted.

Not because we coddled him.

Because we built a system where his precision had a place.

And once a precision like that has a place, it doesn’t stop.

It becomes the rule.
