

# Glom

It tracks — strongly — **with one tweak** so it doesn’t slip into “women using a boy as a lab rat,” which could accidentally undermine your female readership goal.

Right now your idea has a powerful spine:

* Charl is horrified by the lived experience.
* Celeste and Talia gain insight (therapeutic, political, educational).
* Celeste *cares* enough to stop it when it becomes damaging.
* Talia resists because she’s attached to the value of the “data.”
* Celeste chooses Charl anyway.
* Celeste’s attraction intensifies, and she’s disturbed that it’s **Charl** (the socialised/constructed presence) she’s drawn to.

That’s all fertile, and it’s very “your” thematic territory: ethics, agency, and the ugliness of systems.

### The tweak that makes it land

Make it explicit that Talia’s site/work is **about women’s recovery and boundary education**, *not* about parading Charl’s experience as content.

In other words: Charl doesn’t become “material.” He becomes a **mirror** that helps them name what they already knew — and the “rebuild sessions” use anonymised patterns, not his story as spectacle.

If you do that, it’s credible and it stays clean.

## Why it works emotionally

Celeste’s “pull the plug” moment is an outstanding character beat because it proves:

* her power isn’t just sharpness, it’s **ethical governance**
* she can override her own strategic appetite
* she chooses a vulnerable person’s wellbeing over a clever project

That’s a *huge* signal to women readers: she’s not just competent, she’s safe.

And Talia being upset? Also credible, if it’s written as:

* frustration at losing a rare window into how the world operates (through fresh eyes), **and**
* fear that without this mirror, they’ll slide back into minimising their own experience (women do this constantly), **and**
* perhaps a twinge of guilt that she was benefiting from someone else’s distress.

That’s not villainy. That’s realism.

## Celeste being attracted to “Charl, not Charlie”

This is *excellent* and honestly very sophisticated.

Because it’s not “I’m attracted to feminisation.” It’s:

* Celeste is attracted to **competence in adaptation**,
* to **attunement**,
* to **emotional intelligence**,
* to **the shared language of women’s calibration**,
* to someone who “gets it” and can move through the world with that awareness.

And yes — it disturbs her because it forces her to ask:

* Is she attracted to *the person*, or to *the construct she helped create*?
* Is her desire ethical, or is it just her strategist-brain enjoying ownership?
* Is she drawn to him because he’s become legible in her world now?

That is exactly the kind of self-interrogation that keeps you far away from dopamine-bait.

### Make sure Charl doesn’t become a “project” in this revelation

The attraction should scare Celeste because it threatens to make her unethical. That’s the point.

---

## Does it push you off base?

No — if you keep these guardrails:

1. **Charl’s consent stays explicit and revisable**, and Celeste honours that.
2. **Charl’s distress is not a “means.”** The moment it becomes harm, Celeste stops.
3. **Talia’s work uses insights, not Charl’s narrative as content.**
4. Celeste’s attraction is framed as **complicated responsibility**, not gratification.

Do that, and it’s not only on base — it’s an arc that can power the next 2–3 chapters.

## How I’d stage it (high-level beats)

1. **A visible symptom:** Charl dissociates, has a quiet panic episode, or goes numb after a donor interaction.
2. **Talia recognises it first:** she’s the one who names the pattern (“this is not ‘getting used to it,’ this is injury”).
3. **Celeste decides:** “We stop.” No debate.
4. **Talia argues (cleanly):** she frames the value, the scarcity of this perspective, the mission.
5. **Celeste draws a line:** “You don’t get to build women’s healing on someone else’s breakdown.”
6. **Aftermath:** Celeste realises she misses Charl’s presence with a tight, improper ache — and that it’s *Charl* she misses.

That last beat is your disturbed attraction revelation.

## Celeste — The Line We Don’t Cross

You’re right to call it out. That “we print rules and make donors behave” move **does** drift toward a gentler world than most women actually get. It reads like *institutional correction*, and your story’s moral weather is more like: **women build their own scaffolding, men keep their entitlement, and the price of peace is paid by the vigilant.**

So yes: a more realistic next step is **not reform** but **initiation**.

Not “we change the manor,” but:
“Welcome to the reality. We don’t get to fix the room. We get to read it, survive it, and keep each other intact.”

That still gives you forward momentum, still keeps Charl from being a “fragile male,” and still preserves Celeste’s strategist nature—because her strategy becomes *social*, not policy.

Below are a few alternatives that track with what you want, and then I’ll give you a replacement scene (Celeste POV) you can drop in immediately.

---

## What could happen instead

### 1) The “No Illusions” talk

Charl objects to being sent back to Wardrobe because it feels like erasure. Celeste agrees—but she refuses the fantasy of reform.

Celeste says:

* “We’re not bending society to you.”
* “If you want my world, it’s on my terms.”
* “My terms are: you learn the choreography, you don’t romanticise it, and you don’t demand that women become your training wheels.”
* “And you always retain the right to step out.”

Charl chooses to stay anyway, **not because it’s arousing**, not because he likes it, but because:

* he wants proximity to Celeste (more than friendship now),
* and because he’s found the first true community he’s ever had—women who are frank, protective, sharp, and real.

### 2) Women’s support as “infrastructure,” not rescue

Instead of rules for donors, the women create **informal protocols**:

* always enter donor spaces in pairs,
* pre-agreed “extract” phrases (“Could you fetch the ledger?”),
* post-event debriefs where they name what happened (this is the therapeutic value),
* and a strict rule: no one mocks anyone for their coping. Ever.

This is *exactly* how solidarity shows up: practical, not preachy.

### 3) A realistic “test” that isn’t sexy

Charl is reintroduced to public spaces, but in a controlled way:

* not a donor gala,
* but a smaller tour, a tea service, a mild event.

A man says something entitled. Nothing dramatic. Just entitlement.

The women respond with the calibrated smile + boundary + exit. Charl watches it and feels the grief of it—but also learns the skill. Later he admits: “I used to think women were being ‘nice.’ Now I know it’s labour.”

### 4) Celeste’s leverage becomes private, not public

Celeste does **not** change management. She changes *Charl’s relationship to the experience*:

* teaching him how to recover afterwards,
* teaching him that humiliation isn’t the point; safety is,
* teaching him that “success” is getting through without losing yourself.

That’s the realistic path: not reform, but survival + meaning-making.

### Celeste — The Terms

Charl found me where the manor stopped pretending.

The back passage near the laundry doors, where the air was damp with steam and the walls were scuffed from decades of carts and elbows. No portraits. No donors. No performance.

Just the building’s spine.

She didn’t hover this time. She stepped into my path like someone who had decided she was allowed to take up space.

“Celeste,” she said.

I stopped.

Her hair was loosely tied, Wardrobe-simple. Her hands bore the small injuries of work: pinpricks, thread burns, a faint red line across one knuckle where a needle had gone in too far.

Her face was calm—but too calm, the way people get when they’re holding something back by force.

“Yes,” I said.

Charl swallowed. “I don’t want to be sent back as if yesterday proved something about me.”

I didn’t answer immediately.

I let her sit in the silence she’d earned. I let her feel I wasn’t going to shoo her away like an inconvenience.

Then I said, evenly, “What do you think yesterday proved?”

Charl’s mouth tightened. “That… this is the price. That women live in a room where the air is different. And that I—”

She stopped. Her jaw clenched.

“That I couldn’t breathe in it,” she finished, voice rough.

I watched her carefully.

“That’s not what it proved,” I said.

Charl blinked, suspicious. “Then what?”

“It proved the room is poisonous,” I replied. “Women don’t survive it because we’re built for poison. We survive it because we learn to metabolise it. And even then, it costs us.”

Charl’s eyes flicked away. “So why did you pull me out?”

Because I couldn’t bear watching you disappear, I thought.

Because something in me has begun to care in a way that is ethically inconvenient.

I didn’t say those parts.

I said the truth that mattered.

“Because you were injured,” I said. “And because I won’t pretend injury is a lesson you must ‘push through’ to earn belonging.”

Charl’s shoulders loosened slightly, then tightened again.

“But if I go back to Wardrobe,” she said, “it looks like… like the solution is erasure.”

I nodded once. “It can be.”

Charl’s eyes sharpened. “And you’re okay with that?”

“No,” I said. “I’m accustomed to it.”

The words landed between us, heavy and unpretty.

Charl took a breath. “I don’t want to disappear.”

I held her gaze.

“Then don’t,” I said.

She stared. “It’s not that simple.”

“No,” I agreed. “It isn’t.”

A beat.

Then I said the sentence that made the whole thing real.

“We are not going to reform men,” I told her. “Not here. Not in this building. Not in your lifetime, if we’re being honest.”

Charl went still.

I continued, voice calm, almost clinical. “Men will remain entitled. The world will continue. The price will still be paid by the vigilant.”

Charl’s throat moved. “Then what am I supposed to do?”

I stepped closer—not crowding, just making it impossible for her to pretend she was alone.

“You decide what you want badly enough to endure reality,” I said.

Charl flinched slightly, like the words had teeth.

“And if what I want is… you?” she asked, very quietly.

There it was. Not romantic flourishes. Just a raw admission, laid down carefully like a blade.

I didn’t smile.

I didn’t soften it.

I let it be what it was: incentive. Gravity. A dangerous kind of truth.

“You can want me,” I said. “But you don’t get to make your wanting my responsibility.”

Charl’s eyes widened.

I went on, because this was the part women rarely get to say out loud.

“If you stay near me,” I said, “it’s not because you’re useful or because you’re earning anything. It’s because you choose it.”

Charl swallowed hard.

“And,” I added, “if you choose it, you choose the terms of the world I live in.”

Charl’s voice went thin. “Your terms.”

“Yes,” I said.

She stared at me, breathing shallow. “Which are?”

I didn’t hesitate.

“First,” I said, “you don’t romanticise this as strength. When women smile to survive, it isn’t nobility. It’s labour.”

Charl nodded once.

“Second,” I said, “you don’t demand that women change their coping to make you comfortable. You learn from it. You respect it.”

Her jaw tightened again—accepting the sting.

“Third,” I said, “you stop thinking of Wardrobe as a punishment. It’s a refuge. A base. A place where you’re seen. You don’t ‘graduate’ out of it by suffering publicly.”

Charl blinked.

“And fourth,” I finished, quieter, “you retain your exit. Always. If it is too much, you say so. You leave. And nobody—nobody—shames you for protecting yourself.”

Charl’s eyes shone, angry and relieved at once.

“That sounds like you’re… letting me off,” she whispered.

I shook my head once. “It’s not ‘letting you off.’ It’s refusing to turn your breakdown into a morality play.”

Charl’s breath trembled.

Then she said, almost bitterly, “Women don’t get that option.”

“No,” I agreed, voice flat. “They often don’t.”

Charl’s face tightened, grief passing through it like a shadow.

“And that,” I added, “is why you don’t get to treat staying as a love proof.”

A long silence.

Charl looked down at her hands.

When she spoke again, her voice was steadier.

“I didn’t know,” she said. “I didn’t know this was what women live in.”

I didn’t answer. I let the building answer for me—the hush, the damp, the scuffed walls. The truth lives in places like this, not in donor rooms.

Charl lifted her head.

“But now I do,” she said. “And I can’t unsee it.”

She swallowed.

“And I still… want to stay near you,” she said. “Not because I enjoy it. Not because I want the role. Because—”

She paused, searching.

“Because I’ve never had anyone,” she finished quietly, “who looked at me like I wasn’t defective.”

My throat tightened. Annoying.

I kept my voice even.

“Then you stay,” I said. “And you learn.”

Charl nodded once, as if sealing an agreement.

“And I don’t disappear,” she said.

I held her gaze.

“No,” I said. “You don’t disappear.”

A beat.

“You become fluent,” I added.

Charl’s mouth tightened. “At what cost?”

I didn’t lie.

“Some cost,” I said. “But not your self.”

Charl exhaled slowly.

Then she asked, almost timidly, “So what happens next?”

I let myself show a hint of the strategist again—not policy, not reform. Just choreography.

“Next,” I said, “you come with me again—briefly. Not because the world will change.”

Charl’s eyes narrowed.

“Then why?”

“Because you will,” I said.

And for the first time since yesterday, Charl’s face shifted into something that wasn’t panic or numbness.

Resolve.

“Okay,” she said.

Not happy.

Not eager.

Willing.

And that—more than any printed rule—felt like the most realistic kind of hope.

## Celeste — The Small Room Where It Happens

The first time back wasn’t a gala.

I wasn’t cruel enough for that.

It was a “heritage tour,” which is what the manor called a slow procession of money through curated rooms. Ten people. Two couples. One older woman who’d already decided she disliked me. Three men with the relaxed entitlement of people who’ve never had to wonder whether a space is safe.

Charl—Sharl—stood just behind my shoulder, in the quiet position we’d practised: present, useful, unremarkable. Not invisible. Not centre.

I could feel her focus like heat.

I didn’t look at her. I didn’t need to. I knew she was doing what women do without being told: scanning exits, reading faces, listening for tone.

The irony is that she was better at it than most men would ever be.

Not because she was “built for it.”

Because fear is a teacher that never sleeps.

We moved from the entrance hall into the long gallery, where the portraits performed lineage and the windows performed light. The donors murmured appreciatively, as if appreciation were a kind of generosity.

I spoke in the voice management liked: smooth, factual, faintly amused. Charm as a lubricant.

“As you can see, the gallery was designed to make the household look effortless,” I said. “And of course, the effort was… everything else.”

Polite laughter. Safe laughter.

A man near the back—mid-forties, tan too perfect to be local—tilted his head at me. His gaze did the brief, assessing sweep men do when they decide a woman is an object they’re allowed to enjoy.

Then his eyes slid to Charl.

Not hunting, not leering.

Just that mild, casual ownership: *you’re staff; you’re part of the furnishings; I can address you however I like.*

He smiled.

“Well,” he said, voice warm with false friendliness, “aren’t you a pretty little thing.”

It wasn’t obscene.

That was the problem. It was socially admissible, which meant it was harder to fight.

Charl’s shoulders tightened minutely. I felt it like a tug on a thread.

I kept speaking as if I hadn’t heard—because sometimes you don’t confront; you redirect. Strategy isn’t always a battle. Sometimes it’s steering the conversation away from the cliff.

But the man wasn’t finished.

He stepped a half pace closer, hand lifting as if to indicate her cap—an easy gesture, dressed up as helpfulness.

“Your lace is—”

Talia appeared from the side corridor like she’d been conjured. Not dramatic. Not hurried. Just there, suddenly between the hand and Charl’s head, her own clipboard angled casually like it had always belonged in the space.

“Sir,” Talia said, bright and polite. “Staff attire is not adjusted by guests. It breaks the immersion.”

She smiled. A women’s smile: pleasant, absolute, completely uninterested in negotiating.

The man blinked, surprised. His hand stalled in the air for a moment, then lowered as if he’d simply changed his mind.

“Oh,” he said, chuckling. “I was only trying to help.”

“Of course,” Talia replied, still smiling. “And you’re helping beautifully by staying in role.”

The man laughed again, louder, as if volume could reclaim authority.

I watched him decide whether to push. His eyes flicked to me, then to the others. The room’s temperature changed: the little calculation men do when they realise a woman isn’t alone.

He shrugged, with the faint sulk of someone denied a casual pleasure.

“Alright then,” he said. “No harm meant.”

No harm meant is what people say when they’ve already done harm and want you to carry it for them.

We moved on.

Charl didn’t speak. She didn’t need to. Silence was one of the skills women learn early: the difference between holding your ground and offering a target.

In the solar, I pointed out a writing desk, an old map, a set of letters staged for authenticity. Charl’s eyes stayed on the tour group, tracking.

A young woman—one of the couples—glanced at Charl with curiosity. Not predatory. Just interest. She seemed to register something unusual and then, almost instinctively, softened her gaze.

Solidarity has tells. Women know them.

The man from before lingered again at the edge of the group, still mildly annoyed that his access had been limited. His annoyance needed somewhere to go.

He let his eyes travel up and down Charl’s posture and made the smallest, laziest remark—barely a remark at all.

“Must be nice,” he said, to no one in particular, “to have a job where you just stand and look decorative.”

Charl flinched so subtly most people would have missed it.

I didn’t.

Talia didn’t.

Mara, passing through with a tray of glasses, didn’t miss it either—though she usually missed nothing. She paused beside the man and offered him a glass with a calmness that was almost chilling.

“Actually,” Mara said, voice flat, “staff keep the manor running. Decorative is the guests.”

The man’s face tightened. “Excuse me?”

Mara’s expression didn’t change. “It’s part of the historical accuracy,” she added, deadpan. “The household pretends the aristocracy is essential.”

A few people laughed—women, mostly. The older woman who disliked me smiled despite herself.

The man flushed, caught between embarrassment and the fear of looking humourless.

“Well,” he said, trying for a joke, “touché.”

Mara held his gaze for one extra beat—long enough to establish that she was not flirting, not playing, not granting him the comfort of being liked.

Then she moved on.

The room breathed again.

Charl’s shoulders dropped a fraction.

I watched her take in what had happened—how it had happened. Not with a grand confrontation. With a net. With women moving like a practiced unit. With small, clean interventions that prevented the harm from becoming a scene.

We finished the tour without further incidents.

When the last donor finally drifted toward the dining hall, I guided Charl into the narrow staff corridor and closed the door behind us. The noise of the manor softened to a distant hum.

Charl stood very still.

Then she exhaled, once, sharply—like a person realising they’d been holding their breath for an hour.

I didn’t comfort her immediately. I waited, because the first thing a frightened person needs is not reassurance but *space to feel what they feel.*

Talia appeared again, as if on cue, and leaned her shoulder against the opposite wall. Mara passed behind her, paused briefly, and—without a word—set a mug of tea into Charl’s hands.

Charl blinked at the mug as if it had arrived by magic.

Mara’s eyes met mine. She gave the smallest nod—then walked away again, already back inside her own armour.

Talia watched Charl carefully. “Where are you,” she asked, low.

Charl swallowed. “Here.”

“Good,” Talia said. “Feet on the floor?”

Charl nodded once. “Yes.”

Talia’s voice stayed calm, almost bored, which was a gift. “Any nausea. Any ringing in your ears. Any ‘everything is far away’ feeling.”

Charl hesitated, then admitted, “A little.”

Talia nodded, satisfied—not pleased, simply noting it. “That’s your body coming back online.”

Charl stared at the tea mug. Her fingers trembled slightly against the ceramic.

“I didn’t—” she began.

Her voice cracked, and she swallowed hard.

“I didn’t know it would be that constant,” she finished quietly. “Even when nothing ‘happens.’”

I stepped closer, not touching her, but near enough that my presence could be borrowed if she needed it.

“That’s the point,” I said softly. “Most of it isn’t a catastrophe. It’s pressure.”

Charl’s eyes lifted to mine. They were bright again, but this time she looked present. Angry. Awake.

“And you all…” she whispered. “You just—”

She faltered, searching for language that hadn’t existed in her old life.

“You just… appeared,” she finished.

Talia’s mouth twitched into the faintest smile. “Yes.”

Charl stared at her. “How?”

Talia shrugged, as if it were obvious. “We watch.”

Charl’s brow furrowed. “For what?”

Talia’s tone remained practical. “For the shift in air. For the hand that lifts a little too casually. For the comment that’s small enough to be deniable.”

Charl’s jaw tightened.

“That’s…” she began, then stopped.

I supplied the word, because she needed it.

“Labour,” I said.

Charl swallowed, the reality landing like weight.

“And you do that,” she said, voice rough, “all the time.”

Talia’s eyes held hers. “Yes.”

Charl’s gaze flicked to me. “And you.”

“Yes,” I said.

Charl looked down at the tea again. Her hands were steadier now, the warmth doing its quiet work.

“I thought,” she admitted, voice low, “that if I stayed near you, I’d be… learning. Getting stronger.”

Talia didn’t interrupt.

Charl continued anyway, as if the truth were pulling itself out.

“But what I learned,” she said, “is that no one gets stronger alone. They just get… harder. And quieter.”

The sentence hung there, unpretty and accurate.

I felt something twist in my chest—pride and grief braided together.

Charl lifted her eyes again, and in them was something I hadn’t seen when she was Charlie.

Gratitude, yes.

But also a kind of dawning loyalty that wasn’t worship.

Belonging.

“I didn’t understand,” she said. “Not until just now.”

Talia nodded once. “You didn’t need to understand. You needed to survive the hour.”

Charl blinked hard. “I almost didn’t.”

“You did,” I said.

Charl’s mouth tightened. “Only because you— because you all—”

“Yes,” I agreed.

A long beat.

Then Charl let out a breath that sounded almost like a laugh, except it wasn’t amused.

“I would’ve thought,” she murmured, “that the hardest part would be the costume.”

I watched her carefully.

“And it’s not,” I said.

Charl shook her head. “No.”

She glanced up at me, and her voice went quiet and fierce.

“It’s the constant measuring,” she said. “The constant… *deciding what’s safe.*”

I nodded. “Welcome.”

Charl’s throat moved.

“And you still live like this,” she said, voice cracking with something like outrage on our behalf. “Even when you’re not in costume.”

“Yes,” I said.

Talia’s voice softened, just slightly. “We live like this, and we still laugh, and we still work, and we still love people. Don’t turn it into a tragedy, Sharl. Turn it into knowledge.”

Charl’s eyes shone. She nodded once, slow.

“I want to stay,” she said quietly.

Talia didn’t react. She didn’t reward it. She didn’t punish it.

She just asked the only question that mattered.

“Today?” she said.

Charl blinked. Then, with a steadiness that wasn’t bravado, she answered:

“Today,” she said.

I felt the hinge in me swing again—care tightening into something more deliberate.

Not romance.

Not possession.

Responsibility.

I lifted my chin slightly, the smallest signal.

“Alright,” I said.

Not sweetly.

Not as a reward.

As a decision.

Charl took another sip of tea. Her hands were steady now.

And for the first time, I saw it in her face—the delayed understanding, the one you only get after you’ve needed something and discovered it was already there:

The system hadn’t saved her.

The women had.

And that—more than any illusion of reform—was the only realistic kind of hope.










It *can* be an endpoint — and it’s a strong one — because it lands your thesis in a way that feels earned:

* Charl **enters** the lived experience (not as titillation, not as “lesson cosplay,” but as injury + insight).
* She learns the harsh truth: the world doesn’t change on cue.
* And she learns the equally harsh counter-truth: **women do not survive alone; women survive together** — through quiet protocols, attention, and solidarity that looks “small” until you need it.

That’s profound. It’s instructive. And it isn’t a “happy ending” in the cheap sense.

### The only thing an ending needs: closure of the central question

Your central question, as you’ve framed it, is essentially:

> Can a male-coded innocence truly understand women’s lived experience without fetishising it — and what does that understanding cost?

This scene answers it. The cost is not the costume. It’s the constant vigilance. The answer is not “women are tougher,” it’s “women are trained and supported in ways men rarely notice.” Charl *gets it*, and the reader gets it with her.

So yes: as a novella-length endpoint, it works.

---

## But: two small “buttons” that would make it feel finished-finished

You don’t need new plot. Just two short closing beats (a paragraph each) to seal the lid.

### Button 1: A vow with limits (agency preserved)

Charl makes a quiet vow that isn’t devotion, isn’t romance, and isn’t self-sacrifice.

Something like:

* “I’ll stay today,” plus
* “and I’ll keep my exit,” plus
* “and I’ll tell the truth when I’m able.”

It reminds the reader: this isn’t erasure-as-solution; it’s chosen participation with boundaries.

### Button 2: Celeste’s private reckoning (her humanity)

A final Celeste line (internal) that acknowledges:

* her feelings are “inconvenient,”
* her power has a cost,
* and she’ll keep choosing ethics over appetite.

Not sentimental, just a clean, cold promise to herself.

Those two buttons turn a powerful scene into a **complete ending**.

## Optional: what kind of ending is this?

It’s a *threshold ending* — they step into the next life rather than resolve everything. That’s very Sciamma-adjacent. The satisfaction comes from transformation, not from wrapping bows.

If you *want* it to feel slightly more optimistic without betraying realism, you can hint at one thing:

* Charl’s future truth-telling will be in *craft* (writing, music, testimony), not in a grand confrontation. That keeps it believable and avoids “and then he fixed patriarchy.”

Later—after the donors had drifted back into their glittering conversations and the manor reassembled its pleasant face—I watched Sharl walk toward Wardrobe with the tea mug still between her palms, as if warmth were something you could carry like proof.

She didn’t look transformed. She looked *educated.*

Not softened. Not saved. Just newly fluent in a language women are forced to learn early: the way a room can turn on you without raising its voice.

For a moment, I felt the old temptation—to make this mean something neat. To turn it into a narrative where the world improves because we noticed it.

But the manor kept breathing. Men kept smiling. Entitlement kept its casual, well-fed posture.

So I swallowed the fantasy.

This wasn’t a victory over anything external. It was a refusal—small, precise, relentless.

Not changing the world.

Just denying it the pleasure of taking us apart one at a time.
