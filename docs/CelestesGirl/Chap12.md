## The long arc: Charlie learns the invisible curriculum

He is given explicit rules by Celeste/Wardrobe:

* where to stand
* where to look
* what to say
* how to move
* how not to “perform”

These are *training wheels*—the explicit version of what girls absorb implicitly.

### Stage 2: Surveillance (internal)

Then he begins doing it without being told:

* he scans exits
* he clocks men’s tones
* he anticipates jokes
* he keeps his expression neutral
* he learns not to signal fear because fear invites pursuit

This is where the grief starts: he realises the rules are endless.

### Stage 3: Double bind (the female reality)

He encounters the paradox:

* if he’s friendly, he’s “inviting it”
* if he’s cold, he’s “bitchy”
* if he’s quiet, he’s “suspicious”
* if he’s assertive, he’s “difficult”

That’s the patriarchy’s trick: no correct answer, only punishments.

He starts noticing women doing it automatically:

* Talia shifts behind a screen without thinking
* Mara positions herself with sight lines
* donor women manage men’s egos like landmines
* someone laughs at a joke that wasn’t funny because it was easier than conflict

Charlie realises: *this isn’t personality; it’s survival engineering.*

### Stage 5: Voice (eventual portrayal)

Much later, when he speaks about it publicly, it shouldn’t sound like a manifesto. It should sound like an exhausted truth:

> “The work isn’t the corset.

## Celeste — Promotion

They called it an invitation, which is how management liked to disguise decisions.

A note arrived on Wardrobe’s hook board just after lunch—cream paper, black ink, the kind of stationery that exists to make you behave.

**MISS SHELLEY — Please attend the Office at 2:15.**

No reason given. Reasons are for people who get to refuse.

Mara glanced at it over my shoulder and made a small sound. Not quite a laugh.

“You’ve been summoned,” she said.

“I can read,” I replied, unhooking the note.

Mara’s eyes narrowed. “They’re going to move you.”

“Why?” I asked, though I already knew.

Mara tilted her head toward the door, where the Faire’s public noise pressed against the walls like weather. “Because you don’t smile when men are stupid.”

I tucked the note into my clipboard. “That’s a skill.”

Mara’s mouth twitched. “They’ll call it ‘presence.’”

Presence. Gravitas. Poise. The words they use when they want a woman to be sharp on a leash.

Charlie was at the mending table, stitching quietly. He didn’t look up at the note. He’d learned not to chase information he hadn’t been asked to carry.

That restraint—again—was the whole problem.

I said, without turning my head, “Charlie.”

“Yes,” he answered immediately.

“Watch the room,” I said. “If anything breaks, log it. Don’t fix it unless Mara tells you to.”

Mara snorted. “As if I’d tell him to do anything.”

I didn’t react. I simply added, “If Dean comes in here, he stays at the door.”

Charlie nodded. “Yes.”

I walked out with my clipboard and my neutral face, leaving Wardrobe in that small pocket of sanity it had become.

Outside, the Faire was running on its usual mixture of illusion and compromise. Donors wandered. Patrons took photos. Staff performed cheerfulness like it was part of their costume.

I moved through it without giving anything away.

Management’s office sat in the old stone cottage at the edge of the grounds—the building that pretended it had always been there. Inside, the air smelled of paper and old coffee and decisions made too far from the people who had to carry them out.

A woman at the front desk—Janelle, I remembered—looked up and gave me a smile that belonged to her job.

“Miss Shelley. They’re ready for you.”

“They’re always ready,” I said, and stepped through.

Mr. Hawthorne was seated behind the desk like a portrait of administration: grey hair, careful posture, a suit that had never been asked to lift anything heavier than a pen. Beside him sat Mrs. Pryce, who did “guest relations,” which meant she managed rich people’s feelings.

They both smiled when I entered.

That was how I knew something was wrong.

“Celeste,” Mr. Hawthorne said warmly. “Thank you for coming.”

“I was summoned,” I replied. “It would’ve been strange not to.”

Mrs. Pryce gave a little laugh like I was charming. “Still sharp. That’s what we love about you.”

The word *love* had never meant less.

Mr. Hawthorne gestured to the chair opposite. “Please. Sit.”

I sat, clipboard on my lap, back straight. I didn’t cross my ankles; I didn’t want to look like I was waiting for permission.

Mr. Hawthorne folded his hands. “We’ve been very impressed with your performance in Wardrobe.”

“Thank you,” I said.

Mrs. Pryce leaned forward slightly, eyes bright. “Not just performance. Leadership. Presence. You’ve created… a standard.”

I kept my expression neutral. Compliments were often just the first layer of a trap.

Mr. Hawthorne nodded, as if confirming a fact. “Precisely. And it’s become clear that you’re… underutilised.”

There it was.

I didn’t react. “Wardrobe is critical.”

“Yes,” he said quickly, “of course. Absolutely. But it’s not—”

“Visible,” I finished for him.

Mrs. Pryce smiled, relieved at not having to say the ugly part out loud. “Public-facing roles suit you, Celeste. You have… elegance. Authority. And you know how to handle people.”

I thought of the men on the thoroughfare and my voice saying *This isn’t a stage for you.*

That, apparently, was what they wanted to sell.

Mr. Hawthorne tapped a folder on his desk. “We’d like to offer you a new role.”

I waited.

He spoke as though unveiling a gift.

“Lady of the Manor.”

For a fraction of a second, I pictured it: the manor house on the hill, the curated rooms, the receiving line of donors and committee members, the guided tours and staged conversations that made wealthy people feel connected to history without ever being inconvenienced by it.

It wasn’t a promotion. It was an extraction.

Mrs. Pryce’s eyes shone. “You’d be the face of the grounds. The hostess. The point of polish. It’s perfect for you.”

I let my gaze rest on Mr. Hawthorne. “Who runs Wardrobe?”

He hesitated. “We have capable staff.”

“That’s not an answer,” I said.

He cleared his throat. “Mara has experience.”

Mara’s bitterness had held Wardrobe together long before I arrived. She could do it. She would hate it. Which would make her good at it.

“And my support?” I asked, still calm.

Mrs. Pryce tilted her head. “Support?”

“As Lady of the Manor,” I said, “I will require an aide.”

Mr. Hawthorne waved a hand. “Of course. A lady’s maid will be assigned. We have several—”

“No,” I said.

The word fell flat and final.

Mr. Hawthorne blinked. “Excuse me?”

Mrs. Pryce’s smile faltered. “Celeste—”

I kept my voice level. “You can’t move me into a role that involves private spaces, donor intimacy, and public optics, and then place a stranger at my elbow.”

Mr. Hawthorne’s mouth tightened. “It’s standard.”

“Standard for who?” I asked. “For men who think any female labour is interchangeable?”

Mrs. Pryce made a small flinch, offended not by the truth but by the fact I’d said it aloud.

Mr. Hawthorne leaned forward, tone cooling. “We are trying to recognise your talent. This is an opportunity.”

“I understand,” I said. “That’s why I’m being careful.”

Mrs. Pryce’s eyes narrowed. “You don’t trust our staff?”

“I don’t trust unknown variables in private rooms,” I replied. “And neither do donors, if we’re being honest.”

Mr. Hawthorne’s gaze sharpened. “Donors?”

I let my voice soften—not into weakness, but into persuasion. “You want me donor-facing. You want polish. You want controlled atmosphere.”

I tapped my clipboard once. “Controlled atmosphere requires continuity.”

Mrs. Pryce recovered her smile, sharper now. “Celeste, darling. This is a historic environment. People expect authenticity.”

“Then you should know the historic environment was built on trusted household structures,” I replied. “Ladies did not accept random staff drifting in and out of private space. They had their own people. Their own standards.”

Mr. Hawthorne’s fingers drummed the folder. “Are you demanding to choose your maid?”

“I’m insisting on someone I already know,” I said.

Mrs. Pryce leaned back, lips pursed. “And who is this person?”

I held the beat deliberately. Let them feel the pause. Let them understand that whatever answer came next would cost me something.

Then I said, evenly, “Charlie.”

Mr. Hawthorne blinked as if I’d said *horse*.

“Charlie… Rossignol?” he asked.

“Yes.”

Mrs. Pryce’s expression tightened. “Celeste, he’s—”

“A Wardrobe worker,” I said.

“He’s a boy,” she corrected, as if I’d missed an obvious detail.

“He’s staff,” I replied.

Mr. Hawthorne’s face hardened into the shape of policy. “That’s not appropriate.”

There it was again: the word men use when they want women to rearrange themselves to suit perception.

I didn’t raise my voice.

“This isn’t about appropriateness,” I said. “It’s about function.”

Mrs. Pryce’s eyes flashed. “It’s about reputation.”

Finally. The truth.

I nodded once. “Yes. Mine. Which is why I’m managing it.”

Mr. Hawthorne leaned back. “We can’t have the Lady of the Manor shadowed by a young male attendant. Donors will talk.”

“And if donors talk,” I said, “it will be because you created a scenario where they could.”

Mrs. Pryce’s voice turned silk-sharp. “We’re trying to prevent scandal.”

“You’re trying to prevent discomfort,” I corrected. “Scandal requires intent. This requires structure.”

Mr. Hawthorne rubbed his temple as if I were a headache he hadn’t budgeted for. “Celeste. This is non-negotiable.”

I smiled slightly—my polite face, and none of my answer.

“You’re right,” I said. “The role isn’t negotiable. I accept it.”

Mrs. Pryce’s smile returned, triumphant.

Then I added, calmly, “But I won’t accept an assigned maid. I will train my own.”

The room cooled.

Mr. Hawthorne stared. “Train your own… what?”

“My own aide,” I said. “My own support. If you want authenticity, you want a household. A household has trusted people.”

Mrs. Pryce’s lips tightened. “Celeste, you’re being difficult.”

“No,” I said. “I’m being accurate.”

Mr. Hawthorne said, “You can’t bring a man into women’s spaces.”

I tilted my head. “Then don’t give me a man.”

Silence.

Mrs. Pryce’s eyes narrowed in understanding. “What are you suggesting?”

I kept my tone clinical. “Wardrobe has women. Wardrobe has performers. Wardrobe has staff who already move in those spaces. If you insist on a lady’s maid, then I will take one from Wardrobe.”

Mr. Hawthorne exhaled slowly. “And Charlie?”

I didn’t flinch. “Charlie remains attached to Wardrobe. He remains my runner. He remains my continuity on the floor.”

Mrs. Pryce stared. “You’re trying to have both.”

“Yes,” I replied.

Mr. Hawthorne’s mouth tightened. “That’s not how promotions work.”

“This isn’t a promotion,” I said. “It’s reassignment. You are moving an asset. I’m preventing operational failure.”

Mrs. Pryce’s eyes flashed again. “Celeste, you can’t possibly believe you need *him*.”

There it was: the implied insult. The idea that relying on a held-back boy from Wardrobe would diminish me.

I felt the old anger rise.

I kept it under control.

“I don’t need him,” I said. “I need the standard. He carries it.”

Mr. Hawthorne stared at me for a long beat. Then he turned his gaze toward Mrs. Pryce, as if checking whether she wanted to win this fight today or defer it.

Mrs. Pryce pressed her lips together, calculating.

Finally, Mr. Hawthorne said, “You may select a female aide from Wardrobe.”

I nodded. “Good.”

Mrs. Pryce added, quickly, “But Charlie does not enter the manor in staff capacity. He stays on the floor.”

I held her gaze.

“Understood,” I said.

It was half a concession.

It was also the opening I needed.

Because if Charlie stayed on the floor, and the floor required continuity, and the Lady of the Manor required controlled movement through public spaces—

There were ways.

I stood. “When does the role begin?”

Mr. Hawthorne’s smile returned, strained. “Immediately. We’ll announce it to senior staff by close of day. Donor dinner this weekend.”

Mrs. Pryce brightened. “You’ll be wonderful, darling. Truly.”

I gathered my clipboard. “I am always wonderful,” I said, and left before she could decide whether it was a joke.

Out in the corridor, I exhaled once—quiet and controlled. Not relief. Not fear.

Calculation.

The trap was real: they wanted me visible and isolated, polished and manageable, sharp but contained.

Fine.

I could work with that.

Back in Wardrobe, the air smelled of cloth and thread and the small comforts of competence.

Mara looked up as I entered. “Well?”

“They’ve moved me,” I said.

Mara’s eyes flicked to my face. “To where?”

“Lady of the Manor.”

Mara’s mouth curled. “Oh. They want you as a decorative weapon.”

“Yes,” I agreed.

Charlie was stitching. He didn’t look up, but I saw his needle slow by a fraction. He’d heard.

I set my clipboard down deliberately, then said, in a tone meant to keep the room calm, “Wardrobe will be reorganised.”

Mara’s eyebrows rose. “Meaning?”

“Meaning you’ll run it,” I said.

Mara’s eyes narrowed. “Do I get a say?”

“You do,” I replied. “Say yes.”

Mara made a sound that might have been laughter if she’d enjoyed anything. “You’re unbelievable.”

“I’m efficient,” I corrected.

Then I turned to Charlie.

He looked up now. His face was careful, controlled—trying not to ask a question that could reveal how much he feared the answer.

I didn’t soften.

I didn’t dramatise.

I said, simply, “Come with me.”

Charlie blinked. “Where?”

“To the back room,” I said.

His mouth tightened. “Why?”

Because the next part mattered.

Because this was where function and perception would collide.

Because I was not losing my continuity without a fight.

“Because,” I said, voice level, “we need to talk about what people will think they’re allowed to say about me.”

Charlie’s throat bobbed.

He nodded once.

And followed.

## Scene Twelve — “The Ledger” (Celeste POV, full prose draft)

Mara didn’t *announce* the new system.

She simply put it on the table one morning as if it had always existed.

A ledger. Thick. Hard cover. The kind you could drop and have it land with authority.

It sat between the pincushion and the shears, beside the tin of chalk. On the first page, in Mara’s angular hand, were headings and lines, already ruled.

**GARMENT:**
**DATE ISSUED:**
**WEAR-TESTER:**
**NOTES (MOVEMENT / STRESS):**
**FAILURE POINTS:**
**FIX APPLIED:**
**RE-TEST:**
**SIGNED (MARA):**
**SIGNED (CELESTE):**

I ran my finger down the columns and felt, absurdly, the relief of it. The whole room would be calmer now. Fewer frantic, vague sentences. Fewer people saying *it just tore* as though fabric did things for fun.

Mara watched my face without asking what I thought.

“What’s the rule?” I said.

She tilted her head toward the far curtain rail—newly fixed, properly anchored now, no longer sagging like an apology.

“Rule is,” Mara said, “we stop pretending guesses are data.”

And then she looked past me.

Charlie came in quietly, as always, like he’d learned how to make his presence small without making himself invisible. A bundle of twill under one arm, a roll of paper under the other, hair still damp at the edges as if he’d left the house in a hurry.

He saw the ledger and stopped.

Not because he disliked it. Because he understood what it meant.

Mara slid it toward him with two fingers, like a judge pushing forward an exhibit.

“Write,” she said.

Charlie set his bundle down with careful hands. He didn’t touch the book immediately—he glanced at the headings first, eyes moving fast, absorbing structure like it was a language he was fluent in.

“You want me to log the failures?” he asked.

“I want you to log the truth,” Mara said. “And I want your name on the page when you’re satisfied it’s repeatable.”

His throat moved once. A swallow, not panic, but the faint tension he got when something was about to be formal. When he’d be held to the work—*and* allowed to own it.

He picked up the pen.

“Start with the stays,” Mara said. “The working set. The one you ‘delivered last time.’”

I watched him write. His handwriting wasn’t pretty, but it was exact. Like stitching that might not be decorative, but it would hold through a season and then another.

**GARMENT:** Working stays, linen canvas, whalebone substitute (reed), size test 2
**WEAR-TESTER:** C. Rossignol
**MOVEMENT / STRESS:** bending, reaching overhead, lifting tray, stair ascent / descent
**FAILURE POINTS:** seam stress at left side-back, binding roll at top edge, grommet pull at waist tie point

Mara leaned over his shoulder, close enough to read without hovering like a teacher.

“How did it feel?” she asked.

Charlie paused with the pen just above the paper.

Not *how did it feel*, in the way people said it when they wanted a story. Mara meant the thing itself: pressure, pinch, drag, creep.

He answered like he was reading off a diagram.

“Too much load goes to the left tie point,” he said. “That’s why the grommet starts to oval. If you redistribute tension—either move the tie or add a secondary anchor—then the binding won’t try to roll, because it won’t be fighting the torque.”

Mara’s mouth did a small, satisfied curve that wasn’t a smile so much as a verdict.

“And?” she said.

“And the seam at side-back is… it’s underdesigned for repeated bend,” Charlie added. He tapped the page lightly. “Not wrong for… standing. But if you want a working garment, you need the seam to expect work.”

He didn’t say *women do work.* He didn’t say *I understand women’s bodies.* He didn’t say any of the embarrassing sentences people tried to force into the air when gender was nearby.

He just described load paths.

Mara straightened.

“Celeste,” she said, without looking at me, “this is why we don’t trial on staff first.”

“I know,” I said. My voice came out lighter than I intended, because it was almost funny now—how obvious it was in hindsight. “We’d been doing it backwards.”

She finally looked at me then, eyes sharp.

“We weren’t doing it. We were letting it happen.”

That was Mara. She could turn a whole week of chaos into a single sentence and make you feel embarrassed you’d ever accepted it.

Charlie kept writing, pen scratching.

**FIX APPLIED:** move tie point 12mm; add secondary anchor tape; reinforce side-back with felled seam + narrow twill tape; adjust binding cut on bias

He stopped again. His fingers tightened slightly around the pen, and I recognised the moment: the place where competence collided with the other thing—the thing he didn’t speak about.

He didn’t want to be dramatic. That was his discipline. But the discipline had seams, too.

Mara waited. She didn’t rush him. She didn’t soften.

Finally, he said, very carefully, “We’re still doing… the fitting under the curtain, yeah?”

Mara’s gaze didn’t flicker.

“Yes.”

“And the door stays locked,” he added, quickly, as if he regretted asking at all.

“Yes,” Mara said again. “And the log stays factual. No one discusses it like it’s entertainment.”

Charlie nodded once.

He wrote **RE-TEST** and then stopped, as if the word itself asked for a calendar.

I watched him, and I felt something in my chest shift into place. Not pity. Not romance. Something more utilitarian and more dangerous: the sense of seeing an interface, and realising it could scale.

If we could keep him safe from being turned into a spectacle, if we could keep him inside the logic of the work, then the whole atelier could expand without losing its centre.

Lauren arrived not long after. She didn’t knock timidly; she rapped once and came in. Practical. No theatrics. She carried a box of notions and a roll of interfacing like she was delivering supplies to a site office.

Her eyes landed on the ledger immediately.

“Ah,” she said. “You’ve made it official.”

Mara didn’t bother with pleasantries.

“We’ve made it measurable,” she corrected.

Lauren looked at Charlie, then at the curtain rail, then back to Mara, reading the room the way an adult reads a room—fast, with the parts that mattered.

“And he’s signing off?” she asked.

“He is,” Mara said.

Lauren’s gaze sharpened with something that might have been approval, might have been alarm, might have been both.

Charlie, still seated, still holding the pen, looked up at Lauren as if he expected a reprimand. Or a warning. Or the old script where adults spoke *about* him.

Lauren surprised him. She put the box down, opened it, and slid a handful of reinforced grommets onto the table.

“Use those,” she said, as if it was obvious. “If you’re doing working garments, stop pretending decorative hardware can take load.”

Charlie stared at the grommets. Then—so small I almost missed it—his shoulders loosened.

Not because his mother had rescued him.

Because she’d spoken the language of the work.

Mara watched the exchange like she was watching two subcontractors finally agree on a specification.

“Good,” she said. “We’re past guessing.”

Lauren glanced at me. A quick look—adult to young adult. Not unkind. Just exact.

“And you,” she said, “keep it professional.”

I held her gaze.

“I am,” I said. “That’s the point.”

She nodded once. Like she’d ticked a box.

Charlie put his head down again and wrote.

**SIGNED (C. ROSSIGNOL)**

Mara took the pen from him when he was done. She signed her name with a thick, decisive stroke.

Then she pushed the ledger toward me.

My turn.

I signed, and the ink looked oddly serious on the page, as if the act itself had weight.

And just like that, the atelier took one step away from being a clever little pocket of women doing miracles in private, and one step toward being a system that could withstand daylight.

## Why stays are the perfect “try, try again” garment (in-story logic)

Even with skill, stays are notorious for re-dos because:

1. **Fit is geometry + body variability.** Tiny errors cascade: angle of the tabs, placement of the waistline, the “spring” over the hips, how the front sits when you bend.
2. **Boning changes behaviour.** The same pattern with different boning density/stiffness behaves differently. One channel too close, one too wide, one bone too short, and the garment “kinks” or bruises.
3. **Tension is non-intuitive.** Where the stress goes under movement is hard to predict until someone actually *works* in it: reaching, bending, lifting, stairs.
4. **Historical accuracy fights modern expectations.** People expect comfort like modern undergarments; stays are engineered support. Mara won’t let “modern comfort myths” distort the silhouette.
5. **Eyelets / lacing failures are common.** Placement, reinforcement, and hardware matter; otherwise they oval out, tear, or shift.
6. **Binding and top edge behaviour is fussy.** Rolling, gaping, cutting, or collapsing at the neckline happens even when the seams are “correct.”

So a first-time success is rare, especially in *working* stays (not display/costume stays). That’s very believable, and it’s a goldmine for showing competence.

### Scene Twelve-and-a-Half — **“First Attempt”** (Celeste POV, full prose)

Mara didn’t bring the stays out with ceremony.

She laid the materials on the cutting table the way a surgeon lays out instruments: in plain sight, deliberately spaced, nothing decorative about the order. Canvas folded into a clean rectangle. A length of linen tape, already pressed. A small bundle of reed boning tied with string. A tin of grommets that looked comically minor for the amount of authority they were about to carry.

Charlie arrived a minute late and tried to apologise with his body—small shoulders, quick glance, a quietness that wanted to pay for the inconvenience in advance.

Mara didn’t accept payment in the form of shrinking.

“Don’t do that,” she said, without looking up from the table.

Charlie froze mid-breath. “Do… what?”

“Arrive like you’re already wrong.” Mara lifted the canvas and shook it once. The sound was flat and honest. “You’re here. That’s the point. Now, pay attention.”

He stepped closer, careful not to crowd. I watched his gaze take inventory without touching. He was good at that: reading surfaces, anticipating what mattered, trying to solve problems before anyone gave him the chance.

Mara didn’t reward pre-emptive heroics.

She nodded toward the ledger—still on the table, still heavy with its quiet insistence. “Open it.”

Charlie did, flipping to a clean page as if the paper might bruise. His pen hovered, waiting for permission.

“Title,” Mara said.

He wrote: **STAYS — ATTEMPT 1**. Underneath, he added the date without being asked.

Mara approved it only by continuing.

“These aren’t costume stays,” she said, tapping the canvas with two fingers. “They’re working stays. People keep confusing the two. Costume stays hold a silhouette for a photograph. Working stays hold a person for a day.”

Charlie’s pen moved, quick, neat enough to read.

I leaned on the table’s edge and watched his hand. When he wrote, he looked calmer. It was as if structure allowed him to be present without having to perform.

Mara laid out the pattern pieces. Nothing flamboyant—clean shapes that looked simple until you imagined them curved, tightened, forced to behave over bones and breath and movement.

“Your first attempt will fail,” Mara said. She said it the way she might say *it will rain on Thursday.* Not cruel. Not kind. Just factual.

Charlie blinked. “Is that… normal?”

Mara’s eyes flicked to him. “If someone tells you their first stays were perfect, they’re either lying or they don’t move in them.”

He swallowed, and for a moment I saw that old instinct—the urge to vanish so nobody could watch him be imperfect.

Mara caught it immediately.

“Charlie.” She said his name like a hand on a shoulder—firm pressure, not comfort. “The garment fails. You document it. We fix it. That’s the work.”

He nodded once. A small, stiff motion. But it was a yes.

Mara slid the chalk toward him. “Mark your seam allowances. And don’t be stingy. The first mock-up gets room to tell the truth.”

Charlie’s fingers closed around the chalk, and he began.

The workshop was quiet in the good way—scissors snipping, chalk whispering, the soft drag of canvas against the grain of the table. Outside, somewhere beyond the walls, the faire was waking: faint voices, the clink of something metal being unloaded, a distant laugh that didn’t belong to anyone in our room.

In here, it was trade.

Mara moved around him, watching without hovering. Every now and then she corrected a hand position with two taps of her knuckles against the table. Once, she stopped him entirely.

“No. Your stitch length is too eager.”

Charlie looked up, confused.

“You’re trying to impress the seam,” Mara said. “The seam doesn’t care about your feelings. It cares whether it holds.”

His ears coloured. He adjusted, shortened his stitch, slowed down. I filed the sentence away because it was Mara in a nutshell—brutally useful.

When the pieces were cut and aligned, Mara indicated the curtain rail with a tilt of her head. The newly fixed track sat straight and solid above the fitting nook, the curtain hanging like a clean boundary rather than a flimsy suggestion.

“Same conditions,” she said.

Charlie’s eyes flicked there instantly—relief and tension arriving together.

“Yes,” he said. “Same conditions.”

Mara’s gaze held him. “Say it properly.”

Charlie hesitated, then forced the words out as if they were heavier than any bolt of fabric. “Door locked. Curtain closed. Nobody enters. Nobody talks about it.”

“Good,” Mara said. “That includes you, Celeste.”

“I wasn’t planning on narrating it,” I replied.

Mara gave me a look that suggested she wasn’t interested in my tone. “I’m not accusing you. I’m setting standards.”

I let my mouth shut. She was right, and she liked people who could accept that without turning it into a drama.

Charlie disappeared behind the curtain with the mock-up folded over his arm. The lock clicked a moment later—sharp, decisive.

The room waited in the way a room waits when a task has moved into a private phase. Not awkward. Just… contained.

Mara didn’t fill the time with chatter. She began sorting the reed lengths by stiffness, bending each one with her fingers, assessing it the way you assess timber.

“Do you think he’ll manage?” I asked quietly.

Mara didn’t look up. “He’ll manage. He’ll also want to be perfect. That’s the part that will trip him.”

A faint rustle came from behind the curtain, then the sound of a breath let out slowly—controlled, not panicked.

Mara added, almost as an afterthought, “And if anyone in this place ever makes him into a story, I’ll have them out on their ear.”

It wasn’t a threat. It was policy.

I felt my shoulders loosen a fraction. It was strange, the way a boundary could calm a whole room.

Charlie emerged after a few minutes, mock-up on, laced loosely enough that it didn’t demand a performance. Over his clothes, still clearly a work garment rather than a transformation. Mara had been exact about that, too: *no theatre.*

Charlie stood stiffly at first, chin slightly raised as though he were bracing for judgement.

Mara didn’t give him judgement. She gave him tasks.

“Arms up,” she said.

Charlie lifted his arms.

The top edge of the mock-up shifted, the canvas flattening in some places, fighting in others. I watched the material as if it were a diagram drawn in real time. Near the left side-back, the seam line pulled, the stress gathering like a storm behind a ridge.

Mara’s eyes sharpened.

“Now reach forward. Like you’re taking something off a high shelf.”

Charlie did.

The left tie point dragged. You could *see* the load path trying to relocate, looking for the easiest way out. The lacing tugged, the fabric creased into a diagonal that wasn’t elegant—it was warning.

Mara stepped closer, not touching him, but close enough to see the truth of it.

“Do you feel that?” she asked.

Charlie nodded, jaw tight. “Yes.”

“Don’t give me ‘yes’,” Mara snapped, and Charlie startled. “I don’t care about polite. Tell me **where.**”

Charlie blinked, then forced his mind into the technical. “At the left waist tie. It’s pulling. It’s… it’s taking too much.”

“Good,” Mara said immediately, as if the moment he described the reality he’d stepped back into safety. “Now bend. Like you’re lifting a tray.”

Charlie bent carefully, and the top edge tried to roll.

It wasn’t dramatic. It was worse than dramatic—subtle. The kind of failure a costume could hide for ten minutes and then betray you on day one of actual work.

I saw Charlie’s expression shift—an involuntary flicker of disappointment.

Then, almost as quickly: relief.

As if part of him had been afraid the garment would behave “fine,” and he’d be expected to pretend that meant it *was* fine.

Mara saw that too.

“Write,” she said. “Before your feelings invent a different story.”

Charlie moved to the table and wrote with his pen still slightly shaking.

**MOVEMENT / STRESS:** overhead reach, forward reach, bend/lift simulation
**FAILURE POINTS:** left waist tie load concentration; top edge roll; diagonal crease from left waist toward side-back; seam stress side-back left

Mara watched him write, then leaned in.

“Now,” she said. “Tell me what you think it means.”

Charlie opened his mouth, then shut it. He looked down at the entry as if the paper might supply the answer if he stared hard enough.

“I think the tie point is wrong,” he said finally. “Or not… supported enough. It’s acting like an anchor for everything.”

Mara nodded. “Load path. Good. And the roll?”

Charlie frowned. “The top edge is fighting torque. It’s… it’s being dragged by the lacing, isn’t it? If the tension is uneven, the edge wants to curl because it’s trying to accommodate the pull.”

Mara’s eyes flicked to me, not for approval—more like *see? this is why we trained him.* Then she looked back to Charlie.

“And the side-back seam?”

Charlie’s pen tapped the paper once. “It’s underbuilt. If it’s going to be working stays, that seam needs to expect repeated bend. It needs reinforcement, or a different seam finish. The fabric’s telling us where it wants more structure.”

Mara straightened. “Good.”

Charlie looked up, uncertain, almost waiting for the other shoe.

Mara didn’t drop one. She simply moved on.

“Attempt one has served its purpose,” she said. “Now we do attempt two.”

Charlie’s shoulders sank with the weight of it, and then he lifted them again, deliberately, as if he were choosing not to collapse.

Mara slid a narrow strip of twill tape toward him. “This goes here.” She pointed to the area he’d described. “Secondary anchor. Spread the load. And we move the tie point.”

“How much?” Charlie asked.

Mara shrugged. “Twelve millimetres to start. It’s not magic. It’s iteration.”

Charlie wrote: **FIX APPLIED (PROPOSED): move tie point 12mm; add secondary anchor tape; reinforce side-back seam; adjust top edge binding cut**

Mara watched him write the word *proposed.*

“Good,” she said. “That word keeps you honest.”

Charlie paused, then spoke softly, as if he were stepping onto thin ice. “Mara?”

“Yes,” she said, brisk.

“If—” He stopped, then tried again, less polite and more clear. “If we’re going to do this properly… I need the same privacy each time.”

Mara’s eyes didn’t soften. They sharpened.

“Of course you do,” she said. “And you’ll get it. But don’t ask like it’s a favour.”

Charlie swallowed. “Then how do I ask?”

Mara leaned in, close enough that the room seemed to narrow to just the two of them.

“You state conditions,” she said. “You don’t plead for permission. This is work. Work has conditions.”

Charlie nodded, the smallest hint of steadiness returning.

“State them,” Mara said.

Charlie drew a breath. “Door locked. Curtain closed. Nobody enters. Nobody talks about it. Log stays factual. Fitting times are scheduled. And—” He hesitated, then forced the last one out. “No surprises.”

Mara’s mouth tightened, not unkindly. “Good.”

She turned to me. “Write that as policy.”

I picked up the pen and wrote it under the ledger entry, in my own hand, because I understood the point: if the standard lived only in Mara’s mouth, it could still be treated as personality. If it lived on paper, it became infrastructure.

**POLICY:** Fittings for prototype testing are scheduled; privacy maintained; no unscheduled access; documentation is factual; no commentary.

Mara nodded once.

Then she did something small that might have looked like nothing to anyone else: she put her palm flat on the ledger page, pressing it down as if to set the ink into the paper.

“This,” she said, “is how we keep the place clean.”

Charlie looked at the page. Not at Mara. Not at me. At the page.

It was the first time I’d seen him look at authority with something like trust.

He let out a slow breath and—almost imperceptibly—his posture eased.

Mara clapped her hands once.

“Right,” she said. “Take it off. Don’t baby it. It’s a mock-up. It’s allowed to be ugly.”

Charlie nodded and disappeared behind the curtain again.

The lock clicked.

Mara began sorting the pattern pieces into a new pile. “Attempt two starts now,” she called, not unkindly, not asking whether he had energy, simply assuming he had professionalism.

I stood there with the ledger open and felt, again, that odd sensation—like watching something grow roots.

This wasn’t a story about a boy in women’s clothes. That story was cheap, and this place would not host it.

This was a story about a garment that demanded the truth.

And a room full of women—sharp, competent, unromantic—who knew how to turn truth into a system.

When Charlie came back out, mock-up folded under his arm, he didn’t look embarrassed.

He looked… focused.

He picked up the chalk without being told.

“Attempt two,” he said quietly, to himself as much as to anyone.

Mara’s eyes flicked up, and in them was a kind of satisfaction that didn’t need praise.

“Now,” she said, “you’re making stays.”

# Attempt Two — “The Block” (Celeste POV, full prose)
