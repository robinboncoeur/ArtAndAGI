## Celeste — Terms

The back room was where we kept the things that weren’t meant to be seen.

Not secret, exactly. Just… unromantic. Underlayers. Spare pins. The dull mechanics of historical illusion.

It smelled faintly of starch and lavender sachets and the honest metal tang of needles.

I shut the door behind Charlie and leaned my clipboard against the shelf. I didn’t sit. Sitting turns conversations into confessions.

Charlie stood near the centre of the room as if he’d been placed there, hands at his sides, posture careful. He looked at the closed door, then at me.

His voice came out steady, but his throat moved before the first word, betraying him.

“They moved you,” he said.

“Yes.”

He nodded once, as if he’d already done the arithmetic. “Lady of the Manor.”

“Yes.”

His mouth tightened. “They’ll put you in front of donors.”

“Yes.”

“And they’ll—” He paused, searching for the clean word. “They’ll watch you.”

I waited.

Charlie’s gaze flicked down to the floorboards, then up again. He wasn’t shy. He was… choosing accuracy.

“They won’t watch you to see if you do it well,” he said quietly. “They’ll watch you to see if they can catch you doing it wrong.”

It wasn’t melodrama. It was analysis.

The sentence landed in my chest with a cold, familiar recognition. He understood more than most people twice his age.

“Yes,” I said.

Charlie exhaled once, controlled. “And if there’s any… talk—”

“About me,” I supplied.

He flinched, not because I’d named it, but because I’d named it without embarrassment.

“Yes,” he said. “It’s not fair, but it won’t matter. It’ll stick.”

He swallowed.

“Celeste,” he added, voice low, “they’ll ruin you for sport.”

There it was: genuine concern, unperformed. Not obsequious. Not worshipful. Protective in the way women learn to be protective: by anticipating reputational harm and trying to prevent it before it exists.

I let a beat pass.

Then I asked, evenly, “Is that what you’re worried about?”

Charlie’s eyes held mine. For a moment he looked almost angry—at the situation, at the unfairness of it, at his own inability to stop it with simple logic.

“Yes,” he said. “Of course.”

“Only that?” I asked.

He blinked.

I watched him think. That was the tell with Charlie: he didn’t lie quickly. He tried to find a true sentence that didn’t cost too much.

“Also,” he admitted, quieter, “they’ll replace you.”

“They’ve tried,” I said.

“They will,” he insisted, and the insistence wasn’t rebellion—it was fear disguised as practicality. “They’ll give you someone else. A… maid. Someone who’ll be with you all day.”

“Yes,” I said again.

Charlie’s jaw tightened. His hands flexed once at his sides, like he wanted to do something useful with this helplessness.

“And then,” he said, very carefully, “where does that leave me?”

Ah.

There it was—the second truth, the one he’d been trying not to say out loud.

Not jealousy. Not entitlement.

Vulnerability.

His future had become tangled with mine. Not because he’d pursued it. Because I’d built structure around him, and now the structure was being rearranged by people who didn’t care whether he survived it.

I nodded once. “So you’re worried about my reputation and your position.”

He looked faintly ashamed, as if caring about his own survival made him selfish.

I didn’t let him wear that.

“That’s intelligent,” I said.

Charlie blinked, thrown.

“It’s the correct fear,” I added. “It means you understand how this place works.”

He swallowed. “I don’t want you… damaged,” he said, then corrected himself quickly, flustered by the intimacy of the word. “I mean—socially. Professionally.”

I let his correction sit there. Not because it amused me. Because it revealed something useful.

You can tell what someone thinks they’re allowed to want by what they apologise for wanting.

I stepped closer—not invading, simply reducing the distance enough to make the conversation unavoidable.

“Tell me,” I said, “what you think the story will be if you remain attached to me openly.”

Charlie’s face tightened. He didn’t look away.

“They’ll say you… keep a boy,” he said, and the word *keep* tasted sour in his mouth. “They’ll say you’re—”

“Improper,” I finished for him.

He nodded, jaw clenched.

“And what will they say about you?” I asked.

Charlie’s eyes flickered. “That I’m… pathetic,” he said, almost inaudible. “That I’m using you. That I’m—”

He stopped, unable to say the ugliest versions out loud.

I didn’t rescue him from it. I gave him the truth with the same clinical calm I’d used on the man at the thoroughfare.

“They’ll say you’re a joke,” I said. “And they’ll enjoy saying it.”

Charlie’s nostrils flared. A flash of anger. Good. Anger is integrity waking up.

“I don’t care what they say about me,” he muttered.

“That’s a lie,” I replied.

He froze.

I went on, still calm. “You care. Because you’re not stupid. Because you know that words become doors that close.”

Charlie’s shoulders dropped a fraction, the way someone’s do when their bluff is called and they’re relieved not to have to maintain it.

“Yes,” he admitted.

I nodded. “Good.”

Charlie stared at me, uncertain. “So what do we do?”

I picked up my clipboard again and flipped it open, as if the answer were already written. It wasn’t—but the gesture mattered. It framed the next part as policy, not drama.

“We control the optics,” I said.

Charlie’s eyes widened slightly. “How?”

I met his gaze. “You stop reading as a boy at my elbow.”

His throat moved. He went very still.

“No,” he said, immediately.

The speed of it surprised me. Not because he disobeyed—because he hadn’t disobeyed me yet, not in any meaningful way. It surprised me because the refusal wasn’t childish. It was protective.

“Celeste,” he said, voice tight, “if anyone thinks you’ve done that to me—”

“Listen,” I cut in, gently but sharp. “Stop making up a story where I’m a villain, Charlie. I don’t need rescuing from a rumour you invented.”

His face flushed. “I’m not—”

“You are,” I said, still calm. “You’re imagining the worst interpretation and trying to prevent it by sacrificing yourself pre-emptively.”

Charlie swallowed. His hands clenched, then unclenched.

“I’m trying to protect you,” he said, low.

“I know,” I replied. “But you don’t protect me by disappearing. You protect me by making the system work.”

He stared at me like he wanted to argue, but couldn’t find a point that wasn’t just fear.

I pressed.

“This is not about you becoming anything,” I said. “It is not identity. It is not performance. It is uniform.”

Charlie’s jaw worked. “Uniform,” he repeated, as if testing the word for lies.

“Yes,” I said. “Access control. Social camouflage.”

He looked down, then up again. “And what does that make me?”

I didn’t blink.

“My aide,” I said. “My attendant. My continuity.”

Charlie’s breath hitched.

“And if I say no?” he asked.

There it was—the first real boundary he’d offered me. Not defiant. Just… human.

I respected it by answering honestly.

“Then I accept a stranger,” I said. “And Wardrobe becomes a place you no longer belong, because the people above us will decide you were an indulgence.”

Charlie’s face went pale.

I watched him absorb it. Not because I enjoyed it. Because truth is the only ethical lever.

He looked at me with a kind of quiet panic now—adult panic, the fear of being made disposable.

“Celeste,” he said, “I can’t— I don’t know how to—”

“Then you’ll learn,” I replied.

His eyes flashed. “Learn *what*?”

I held his gaze. “The invisible curriculum.”

He stared, breathing shallow. “That’s not fair.”

“No,” I agreed. “It isn’t.”

We stood in the small, practical room, the shelves around us full of fabric and pins and the dull mechanics of roles.

Then Charlie said, almost pleading, “People will think you’re… doing it for fun.”

I stepped closer again, just one pace. Enough to make the next sentence land like a rule.

“People will think whatever is easiest for them,” I said. “Our job is to make it expensive for them to say it out loud.”

Charlie swallowed.

“How?” he whispered.

“By removing the spectacle,” I said. “By making it boring. By making it look like work.”

He stared at me. I could see his mind fighting—wanting the answer to be something else, something simpler, something that didn’t require his body as a solution.

But he also understood something deeper: the institution didn’t care about fairness. It cared about optics.

I turned slightly toward the shelf and took down a folded bundle.

It was not a dress. Not a fantasy. Not “pretty.”

It was a plain maid’s kit: shift, stays, petticoat, modest gown, apron, cap. Practical. Period-correct. Functional.

I placed it on the table like equipment.

Charlie’s eyes fixed on it. His face tightened.

“I won’t—” he began.

“You will,” I said, not cruelly. “Because you said you care about my future.”

His throat bobbed. “I do.”

“Then prove it,” I replied.

Charlie closed his eyes for a second, like someone bracing for cold water.

When he opened them, he looked at the kit again—less like an object of shame and more like a problem to be solved.

He spoke quietly. “If I do this… it’s not because I want it.”

“I know,” I said.

“And you’re not—” He swallowed. “You’re not making me… into something.”

“I’m not,” I said, flat. “I’m putting you in a uniform that makes men leave me alone.”

Charlie flinched, because the truth was sharp.

Then he nodded once—small, unwilling, but real.

“Alright,” he said. “Tell me what to do.”

Good.

That sentence wasn’t obedience. It was consent under constraint. Different thing. Important thing.

I kept my voice clinical, as promised—no softness, no teasing, no spectacle.

“First,” I said, “we remove your shirt and trousers. Not because this is intimate. Because underlayers can’t sit correctly over seams and buckles.”

Charlie’s ears went red. “Celeste—”

“Charlie,” I said, and the use of his name steadied him. “This room is Wardrobe. Not a story. You will not make this dramatic.”

He swallowed and nodded.

“Second,” I continued, “the shift goes on first. It’s linen. It prevents chafing and keeps outer layers clean. You will appreciate that, because you’re the kind of person who appreciates clean.”

Charlie gave a thin, reluctant exhale that might have been a laugh if he weren’t terrified.

“Third,” I said, lifting the stays, “this goes on over the shift. Not tight. Not to shape you. To support posture and distribute the weight of the gown. If you’re going to carry my standard, you don’t slouch.”

Charlie stared at the stays as if they were a device from another century—which, of course, they were.

“I can’t breathe in that,” he said.

“You can,” I replied. “Because we won’t lace it like a performance. We lace it like work.”

He swallowed. “Okay.”

“Fourth,” I said, “petticoat. It creates the line and prevents the gown from tangling your legs. You are small. If you trip, the joke writes itself. So we remove the opportunity.”

Charlie’s jaw clenched. “You really think of everything.”

“Yes,” I said. “That’s why I’m inconvenient.”

He blinked at that, startled.

I didn’t let the moment open into anything tender.

“Fifth,” I continued, “gown. Plain. Dark. No ornament. You are not here to be admired. You are here to be overlooked.”

Charlie nodded again, eyes fixed on the table, breathing controlled.

“And last,” I said, lifting the cap, “hair. Your hair is your risk. Loose hair reads romantic. Loose hair reads ‘girl.’ Loose hair invites commentary. We pin it and cap it so it reads labour.”

Charlie looked up sharply. “My hair is… romantic?”

“It can be,” I said. “And we don’t allow that.”

His mouth tightened, but he nodded.

He stood there for a moment, hands hovering uselessly near the table, as if he didn’t know where to start.

I watched him—this slender boy who had learned to carry rules like tools, now facing the realisation that women carry rules like *skin.*

He looked up again, voice small. “And if someone recognises me?”

I met his eyes.

“Then you don’t panic,” I said. “You don’t apologise. You say: *Wardrobe.* You say: *work.* And you keep moving.”

Charlie swallowed hard.

“And if they laugh?” he asked.

I paused.

Then I gave him the most honest answer I had.

“They will,” I said. “At first. And then they’ll get bored, because boredom is the only thing that starves cruelty.”

Charlie’s eyes shone with something he didn’t want to feel—fear, grief, anger, all braided together.

He looked away quickly, blinking it back into obedience.

“I hate this,” he whispered.

“I know,” I said.

He exhaled, shoulders rising and falling once.

Then he reached, finally, for the first layer.

“Tell me again,” he said quietly, “why we’re doing this.”

I watched his hands shake, just slightly, as he unfolded the linen.

I kept my voice steady, clinical, and true.

“Because they want to isolate me,” I said. “And I’m not letting them.”

Charlie nodded once.

And began.

## Scene One — **The Numbers** (Celeste POV)

Mara didn’t summon people for speeches.

If she called you to the cutting table, it meant she had something that would either save the shop or cut it open to see what was wrong.

That morning she had the ledger, three printouts, and a pencil sharpened to a point that looked faintly accusatory.

“Sit,” she said.

Charlie sat first, too quickly—eager the way he’d become eager in the last month, which was to say: not in a needy way, but in the way a person gets when the work finally fits. He brought his own notebook as if it were a reflex. The boy used to arrive like he was expecting to be told off for breathing; now he arrived like someone who had tasks waiting.

Mara pinned a printout to the table with her fingertip.

“Throughput,” she said.

Numbers. Rows. Columns. A simple graph that sloped upward like a verdict.

I leaned in and read the headings: repairs completed, prototypes tested, defects returned, defects caught before deployment.

Mara tapped one column.

“See that?” she said. “Defects caught before deployment. That’s the difference between a clever shop and a professional one.”

Charlie’s pen hovered.

Mara didn’t look at him, but she spoke as if she knew exactly how fast he was writing.

“That jump,” she said, “happens the week you started logging wear-tests properly.”

Charlie’s ears went slightly pink. He kept writing anyway.

Mara slid the next sheet forward.

“Money,” she said, blunt.

She didn’t say it like money was shameful or romantic. She said it like money was thread: you either had enough to hold the seam, or you didn’t.

“This,” she said, tapping the bottom line, “is surplus. Actual surplus. Not ‘we hope the bills are paid’ surplus.”

I looked at the figure and felt my mind do what it always did: convert it into possibilities. Better equipment. More fabric options. Paid research time instead of stolen hours. The sort of structural improvements that turn a talented room into a durable system.

Charlie, meanwhile, had gone very still.

It took me a second to understand why.

He was looking at the surplus not like a strategist.

He was looking at it like a promise.

Not about the atelier. About himself.

The change in him was subtle—he didn’t puff up or grin—but I could see the internal arithmetic running: *If there’s surplus, there’s wages. If there are wages, there’s stability. If there’s stability, I can…*

He didn’t finish the thought out loud. He never would. Charlie’s fantasies lived behind his eyes, where no one could laugh at them.

Mara pushed the third sheet forward.

“Hours,” she said. “Paid hours.”

Charlie’s pen stopped entirely at that.

Mara glanced at him at last. Not warmly. Not coldly. With professional focus.

“You’ve been doing an operations role without being paid for an operations role,” she said. “That’s not noble. It’s stupid.”

Charlie blinked. “I— I didn’t mind.”

“I didn’t ask if you minded,” Mara snapped. “I asked if it’s sustainable.”

He swallowed. “No.”

“Correct,” Mara said. “So this changes.”

She drew a line on the paper with her pencil.

“Here’s your new rate,” she said. “Here are your hours. Here are the conditions: you keep the logs clean, you keep the testing protocol, you keep the stock inventory accurate. If you want to work in this place, you work as a professional.”

Charlie stared at the line as if it were a doorway he hadn’t realised he was allowed to walk through.

Then he nodded once. Carefully. Like he was afraid the room might snatch it back if he moved too fast.

Mara went on, as if this were the simplest thing in the world.

“And before you get sentimental,” she said, “this is not charity. This is because you made my shop better.”

Charlie’s jaw tightened with something that wasn’t sadness. It was… pressure. The pressure of being seen accurately.

He wrote the number down anyway, as if anchoring it on paper would stop it from being a dream.

Mara’s gaze flicked to me.

“This shop is growing,” she said.

I didn’t pretend surprise. We both knew it. Clients were returning. Word was spreading. The work had that particular gravity: people could feel, even without knowing why, that our garments didn’t collapse under reality.

Mara continued.

“Growth means structure,” she said. “Structure means decisions. Decisions mean someone who can do more than sew.”

Her eyes held mine.

The implication wasn’t flattering. It was practical.

Charlie shifted beside me, and I felt it again—that faint, private surge in him. His shoulders squared a fraction, as if he’d heard Mara say *provider* when she hadn’t.

He was still imagining, I realised, that his usefulness would eventually translate into a role that mattered socially. A role with leverage. A role that would make him worthy in the way boys were taught worthiness worked.

I kept my face neutral.

Let him imagine. For now.

Mara tapped the printouts again.

“This isn’t just garments anymore,” she said. “It’s a business.”

And then, with that perfectly Mara precision, she landed the real point:

“And I hate businesses run by idiots.”

Charlie’s pen twitched.

“So do I,” I said.

Mara nodded once, satisfied.

“Good,” she said. “Then we’re going to prevent it.”

She gathered the printouts into a neat stack and slid them toward me like a case file.

“Look at these,” she said. “Then tell me what we’re missing.”

I took the papers and skimmed, my mind already assembling lists: cashflow planning, supplier contracts, scheduling systems, client pipeline, training protocols, quality assurance standards that didn’t depend on any single person being present.

Charlie watched me. Not openly. Not hungrily. Just… attentive. Like he wanted to understand what I was thinking, and also like he enjoyed watching me think.

It was the kind of attention that could become devotion if you let it.

Mara’s voice cut through my thoughts.

“And Charlie,” she said, “don’t get any ideas.”

Charlie’s head snapped up. “What?”

Mara’s eyes were level.

“You’re not buying your way into importance,” she said. “You’re earning it.”

His face flushed, mortified that she could see inside him at all.

“I wasn’t—” he began.

Mara lifted a hand.

“I don’t care what you were,” she said. “I care what you do.”

Charlie shut his mouth. He nodded. He wrote something in his notebook, and I suspected it wasn’t a technical note.

Mara stood.

“Good,” she said. “Now back to work. We’ve got money on the table and no time for fantasies.”

As we dispersed, Charlie moved beside me for half a step—close enough that I could feel him wanting to speak.

He didn’t.

But I saw it: the quiet, budding myth he was building for himself.

*If I’m useful enough…*

It was almost sweet.

Almost.

## Scene Two — **Mara’s Proposal** (Celeste POV)

Mara didn’t let the numbers sit.

She let them sharpen.

That afternoon she pulled me aside while Charlie was testing a bodice under the curtain—movement list, chalk marks, tape measures, the whole ritual. The lock clicked; the atelier resumed its familiar hum.

Mara led me to the back table where the light was harsher and the conversations were always shorter.

She didn’t sit.

She placed her hands flat on the tabletop and looked straight at me.

“You need training,” she said.

I blinked, because it wasn’t what I expected. “I have training.”

Mara’s mouth tightened.

“You have taste,” she corrected. “You have research instincts. You have a good eye and a spine. You do not have business training.”

I felt irritation flare—not because she was wrong, but because she’d said it aloud, like a fact in public.

I kept my voice even.

“You think I can’t run it,” I said.

Mara’s eyes didn’t soften.

“I think you could run it into the ground,” she said. “Not from stupidity. From ignorance.”

That was Mara’s particular tenderness: refusing to let you comfort yourself with the idea that talent was enough.

I folded my arms. “So what do you suggest?”

Mara didn’t hesitate.

“MBA,” she said.

The word sat between us like a door I’d wanted and pretended I didn’t.

I held her gaze. “Those cost money.”

“Yes,” Mara said.

I felt my jaw tighten. “I don’t have money.”

“I know,” Mara replied, and her tone made it clear she wasn’t interested in my pride. “And I don’t have money to waste on hiring some glossy consultant who’s never held a needle and thinks women’s work is a hobby.”

I could feel my heartbeat in my throat, not from sentiment, from ambition. The thought of leaving this place for lectures and coursework and frameworks, then returning with tools sharp enough to protect the atelier from stupidity—mine included—made my mind run hot.

But money was still money.

I didn’t “pour my heart out.” I didn’t sigh. I didn’t romanticise.

I said the truth.

“Even part-time, I can’t fund it,” I said. “Not without draining everything I’ve already got.”

Mara nodded once.

“Good,” she said. “Then we don’t drain you. We do it properly.”

My eyes narrowed. “Meaning?”

Mara reached into the folder she carried—the one that made her look like she’d stepped out of a courtroom and into a workroom—and pulled out a single sheet of paper.

She slid it toward me.

It was not a love letter.

It was terms.

At the top she’d written:

**SPONSORSHIP PROPOSAL — TRAINING INVESTMENT (CELESTE)**

Underneath: bullet points. Clean. Cold. Perfect.

* Atelier contributes tuition up to a capped amount per semester
* Celeste enrols part-time while maintaining agreed hours at atelier
* Celeste commits to return as Operations / Business Lead for X years post-completion
* Performance milestones: semester completion, systems implemented, measurable outcomes
* Exit clause for both parties

I stared at it, reading it twice because my brain couldn’t decide whether to be offended or grateful.

“You wrote a contract,” I said.

“I wrote structure,” Mara corrected.

A ridiculous warmth flickered in my chest—annoying, unwanted, and not the kind that made you weak, more the kind that made you *seen.* She was investing not because she liked me, but because she believed I could protect her life’s work.

It was better than affection.

“And where do you get the money?” I asked, because I didn’t believe in miracles.

Mara tapped the paper. “From growth,” she said. “From what the shop is earning. From not running this place like a nice little cottage industry.”

I thought of Charlie’s new rate. The surplus. The way the shop was beginning to produce enough stability to allow planning rather than scrambling.

Then I thought, inevitably, of Charlie.

He was earning more now. He was already imagining things.

Mara watched my expression change, the way she always did—like she could hear gears turning.

“No,” she said instantly.

I blinked. “No what?”

Mara leaned forward.

“No leaning on him,” she said. “Not informally. Not with hints. Not with gratitude that turns into expectation.”

I held her gaze. “I wasn’t planning on manipulating him.”

Mara’s face stayed stern.

“I’m not accusing you of cruelty,” she said. “I’m warning you against laziness. He’s devoted. Devotion is easy to use by accident.”

Her words hit cleanly.

I exhaled once. “So what happens if he offers?”

Mara’s answer was immediate.

“Then we handle it like adults,” she said. “If he wants to contribute, it goes through written terms that protect him *and* you. But the sponsorship is between you and me. Between you and the atelier.”

I stared at the proposal again.

It was the most Mara thing I’d ever seen: a future drawn with straight lines and consequences.

A part of me wanted to snatch it up and sign it immediately.

Another part of me—more cautious, more political—wanted to understand every lever.

“You’re asking me to bind myself to the shop,” I said.

Mara nodded. “Yes.”

“And you’re binding the shop to me,” I replied.

Mara’s mouth tightened.

“That’s the point,” she said. “I don’t want your loyalty. I want your competence. Loyalty goes soft. Competence holds.”

A sound came from the other side of the room—the curtain rustling, Charlie stepping out, ledger in hand. He moved like he belonged there now, like the space had stopped being something he tiptoed through and started being something he maintained.

He noticed us.

He hesitated for the briefest moment, as if sensing that the conversation was about futures and not hems.

He didn’t approach.

Good boy, I thought, not unkindly. He was learning boundaries.

Mara’s voice dropped slightly.

“We’ll have Lauren read it,” she said. “She’s got sense, and she’ll smell coercion even if we pretend we don’t.”

I almost smiled. “Lauren will enjoy that.”

Mara gave a small snort.

“Good,” she said. “Then she’ll do it properly.”

Charlie came closer at last, stopping at a respectful distance, eyes flicking from Mara to me to the paper.

“What’s that?” he asked quietly.

Mara looked at him, expression unreadable.

“Not your business,” she said.

Charlie flushed. “Right. Sorry.”

I watched him—the quick retreat, the reflexive shame—and I filed it away with everything else I’d learned about him.

He still thought access was something you earned by being useful.

He still thought being needed would lead to being chosen.

And I could see, as clearly as if it were written in the ledger, where his mind was already trying to go:

*If I can pay…*

Mara saw it too. She didn’t soften. She didn’t scold him, either.

She simply set the boundary like a beam in a wall.

“Go log your test,” she said. “Then get back to cutting. We’re not a romance novel.”

Charlie nodded, chastened, and returned to the ledger.

Mara turned back to me.

“You want the MBA?” she asked.

I looked down at the proposal again. At the clean lines. At the way it turned longing into infrastructure.

“Yes,” I said.

Mara nodded once.

“Then we do it like this,” she said. “And nobody gets to buy you.”

I met her gaze.

“And nobody gets to own the atelier,” I replied.

Mara’s mouth tightened in approval.

“Exactly,” she said. “Now sign when you’re ready. Not before.”

And for the first time all day, I felt the future stop being a wish and start being a plan.

### 1) The Envelope That Isn’t Romantic

The first tuition payment didn’t arrive with flowers.

She arrived like a declaration.

Not loud, exactly—she wasn’t shouting—but she filled the doorway in the way people do when they expect the room to revolve around them. Hair loose in glossy waves, a flower crown that looked fresh from a craft store, and a bodice that wasn’t a bodice so much as a promise: stiffened, lace-up, cut low enough to require courage and one very specific posture.

She was pretty. That wasn’t the issue.

The issue was function.

Mara looked up from the cutting table and assessed her the way she assessed everything: not with envy, not with disdain—like a mechanic listening to an engine.

“Can I help you?” Mara asked.

The young woman smiled as if she was used to being rewarded for arriving.

“I’m here to check in,” she said brightly. “I’m doing the immersive week. I brought my own kit.” She turned a little, showing off the skirt—shorter than anything practical, layered tulle pretending to be history. “But I thought I might need a little… adjustment.”

Her gaze flicked to Charlie for half a second, a look that was less curiosity than assumption. A flicker of *oh, so that’s the vibe here.*

Charlie, to his credit, didn’t react. He kept his eyes on the ledger like it was a life raft.

Mara’s expression didn’t change.

“Immersive week,” Mara repeated.

“Yes,” the woman said, leaning her elbows onto the counter in a way that pushed her chest forward. “I’ve done fairs. I’m fine with corsets.” She tapped the laces at her sternum like she was tapping a badge.

Mara stepped closer—not into her space, but into professional distance.

“That’s not a corset,” Mara said.

The woman blinked, smile still pinned in place. “It’s stays.”

Mara’s mouth tightened. Not annoyance. Correction.

“No,” she said, calmly. “It’s a costume top with laces.”

The woman’s cheeks coloured. “Excuse me?”

I saw it happen in her eyes: the reflex that expected shame as the weapon. The pre-emptive bracing for judgement.

Mara didn’t offer it.

She didn’t call her trashy, or silly, or immoral. She didn’t moralise.

She simply kept going, like this was a technical matter.

“Stays are underwear,” Mara said. “Structural underwear. They’re built to distribute load, support the bodice, and keep the shape stable under movement. This”—she gestured once at the low neckline—“is built to be looked at.”

The woman’s smile cracked. “What’s wrong with being looked at?”

“Nothing,” Mara said, immediate. “In the market area. In the tavern. On your own time.”

The woman lifted her chin. “And here?”

Mara’s eyes held hers.

“Here is Wardrobe,” Mara said. “And the immersive week is not the tavern.”

The woman glanced around as if checking whether she’d walked into the wrong building. She saw bolts of linen, stacks of petticoats, labelled boxes, the ledger, the chalk marks, the pinned fabric tests. Not a dressing room. A shop.

I spoke then—not because Mara needed backup, but because the newcomer needed a translation that didn’t feel like punishment.

“The immersive group has standards,” I said. “Not because anyone’s trying to police your body. Because the point is to live in the clothing. Work in it. Move in it. It needs to hold.”

The woman’s eyes narrowed. “So you’re saying I’m not allowed to be… feminine.”

It was the oldest pivot in the book: turning a boundary into an accusation.

Mara didn’t bite.

Mara nodded once, as if she’d been waiting for exactly that line.

“Feminine is not cleavage,” she said. “Feminine is competence.”

The woman stared, speechless for a beat.

Mara continued, tone unchanged.

“If you want to join immersive, we’ll fit you in proper stays and a shift,” Mara said. “We’ll give you a gown that flatters you and functions. You’ll still look beautiful. You just won’t be dressed for a modern photoshoot.”

The woman’s gaze flicked again toward Charlie, and this time there was something almost resentful in it, as if she’d expected her look to earn her power over him.

Charlie didn’t look up.

Mara saw the glance. Of course she did.

Her voice sharpened—not with anger, with policy.

“And,” she added, “you don’t assess my staff like they’re props. Everyone in here is working.”

The woman bristled. “I wasn’t—”

“You were,” Mara said, simply. No drama. No flourish. Just truth.

I watched the newcomer struggle for a moment—the internal conflict of someone who’d come expecting praise and instead encountered a world that didn’t barter attention for access.

Then her shoulders shifted. The smile disappeared. A more honest expression arrived: uncertainty.

“So what,” she said, quieter now, “I have to cover up?”

Mara’s mouth tightened.

“You have to dress for the role you signed up for,” Mara said. “If you want attention, the faire will give it to you. If you want immersion, you dress for immersion. Choose.”

The woman stared at the floor for a second.

Then she looked up, eyes still defensive, but less theatrical.

“What if I don’t have time to—” she began.

“You do,” Mara said. “Because you paid for a week.”

She paused, then added—almost kindly, in Mara’s language.

The woman blinked at that, as if she hadn’t expected anyone to say it out loud.

“Okay,” she said finally, and it sounded like the first real word she’d spoken since she walked in. “Okay. Show me what you mean.”

It became what it always became in here:

Standards.
