**Ted learns firsthand what being creatively 'erased' by a society is like.**

TED Labruyère is an 18-year-old with long hair and delicate features. Fresh out of high school, he is trying to find a job. The employment agency clerk considers his build and aptitudes and suggests joining a troupe of girls who are going to work at a history fair on the other side of the country. The owner of the 'fair' (KATHY de Belle Chasse) - which is more of a history experience site than a traditional fair - is a history buff. As the troupe drive across the country (Australia) to the chateau, the girls tease Ted regarding his delicate features and because he is using a woman's handbag as a travel bag, even to the point where they suggest he should try on some of their fancy gowns. They don't realise what Kathy has in store for him.

In addition to being a history buff, Kathy is also a feminist who is concerned with the historical erasing of female accomplishments by men. She realises that she needs to place an actual male into the role of being successful at some art form and then, erasing that individual's accomplishments in order for an understanding to develop that this has happened persistently to women in the past. The male she has chosen for that experience is Ted. She has learned that he composes music: he will therefore teach musical composition to the girls in the troupe, who will play the role of royalty. Kathy's plan is that they will excel in that art form and will end up eclipsing his works, essentially 'erasing' him.

The setting is a faithfully reproduced chateau built in the style of the 17th century. Whilst the girls focus on their education, Ted is initially assigned to Pauline, the French maid-servant, to learn to exist at a socially inferior level. The social status of composers in the 18th century was they were essentially servants themselves. 

Despite mild resistence, Pauline pushes him forcefully into a sevant's role by requiring that he dress like her in a chemise, stays, petticoats, gown, apron and even a white cap, and serve the girls as she did (tending to all their needs) for meals and elsewhere. This serves - in Kathy and Pauline's view - to level the playing field, so that despite his natural talent and ability at composition, Ted remains statuswise subordinate to the girls, who are now considered royalty.

Several of the girls - EMMA Barton, the cowgirl and LUCY, the gay bartender and CHLOE, her lover - become quite proficient at writing melodies and also playing musical instruments, something Ted, despite his talents at composition, has never learnt to do. The girls end up teaching him the violin and clavecin, but he never achieves their level of playing. He is frequently reminded of is social status in a variety of ways, both spoken and other means.

In a particularly harrowing scene - reminiscent of the abortion scene in 'Portait de la Jeune Fille en Feu' - Ted is subjected to a rather invasive treatment by the village healers to alter ('correct', the woman calls it) the direction of Ted's development using exotic preparations from herbs brought in from foreign lands. In this case, the Thai herb Pueraria Mirifica is used liberally, with powerful effect. Ted fills out the maid's outfit properly, looking from every perspective more and more female.

Ted finally learns to accept his social status and his new gender. He develops an amorous relationship with Emma, the cowgirl. She woos him as a suitor would. She writes powerful orchestral works commemorating her love for Ted. His compositions are also published, but under Emma's surname, as he becomes Emma's wife. The story ends with a grand ball scene, with Emma walking into the ball with "Dora" (i.e., 'Theodora', feminine for Ted) on her arm.

------
Act 01
------

**Introduction to Ted**

TED Labruyère, 18, fresh out of high school, struggles to find a job. With long hair and delicate features, he often faces awkward situations. At an employment agency, a clerk suggests an unusual opportunity: joining a troupe of girls heading to a historical fair across the country. Despite initial hesitation, Ted agrees, drawn by the promise of employment and adventure.

**The Journey**

Ted joins the troupe on a road trip across Australia. As they travel in a VW combivan, the girls—EMMA, LUCY, CHLOE, and others—tease Ted about his feminine features and his use of a woman's handbag. They jokingly suggest he try on some of their gowns. Ted blushes, feeling both amused and embarrassed, unaware of the deeper implications of this journey.


------
Act 02
------

**Arrival at the Chateau**

The troupe arrives at a faithfully recreated 17th-century chateau in the New South Wales hinterland. The fair, run by KATHY de Belle Chasse, is more than a typical fair; it's a historical experience site. Kathy, a passionate historian and feminist, has a unique vision for the site.

**Kathy's Plan**

Kathy introduces herself and explains the fair’s purpose: to immerse visitors in an authentic 18th-century experience. Privately, Kathy reveals her deeper agenda. She believes in highlighting the historical erasure of female accomplishments. To do this, she plans to place Ted in a traditionally female role, have him achieve success, and then erase his accomplishments to demonstrate how this has happened to women throughout history.

**Ted's Transformation**

Ted, an aspiring composer, is chosen for Kathy's experiment. He is assigned to Pauline, the French maid-servant, to learn to live at a socially inferior level. Initially resistant, Ted is gradually pushed by Pauline to adopt the attire and duties of a maid. He dons a chemise, stays, petticoats, gown, apron, and cap, serving the girls who are being trained as royalty.


------
Act 03
------

**Challenges and Growth**

As Ted adapts to his new role, several girls, including Emma, a cowgirl, and Lucy, a gay bartender, excel in writing melodies and playing musical instruments. Although Ted is talented at composition, he struggles with the instruments. The girls teach him, but he never reaches their level of proficiency. Ted’s social status is constantly reinforced through various spoken and unspoken means.

**The Turning Point**

In a harrowing scene, reminiscent of the abortion scene in 'Portrait de la Jeune Fille en Feu,' village healers administer an invasive treatment to Ted using the Thai herb Pueraria Mirifica. This 'corrective' treatment dramatically alters Ted’s physical appearance, making him fill out the maid's outfit and look increasingly female.

**Acceptance and Love**

Ted begins to accept his new gender and social status. He forms a romantic relationship with Emma, who woos him as a suitor would. Emma writes powerful orchestral works celebrating her love for Ted. His compositions are also published, but under Emma's surname, signifying his complete transformation.

**Climax and Resolution**

The story culminates in a grand ball at the chateau. Emma, now an accomplished composer, enters the ball with "Dora" (Theodora, the feminine form of Ted) on her arm. They are greeted with admiration and respect, symbolizing the acceptance of their new identities and roles.

**Final Reflection**

The ball scene is a moment of triumph and bittersweet reflection. Ted, now fully Theodora, embraces his new life, having learned the profound lessons of gender, identity, and historical recognition. The film ends with a lingering shot of the couple, dancing gracefully, as the camera pans out to the grandiose setting of the chateau.
