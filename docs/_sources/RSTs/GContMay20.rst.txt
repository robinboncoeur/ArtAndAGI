The Chateau
***********

The château used in the filming of "Portrait de la Jeune Fille en Feu" (Portrait of a Lady on Fire) is the Château de La Chapelle Gauthier, located in Seine-et-Marne, about an hour from Paris. This château was primarily constructed in the 17th century. The setting in the film, although depicted as being in Brittany, was chosen for its historical ambiance and proximity to Paris, which was convenient for the production team. 

From `the site <https://www.france.fr/fr/article/visite-chateau-film-portrait-jeune-fille-en-feu/#le-chateau-dheloise-1>`_:

L'amour entre les deux jeunes filles jaillit au milieu des falaises solitaires de Bretagne, mais s'enflamme sous le toit protecteur du château ancestral d'Héloïse, dans une oasis réservée aux femmes, avant que l'héritière ne soit envoyée à Milan pour épouser un homme qu'elle n'aime, ni ne connaît.

        Welcome to our tour of the castle's historical wardrobe! Today, we’ll explore the intricate garments worn by women in the 1750s. Each outfit was composed of several distinct parts, each serving a specific function and reflecting the fashion of the time.

        1. **Chemise**:
        
           * The chemise, or shift, was the innermost layer, made of linen or cotton. It was a simple, loose-fitting garment that protected the outer clothing from sweat and body oils. The chemise also served as a nightgown.

        2. **Stays**:
        
           * Over the chemise, women wore stays, which are similar to what we might call a corset today. Stays were made of layers of fabric stiffened with boning, usually from whale baleen or reeds. They provided support, shaped the torso, and promoted good posture. Contrary to popular belief, stays were not typically as restrictive as corsets from later periods.

        3. **Petticoats**:
        
           * Petticoats were worn over the stays and chemise. These were full skirts that added volume and warmth. Depending on the formality of the occasion and the wealth of the wearer, a woman might wear several petticoats at once. The outermost petticoat was often decorative and matched the gown.

        4. **Gown**:

           * The gown itself, which could come in various styles such as the Robe à la Française or Robe à l'Anglaise, was worn over the petticoats. The Robe à la Française featured pleats at the back that flowed down from the shoulders to the floor, while the Robe à l'Anglaise had a more fitted back. These gowns were often made of silk, brocade, or fine wool, with elaborate patterns and decorations.

5. **Engageantes**:

   * Engageantes were detachable lace or linen sleeves that extended from the elbow to the wrist, adding a touch of elegance to the gown. These could be changed to match different outfits or occasions.

6. **Stomacher**:

   * The stomacher was a triangular panel that filled in the front opening of the gown. It was often elaborately decorated with embroidery, ribbons, or jewels. The stomacher was pinned or sewn in place, and could be swapped out to give a gown a different look.

7. **Accessories**:

   * Accessories were an essential part of a woman’s outfit. These included caps or bonnets, often decorated with lace and ribbons, worn to cover the hair. Gloves, fans, and jewelry such as brooches and necklaces completed the ensemble. Shoes were typically low-heeled and made of silk or leather, often with decorative buckles.

8. **Mantua or Cloak**:

   * For outdoor wear, women would don a mantua or cloak. These were large, loose overgarments that provided warmth and protection from the elements. They were often lined with fur or quilted for additional insulation.

Shoes and Alternatives
======================

Hi Emily. Question about footwear for women in the 1750s:  were there alternatives to shoes?

Hi! In the 1750s, while shoes were the primary form of footwear for women, there were a few alternative options and variations based on occasion, social status, and environment. Here are some alternatives and variations to consider:

**Alternative Footwear Options**:

    1. **Mules (Slippers)**:
    
       - **Description**: Mules were backless shoes that could easily be slipped on and off. They were often worn indoors as casual or house shoes.
       - **Materials**: Made from leather, silk, or brocade, sometimes with decorative embroidery or embellishments.

    2. **Pattens**:
    
       - **Description**: Pattens were wooden or metal overshoes worn over regular shoes to protect them from mud and wet ground. They elevated the wearer a few inches off the ground.
       - **Materials**: Typically made of wood with metal fittings or entirely from metal. The design included a wooden sole with metal supports.

    3. **Clogs**:
    
       - **Description**: Similar to pattens, clogs were also used to protect shoes from dirt and water. They were more commonly used by working-class women.
       - **Materials**: Made from wood, sometimes with leather straps.

    4. **Stockings**:
    
       - **Description**: While not footwear per se, stockings were an essential part of a woman’s attire. Made from wool, silk, or cotton, they provided warmth and modesty.
       - **Materials**: Depending on the social status, they could range from coarse wool to fine silk.

**Contextual Footwear Usage**:

    - **Indoors**:
    
      - Women often wore more delicate and decorative slippers or mules when indoors. These were less about protection and more about comfort and fashion.
    
    - **Outdoors**:
    
      - Sturdier shoes with thicker soles were worn outside. When conditions were particularly poor (e.g., muddy or rainy), pattens or clogs were used to keep shoes clean and dry.

**Footwear for Different Classes**:

    - **Upper Class**:
    
      - Women of higher social status wore finely made shoes, often with decorative elements like buckles, bows, and embroidery. Materials included silk, brocade, and fine leather.
    
    - **Working Class**:
    
      - Working-class women wore more practical, durable footwear. Their shoes were made from sturdier leather and were designed for comfort and longevity. Pattens and clogs were more commonly used among this group.

**Footwear in Specific Situations**:

    - **Formal Events**:
    
      - For formal occasions and social gatherings, women wore their best shoes, often with heels and intricate designs.
      
    - **Household Work**:
    
      - During household chores, simpler and more comfortable shoes or slippers were preferred.

Overall, while shoes were the predominant form of footwear, alternatives like mules, pattens, and clogs provided functional variations suited to different environments and activities. If you need any more details or have specific questions, feel free to ask!

Servant Girl Footwear 
---------------------

For female servants in the 1750s, practical and durable footwear was essential due to the nature of their work. While shoes were the primary form of footwear, there were some alternatives or supplementary options that servants might have used:

**Alternatives to Shoes for Female Servants in the 1750s**:

    1. **Pattens**:
    
       - **Description**: Pattens were overshoes designed to protect regular shoes from mud, dirt, and wet conditions. They elevated the wearer a few inches off the ground.
       - **Usage**: Commonly used outdoors or in muddy conditions to keep shoes clean and dry.
       - **Materials**: Made from wood with metal fittings or entirely from metal. They featured a wooden sole with metal supports that strapped over the shoes.

    2. **Clogs**:
    
       - **Description**: Similar to pattens, clogs were wooden shoes or overshoes that provided protection against wet and dirty conditions.
       - **Usage**: More frequently used by working-class women and servants for outdoor tasks.
       - **Materials**: Typically made from wood, sometimes with leather straps for securing the foot.

    3. **Stockings and Bare Feet**:
    
       - **Stockings**: Servants often wore stockings made from wool or linen. While stockings were not footwear, they provided some level of protection and modesty.
       - **Bare Feet**: In some cases, especially indoors or in warm weather, servants might work barefoot. This was more common in less formal or rural settings.

**Contextual Usage**:

    - **Indoors**:
    
      - Indoors, servants typically wore their regular shoes, often made from sturdy leather. When performing lighter tasks, they might wear simpler slippers or even go barefoot for comfort.
      
    - **Outdoors**:
    
      - Pattens and clogs were particularly useful for outdoor tasks where the ground was wet or muddy. These items helped preserve the life of their regular shoes by keeping them clean and dry.

**Footwear Materials and Durability**:

    - **Servant Shoes**:
    
      - Made from durable leather, these shoes were designed to withstand daily wear and tear. They were practical, without the decorative elements seen in upper-class footwear.
      
    - **Maintenance**:
    
      - Servants often had to maintain their footwear, cleaning and repairing them as needed to ensure longevity.

**Social and Economic Considerations**:

    - **Affordability**:
    
      - Servants' footwear was typically more affordable and less ornate than that of higher social classes. Practicality and durability were prioritized over fashion.
      
    - **Work Environment**:
    
      - The type of footwear worn could vary depending on the work environment and specific tasks performed. For example, a kitchen maid might have different needs compared to a housemaid working in more formal areas of the house.

In summary, while shoes were the primary form of footwear for female servants in the 1750s, alternatives like pattens and clogs were used to protect their shoes in specific conditions. Stockings provided additional coverage, and going barefoot was not uncommon in certain contexts.

Fetching Water from a Well in the 18th Century
==============================================

Fetching water from a well in the 18th century was a common and essential task, especially for households without direct access to running water. Here’s a detailed description of how this was typically done:

    1. **Well Construction:**
    
       - **Structure**: Wells were often stone or brick-lined shafts dug deep into the ground to access underground water sources. The top of the well was usually covered with a well house or a simple structure to protect the water from contamination and debris.
       - **Covering**: Many wells had a wooden cover or a small roof to prevent dirt and leaves from falling in.

    2. **Drawing Water:**
    
       - **Bucket and Rope**: The most common method involved using a wooden or metal bucket attached to a rope. The rope would be either wound around a windlass (a horizontal drum turned by a handle) or simply tied to the bucket and pulled up manually.
       - **Windlass**: A windlass consisted of a rotating horizontal cylinder mounted on a frame, with a handle for turning. The rope would be wound around this cylinder. By turning the handle, the bucket could be lowered into the well and then raised once filled with water.

    3. **Steps to Fetch Water:**
    
       - **Approach the Well**: Ted, dressed as an 18th-century maid, would approach the well with a clean bucket.
       - **Remove Cover**: If the well had a cover, he would remove it to access the opening.
       - **Lower the Bucket**: Ted would lower the bucket into the well by unwinding the rope from the windlass or manually lowering it until it reached the water.
       - **Fill the Bucket**: Once the bucket touched the water, it would begin to fill. Sometimes a slight tug or shake of the rope was needed to ensure the bucket tipped and filled completely.
       - **Raise the Bucket**: After the bucket was filled, Ted would turn the handle of the windlass to wind the rope back up, lifting the bucket out of the well. If there was no windlass, he would pull the rope up hand-over-hand.
       - **Secure the Water**: Upon reaching the top, Ted would secure the bucket on the edge of the well or on the ground to avoid spillage.
       - **Pour into Larger Containers**: He would then pour the water into larger containers or water barrels for household use.

    4. **Additional Considerations:**
    
       - **Repeat Process**: Depending on the amount of water needed, this process might be repeated several times.
       - **Cleanliness**: It was important to keep the bucket and rope clean to ensure the water remained uncontaminated.
       - **Physical Effort**: Fetching water required significant physical effort, particularly if the well was deep or if large quantities of water were needed.

**Visual Description**

He attaches the bucket to the rope and begins to unwind the windlass, lowering the bucket into the depths. The sound of the bucket splashing into the water echoes up the well shaft. **Ted learns firsthand what being creatively 'erased' by a society is like.**

TED Labruyère is an 18-year-old with long hair and delicate features. Fresh out of high school, he is trying to find a job. The employment agency clerk considers his build and aptitudes and suggests joining a troupe of girls who are going to work at a history fair on the other side of the country. The owner of the 'fair' (KATHY de Belle Chasse) - which is more of a history experience site than a traditional fair - is a history buff. As the troupe drive across the country (Australia) to the chateau, the girls tease Ted regarding his delicate features and because he is using a woman's handbag as a travel bag, even to the point where they suggest he should try on some of their fancy gowns. They don't realise what Kathy has in store for him.

In addition to being a history buff, Kathy is also a feminist who is concerned with the historical erasing of female accomplishments by men. She realises that she needs to place an actual male into the role of being successful at some art form and then, erasing that individual's accomplishments in order for an understanding to develop that this has happened persistently to women in the past. The male she has chosen for that experience is Ted. She has learned that he composes music: he will therefore teach musical composition to the girls in the troupe, who will play the role of royalty. Kathy's plan is that they will excel in that art form and will end up eclipsing his works, essentially 'erasing' him.

The setting is a faithfully reproduced chateau built in the style of the 17th century. Whilst the girls focus on their education, Ted is initially assigned to Pauline, the French maid-servant, to learn to exist at a socially inferior level. The social status of composers in the 18th century was they were essentially servants themselves. 

Despite mild resistence, Pauline pushes him forcefully into a sevant's role by requiring that he dress like her in a chemise, stays, petticoats, gown, apron and even a white cap, and serve the girls as she did (tending to all their needs) for meals and elsewhere. This serves - in Kathy and Pauline's view - to level the playing field, so that despite his natural talent and ability at composition, Ted remains statuswise subordinate to the girls, who are now considered royalty.

Several of the girls - EMMA Barton, the cowgirl and LUCY, the gay bartender and CHLOE, her lover - become quite proficient at writing melodies and also playing musical instruments, something Ted, despite his talents at composition, has never learnt to do. The girls end up teaching him the violin and clavecin, but he never achieves their level of playing. He is frequently reminded of is social status in a variety of ways, both spoken and other means.

In a particularly harrowing scene - reminiscent of the abortion scene in 'Portait de la Jeune Fille en Feu' - Ted is subjected to a rather invasive treatment by the village healers to alter ('correct', the woman calls it) the direction of Ted's development using exotic preparations from herbs brought in from foreign lands. In this case, the Thai herb Pueraria Mirifica is used liberally, with powerful effect. Ted fills out the maid's outfit properly, looking from every perspective more and more female.

Ted finally learns to accept his social status and his new gender. He develops an amorous relationship with Emma, the cowgirl. She woos him as a suitor would. She writes powerful orchestral works commemorating her love for Ted. His compositions are also published, but under Emma's surname, as he becomes Emma's wife. The story ends with a grand ball scene, with Emma walking into the ball with "Dora" (i.e., 'Theodora', feminine for Ted) on her arm.

**Title**

*Theodora: Rien que Moins*

**Logline**: In a modern-day historical experience site, a young man's delicate features and musical talent lead him into an unexpected journey of gender transformation and social redefinition, orchestrated by a feminist historian determined to highlight the historical erasure of female accomplishments.


=========
Treatment
=========

------
Act 01
------

**Introduction to Ted**

TED Labruyère, 18, fresh out of high school, struggles to find a job. With long hair and delicate features, he often faces awkward situations. At an employment agency, a clerk suggests an unusual opportunity: joining a troupe of girls heading to a historical fair across the country. Despite initial hesitation, Ted agrees, drawn by the promise of employment and adventure.

**The Journey**

Ted joins the troupe on a road trip across Australia. As they travel in a VW combivan, the girls—EMMA, LUCY, CHLOE, and others—tease Ted about his feminine features and his use of a woman's handbag. They jokingly suggest he try on some of their gowns. Ted blushes, feeling both amused and embarrassed, unaware of the deeper implications of this journey.


------
Act 02
------

**Arrival at the Chateau**

The troupe arrives at a faithfully recreated 17th-century chateau in the New South Wales hinterland. The fair, run by KATHY de Belle Chasse, is more than a typical fair; it's a historical experience site. Kathy, a passionate historian and feminist, has a unique vision for the site.

**Kathy's Plan**

Kathy introduces herself and explains the fair’s purpose: to immerse visitors in an authentic 18th-century experience. Privately, Kathy reveals her deeper agenda. She believes in highlighting the historical erasure of female accomplishments. To do this, she plans to place Ted in a traditionally female role, have him achieve success, and then erase his accomplishments to demonstrate how this has happened to women throughout history.

**Ted's Transformation**

Ted, an aspiring composer, is chosen for Kathy's experiment. He is assigned to Pauline, the French maid-servant, to learn to live at a socially inferior level. Initially resistant, Ted is gradually pushed by Pauline to adopt the attire and duties of a maid. He dons a chemise, stays, petticoats, gown, apron, and cap, serving the girls who are being trained as royalty.


------
Act 03
------

**Challenges and Growth**

As Ted adapts to his new role, several girls, including Emma, a cowgirl, and Lucy, a gay bartender, excel in writing melodies and playing musical instruments. Although Ted is talented at composition, he struggles with the instruments. The girls teach him, but he never reaches their level of proficiency. Ted’s social status is constantly reinforced through various spoken and unspoken means.

**The Turning Point**

In a harrowing scene, reminiscent of the abortion scene in 'Portrait de la Jeune Fille en Feu,' village healers administer an invasive treatment to Ted using the Thai herb Pueraria Mirifica. This 'corrective' treatment dramatically alters Ted’s physical appearance, making him fill out the maid's outfit and look increasingly female.

**Acceptance and Love**

Ted begins to accept his new gender and social status. He forms a romantic relationship with Emma, who woos him as a suitor would. Emma writes powerful orchestral works celebrating her love for Ted. His compositions are also published, but under Emma's surname, signifying his complete transformation.


------
Act 04
------

**Climax and Resolution**

The story culminates in a grand ball at the chateau. Emma, now an accomplished composer, enters the ball with "Dora" (Theodora, the feminine form of Ted) on her arm. They are greeted with admiration and respect, symbolizing the acceptance of their new identities and roles.

**Final Reflection**

The ball scene is a moment of triumph and bittersweet reflection. Ted, now fully Theodora, embraces his new life, having learned the profound lessons of gender, identity, and historical recognition. The film ends with a lingering shot of the couple, dancing gracefully, as the camera pans out to the grandiose setting of the chateau.
