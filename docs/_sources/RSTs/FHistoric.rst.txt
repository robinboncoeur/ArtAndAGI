The Chateau
***********

The château used in the filming of "Portrait de la Jeune Fille en Feu" (Portrait of a Lady on Fire) is the Château de La Chapelle Gauthier, located in Seine-et-Marne, about an hour from Paris. This château was primarily constructed in the 17th century. The setting in the film, although depicted as being in Brittany, was chosen for its historical ambiance and proximity to Paris, which was convenient for the production team. 

From `the site <https://www.france.fr/fr/article/visite-chateau-film-portrait-jeune-fille-en-feu/#le-chateau-dheloise-1>`_:

Céline Sciamma raconte l'histoire d'amour de Marianne (Noémie Merlant), peintre, et d'Héloïse (Adèle Haenel), sa muse réticente à l'approche d'un mariage arrangé dans la France du XVIIIe siècle. L'amour entre les deux jeunes filles jaillit au milieu des falaises solitaires de Bretagne, mais s'enflamme sous le toit protecteur du château ancestral d'Héloïse, dans une oasis réservée aux femmes, avant que l'héritière ne soit envoyée à Milan pour épouser un homme qu'elle n'aime, ni ne connaît.

Entre les eaux déferlantes, le feu qui crépite et la passion à couper le souffle se trouve le château de pierre. Situé le long de la côte bretonne dans le film, la demeure se trouve en fait à une heure de Paris, dans le luxuriant département de la Seine-et-Marne. Fetching water from a well in the 18th century was a common and essential task, especially for households without direct access to running water. Here’s a detailed description of how this was typically done:

    1. **Well Construction:**
    
       - **Structure**: Wells were often stone or brick-lined shafts dug deep into the ground to access underground water sources. The top of the well was usually covered with a well house or a simple structure to protect the water from contamination and debris.
       - **Covering**: Many wells had a wooden cover or a small roof to prevent dirt and leaves from falling in.

    2. **Drawing Water:**
    
       - **Bucket and Rope**: The most common method involved using a wooden or metal bucket attached to a rope. The rope would be either wound around a windlass (a horizontal drum turned by a handle) or simply tied to the bucket and pulled up manually.
       - **Windlass**: A windlass consisted of a rotating horizontal cylinder mounted on a frame, with a handle for turning. The rope would be wound around this cylinder. By turning the handle, the bucket could be lowered into the well and then raised once filled with water.

    3. **Steps to Fetch Water:**
    
       - **Approach the Well**: Ted, dressed as an 18th-century maid, would approach the well with a clean bucket.
       - **Remove Cover**: If the well had a cover, he would remove it to access the opening.
       - **Lower the Bucket**: Ted would lower the bucket into the well by unwinding the rope from the windlass or manually lowering it until it reached the water.
       - **Fill the Bucket**: Once the bucket touched the water, it would begin to fill. Sometimes a slight tug or shake of the rope was needed to ensure the bucket tipped and filled completely.
       - **Raise the Bucket**: After the bucket was filled, Ted would turn the handle of the windlass to wind the rope back up, lifting the bucket out of the well. If there was no windlass, he would pull the rope up hand-over-hand.
       - **Secure the Water**: Upon reaching the top, Ted would secure the bucket on the edge of the well or on the ground to avoid spillage.
       - **Pour into Larger Containers**: He would then pour the water into larger containers or water barrels for household use.

    4. **Additional Considerations:**
    
       - **Repeat Process**: Depending on the amount of water needed, this process might be repeated several times.
       - **Cleanliness**: It was important to keep the bucket and rope clean to ensure the water remained uncontaminated.
       - **Physical Effort**: Fetching water required significant physical effort, particularly if the well was deep or if large quantities of water were needed.

**Visual Description**

He attaches the bucket to the rope and begins to unwind the windlass, lowering the bucket into the depths. The sound of the bucket splashing into the water echoes up the well shaft. He then winds the handle, slowly lifting the heavy, water-filled bucket back to the surface. This task, while labor-intensive, was a crucial part of daily life in the 18th century, ensuring the household had the necessary water for cooking, cleaning, and other essential activities.

       - **Usage**: Used for storing liquids such as oils, vinegar, wines, spirits, and medicinal concoctions. Also used for preserves like jams and pickles.

    2. **Ceramic and Earthenware Jars**
       - **Material**: Glazed or unglazed ceramic and earthenware
       - **Usage**: Ideal for storing dry goods such as grains, flour, spices, and dried herbs. Also used for fermented foods like sauerkraut.

       - **Material**: Stoneware, a type of pottery fired at high temperatures

    4. **Wooden Barrels and Casks**
       - **Material**: Wood, typically oak
       - **Usage**: Used for bulk storage and transportation of liquids such as beer, wine, cider, and sometimes dry goods.

    5. **Metal Containers**

       - **Material**: Treated leather
       - **Usage**: Portable containers for liquids, often used by travelers.
       - **Sealing**: Cork stoppers or tied-off openings.

    7. **Wicker Baskets**
       - **Material**: Wicker or woven reeds

    8. **Cloth Sacks and Bags**

       - **Usage**: Used for storing and transporting dry goods like grains, flour, and dried fruits.

       - **Material**: Parchment or cloth coated with beeswax
       - **Usage**: Used to wrap foods like cheese, butter, and baked goods to keep them fresh.
       - **Sealing**: Tied with string or sealed with additional wax.

    10. **Horn and Shell Containers**
    
       - **Material**: Hollowed-out animal horns or large seashells
       - **Usage**: Used for storing small quantities of dry goods, spices, or snuff.
       - **Sealing**: Often fitted with wooden or cork stoppers.

Details on Atropine

[Q] Is there an association between belladona and the medication we know today called 'Atropine', which generally has cardio-active properties?

[A] Yes, there is a direct association between belladonna and atropine. Atropine is one of the primary active compounds found in the belladonna plant (Atropa belladonna) and other plants in the Solanaceae family, such as henbane (Hyoscyamus niger) and jimsonweed (Datura stramonium).

**Relationship Between Belladonna and Atropine**:

1. **Source**:
   - **Belladonna**: Atropa belladonna, commonly known as deadly nightshade, contains several tropane alkaloids, including atropine, hyoscyamine, and scopolamine.
   - **Atropine**: This is a specific alkaloid derived from belladonna and other related plants.

2. **Historical Use**:
   - In historical contexts, extracts from belladonna plants were used for their medicinal properties, such as muscle relaxation, pain relief, and dilating pupils.
   - The cosmetic use of belladonna for pupil dilation led to its name, "beautiful lady" in Italian.

3. **Modern Medicine**:
   - **Atropine**: Today, atropine is a well-known medication used in various medical settings. It is used to:
   
     - Treat bradycardia (slow heart rate) by increasing the heart rate.
     - Dilate pupils during eye examinations.
     - Reduce saliva production during surgery.
     - Act as an antidote for certain types of poisoning, including organophosphates and nerve agents.
   
   - Atropine works by blocking the action of the neurotransmitter acetylcholine at muscarinic receptors, which affects the parasympathetic nervous system.

4. **Effects**:
   - **Pupil Dilation**: One of the notable effects of atropine is mydriasis (pupil dilation), which is why belladonna was historically used in cosmetics.
   - **Euphoria and Hallucinations**: In higher doses, atropine can cross the blood-brain barrier and cause central nervous system effects, including euphoria, hallucinations, and a sense of altered time perception.

**Summary**

Atropine is a purified and concentrated form of one of the active compounds found in belladonna. The historical use of belladonna for its medicinal and cosmetic properties directly relates to the effects of atropine, which is used in modern medicine for its various pharmacological actions, particularly on the heart and eyes.

So, to answer your question, yes, there is a strong association between belladonna and atropine. The compound atropine is derived from belladonna and shares many of the same properties that made belladonna notable in historical practices.
