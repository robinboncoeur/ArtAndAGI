Scene 24¶

✨ The Operating Surface ✨¶

(Celeste POV) — polish pass

I cleared the table the way you clear a bench before you do something you can’t afford to botch. Not ceremoniously or dramatically. Just... quietly, completely. Mug rings wiped away. Crumbs vanished into my palm. The loose thread someone had left like a dead spider got flicked into the bin. I laid everything out with that flat, clinical care you see in a good salon when the colourist lines up foils. You’re not playing at precision: you’re committing to it.

Ledger. Invoices. Swatches of linen and wool pinned with little flags. A cheap calculator with worn buttons that had seen too many rushed additions. A laptop that had survived coffee, fabric dust, and at least one rage-tap hard enough to make the screen stutter. A stack of envelopes that weren’t romantic in any way whatsoever.

It was an operating surface.

Wardrobe wasn’t dying: it was thriving. That was the danger. Thriving meant multiplying. Multiplying meant mistakes. Mistakes meant the whole thing could get infected by sloppiness and sympathy and “we’ll fix it later” thinking. And I did not build this place — Mara did not build this place — so it could be ruined by the soft, lazy part of human nature.

I drew a line down a blank page and wrote:

JAN — COSTS / INCOME / WASTE

Then another line. Then another. It was reassuring, in the way locking a door is reassuring.

The first invoice I opened was for thread: ten spools, quality, not cheap. The second was for grommets. The third was for a bolt of linen that arrived like a dare.

My phone vibrated. A message from Lauren.

How are the numbers?

I stared at it, thumb hovering, and realised I’d been waiting for that question the way you wait for someone to come stand beside you at a lookout so you can stop pretending you’re fine with the height by yourself.

Doing them now, I typed. Then — without thinking — added: We’re doing them now.

I didn’t even notice the word until it was sent.

We.

As if it was already decided. As if routing someone into “we” was as simple as changing a pronoun in a text. I set the phone down face-up, daring the universe to correct me.

Footsteps in the hallway. Soft, familiar. Not Mara: Mara’s steps had a blunt certainty, like she was driving nails into the floorboards simply by walking. These were lighter, careful. Someone who’d learned to move quietly in other people’s spaces. Charlie paused at the doorway like he was checking whether his presence would be tolerated by the air itself. He didn’t look like he belonged at an operating surface. He looked like someone you’d find in the margins of a library: delicate, intent, carrying too much thought in too thin a frame. His hair was still damp at the edges as if he’d run water over it and then forgotten to finish the job. There was a faint smear of pencil on his thumb.

He saw the table and went still.

“Is this... for me?” he asked, voiced carefully, trying to sound casual while bracing for impact.

“For us.”

Not loudly or pointedly: just like it was the only accurate word available. His eyes flicked up. He processed the word the way you process a new weight in your hands: is it mine to carry? am I allowed? I tapped the chair opposite mine with two fingers.

“Sit.”

He sat.

No debate. That was the thing about Charlie: he tried to be brave, but he was even better at being compliant when the rules were clear. I slid the ledger toward him, open to the column headings Mara had ruled with that severe, beautiful logic of hers.

“Read it.”

His eyes moved down the page. Movement. Stress. Failure points. Fix applied. Re-test. Signed.

“This is... intense.”

“Actually, it’s boring,” I corrected. “That’s why it works.”

He let out a faint breath that might have been a laugh if he’d trusted himself to make it. I pointed at the laptop.

“Open the spreadsheet. Tab marked January.”

He reached for it. Fingers quick, sure. He wasn’t a finance person — he didn’t have the swagger of someone who’d been told numbers were his territory — but he had the kind of competence that comes from caring. The kind that doesn’t seek applause. I watched him centre the window, bring up the sheet, settle.

“Now,” I said, flipping an invoice so it faced him, “tell me what you see.”

He leaned forward; a strand of hair fell across his forehead. He didn’t push it back.

“I see thread,” he said. “And... we’re paying more than last month.”

“Yes, we are.”

He glanced up again, and something in him adjusted at my emphasis. We.

This is about us. We... do this. Not you. Not I’ll cover it. Not I’ll handle it like a man. Just... we.

He looked back down.

“The output’s higher too,” he said slowly. “If we’re making more garments, the thread cost scales. The question is whether waste is scaling with it.”

I nodded.

“Exactly.”

The word landed like a seal of approval. He went quieter after that, as if he’d been given permission to do what he was already good at: focus without posturing. He pulled the calculator closer, checked a couple of sums, then typed the numbers into the sheet with a neat, almost reverent care. Silence formed, not empty but weighted. Useful.

And in that silence, watching him work, the whole quiet head-down, bum-up posture he assumed with the task, something in my nervous system unclenched. Like a nerve in your back the masseuse unknots. That breath out.

Relief.

Instinctively, the whole thing felt ridiculous. I didn’t need taking care of, not by anyone. I certainly didn’t need a man! I didn’t need anybody to rescue me from... my own competence. I got this... I'm good!

And yet — having him here, on the other side of the table, quietly, steadily — made something stop tilting I didn't realise was tilting.

I watched him work and realised, with a bizarre yet needle sharp clarity, that I was starting to depend on him. It was not a romantic thing. It was structural. The way you depend on a beam once you’ve built the roof.

Who in the world does that sort of thing? Just, quietly doing their job, not expecting any recognition? Who doesn’t seem to flinch if something else gets dumped on their plate? And that was the moment the thought figurative had me sit up staight in my chair. It was undeniable, clean as a a fresh scalpel... one word:

Wife.

He’s my wife.

There was instant dissonance. The term was so gendered... it didn't fit at all. I tried to come up with one like it, one that would easily encompass the roles he’d accepted.

Nothing else fit. He was a wife, not in clothes, not in pronouns... but in function. Labour that never gets or expects a spotlight. Support: the kind of support that keeps the whole place standing while most people only notice the roof.

The part that made my stomach go tight wasn’t the thought itself. It was that I dreaded the thought of losing it.

Which meant, sooner or later, I would have to define it: to myself, to Charlie, to Wardrobe... to everyone. Spell it out. Clearly. As a boundary.

Charlie’s my wife.

The sheer enormity of what I was having to propose did give me pause. I felt there just had to be a different, a more socially acceptable, less stigmatising way to see the role, to define his role. I went over them again. What else fits? What else can we call this?

There was nothing else.

Wife. That’s it. And it solved some looming problems, that title, that definition. I could already see the old story trying to crawl back whenever he saw a need, that provider story... like a reflex, like a well-rehearsed script. I could see it in the way his shoulders tensed whenever he found a cost spike, as if it was asking him to personally resolve it.

The provider fantasy wasn’t dead. Just dormant.

He finished entering the thread invoice and looked up.

“Do you want me to... pay this one?”

There it was.

He didn’t shout the words. He didn’t ask arrogantly. But there: in that tiny offering dressed up as practical help, was an old expectation rearing its head.

My first instinct was to snap, because snapping is easy and I am very good at easy when I’m tired.

But I didn’t.

I looked at him for a long moment instead — long enough that he started to fidget. He noticed he was fidgeting and so stopped himself, but then started fidgeted again. He was trying so hard. Which mattered too.

“No,” I said finally. Calm. Final. I wanted to be kind. “Look, you don’t get to buy your place here.”

His eyes widened a fraction. I calmly held his gaze and softened the next sentence without weakening it.

“You don’t have to earn us,” I said. “You just have to work with us.”

Work with us.

Not for me. Not as my man. Not as a hero.

With.

The tension in his shoulders didn’t vanish, but it changed into something else — the difference between bracing for a punch and bracing to lift a weight.

He swallowed.

“Okay,” he said. Quiet. Real. “Okay.”

Hhe looked back down as if grateful to be given something concrete to do.

“Then... tell me what you want me to look at next.”

I didn’t miss the phrasing.

Not what should I do. Not how can I help. Not I’ll take care of it.

Just: tell me.

I slid the next envelope across.

“Waste,” I said. “Find me waste.”

He nodded.

As he bent over the numbers again, pencil tapping once against the table, it hit me with the kind of certainty you can’t argue with: this was exactly the right time to test the water.

Not with clothes. Not with pronouns — not yet.

With structure.

With language.

With we.

Because the safest, sanest way to change someone’s life isn’t to push them off a cliff. It’s to build a room around them so gradually they stop remembering what it felt like to stand outside.

I picked up my phone and typed to Lauren before I could overthink it:

Numbers are stabilising. Waste is the target. We’ve got Charlie on it.

Then I added, almost without meaning to:

He’s good. Keep him close.

Chosen-ness, delivered like a logistical note. No theatre. No confession. Just a preference stated as fact.

I sent it.

Across the table, Charlie looked up as if he’d felt the air shift, then caught my eye. He didn’t smile. He didn’t try to charm. He just waited — present, attentive, ready.

And I realised something else, unexpectedly sharp: I didn’t need him to be a man. I didn’t even need him to be anything at all, yet. I needed him to stay exactly like this — quiet, steady, inside the “we” — long enough for the old story in him to finally starve.

“Good,” I said once, when he circled a waste line item and drew a neat arrow.

He blinked at the word like it warmed him. Then he went back to work.

And the operating surface — ledger, invoices, swatches, calculator, laptop — felt less like a crisis.

More like a plan.

“Side-back,” I confirmed. “Under movement.”

He nodded and reached for the pencil.

That’s how he’d been since the definitions conversation: less boyish hope, more procedural calm. When he got uncertain, he didn’t perform. He asked. When he felt the old story twitch—let me pay, let me prove—he swallowed it and returned to the work.

He was learning to belong through accuracy.

And accuracy, at Wardrobe, is a form of obedience.

My phone buzzed once. Lauren.

How’s Charlie going?

I stared at it longer than the question deserved. Not because I didn’t know how he was going—I did—but because I could feel the mother behind the words. Tell me he’s okay. Tell me he’s still mine. Tell me the room isn’t changing him faster than I can keep up.

He’s steady, I typed. He’s working. Waste is improving.

Then, without thinking, because my mind had started doing it the way the room had started doing it, my thumb added a letter.

Charli’s steady.

I saw it only after it landed.

An i. A stupid little i. No ceremony. No permission slip. Just the spelling that matched what I’d been watching for days: the way he’d stopped puffing and started holding. The way the provider reflex had begun to starve.

I didn’t delete it.

I sent it.

There are moments where you can feel a boundary being crossed, not loudly, but cleanly—like a knife going through paper.

Lauren’s reply came almost immediately.

…Charli?

A mother’s pause, compressed into punctuation.

I didn’t soften. I didn’t reassure. Reassurance would have turned this into feelings. Wardrobe doesn’t run on feelings.

He hasn’t asked for it, I typed. It’s just what we’re using in-house. He’s calmer. It’s working.

I stared at my own words for a beat.

He. Still he.

Because Wardrobe moves in layers. It doesn’t leap. It installs.

Across the table, Charlie kept working. He hadn’t heard my phone. Or he’d heard it and chosen not to chase it. Either way—good.

Sarah drifted past the doorway with a bundle of blue aprons folded over her arms. She paused long enough to clock the petticoat bag, the ledger, Charlie’s posture.

“Numbers again,” she said, voice dry. “You’re going to turn him into a proper housewife at this rate, Celeste.”

Charlie’s pencil stopped.

Not because of the word. Because Sarah had entered the room. He did that with everyone now: a micro-pause, a check for instruction, then return to task.

I looked up, unimpressed.

“Don’t,” I said. Flat.

Sarah’s mouth twitched, unbothered.

“Fine. Assistant accountant,” she amended, and then—like it slipped out because the shape of him had changed in her head—she added, “Charli.”

Charlie looked up at that. A blink. A tiny recalculation.

He didn’t correct her. He didn’t blush. He didn’t reach for a joke.

He simply said, “I’m finding the same waste points repeating.”

Sarah lifted one brow.

“Of course you are,” she said, as if this was deeply irritating. Then she left, because that’s what Sarah did when she approved: she refused to linger and turn approval into a scene.

The room stayed quiet again.

Charlie went back to the seam as if nothing had happened. Not because he was oblivious—because he’d learned the Wardrobe trick: don’t grab language like it’s a prize.

I slid the petticoat out of the bag and laid it flat.

“Movement sequence,” I said.

He stood without hesitation. Took the fabric without self-consciousness. A week ago he would have worried about what he looked like holding a women’s garment. Now he held it because it was his job to.

Step. Pivot. Tension. Release.

His eyes tracked the seam, not his hands.

My phone buzzed again.

Lauren.

He’s okay with you calling him that?

There it was. The conflict, properly stated. Not “what are you doing to my son.” Not drama. A question about consent and safety.

I answered honestly.

He didn’t react. He’s more accepted, not less.

Then I added what she actually needed, because Lauren doesn’t need soothing—she needs structure.

It’s not a costume. It’s a work-name. It’s about the attitude shift you wanted.

A longer pause this time.

Then one word came back.

Okay.

Not enthusiastic. Not celebratory. Maternal steel choosing the safest system available.

I put the phone down and watched Charlie do the pivot again.

It was, in fact, a nothing-burger to him. He wasn’t glowing. He wasn’t crumbling. He was just… inside the job, where he was allowed to be useful without performing masculinity.

That was the win.

A knock sounded at the doorway.

Mara appeared without apology. She didn’t look at Charlie first. She looked at the table: layout, posture, the way the room held itself. Mara didn’t care what anyone called themselves until she knew whether the work held.

“Numbers?” she asked.

“Stable,” I said. “Waste is improving. He’s flagging anomalies.”

Charlie didn’t sit up straighter to impress her. He didn’t shrink either.

“I’ve marked three recurring points,” he said simply. “Over-ordering interfacing. Miscut allowance on blue aprons. Time waste on re-threading.”

Mara’s gaze flicked to the ledger entry. Then to the petticoat. Then, finally, to him.

A long beat.

Approval, the only kind Mara gave: the kind that acted.

“Pen,” she said.

Charlie handed it over without hesitation.

Mara opened the ledger to the inside cover where the rules lived—those short, brutal sentences that made Wardrobe survivable—and wrote in her angular hand:

WORKROOM NAME: CHARLI
ROLE: WEAR-TESTER / LEDGER ASSIST
REQUIREMENT: ASK DEFINITIONS. NO THEATRE. HOLD LOAD.

She underlined the first line once.

Then she slid the ledger back across the table like it was a tool being returned to its place.

“That,” Mara said, voice level, “is what we use.”

Ratified.

No applause. No pep talk.

Just ink.

Charlie glanced at the new line, then back at the petticoat.

He didn’t look thrilled.

He didn’t look horrified.

He looked… relieved, in the way a person looks when a room stops wobbling.

“Okay,” he said, and it sounded like he was accepting a measurement.

Mara was already turning away.

“Re-test schedule,” she said to me. “Green petticoats. Seam stress shifting.”

“Put it in,” I said.

Charlie reached for the pen again and opened to the correct page. He paused, looked at me—not for permission to matter, but for confirmation of process.

I nodded once.

He wrote the entry cleanly and left the signature spaces.

Mara’s gaze flicked to the blank lines.

“Not yet,” she said, as if reading his mind. “But you will.”

Then she was gone.

The room changed by half a degree—quieter, but not emptier. Like the air had been tested and found capable of holding.

That was when Pauline arrived.

Soft steps. Sensible shoes. Fabric whisper. A folded bundle under one arm. Hair pinned back, cap neat, expression serene until it wasn’t.

She placed the bundle down and looked at the table: ledger open, tools aligned, petticoat under test. She read the room with the same certainty Mara did, only with less bluntness and more intimacy.

“Bonjour,” she said, because she always did. Not a greeting so much as a reminder: the standard exists.

Charlie answered automatically—still “he” in his own head, still the old reflex in his voice.

“Hello, Pauline.”

Pauline’s gaze went to the ledger, and her eyes caught the new line on the inside cover.

WORKROOM NAME: CHARLI.

Nothing in her face changed, but something in her attention did. Like a decision had been made.

She stepped closer and took the petticoat gently, shook it out once, and demonstrated the step-pivot again.

“Look,” she said. “Here.”

She held the fabric where the seam tightened.

“Here,” she repeated, and the word that followed was so effortless it might have been cruel if it wasn’t so plainly functional.

“—she pulls when she steps.”

She.

The room didn’t flinch. The room didn’t correct her. The room accepted it as naturally as steam in the air.

Charlie froze.

Not offended. Not collapsing. Not making a scene.

Just—still, like someone hearing a new rule land.

Pauline did not apologise. Pauline did not make it about his feelings. That was her welcome: you don’t get to be fragile about this; you get to be held to standard like everyone else.

She adjusted his grip with a light, competent touch.

“No, like this,” she said. “So she can move and you can see.”

Charlie’s throat moved—one swallow, contained.

He looked at the seam, not at Pauline.

Then he did the brave thing: he kept working.

“Okay,” he said, quiet. “Okay.”

Pauline nodded once, satisfied.

She turned slightly, addressing me, but the decision was for the room.

“In Wardrobe,” she said, fluent, calm, absolute, “she is one of us. So I say she. Because she fits.”

It wasn’t political.

It was operational.

It was Pauline’s version of a signature.

Charlie blinked again, cheeks warm, and then something in his face loosened—tiny, private relief.

Not because he’d been feminised.

Because he’d been accepted without ridicule.

He exhaled once and, very softly, as if testing the word for its weight rather than its meaning, he said:

“She.”

Pauline didn’t praise him. She didn’t soften. She simply pointed to the seam.

“Now write,” she said. “Failure point. Fix. Re-test. So Mara sees.”

Charlie nodded.

He picked up the pen and wrote.

And by the time his hand stopped, the room had already moved on—as Wardrobe always did—to the only thing that ever mattered here:

Correctness.

And Charlie—Charli—sat inside the “we” like it had been waiting for him all along.
