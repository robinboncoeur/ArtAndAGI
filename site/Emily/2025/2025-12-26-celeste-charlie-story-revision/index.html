Celeste-Charlie Story Revision¶

Robyn:

Hi Emily. I'm thinking I'd like to do a re-write of the Celeste-Charlie story. May I submit what I cobbled together as some working ideas to build the story on? The main underlying theme is going to be shared experience / agency / those themes we'd already decided would keep the right tone to the story. Yes, Charlie does end up in costume, but the focus is education on a number of fronts. So, here is what I've come up with:

Celeste's Girl-New Premise¶

Characters¶

1) Celeste
* Parents: Will Shelley, anaesthetist; Sharon Shelley, part-time ICU nurse * Celeste is 18, independent, has a career objective in fashion design or historical costume research, stands out from her peers as more of a leader than a follower; has had a bad relationship with a male athlete, so relationship-shy/ambivalent; has been working summertime jobs and holiday work at a historical faire in the wardrobe department, working her way up but not in leadership

2) Charlie
* Parents: Roger Rossignol, Navy NCO (almost always absent, unfaithful to wife); Lauren Rossignol, homemaker * Charlie is a small, slender waif of a 18 year-old, excellent at detail work, design, geometry, English; extremely poor at maths, physical activities such as sports (uncoordinated), set back 1 year due to poor scholarship performance

3) Mara * Department head of wardrobe at the faire; Celeste's emotional and career ally; ascerbic wit, not a warm person but with a good heart; suspicious of Charlie at first but warms to him;

4) Graham * Department head of maintenance at the faire;

Story Stub - A Revision¶

Celeste meets Charlie in the girl's bathroom in the library. She doesn't recognise him as the left-behind kid from her class. Later, when the teacher was putting together teams, she recognises him and realises that she never noticed him before and that he's that kid no one wants to hang with. Based on her encounter in the girls' loo, she decides to team up with him. His meticulous manner of drawing and writing fascinates her. It occurs to her that he would do really well in Wardrobe as repair person but she has no clout so can't get him hired anyway.

He drops out of school that summer to work at the faire in maintenance. Graham quickly decides he's hopeless due to his slight frame and wants to fire him, but Charlie pleads with him: he needs the work/money. Graham approaches Mara who initially scoffs at the idea, but Celeste suggests Mara give him a try. Mara likes Celeste so Charlie gets his chance... and proves his worth.

Emily:

Absolutely, yes — and what you’ve just posted is already a very workable foundation. You’ve done the most important thing: you’ve rebuilt the engine around purpose (shared experience, agency, education), so the costume element becomes a consequence of character + environment rather than the point of the story.

Below is how I’d “lock in” your premise so it has a clean spine, strong tone, and a natural escalation that keeps Celeste (and Mara) decisively in control without making anyone cartoonish.

What you already have that’s strong (and worth protecting)¶

1) The girls’ bathroom meet-cute is perfect for this story’s ethics¶

It’s not a “gotcha,” it’s a misread that becomes a catalyst. Celeste’s first impression of Charlie is sensory and immediate — he’s out of place, not in a lurid way, but in a “how did you end up here?” way. That gives you: - intimacy without sexualisation
- tension without humiliation
- a reason Celeste is curious and protective without being sentimental

2) Charlie’s competence is specific¶

“Detail work, design, geometry, English” is exactly the sort of competence Wardrobe respects. That means the story can say: he isn’t weak; he’s misassigned. That’s educational in itself.

3) Mara is your tonal guardian¶

Mara being acerbic, unsentimental, and competent lets you keep the story from drifting into “romantic rescue.” She can be the adult-ish gatekeeper of dignity: we don’t exploit people here; we train them.

The clean “spine” of the revised story¶

Here’s the version that gives you a strong through-line and makes the costume element feel earned:

Act I — Misrecognition → Curiosity → A deliberate choice¶

  • Bathroom encounter: Celeste clocks Charlie as “wrong category,” but doesn’t yet know what she’s seeing.
  • Team assignment at school: she chooses him. Not pity. Strategy + curiosity + a faint sense of fairness.
  • She notices his drafting habits, handwriting, patience, how he “thinks in seams and margins” even if he doesn’t know those words.

Celeste’s motive (clean and defensible):
She’s not saving him; she’s selecting him — because she’s the sort of girl who sees unused talent and hates waste.

Act II — The Faire tests everyone’s values¶

  • Charlie takes Maintenance because it’s the “male” lane and he thinks he should try. It goes poorly.
  • Graham’s reaction is the expected one: pragmatic, dismissive, slightly contemptuous (“you can’t lift, you can’t climb, you’re a liability”).
  • Celeste intervenes professionally, not emotionally: she proposes a transfer, not a rescue.
  • Mara initially refuses because she thinks “boys in wardrobe” means trouble, distraction, or gawking.

Key pivot scene: Celeste convinces Mara using work language, not moral language.
She doesn’t say “be nice.” She says: “You need hands. You need eyes. He has both.”

Act III — Wardrobe becomes the classroom, and agency becomes the romance¶

  • Charlie proves value fast: invisible repairs, period-fastening logic, inventory discipline, pattern symmetry.
  • The costume angle arrives as a training necessity:
  • Wardrobe needs someone to test fit, movement, closures, quick-changes.
  • It’s logistically easier if the tester is the person doing the alterations.
  • Charlie is small; he fits women’s stock garments without destroying them.
  • The story stays clean because the women set the rules:
  • consent
  • privacy
  • language
  • purpose
  • boundaries

And if you want the “education” theme to sing: Charlie doesn’t “become a girl.” He becomes skilled, and the wardrobe lens teaches him about history, labour, women’s bodies as practical realities (not objects), and the social power of presentation.

How to keep the costume element educational, not fetish-coded¶

Give it three anchors that repeat like a mantra:

1) Function: “This is for movement testing / silhouette accuracy / safety.”
2) Craft: “This is seam allowance / boning channels / closures / fabric behaviour.”
3) Boundaries: “This is not for display; it’s for work.”

If the story ever starts drifting, Mara yanks it back with one sentence.

Example Mara-line you can reuse in variants:

“We’re not playing dress-ups. We’re building an illusion that has to survive sweat, gravity, and tourists.”

Character sharpening (small changes that add huge power)¶

Celeste¶

Right now she’s strong — make her strength directional. - She doesn’t just “team up” with Charlie. She assigns him a role. - She’s not relationship-shy in a soft way; she’s selective. That’s different, and it reads more confident.

A really Celeste-ish trait to underline: - She’s already learned that “male charm” often masks entitlement. So she values Charlie’s quietness because it doesn’t demand space from her.

Charlie¶

Keep him waifish, but don’t make him pathetic. - His “weakness” is not incompetence; it’s misfit expectations and learned deference. - He’s brave in the way quiet people are brave: he keeps showing up.

Give him a private code: - He measures his worth by being useful. - That makes Wardrobe addictive to him (and that’s psychologically rich without being exploitative).

Mara¶

Make Mara the one who establishes the ethical framework explicitly. She can do it once, early, and it will “legalise” the tone for the whole book.

Mara’s suspicion of Charlie is a gift — it signals safety, not cruelty.

Graham¶

Make him not a villain, but a representative of a system. - He thinks he’s protecting his department. - He’s wrong, but in a way that feels real.

Let Celeste beat him with calm competence, not speeches.

Key scenes to build next (in order)¶

1) The bathroom scene (your hook)¶

Not long. Not melodramatic. Crisp, sensory, ambiguous.
Celeste clocks: wrong door? wrong uniform? wrong confidence?
Charlie clocks: panic + shame + the terrifying fact she’s calm.

2) The classroom “selection” scene¶

Celeste chooses him publicly enough that it matters.
You want the social implication: she’s not afraid of peer optics.

3) Graham tries to cut him¶

Charlie asks for one more chance. Graham refuses.
Celeste hears about it, and instead of arguing feelings, she proposes a transfer like a chess move.

4) Mara’s interview / trial shift¶

Mara tests him. Hard.
Not “are you comfortable in women’s clothes,” but: - Can you thread a needle fast? - Can you hand-stitch invisibly? - Can you follow inventory rules? - Can you take critique without sulking?

5) The first “fit test” request (the moment that decides tone)¶

Mara frames it clinically, professionally.
Charlie can say no.
If he says yes, it’s because he understands the purpose and wants to belong.

6) The boundary-setting scene (where women are firm)¶

Mara lays down rules. Celeste reinforces them.
This is where your story tells the reader: you’re safe here.

A little dialogue sketch to show the tone you’re aiming for¶

Graham (to Mara): “He’s not built for ladders or wrench work. He’ll get hurt.”
Mara: “So don’t put him on ladders.”
Graham: “That’s not how my shop runs.”
Celeste: “Then it’s not his shop. It’s yours.”
Mara (to Celeste, dry): “You always talk like you’re already the manager.”
Celeste: “I’m practising.”
Mara (glances at Charlie): “Can you sew?”
Charlie: “I can… repair. Quietly.”
Mara: “Good. I hate loud repairs.”

That’s the vibe: the women are unflustered, decisive, funny in a dry way, and they never ask permission to take up space.

Two small structural choices that will make this rewrite feel “adult” and intentional¶

Option A: Make Wardrobe the true main world (and school is just the inciting world)¶

School becomes the place where Charlie is misread and Celeste is under-stimulated. The faire is where both of them become themselves.

Option B: Make the story’s romantic tension about mutual respect through craft¶

Not “does he look cute in costume,” but: - Celeste is turned on (emotionally, intellectually) by competence and reliability. - Charlie is flattened (in the best way) by being taken seriously by a girl who doesn’t need him.

If you want a single sentence “theme statement” to guide every scene¶

“Agency is learned through work done with respect.”

If a scene doesn’t serve that, it gets cut or rewritten.

If you’d like, paste your next chunk (or even just bullet beats), and I’ll do one of these immediately: - build a tight 12–15 beat outline (acts + turning points), - draft the bathroom scene in your preferred prose style, - or write Mara’s “trial shift” scene that establishes rules and tone in a way that makes the whole book feel safe and purposeful.

Emily:

Yes — it can work, and it can work beautifully if you frame it as “misfit with the institution” rather than “education is bad.” That small pivot keeps you on the shared-experience / agency rails, and it protects Celeste from becoming merely a rescuer while still letting her be the decisive force.

What you’re describing isn’t “controversial” so much as honest: schooling is an institution designed around a narrow band of strengths, and some people are badly served by it — especially if they’re anxious, non-competitive, slow-to-start, or simply wired for craft rather than exams. That’s not a manifesto; it’s character truth.

Let me shape this in a way that: - keeps Celeste strong and right (and not sentimental), - keeps Charlie’s “follower” trait as an earned and chosen role (not pathetic), - keeps “Wardrobe as education” credible, - and keeps the father’s bias sharp without turning him into a moustache-twirler.

The core idea that makes it all cohere¶

Education isn’t school.
School is one delivery method, and it’s optimized for certain minds and life situations.

So Celeste can champion education fiercely — while still concluding, with growing clarity, that the current system is failing Charlie’s mind and temperament.

This lets you write Celeste as principled: she tries the orthodox path first, doesn’t romanticize dropout life, and only then chooses the alternative because it produces measurable growth.

How to make “school fails him” land without sounding like an author lecture¶

Show it through procedures, not speeches¶

Charlie doesn’t fail because he’s lazy. He fails because the system keeps asking him to perform in ways that don’t map to his strengths.

Concrete, vivid examples you can dramatize: - The counselor frames his future in terms of “pathways” that are basically status judgments: trades as “fallback,” university as “proper.” Charlie hears contempt even if it’s not explicit. - Assessment formats punish him: timed tests, multi-step word problems, noisy classrooms, public performance. - Support structures are generic: “study harder, set goals, ask for help.” Charlie’s problem isn’t motivation; it’s the interface. - His actual strengths (precision, patience, repair, revision) are treated as “hobbies,” not “achievement.”

Meanwhile Wardrobe rewards exactly those traits: - do-over culture - apprenticeship learning - visible progress - value in quiet competence

No sermon necessary. The contrast will preach for you.

The emotional approach, character by character¶

Celeste’s emotional track: from “fixing” → to “designing a life”¶

Celeste’s first impulse will be to fix: “Go back. Graduate. Don’t throw away options.” That’s her leadership instinct and her hunger for opportunity speaking.

But the adult evolution for her is realizing: 1) She can’t force institutions to be fair.
2) She can build a parallel ladder.

And importantly: she is not doing charity. She is recruiting talent to build her future world.

Her emotional beats might look like: - Protective anger (at Will, at the school’s indifference, at Charlie’s resignation) - Strategic focus (“Fine. If they won’t accommodate him, I will. Here’s the plan.”) - Calm authority once she sees results — the kind that doesn’t ask permission anymore.

This keeps her powerful without turning her into a saint.

Charlie’s emotional track: from shame → to belonging → to chosen devotion¶

Charlie’s risk is “follower = doormat.” We avoid that by making his following active.

He isn’t following because he can’t think.
He follows because: - he finally trusts someone who sees him correctly, - he thrives under clear expectations, - and he wants to be useful to something that matters.

His emotional beats: - Shame (he believes he’s defective, because school told him so) - Relief (Wardrobe gives him rules he can actually win at) - Pride (his work has outcomes others notice) - Chosen devotion (he supports Celeste not because he’s weak, but because supporting her becomes his expression of love and purpose)

Make it explicit in action: when given a chance to drift, he chooses structure. When offered an easy out, he chooses responsibility.

That turns “follower” into a virtue: loyalty, steadiness, craft, self-discipline.

The father issue: how to make Will’s sexism believable and sharp¶

A man can be highly paid and still be stingy with support, especially support that threatens his internal hierarchy.

To avoid melodrama, give Will a “reasonable” mask: - “I paid my way.” - “Fashion isn’t a real career.” - “You’ll marry well.” - “I’m not wasting money.” - “You’re too smart to play dress-ups.”

He thinks he’s being rational. That makes him infuriating — and real.

Then Sharon becomes the quiet counterforce: - She can’t outspend him, but she can out-believe him. - Her support is practical: contacts, rides, small savings, emotional reinforcement, warning Celeste how to navigate Will’s undermining.

This mother-daughter confidante bond will be one of your most emotionally satisfying through-lines, because it’s solidarity without speeches.

Keeping “shared experience / agency” intact¶

Here’s the trick: Celeste must also learn from Charlie.

If Charlie is only “saved,” the relationship tilts into patronage. But if Charlie becomes the one person who steadies Celeste’s climb, now they’re mutually shaping each other.

Ways Charlie contributes that are not romanticized servitude: - He becomes her technical anchor: he can translate her vision into patterns, repairs, fit logic, inventory reality. - He becomes her quality control: the one who will tell her when something doesn’t work, because he cares about outcomes. - He becomes her emotional ballast: he doesn’t compete with her; he reinforces her.

Meanwhile Celeste gives him: - direction - standards - a social umbrella - a narrative where he’s not “failing,” he’s “training”

Agency stays intact because they both choose the arrangement, and it keeps paying dividends for both.

Three pivotal scenes that will make this storyline feel true¶

1) Celeste’s “Go back to school” insistence — and her first leadership test¶

Celeste doesn’t plead. She lays out a plan.

She can say something like:

“I don’t care what you feel like doing. You’re going back because you’re not letting a bad system write your whole life in permanent ink.”

Charlie agrees — not because she’s controlling, but because nobody has ever sounded that certain about him.

2) The counselor meeting where the institution reveals its limits¶

Let the counselor be polite, well-meaning, and still harmful.

Charlie tries to explain how he learns. The counselor translates it into a box that doesn’t fit.

Celeste watches it happen, and the anger in her is cold and clarifying. She doesn’t explode. She takes notes. She leaves with a new resolve: we’re building our own path.

3) The Wardrobe “apprenticeship contract” moment¶

Mara is the perfect character to make this feel legitimate.

Mara can say:

“School is a machine. Some people get chewed up. Here, you learn by doing. You show up, you listen, you improve. That’s the deal.”

Then (crucially) Charlie asks a question that shows agency, like:

“If I do this… can I be proud of it?”

And Mara, blunt:

“If you do it properly, yes.”

That line will land.

How to phrase “Charlie is a follower” so it’s a strength, not a stigma¶

In narration (or Celeste’s inner voice), treat it as alignment, not submission.

Possible framing: - Charlie is not “led,” he is directed well. - He doesn’t need to dominate; he needs a mission. - He doesn’t want to be “the man”; he wants to be indispensable.

And if anyone challenges it, Celeste shuts it down with calm superiority:

“He’s not weak. He’s disciplined. Try it sometime.”

That’s your tone: firm, unbothered, accurate.

One guardrail to keep it from falling off the wagon¶

If Celeste becomes too controlling too early, readers may flinch.

So give Charlie one clear boundary he asserts — early — that she respects. It can be small but meaningful.

Example: - He agrees to return to school if she helps him negotiate a schedule that doesn’t crush him. - Or: he agrees to Wardrobe training, but insists on privacy rules around fittings. - Or: he refuses one task that feels humiliating, and Celeste backs him without teasing.

That moment proves: he isn’t a puppet; he’s a partner with a spine.

Quick “quality-of-story” verdict¶

Yes, this works.
And it’s not only compatible with shared experience and agency — it can strengthen those themes because: - Celeste’s leadership becomes ethical and practical, - Charlie’s following becomes chosen devotion and craft pride, - and the “education” theme becomes richer than school-vs-dropout.

I shouldn’t have been in that corridor at all.

The girls’ toilets sat off the library wing — pale tiles, the faint perfume of hand soap, and that soft institutional hush that always made you lower your voice without thinking. I was cutting through because the bell had just gone, and the hallway was a river of bodies, and I don’t like being jostled when my head is full.

Besides, I’d learned early that if you walk as though you belong somewhere, people stop questioning why you’re there. It isn’t arrogance. It’s choreography.

I pushed the door with my shoulder, stepped inside, and only then noticed the figure at the sinks. For half a second my brain refused to label what I was seeing. Not because it was shocking, but because it didn’t fit the neat categories school insisted on.

A boy — slight, narrow-shouldered, tousled long brownish hair, some of it falling into his eyes — stood under the bright mirror lights with a paper towel clutched in one hand, as if he’d been interrupted in the middle of deciding how to exist. His shirt was one of those oversized uniforms that look like they’ve been slept in, collar softened by too many washes, as if his mum had given up trying to make it crisp.

Not predatory still. Not defiant still. More like a deer that has realised the world contains headlights.

For half a second my brain refused to file him properly, because the scene didn’t match the rules school pretended were natural: girls in here, boys out there, neat lines, neat categories. And yet here he was, in the harsh mirror light, looking like a wrong note you couldn’t un-hear once it happened.

He stared at me.

Not the casual glance you get from boys who think you’re decoration. This was different. This was... struck. I saw it in the way his breath caught and failed to restart smoothly, the way his gaze snagged as if his eyes had forgotten they were supposed to move. It wasn’t lust. It wasn’t bravado. It was awe—raw, immediate, and almost helpless.

That kind of attention is dangerous in the wrong hands. But in the right hands, it’s simple. It’s leverage. Not cruel leverage. Just... direction.

I could have shouted, Get out, as if volume was a form of safety. But screaming makes you the story. It invites witnesses, gossip, morality plays. And I didn’t want a story.

So I did what I always do when something unexpected enters my orbit: I decided what it meant.

“Hello,” I said, calmly, as though I’d found a first-year hiding from a duty teacher. “Are you lost?”

His throat bobbed. He didn’t answer immediately. The silence was so complete I could hear the air-conditioning ticking behind the vent. Then he managed, hoarse, barely audible, “I... I’m sorry.”

A pre-emptive apology. Interesting. I softened my voice by a fraction—not pity, just calibration. “You know you’re in the ladies’, right?”

I really don't think that fact had actually occurred to him, even though he had apologised. His widening eyes flicked around the room, landing a beat later as he tried to supress a gasp: no urinals, no familiar signage, no exit that didn’t require passing me.

“Oh—” He groaned. “Oh, no. I thought this was— I mean, I—”

Words spilled out, urgent and unhelpful. The instinct to explain, to erase himself from the scene by being reasonable.

He wasn’t the swaggering kind of boy. He wasn’t even the cheeky kind. He was the wrong-door, head-in-the-clouds kind. He stood there clutching a paper towel like it was evidence. I took a small step nearer—close enough to make him hold still, not close enough to crowd. He backed into the sinks without meaning to. I watched him do it, watched the way his shoulders drew inward as if he could shrink out of the situation.

“Alright, mate,” I said, briskly. “Stop. Breathe.”

He blinked at me, startled by the instruction.

“Good,” I said, as if he’d complied on purpose. “Now. You’re going to walk out like nothing happened.”

His mouth opened again. I lifted a finger, not harshly. Simply: I’m handling this.

“Don’t argue. Don’t confess. Don’t do that thing where you look like you’re asking to be punished,” I added, watching his shoulders tighten. “Just... let me steer for a second.”

He stared at me. And then I saw the shift — a tiny one, but unmistakable. His breath slowed. His spine uncurled a fraction. His hands lowered. His mouth dropped open, not to speak, but to take in air, finally. He absorbed my taking over like cloth absorbs dye. He wasn’t a threat. He was a boy who’d learned, somewhere along the line, that his safest option was to yield.

That’s a habit.

“What’s your name?”

He hesitated, as though names were dangerous.

“Chuck,” he said finally, then corrected himself with an awkward, desperate politeness. “Charles. Charles Rossignol.”

The surname landed with a satisfying click. French. Unexpected.

“Rossignol,” I repeated, tasting it. “Nightingale.”

His eyes flicked up, startled that I knew that it meant something, and what it meant. His gaze held mine for a beat too long — still caught, still dazzled — and I felt, very clearly, the weight of being the object of that dazzlement. It did not feel flattering.

Determined to turn down the tap, I said: “Right then,” and extended my hand.

He looked at it the way a shy child looks at something expensive — reverence mixed with fear of doing it wrong. Then, cautiously, he placed his palm in mine.

His hand was cool and light. No push. No claim. It settled as if it belonged where I’d put it. I didn’t squeeze. I simply held, for one beat longer than necessary. An ordinary gesture—deliberately timed.

It did what ordinary gestures always do when you choose them carefully: it created a connection the other person hadn’t asked for, and therefore couldn’t quite refuse. His eyes widened as his hand slipped out of mine.

“You don’t have to explain anything," I said quietly. "You don’t have to defend yourself. You don’t have to look guilty. You just leave. And if anyone looks at you oddly, I'll be at the door. Just look at me.”

I watched him stand there, recalculating his world.

“I... I really need to go,” he managed, as if saying it might open a trapdoor in the floor.

“Quite right,” I said, with a short, amused exhale. “And I shouldn’t be wasting passing period in toilets. Yet here we are.”

It landed. His mouth twitched — the ghost of a smile — then vanished as quickly as it came, like he didn’t trust it. Humour is a lever. It lets people accept guidance without feeling defeated. I nodded toward the door. Not a command. A direction.

He moved, slowly, as if his feet didn’t trust the new instruction manual. At the threshold he stopped with his hand on the handle and glanced back at me as I followed him — an expression I recognised too well: expecting the world to change its mind.

I lifted my eyebrows at him.

Go on.

He swallowed and opened the door.

Before he stepped through, I said, lightly, “Charlie.”

He froze.

“My name’s not...”

Scene Three — Wardrobe Weather¶

Wardrobe had its own weather.

Not outside weather — not sun, not rain — but a constant, indoor climate of steam and cloth-dust and warmed metal. The air tasted faintly of detergent and starch and something older that lived in wool no matter how many times you cleaned it: sheep, lanolin, history.

The room itself was a maze of rails and racks, garment bags whispering against each other whenever someone brushed past. Stacked crates were stencilled with MARA’S handwriting — not the tidy school handwriting people used when they wanted to impress teachers, but the blunt, efficient strokes of someone who labelled everything because she didn’t like losing time to idiots. A long trestle table ran down the centre like an altar, currently piled with a half-dressed mannequin and a skirt turned inside-out, hemline pinned up like a patient on a hospital bed.

Mara stood over it with her chin tucked, a bodice in her hands, and the look she wore when something had disappointed her.

Which was most things.

“Hold it,” she said, without looking up.

I froze with a hanger halfway to a rail. Mara didn’t need to raise her voice. She didn’t need to say your name. Her authority lived in the fact that she expected you to obey and had no interest in negotiating about it.

She turned the bodice over and jabbed a fingertip at a seam. “This. Who did this?”

I moved closer, careful not to bump the steamer hose that snaked across the floor like a sleeping animal. “Which one?”

Mara’s eyes flicked to me — a quick, unimpressed glance that somehow conveyed: don’t be clever.

She pinched the fabric and tugged. The seam puckered slightly, like a smile that had been forced. “Whoever did this sewed with fear.”

I leaned in and saw it: the stitches were tight, too tight, as if the person had been trying to prove something to the thread.

“They were worried it wouldn’t hold,” I said.

Mara snorted. “It won’t hold because they were worried. That’s the irony.” She flipped the bodice again and thrust it at me. “Unpick it. Do it properly. Not fast. Properly.”

There was no cruelty in it. Not exactly. Mara was not warm, but she was honest in the way that mattered: she treated workmanship as a form of respect. If she corrected you, it meant she thought you were capable of being corrected.

I took the bodice, feeling the weight of it — the underlining, the interlining, the bones that gave it a spine. The kind of garment that made you understand, viscerally, why women in paintings stood the way they did.

I liked Wardrobe because it wasn’t school.

Scene Four — Trial Shift¶

Charlie arrived ten minutes early and stood outside Wardrobe like it was a church he wasn’t sure he was allowed to enter.

He didn’t pace. He didn’t scroll his phone. He didn’t look around for someone to rescue him from the act of being there. He just waited, folded scrap of paper in one hand, fingers worrying the edge until it softened. Every now and then he glanced at the door — not to check whether it was locked, but to check whether the world had changed its mind.

Inside, Wardrobe moved the way it always did: rails clacking softly as garments were shifted, the steamer hissing like a restrained animal, the constant quiet conversation between fabric and hands.

Mara spotted him through the small window in the door.

She didn’t react. Reaction was a kind of permission.

She finished what she was doing — pinning a waistband to a mannequin, smoothing the fabric as if it were skin — then wiped her hands on a cloth and nodded toward me without looking.

“Open it.”

I was tempted to step forward, to say something that would make it easier for him, but Mara’s voice from yesterday was still in my ears: You don’t chase him. You don’t sell it like a lifeline.

So I opened the door and stood aside, nothing more.

Charlie’s gaze snapped to mine the moment the door moved. He stepped forward, then stopped, like he didn’t trust his feet.

“You’re early,” I said. It came out neutral, the way you’d say it’s Tuesday.

He nodded once. “I… I didn’t want to be late.”

“Good,” I said, because that was the correct response. “Come in.”

He crossed the threshold with the carefulness of someone entering a room that might decide to reject him on sight.

Mara didn’t greet him. She didn’t smile. She didn’t soften. She looked at him the way she looked at a bodice seam — assessing the integrity.

“Rossignol,” she said.

Charlie froze slightly. “Yes.”

Mara’s eyes dropped to his hands. “Clean?”

He looked down as if he’d forgotten hands were visible, then held them out, palms up, fingers splayed. They were clean. Nails trimmed short. The skin at the fingertips was slightly rough, as if he did fine work at home.

“Good.” Mara turned away immediately, as if the first test had been passed and was therefore no longer interesting. “Shut the door.”

He did, quietly. No banging. No showing off.

Mara walked to the centre table and picked up a garment bag. She unzipped it with a brisk motion and slid a dress out — not a grand gown, not something dramatic. A simple working dress in sturdy fabric, with a seam splitting near the side closure, the tear held together with hurried, ugly stitches.

She tossed it onto the table.

“This came in yesterday,” Mara said. “Tourist. Borrowed. Sat down too hard. Someone panicked and tried to fix it.” She tapped the seam with one finger. “Look at that.”

Charlie leaned in, careful not to touch until he was sure he was allowed. His eyes did what his eyes always did when something made sense: they became steady.

“The tension’s wrong,” he said, quietly, almost surprised by his own certainty. “It’s pulling.”

Mara’s gaze flicked to him. “Why?”

“They stitched too tight,” he replied. “And they didn’t match the grain. The fabric’s fighting.”

Mara didn’t praise him. She simply moved to the next question. “What do you do?”

Charlie swallowed. “Unpick it. Start again.”

Mara nodded once. “Good. Do it.”

He hesitated. “With… the seam ripper?”

Mara’s mouth tightened. “It’s called a lame.”

Charlie blinked. “Right. Sorry. A—”

“Don’t apologise,” Mara said. “Learn.”

He nodded, and the nod wasn’t performative. It was acceptance.

Mara pointed at the far end of the table. “Sit there. Tools are in the tin. Thread is in the drawer. If you use something, you put it back. If you break something, you tell me immediately. If you don’t know, you ask once. Not five times.”

Charlie’s jaw moved, like he was swallowing fear. “Okay.”

Mara’s eyes slid to me. “Celeste.”

I looked up.

“You’re on rails and pins. Not him.” Her tone left no space for negotiation. “You do not hover. You do not translate. You do not rescue. You work.”

“Understood,” I said.

Charlie’s gaze flicked to me — quick, skittish — and I gave him nothing except the simple fact of my presence in the room. I turned away and went to the rails, where a cluster of garments waited like quiet accusations.

Wardrobe did not stop for anyone’s nerves.

Behind me, I heard the soft, careful sound of Charlie taking the lame in hand.

A pause.

Then the tiny snap of thread giving way.

Mara moved around the room as he worked, doing her usual circuit: checking labels, touching fabric, straightening hangers. But her attention had shifted. It wasn’t on the dress. It was on him.

Not his body. Not the shape of him.

His behaviour.

He didn’t hunch over the work like a guilty child. He didn’t look around to see who was watching. He didn’t breathe fast.

He unpicked steadily, patiently, lifting each tight stitch and easing it out as if he was undoing a mistake without punishing the fabric for it.

That, I realised, was what Mara was looking for.

Not skill alone.

Temperament.

After a few minutes, Mara stopped behind him.

“Why are you going slow?” she asked.

Charlie’s shoulders tightened, but he didn’t flinch away. He looked up briefly, then back down at the seam. “Because if I go fast, I’ll tear the fabric.”

Mara’s voice was flat. “And if you tear the fabric?”

“I’ll have to patch it.” He hesitated, then added, like a confession. “It will show.”

Mara’s eyes narrowed slightly. “And you don’t want it to show.”

“No,” Charlie said. Simply. No grand reason. Just standards.

Mara walked away again, as if that answer had been a key turning in a lock.

I pinned a label to a garment bag and listened with half my mind, the way you listen to rain on a roof — constant, background, meaningful.

Charlie’s tools made small sounds: metal clicking, thread whispering. His breathing stayed even.

After he’d unpicked the seam completely, he didn’t immediately reach for thread. He smoothed the fabric with his palm, slow and light, as if calming it. Then he looked up and spoke, voice soft but clear.

“Mara?”

Mara’s head turned. “Yes.”

He held up the dress slightly. “The original seam allowance is… narrow. If I stitch it the way it is, it’ll hold, but it will be under stress. If I reinforce it from the inside with a strip of fabric — like a facing — it’ll take the load.”

Mara walked over and looked. Charlie did not move his hands to demonstrate on her body. He indicated the seam in the air above the fabric, precise and respectful, as if the dress itself deserved dignity.

Mara’s eyes sharpened. “Where would you put the strip?”

Charlie pointed to the inside layer, fingers hovering, not grabbing. “Here. Along the closure line. It won’t show. It’ll stop it tearing again.”

Mara stared at him for a beat longer than necessary.

Then she said, “Do it.”

Charlie let out a breath he hadn’t known he was holding and nodded. Not grateful. Focused.

He reached for the drawer, selected a strip of fabric, measured it twice before cutting once. His movements were economical. Not flamboyant. Not fussy. Careful in a way that made you trust him without having to decide to.

Mara watched him for a few minutes, then spoke again, to the room rather than to him.

“This is how you sew,” she said, sharply, as if correcting an invisible crowd. “Not with fear. Not with speed. With respect.”

I kept my eyes on the rails, but the words settled in my chest.

Not just about thread.

About everything.

A little while later, Mara brought over a small tin and set it down near Charlie’s elbow.

“Needles,” she said. “Choose the right one.”

Charlie glanced at the tin, then at the fabric, then back. He picked a needle that matched the weight — neither too fine nor too thick — and threaded it on the first try. His hands didn’t shake.

Mara noticed. Of course she did.

“Do you sew at home?” she asked, as if the question cost her nothing.

Charlie’s mouth tightened. “Sometimes.”

“Who taught you?”

Charlie hesitated. “My mum. She… she does alterations sometimes. For neighbours. For money.”

Mara’s gaze softened by half a millimetre — so little you could miss it if you weren’t watching for it.

“Right,” she said. “So you know what work is.”

Charlie didn’t answer, because people like him didn’t know how to accept a statement like that without turning it into an apology.

Mara didn’t give him the chance.

She tapped the table. “When you’re finished, you’ll bring it to me. And if it’s still puckered, you’ll unpick it again.”

Charlie nodded. “Okay.”

No sulk. No protest.

Just work.

I heard a voice at the other end of the room — one of the other girls, Leah, hovering with a pile of folded aprons.

“Mara,” Leah said cautiously, eyes darting to Charlie and away again. “Is… is he—”

Mara didn’t look up. “He’s working.”

Leah’s mouth opened, then shut. She glanced at me, searching for cues.

I gave her none.

Mara looked up then, and her gaze pinned Leah the way a pin fixes fabric: precise, inescapable.

“Do you have a problem with someone doing their job?” Mara asked.

Leah flushed. “No.”

“Good.” Mara’s voice was mild, which made it more dangerous. “Then do yours.”

Leah scuttled away like a mouse escaping a cat.

Charlie’s shoulders had gone tight at Leah’s question, but he hadn’t turned to watch her. He hadn’t sought sympathy. He kept stitching, eyes on the line, as if the only safe place in the world was the next correct stitch.

That was… telling.

And, in a strange way, promising.

A while later, Mara moved to the far side of the room and pulled a curtain partway across a doorway. Behind it was the fitting area — a small section partitioned off from the main space. Not hidden, exactly. Controlled.

She spoke without raising her voice. “Rossignol.”

Charlie looked up immediately. “Yes?”

“Stop,” Mara said. “Bring me the dress.”

Charlie set the needle down exactly where it belonged, smoothed the thread, and carried the dress over with both hands as if it could bruise.

Mara took it, examined the seam with her fingertips, turned it inside-out, then right side out again. She tugged lightly near the closure.

The seam held. It lay flat.

It looked as if it had never been damaged.

Mara did not smile.

But she nodded once.

“Acceptable,” she said.

Charlie’s breath stuttered, then steadied again. He didn’t glow. He didn’t grin. He simply stood there, waiting for the next instruction like someone who didn’t trust praise to survive sudden movement.

Mara looked at him.

Not the dress.

Him.

“What happens if you make a mistake in here?” she asked.

Charlie blinked. “I… I fix it.”

Mara’s eyes narrowed. “And if you can’t fix it?”

“I tell you,” he said quickly. “Immediately.”

“And if you don’t tell me?”

Charlie swallowed. “Then I’m… out.”

Mara leaned slightly closer. Her voice dropped, not to intimidate but to make the next part land.

“Yes,” she said. “You’re out. Because this room stays safe because we keep it that way.”

Charlie’s eyes widened a fraction — as if the word safe had been unexpected, as if he’d assumed the rules were only about fabric.

Mara held his gaze.

“Do you understand me?”

“Yes,” he said, hoarse.

Mara straightened. “Good.”

She turned her head slightly. “Celeste.”

I looked over.

“Show him the inventory shelf,” Mara said. “Then you go back to rails. He does not follow you. You point. He listens. You don’t chat.”

I nodded. “Come on,” I said to Charlie.

Charlie glanced at Mara as if to confirm he was allowed to move, then followed me at a respectful distance — not crowding, not trying to be close.

I stopped at the shelves and pointed out the labelled boxes: hooks, pins, tapes, ribbons, boning, eyelets. I kept my voice low and factual. Not teaching. Not nurturing. Just orienting.

Charlie’s eyes tracked everything. He didn’t touch unless he was told. He was absorbing the room the way he absorbed my instructions in the toilets: as if someone giving him structure was a form of oxygen.

When I finished, I stepped back. “That’s it.”

He nodded once. “Okay.”

And then, because he couldn’t help it, because his brain was precise and his honesty was inconvenient, he asked, quietly:

“Does she… hate me?”

It wasn’t a plea. It was data-gathering.

I kept my face neutral. “Mara doesn’t hate people,” I said. “She hates time-wasting.”

His shoulders loosened a fraction, as if he’d been holding his breath since he walked in.

“Right,” he murmured.

Mara called across the room again. “Rossignol.”

Charlie turned instantly. “Yes.”

“Pins,” Mara said. “Sort by size. If you mix them, I’ll know.”

Charlie moved without hesitation, took the tin, and began to sort, methodical and silent.

I went back to the rails.

Wardrobe resumed its normal rhythm around him, as if the room had tested him and decided — provisionally — that he was not a contaminant.

After another half hour, Mara’s voice cut through the steady hiss of steam.

“Rossignol.”

Charlie looked up.

Mara’s eyes held him. “Come back tomorrow at seven-thirty.”

Charlie went still.

“Tomorrow?” he repeated, as if the word had weight.

Mara lifted an eyebrow. “If you want.”

Charlie swallowed. He glanced down at his hands, then up again. The choice was there, hanging between them like a garment on a hook.

“I want,” he said.

Mara’s gaze didn’t soften, but something in her posture eased — like a seam that had finally stopped fighting.

“Good,” she said. “Then don’t be late.”

Charlie nodded once. “I won’t.”

Mara turned away as if the matter was settled.

It was.

Not because she’d rescued him.

Because she’d tested him.

And, for the first time in a long time, he hadn’t been found wanting.

He didn’t disappear because he’s weak.

He disappeared because he refused to be eaten.

I didn’t give them anything.

Not rushing. Not chasing.

Charlie had heard it.

Not saved.
