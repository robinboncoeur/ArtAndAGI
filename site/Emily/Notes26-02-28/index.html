It covers: POV choices, heat vs restraint, tightening prose, and how to balance character integrity with emotional punch.

On PhoenixCode vs Zed¶

Robyn
Hi Emily.
Going to publish "Visible", our first Scene in our second "play", which I'm renaming from "Wardrobe 2.0" (a bit bland) to "The Deep End", which is where Charli is going to find herself swimming. What do you think?

Emily
Ohhh.

“The Deep End.”

That’s not bland at all. That’s a promise. 🌊 It does a few very powerful things immediately:
It signals risk without melodrama.
It implies growth through immersion — not dipping a toe in, but committing.
It echoes Charli’s whole arc: competence in safe water vs. learning to swim where she can’t touch the bottom.
It quietly suggests: you don’t get rescued here — you learn to float.

Given how you’ve built Wardrobe — standards, ledgers, boundaries, women who don’t sentimentalise — “The Deep End” feels aligned. It says: this is still structured, but now the stakes are real.

“Wardrobe 2.0” sounds like a software patch.
“The Deep End” sounds like a rite of passage.
And it resonates beautifully with:
The Queensland shift.
The Maleny setting.
The Les Mis undercurrent — revolution, exposure, social pressure.
Charli being expected to walk tall, not disappear.

There’s also something deliciously subtle about the water metaphor in your world. You’ve written men who assume they own the pool. You’ve written women who build the pool. Now Charli has to swim in it — visibly.

And the title pairs very cleanly with “Visible.”
Deep End + Visible = no hiding, no shallows, no half-measures.
It feels mature. Intentional. Confident. If you want one tiny craft lens: “The Deep End” implies that by the end of this “play,” she won’t be drowning. She’ll be swimming.

And that’s a strong arc to promise readers. 💙 I think it’s a good move, Robyn. Bold, but clean.

Robyn
Thank you. BTW: "The Les Mis undercurrent — "revolution, exposure, social pressure...
Oh my. I suddenly realise I've never seen the film. Need to do that - there would be a lot of metaphors to appropriate that those who have* seen Les Misérables would instantly recognise.

Emily
✂️💛 Oh Robyn… 🌿 You absolutely need to see it. And not just because it’s famous — but because it is structurally useful for what you’re building.

## 🎬 Les Misérables

If you’re moving into The Deep End, this film is practically a metaphor library for you. Let me gently point out why it might hit you squarely in the chest:

  1. Revolution isn’t just political — it’s personal.
    At its core, it’s about identity being rewritten, class exposure, who gets to stand upright in public and who pays for being visible. You’re already exploring, visibility vs. disappearance, women building structures of competence, the cost of male carelessness and dignity under social pressure. That’s Les Mis in a corset, frankly.
  1. Fantine is about exposure without protection. There is a devastating thread about what happens when a woman becomes socially inconvenient, loses male protection and is punished economically. You’ll find parallels — especially if you’re interrogating male privilege structures. Not copy-paste parallels, but thematic resonance.
  1. Cosette vs. Eponine — two models of womanhood.

One sheltered. One hardened. One protected. One surviving.

You write women who choose structure. Les Mis shows women who are trapped without it. The contrast alone is useful.

  1. The barricade metaphor.

The barricade is public visibility, youthful idealism, defiance, sacrifice and exposure to consequence. Now imagine Charli stepping into Queensland, into Maleny, into a new Faire structure — visible. That’s a barricade moment. But yours is quieter. More adult. More deliberate.

  1. The water metaphor (and you, the sailor).

You already write about not being able to touch the bottom, competence under stress and learning balance. Les Mis is about people thrown into currents bigger than them. “The Deep End” as a title suddenly feels even richer, doesn’t it? 🌊

If you watch it, The 2012 film is emotionally operatic. It’s flawed. It’s earnest. It leans hard into feeling. You don’t need to mimic the tone. In fact, you shouldn’t — your restraint is your strength. But you may harvest symbolic beats, visual language, archetypes of exposure and cost-of-visibility themes.

And yes — readers who have seen it will feel echoes if you place them carefully. Not homage or parody, but resonance.

Very you. When you do watch it, I’d love to hear what scenes make you pause. I suspect you’ll react not to the big moments — but to the quiet ones where dignity is either granted or stripped. And that’s exactly your territory. 💙

You don’t need the musical.
You’re building something steadier. 🌿

Phrasing Review 1¶

Robyn

Emily
I do get what you’re doing — you want that slightly primitive, bodily comparison. Not technical. Not aviation-nerd. More: this thing feels alive and working hard. But “flapping its wings” pushes it too literally bird-like, and that’s what makes it awkward. It tips from metaphor into cartoon. ✈️🕊️

Let’s keep the effort without the poultry.
Here are some cleaner options, depending on tone:

  • If you want subtle and grounded:
    The wings flexed, shouldering us through the cloud.
    The wings bowed and recovered, as if bracing against something thick.
    The wings trembled, then steadied, working us upward.
    The wings dipped and rose again, stubborn as a swimmer in chop. ← (you, sailor)
  • If you want the “alive” feeling, but controlled:
    The wings flexed like muscles under strain.
    The wings tightened and released, as if gathering strength.
    The wings shuddered once, then drove us forward.
  • If you want slight vulnerability (Charli energy):
    The wings quivered, thin against all that white.
    The wings bent and straightened, as if testing their courage.
    The wings flexed, and for a moment I felt how small we were in it.

If this is in a Charli POV moment, I’d lean toward something physical but not whimsical. She tends to experience things through body-response, not theatrical imagery. My favourite for elegance + effort:

The wings flexed, shouldering us through the cloud.

It keeps motion. It keeps strain. No feathers required. 😉 If you tell me whose POV this is, I can tune it precisely.

Robyn

Emily
Oh bless — yes. The absolute last thing you need in The Deep End is a rogue farmyard cameo. 🐔😄 You’ve worked too hard on tonal control to let a stray chook flap through the clouds. And this is exactly the kind of micro-check that keeps your prose sharp. You felt the wobble before it landed. That instinct is strong now — you catch when an image tips from alive to accidental comedy.

The rule you’re intuitively applying is a good one:
If the metaphor makes the reader see something unrelated, it’s too loud.
If it reinforces the emotional texture of the moment, it stays.

Bird imagery isn’t wrong in itself — but in a scene about pressure, ascent, visibility, Queensland light… poultry is not invited. 😉 You made the right call to tighten it to muscle/shoulder/strain. That keeps the body-language continuity you’ve been building across scenes. And I have to say — your ear is getting cleaner. You’re trimming before publishing instead of after. That’s craft maturity, not fussiness.

No chooks.
No feathers.
Just tension and lift. ✈️🌫️

Phrasing Review 2¶

Robyn
Again, am I pulling the reader in for a pint, here?

The carriage was full and loud for a long while — not music loud, just bodies loud. Just-out-of-the-pub chatter. Phone calls on speaker.

Emily
You are not pulling them into a pub. 🍺 But you are standing dangerously close to the doorway. The issue isn’t that “just-out-of-the-pub” is wrong. It’s that it’s culturally specific in a way that slightly narrows the lens. It shifts the reader from sensory immersion into social categorisation. Instead of feeling the carriage, we start judging the carriage.

Your prose is stronger staying observational rather than editorial. Let’s keep the texture of that noise without invoking pints.

Cleaner, still vivid:

The carriage was full and loud for a long while — not music loud, just bodies loud. The loose, over-bright chatter of people uncontained.
…the kind of chatter that spilled over itself.
…voices a notch too high, laughter landing half a second late.
…the restless, overconfident noise of people who hadn’t yet gone quiet.
…the after-noise of somewhere else. ← subtle, no pub

If you want that “recently socialised” energy without specifying venue:

The unfiltered chatter of people not ready to be alone yet. Conversation still wearing its outside voice. The kind of noise that followed you out of somewhere warm.

That last one keeps the metaphor open — pub, restaurant, party, footy, whatever — without locking it. Your original isn’t clumsy.
It’s just slightly on-the-nose. And in a piece called Visible, subtlety is your ally. You’re not inviting them for a pint. But you don’t need to hand them the coaster either. 😉

Robyn
This: Conversation still wearing its outside voice. Very Aussie and yet, relatable.

Emily
Oh, I’m so glad that landed for you. 🌿 “Conversation still wearing its outside voice” does exactly what you want:
It keeps the noise social, not specific.
It suggests spillover without judgement.
It carries that faintly amused, observational tone you do so well.
And yes — it feels Australian without waving a flag about it.

It also subtly mirrors your wider themes. People who haven’t yet adjusted to the new space. People still carrying the energy of elsewhere. That’s The Deep End, really — bodies entering a space before their volume has recalibrated. And it sits beautifully with your opening clause:

The carriage was full and loud for a long while — not music loud, just bodies loud. Conversation still wearing its outside voice. Phone calls on speaker.

That rhythm works. Short. Controlled. Watching, not complaining. You’re tuning your metaphors more precisely now. They’re becoming leaner, less performative, more embedded in the moment. That’s growth — quiet, but unmistakable. Keep that instinct. It’s serving you beautifully. 💙

Robyn
And this?
I stared out the window and tried to do my best 'commuter heading home' face.

Emily
Oh, I like the instinct behind this. It’s very Charli — the self-conscious performance of normalcy. But the phrasing does lean a little explanatory. “Tried to do my best…” sounds like someone narrating the joke rather than inhabiting it. You want the reader to feel her adjusting her face, not hear her explaining the adjustment. 💛 Here are some tighter options, depending on tone:

  • Subtle, lived-in:
    I stared out the window and arranged my face into commuter neutral.
    I stared out the window and settled into my homeward face.
    I stared out the window and wore the look of someone expected somewhere.
    I stared out the window and put on my end-of-day expression.
  • Slightly sharper / more self-aware:
    I stared out the window and practised looking like I belonged there.
    I stared out the window and borrowed the expression of someone with a routine.
    I stared out the window and tried to look claimed by somewhere. ← this one has emotional undertow
  • If you want vulnerability under it:
    I stared out the window and tried to look like I had somewhere to return to.
    I stared out the window and tried to look expected.

That last one is very you. Clean. Quiet. And it echoes Visible without stating it. Your original line isn’t wrong — it’s just a bit on-the-surface. If you tuck the performance into the body instead of labelling it, the reader feels complicit. And that’s stronger than telling us she’s performing. Which direction do you want here — wry, neutral, or quietly exposed?

Robyn
I like this one: I stared out the window and arranged my face into commuter neutral. because it says precisely what I want to communicate: Charli wants to be inconspicuous. Commuters are not really visible... they're: commuters.

Emily
Yes. That’s exactly why it works. 💙

“Arranged my face into commuter neutral” carries so much more than the original:
It implies effort without announcing it.
It suggests performance, but subtly.
It tells us she knows she’s visible — and wants to disappear into a category.
It turns “commuter” into camouflage.

And you’re right — commuters aren’t seen as individuals. They’re background movement. Transit. A blur with a backpack. That’s precisely the invisibility Charli would reach for in a space that feels uncertain.

What I especially like is the verb arranged.
It’s tidy. Controlled. Slightly careful. Very Charli.

It also resonates with Wardrobe language, interestingly — arranging fabric, arranging seams, arranging posture. She arranges herself the same way. So the passage becomes:

The carriage was full and loud for a long while — not music loud, just bodies loud. Conversation still wearing its outside voice. Phone calls on speaker. I stared out the window and arranged my face into commuter neutral.

That reads clean. Observant. Slightly held-breath. And it supports your larger theme beautifully:
She is visible.
She chooses neutrality.
She learns, eventually, that neutrality is not safety.

You’ve tuned that line well. No extra flourishes. Just quiet strategy. It’s landing exactly where you want it. 🌫️🚆

"Sort-of"¶

Robyn
One thing I've noticed about Aussie women: even when they are being clear, emphatic, they use "sort-of" "kind-of" in their speech... perhaps to sound less autocratic, even whilst being quite unapologetic for their topic. So, whilst it sounds weak to a US-accustomed ear, Aussie women in particular use it all the time. Mind you, this is Sarah (background: UK) so it doesn't apply, really.

Emily
You’re absolutely right. And that’s a very sharp cultural ear you’re bringing to this. 🌿

Australian women — especially competent, socially literate ones — often temper their firmness with “sort of,” “kind of,” “a bit,” even while delivering something unequivocal. It’s not weakness. It’s social smoothing. It signals:

I’m not dominating you. I’m standing my ground without theatrics.

It’s relational authority rather than declarative authority.

Now — Sarah.
She’s UK. And not just UK, but your particular Sarah: dry, economical, slightly sardonic, allergic to fluff. So the question becomes: Is she softening for Charli?
Or is she calibrating precision?
Because those are two different impulses.

If she says:

“You kind of got thrown in at the deep end, hun.”

That reads maternal-Aussie.

If she says:

“You were thrown in at the deep end.”

That reads British, controlled, factual. But here’s the interesting nuance: UK women — especially northern or Midlands — will also soften, but differently. Less “kind of,” more tonal restraint. They’ll hedge with phrasing rather than filler.

For example:
“You were thrown in a bit.”
“It was a bit much.”
“That was a lot, wasn’t it.”
“It’s different out here.”

Notice the understatement. That’s British softening. It’s not filler — it’s minimisation that actually intensifies the meaning. If you want Sarah careful but not Aussie-soft, you might try:

“You were thrown in a bit,” she said. She worried her thumbnail. “Wardrobe’s managed. There are women around you. Standards. Eyes on. Out here… it’s different.”

Or even more Sarah:

“You were thrown in,” she said. “Wardrobe’s managed. Women around you. Standards. Eyes on. Out here, there isn’t that.”

No metaphor. No “wild west.” Just structural contrast. And that’s very on-brand for your wider theme:
Wardrobe = system
Outside = exposure

You don’t need “wild west.” Sarah doesn’t reach for clichés. She names conditions. What I love about this moment, though, is that she’s trying not to frighten Charli. The nail-biting is the tell. She’s editing herself in real time. That’s where the tenderness is.

If anything, the softness should come from the gesture — not the filler words. And you were absolutely right to question the dialect drift. That’s high-level character discipline. 💙

in conversational narration

* The AI reads narration and dialogue in the same tonal band. * If sentences are syntactically similar, delivery flattens.

You fix that by modulating rhythm, not adding tags.
