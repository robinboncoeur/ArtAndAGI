Robyn, this is already landing beautifully — the power dynamic is clear (Charli competent, Brittany attentive), and the “Wardrobe face” is exactly the kind of internal shorthand that makes a place feel real. 😊

A few tiny line-level tweaks first (just to smooth the read), then I’ll give you three believable “young woman” segue options Brittany can use to drift from seams into past without it feeling like an interrogation.

Micro-edits / little snags worth fixing ✍️¶

  • “sort-of” → “sort of” (unless you deliberately want Charli’s voice to look slightly fussy on the page)
  • “was was” in “a seam that was was weak” (easy catch)

  • “eighteen hundreds” can sound a touch casual/modern in a way that bumps period-costume talk. Consider:

  • “early nineteenth century”

  • “the 1800s”
  • “the original patterns” (if you want to keep it non-technical)
  • “For today’s bodies” is a great point, but you can make it feel less like a lecture by letting Charli gesture at the garment or at her own torso/hips/shoulders. Small physical anchor = instant naturalness.

Also: Brittany’s line “Side benefit: we stay in touch.” is a good intention but reads a little “stated.” If you tuck it into something practical/flirty-in-a-friendly-way, it’ll feel more like a real young woman talking.

Example:

“So I’m allowed to text you the moment a hem explodes?”

Same meaning. Less “explaining itself.” 😉

How Brittany can segue “like a young woman” 💬🌿¶

A young woman who wants personal info without being rude usually does one (or a blend) of these:

  1. Permission + softness: “Can I ask something not-work?” (gives Charli control)
  2. Professional justification: “I learn better if I know where the system came from.”
  3. Self-disclosure bait: Brittany offers a small personal fact first, then asks (creates safety and reciprocity)

And importantly: she’ll do it while her hands are busy (threading a needle, smoothing seam tape, setting up the phone for photos). When people ask intimate questions, they often avoid direct eye contact — not from shame, but to reduce pressure.

Yes: it’s cadence. Here’s what cadence actually is on the page¶

Cadence isn’t “pretty writing.” It’s three mechanical things you can learn:

A) Breath units¶

Sentences should match human breath and emotion.

  • Calm = longer sentences, smoother clauses.
  • Alarm/shame = short sentences. Fragments. Hard stops.

If everything is medium-long, the reader’s nervous system stays flat.

B) Contrast¶

You want a pattern like:

  • long → short → medium
  • descriptive → punchline
  • external fact → internal sting

Contrast is what makes prose feel “alive.”

C) Placement of weight¶
