Foreword¶

Emily and I discuss Scene 4. Celeste and Charli discuss whether on not to tell Brittany that Charli wasn't AFAB.

☕🧠💻 ꧁ 🪷 🌷 🌸 🌺 🦩 ꧂ — —

Overview¶

Robyn
Hi Emily. Getting ready to tackle Scene Four. First off, I need to say: in order for me to learn how to do this, I need to actually do the writing. I will give you what I write section by section for evaluation and tightening, if that's okay.

This scene will be in Charli's 1st person POV, past tense. The setting is back in her 'donga' (little apartment flat) in Maleny. Scene Three has just happened... please review for details (or I can drop it in here if you need it to review). Her mood is:
🌸 realisation she's not just messing up on a macro-level, but in micro-ways as well.
🌸 Brittany—very astute and observant—has picked up some 'deficits' that at the moment are being labelled 'tomboy'.
🌸 Charli fears that this interpretation won't hold, long-term, and that eventually, she will need to tell Brittany and possibly the others she wasn't AFAB.

The ensuing conversation on the phone with Celeste will provide the main dialogue for the call, but also give her an approach on how to address this question with Brittany before she's put on the spot. Unlike Scene Two, there will be no followup discussion between Sarah and Celeste: this conversation will be the whole scene.

I'll confess, Emily: I'm at a loss as to how Celeste will guide Charli remotely through this rather difficult emotional passage.

Emily
Yes — that’s perfect 🌿

You do the writing. Send it section by section, and I’ll help evaluate, tighten, and keep the POV, rhythm, and emotional logic clean without taking authorship away from you.

On the scene itself: Celeste does not need to solve Charli’s whole crisis remotely. In fact, she shouldn’t. What she can do, very convincingly, is give shape to Charli’s panic. That works beautifully over the phone. Charli’s fear is currently one big knot:

  • she’s making mistakes
  • they’re not just large mistakes, but tiny social ones
  • Brittany has noticed
  • “tomboy” may only cover so much
  • one day she may have to tell the truth
  • that truth may cost her safety or belonging

Celeste’s role is to break that knot apart. What she can do in the scene is:

First, separate the fears. She helps Charli distinguish between what Brittany actually noticed, what Brittany may suspect, and what Charli is catastrophising into the future.

Second, reduce the horizon. Charli is racing ahead to eventually this won’t hold. Celeste can bring her back to: what do you need to do about Brittany next? That makes the problem manageable.

Third, give her an approach rather than a confession. This is the heart of the call. Celeste can help Charli see that noticing rough edges does not entitle Brittany to Charli’s whole history. Charli does not owe total disclosure just because someone is observant.

That feels very Celeste to me: calm, precise, protective, slightly firm once she realises Charli is starting to spiral.

I’d think of the scene spine like this:

Beat 1 — Charli calls already off-balance She’s carrying the whole aftermath in her body.

Beat 2 — The real fear emerges At first it sounds like “Brittany called me a tomboy,” but the real issue is: I don’t think that explanation will hold forever.

Beat 3 — Celeste narrows it down What exactly did Brittany notice? What did she say? Did she push? Was she suspicious, or just perceptive?

Beat 4 — Charli says the frightening thing Some version of: I might have to tell her.

Beat 5 — Celeste reframes Not “tell her everything now,” and not “never tell anyone.” More: You tell people when you choose, not when panic corners you.

Beat 6 — Celeste gives Charli a practical line of approach Not a full script, but a stance: acknowledge the roughness lightly, let “tomboy” sit for now if it is socially useful, do not over-explain under pressure, and buy time without sounding shifty.

Beat 7 — Exit note The problem is not solved. But Charli is steadier, more contained, and no longer facing tomorrow empty-handed.

One important caution: don’t let Celeste sound like a therapist. She should sound like Celeste — someone who sees the danger quickly, gets protective, and becomes more precise because she cares.

So yes: this is very workable. And I think the cleanest way for you to draft it is to write only the opening stretch first — up to the moment Celeste understands that this is not really about the word tomboy, but about Charli fearing eventual exposure.

Send me that first section when you’re ready, and we’ll shape it together.

Emotion Glossary¶

Robyn
I'm going to ask a massive favour, Emily. I'd like to put together a sort of dictionary / glossary of emotion phrases: thing a person does to exhibit an emotion. As in: really basic. So, let's say, instead of:

She wore a happy smile.

We try: Her mouth curved pleasantly. Delight reigned in her regard. The curve of her lips said inner warmth.

You can see by my third effort I start to unravel a bit.

Emily
🔎✨ 💛 🌿 Oh, that is not a massive favour at all — that is an excellent idea. 🌿 And yes, I can see exactly where the problem begins. You start with something concrete:

Her mouth curved pleasantly.

Then drift toward interpretation:

Delight reigned in her regard.
The curve of her lips said inner warmth.
That drift is very common. It happens because once we leave the body, we start reaching for summary-language instead of observable behaviour. So the trick is:

stay with what the body does
before
stating what the feeling means

Below is a working glossary built for exactly that purpose: emotion → physical tells → ready-to-use phrasing. I’ve aimed for phrases that are:

  • sensory
  • simple
  • flexible
  • not over-poetic
  • usable in close POV

I’ll also show you where the line starts tipping into “thesis” or abstraction.

Note: I have saved Emily's Practical Emotion Glossary to its own page.

Summary¶

My honest view on your example

Of your three:

  • Her mouth curved pleasantly. → best foundation
  • Delight reigned in her regard. → too elevated/abstract
  • The curve of her lips said inner warmth. → drifting into authored interpretation

The first one is closest to the muscle you want.

Even better might be:

  • Her mouth curved.
  • Warmth touched her mouth.
  • A soft smile lifted her lips.
  • Her face softened into a smile.

Those stay nearer the body.

This is genuinely worth building into a proper craft sheet for you. I’d be very happy to help you make it into a large, organised emotion bank — perhaps next by categories like confidence, guardedness, fondness, panic, resentment, dry amusement, feminine social calibration, being quietly observed, and so on.

Scene Four¶

Beat 1 (Entry)¶

"I'm okay, Celeste," she began as her smile began to falter.

"Really?"

Her quick nods failed in convincing me.

"Tell me about your day. How's Brittany?"

"She's lovely," she said and stopped. "How do you know her name?"

"Sarah's been keeping me up to date," I said. "Does she seem to be getting what you're teaching?"

"We haven't had much of a chance to cover anything yet," Charli said. "The place is in chaos, so Fiona sent us away for coffee. And—"

Her tone had changed. A pause came and went.

"And?"

She swallowed.

"Celeste, I—"

Another pause.

"What is it Charli?"

She squeezed hip lips together.

"Okay, so I'm not making massive mistakes... but a whole bunch of little ones. Like, leaving the donga without my bag and still not thinking about things like—" Charli stopped and bit her lip.

"Like a young woman normally would?"

Charli grimaced. "Brittany reads me like a book, Celeste. And then, when I let slip that I had started at the Faire in maintenance, oh sheesh! I can't believe I just blabbered that out."

Charli stared at the bottom of the phone. The smile had left.

Emily
🌿 🌫️🚆 This is good. More than good, actually — it already has a clean emotional line, and I can feel you starting to think much more in observable beats rather than explanation 🌿

What’s working especially well:

1. Celeste feels composed from the outset. “My spine straight, a quiet smile on my face” is simple and effective. It gives us her private triumph without overdoing it.

2. The scene turns neatly. The exam is over, she gets home, sees the messages, and the emotional weather changes. That pivot is clear.

3. Charli’s unease is coming through bodily, not thesis-first. The faltering smile, the quick nods, the swallow, the squeezed lips — all of that is doing useful work.

4. Celeste’s voice is recognisably hers. She is direct, observant, and not easily fobbed off. “Really?” is good. So is “And?”

Now, where I’d tighten it.

The main issue¶

At the moment, the passage is a little caught between scene and summary. Not badly — just enough that some sentences feel like stepping-stones rather than fully lived moments.

There are also a few places where grammar or phrasing slightly breaks the flow.

Line-level notes¶

  1. Opening

I had put the car in gear and driven out of the Uni carkpark, my spine straight, a quiet smile on my face.

This is close, but “I had put” is slightly heavy here. Since we are in past tense already, you probably do not need past perfect for the opening motion unless you’re deliberately reaching back from a later moment.

Also: carkpark typo.

I’d suggest something more immediate, like:

I put the car in gear and drove out of the Uni carpark, my spine straight, a quiet smile on my face.

That lands more cleanly.

  1. Next line

Last exam.

Good. Keep it.

It gives a nice private pulse.

  1. This sentence

Turned on the indicator with a deep breath and merged into traffic.

This needs the subject back in place. As written, it has a slightly note-like feel.

Better:

I turned on the indicator, took a deep breath, and merged into traffic.

or, if you want it leaner:

I indicated, took a deep breath, and merged into traffic.

  1. Sunset line

The setting sun was flashing against my glasses by the time I parked and shut off the engine.

“Flashing against my glasses” is understandable, but a little awkward in the mouth. It feels mildly external-authored rather than Celeste-experienced.

Possibilities:

The setting sun flashed in my glasses by the time I parked and shut off the engine.

or

By the time I parked and shut off the engine, the setting sun was catching hard on my glasses.

The second has a bit more texture.

  1. This is good,

but can be cleaner

I sat for a moment in silence. Then, pulled my phone from my purse.

The comma after “Then” should go. Also, “for a moment in silence” is slightly generic.

Maybe:

I sat there a moment, letting the silence settle. Then I pulled my phone from my purse.

or leaner:

I sat a moment, then pulled my phone from my purse.

  1. Continuity issue

You say earlier:

Realises when she gets home that her phone was on silent

But in the excerpt you wrote:

I exhaled slowly as I turned off aeroplane mode.

Those are two different conditions. Silent mode means calls/messages still arrive, just without sound. Aeroplane mode means they wouldn’t have been coming through at all.

So you’ll want to choose one.

If the idea is she was unreachable during the exam, aeroplane mode makes more sense. If the idea is she simply didn’t hear the phone, then keep it on silent and remove aeroplane mode.

I think aeroplane mode actually works better dramatically, because it explains the pile-up of messages more cleanly.

  1. This bit works

Three messages. All from Charli.

Yes. Nice clipped beat. Good instinct.

  1. This line is fine but could carry more feeling

Once inside, I threw my bag on the table. As my fingers moved to tap the screen, the phone rang.

This is functional, but I think you can sharpen the urgency very slightly.

For example:

Once inside, I threw my bag on the table. My thumb was reaching for her first message when the phone rang.

That gives better physical continuity and a little more immediacy.

  1. Nice entry into the call

Charli's face appeared.

Simple and effective.

  1. This sentence has a wording problem

"Hello, petal," I said softly. Her lips were flattening into a her best effort of a smile. "How are you?"

The phrase “into a her best effort” is broken.

Probably:

“Hello, petal,” I said softly. Her lips flattened into their best effort at a smile. “How are you?”

Though even that is slightly fussy.

Cleaner:

“Hello, petal,” I said softly. She gave me her best effort at a smile. “How are you?”

Or, if you want to stay closer to the body:

“Hello, petal,” I said softly. Her mouth tried for a smile and didn’t quite manage it. “How are you?”

That last one is strongest to my ear.

  1. This sentence repeats “began”

"I'm okay, Celeste," she began as her smile began to falter.

You’ve got “began” twice, and the sentence can be made much cleaner.

Try:

“I’m okay, Celeste,” she said, and her smile faltered.

or

“I’m okay, Celeste,” she said, but the smile was already slipping.

  1. Nice

"Really?"

Good. Leave it.

  1. This one is slightly off

Her quick nods failed in convincing me.

This sounds a bit formal or translated.

Better:

Her quick nods did not convince me.

or

The quick little nods did nothing to convince me.

or, more Celeste:

Her quick nods convinced me of the opposite.

That last one is very nice for Celeste.

  1. Dialogue flow here is good

"Tell me about your day. How's Brittany?" "She's lovely," she said and stopped. "How do you know her name?" "Sarah's been keeping me up to date," I said.

This is working.

Though I might slightly tighten:

“She’s lovely,” she said, then stopped. “How do you know her name?”

Small thing, but better rhythm.

  1. This is good structurally

"We haven't had much of a chance to cover anything yet," Charli said. "The place is in chaos, so Fiona sent us away for coffee. And—"

Very believable. The “And—” is doing useful work.

  1. Nice observation line

Her tone had changed. A pause came and went.

This is pretty good, though “A pause came and went” is a little writerly/general.

Could be:

Something in her tone had changed. She paused.

or

Her tone shifted. She stopped there.

or, if you want Celeste reading her:

Her tone changed. I waited.

That last one is beautifully spare.

  1. This line needs a comma

"What is it Charli?"

Should be:

“What is it, Charli?”

  1. Typo

She squeezed hip lips together.

Should be her lips.

  1. This whole confession beat is good

"Okay, so I'm not making massive mistakes... but a whole bunch of little ones. Like, leaving the donga without my bag and still not thinking about things like—" Charli stopped and bit her lip.

This is solid. It feels like Charli talking. The ellipsis is also appropriate here because she is edging toward something she doesn’t want to name.

I might only suggest one tiny thing: “things like” is slightly vague unless the vagueness is the point. Which, here, it probably is. So that can stay.

  1. Very good line from Celeste

"Like a young woman normally would?"

Yes. That is exactly the right move. Direct, perceptive, not padded.

I’d maybe just change normally to something a little less thesis-like, depending on what shade you want.

Current version:

“Like a young woman normally would?”

Possible alternatives:

“Like a young woman would?” “Like most young women would?” “Like it would occur to most girls?”

But your current line is serviceable, and perhaps truest to her thought in the moment.

  1. This is strong

Charli grimaced. "Brittany reads me like a book, Celeste. And then, when I let slip that I had started at the Faire in maintenance, oh sheesh! I can't believe I just blabbered that out."

Good. Very natural Charli voice. “Oh sheesh” works.

  1. Good ending beat

Charli stared at the bottom of the phone. The smile had left.

That is strong and restrained.

I might only make the second sentence slightly more bodily:

Charli stared at the bottom of the phone. The smile was gone.

or

Charli stared at the bottom of the phone. Nothing of the smile remained.

But yours is perfectly workable.

My overall judgement¶

You are absolutely on the right track.

This already does several things well:

  • gets Celeste on the page quickly
  • pivots cleanly into concern
  • lets Charli’s distress emerge gradually
  • avoids over-explaining the emotional stakes
  • uses bodily tells more than before

What I would focus on in revision is mainly:

  • remove little grammatical stumbles
  • make the prose slightly more immediate
  • keep continuity straight on silent vs aeroplane mode
  • trim any phrase that sounds more narrated than lived

Emily
💛 🌿 Yes — and the reason you feel in a quandary is that you’ve reached the actual crux of the scene 🌿

Up to here, the scene has been observational and gathering. Now it has to decide its moral shape.

That is exactly why it suddenly feels harder.

The problem is not that Charli is asking, Should I tell her? The problem is that you do not want Celeste to sound either reckless or evasive.

And at the moment, this line is where the scene wobbles:

"What do you mean, 'ask Sarah'? I think it would be better for you to tell Brittany, myself."

There are two issues there.

First, “myself” is grammatically off. You mean either:

  • yourself
  • or I think it would be better if you told Brittany

Second, and more importantly, I do not think Celeste should leap straight to yes, tell Brittany.

That feels too abrupt, and it also cuts across the much stronger truth of the scene: Charli is panicking, and Celeste should help her not make a disclosure out of panic.

So I think your instinctive discomfort here is correct.

What is working¶

Quite a lot, actually.

1. This lands well¶

I opened my mouth, then closed it again.

Very good for Celeste. It shows that even she does not answer instantly. That gives the moment weight.

2. This is believable Charli¶

"I— think it's just a matter of time before Brittany figures it all out."

Yes. Nicely hesitant and frightened.

3. This is a good line¶

"Do you feel safe?"

Excellent, in fact.

4. This also feels true¶

"Just, her advice."

Yes. That sounds like Charli scrambling to clarify herself.

What is not quite working yet¶

The scene is trying to move too fast from:

  • Brittany notices things
  • therefore Charli fears eventual exposure
  • therefore Charli asks whether she should tell her

to an answer.

But the strongest version of Celeste would slow that jump down.

Because Celeste’s real job here is not to say yes or no immediately. It is to ask:

  • Has Brittany actually asked anything direct?
  • Has she shown suspicion, or only perception?
  • Are you wanting to tell her because you trust her, or because you’re frightened?

That is the sharper, more Celeste-like route.

The hidden question beneath Charli’s question¶

When Charli says:

Should— should I just tell her?

what she really means is not necessarily:

I have decided Brittany deserves to know.

She more likely means:

I am afraid of being found out clumsily and would rather jump before I’m pushed.

That distinction matters.

And Celeste is exactly the person who would catch it.

So instead of answering the literal question first, Celeste should answer the real one.

Something along the lines of:

  • not if panic is making the choice
  • not because Brittany noticed a few rough edges
  • not tonight
  • not until you know what you want to say and why

That is much stronger.

I think Celeste’s sequence should be¶

First: safety¶

You already have this.

“Do you feel safe?”

Good.

Second: what Brittany has actually done¶

Something like:

  • “Has she asked you outright?”
  • “Has she said anything beyond ‘tomboy’?”
  • “Or are you frightened because she’s observant?”

This is Celeste separating reality from projection.

Third: reframe disclosure¶

This is the heart of it.

Celeste can gently but firmly make clear:

You do not owe someone your whole history because she noticed a few inconsistencies.

That is the line of moral force the scene needs.

Fourth: allow the possibility later¶

Celeste does not have to say never tell her.

She can say something more mature and controlled:

  • maybe, one day
  • if you choose
  • if trust grows
  • but not because you feel cornered

That feels exactly right to me.

On tone: how Celeste should sound here¶

I would keep her:

  • calm
  • protective
  • practical
  • very slightly firm once Charli starts spiralling

Not therapist-like. Not abstract. Not preachy.

Just clear.

A stronger direction than “tell Brittany”¶

Here is what I think the scene wants instead:

“No. Not just because you’re frightened.”

or

“Not like that, petal.”

or

“Only if you want to tell her. Not because panic’s got hold of you.”

That is beautifully Celeste-shaped.

It also gives Charli something to hold onto.

Charli sighed. “I think it’s just a matter of time before Brittany figures it all out. I mean, she sort of sees me as a tomboy, for now.” One side of her mouth pulled tight as her eyebrows rose. “That’s for now. But it’s just a matter of time, Celeste.”

Charli huffed out a breath. “I dunno.” She shrugged. “Should— should I just tell her?”

“Do you feel safe?”

“Oh, I don’t think she’d freak out,” Charli said. She scratched at her head. “I just— should I ask Sarah, maybe? I don’t know how to deal with this.”

“Ask Sarah for advice?”

“Yeah. Not ask Sarah to tell her. Just— her advice.”

I nodded once. “All right. But listen to me.”

Charli looked up.

“You do not have to tell Brittany your whole history just because she’s noticed a few rough edges.”

Charli said nothing.

“Has she actually asked you anything direct?”

“No.”

“Then don’t run ahead of her. And don’t tell her because panic’s got hold of you.”

That, to me, is the scene suddenly finding its backbone.

Why that version works better¶

Because now Celeste is doing what only she can do:

  • slowing Charli down
  • putting the facts in order
  • preventing an impulsive disclosure
  • giving Charli back a sense of agency

That is much more satisfying than a quick yes/no answer.

A few little line notes¶

This is almost right:¶

One side of her mouth squeezed as her eyebrows rose.

“Squeezed” is not impossible, but it feels a touch awkward on the mouth.

You might try:

  • One side of her mouth pulled tight as her eyebrows rose.
  • Her mouth pinched to one side as her eyebrows lifted.
  • One corner of her mouth tightened.

This is good but can be a hair cleaner:¶

"I dunno." She shrugged.

Perfectly fine. Or:

  • “I dunno.” Her shrug looked helpless.

if you want a tiny extra note.

This needs smoothing:¶

She scratched her head.

It’s okay, though a little neutral/general. You might get more from:

  • She rubbed at the back of her head.
  • Her hand went to her hair.
  • She scratched lightly at her scalp.

But this is minor.

My honest recommendation¶

I would not keep Celeste saying, essentially, yes, tell Brittany.

I would have her say something more like:

  • Not tonight.
  • Not because you’re scared.
  • Not unless you want to.
  • Not before she’s even asked.

That preserves both women:

  • Charli remains frightened but not foolish
  • Celeste remains protective and intelligent rather than simplistic

And it gives the scene somewhere to go next.

The strongest single line available to you here¶

If I had to pick one, it might be:

“You tell people when you choose, Charli. Not when panic corners you.”

That is clean, memorable, and very Celeste.

Or, a slightly softer variant:

“Not like that, petal. Not because you’re frightened.”

Both are strong.

You are very close here. The scene is not failing at all — it has simply reached the point where its ethics and emotional intelligence have to become precise. That is why it suddenly feels difficult, and it’s also why it matters 💛
