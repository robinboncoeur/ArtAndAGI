Scene 3-Brittany¶

Robyn
Hi Emily. I've started on Scene 3 "Brittany". I keep backing up to re-write description into sensory (thinking English to translate into French). Eventually, I'll be able to do 'sensory' without the intermediary description. Here is the outline for Scene 3 in Charli's 1st person POV, past tense - very rough:

[ Charli ]

Beat 1 — Entry

My stomach was a bit tight, scratchy eyes (not enough sleep). Skin damp, scratching a mosquito bite on the way to Wardrobe. My hair is a messy updo because I had overslept.

Beat 2 — Inciting Shift (PRIMARY)

Sarah was already there; introduced me to Brittany, Ellie and Natalie. I smiled bravely. The girls asked questions. I produced a paper with an outline and started to write the questions down.

Beat 3 — Internal Calibration

The new wardrobe was dusty, with workmen talking loudly, drills growling at the walls, a skill-saw whining at a piece of plywood, a decisive hammer driving a point home. The ache in my stomach was not because it was empty, but from the heat of talking to new people. My ribs loosened more and more: Brittany smiling at me easily with kind eyes, my shoulders eased slowly down: the girls listened closely, leaning towards me with wide eyes.

Beat 4 — Choice

At morning tea, Brittany sat next to me. Asked me a million questions.

Beat 5 — Power Adjustment (TURN)

My entire body relaxed around Brittany. A warmth grew deep inside every time I heard her easy laugh, her bright eyes sparkling.

Sarah noticed it. She distanced herself more. I saw less of her.

Beat 6 — Exit Note

I waved Brittany goodbye as she drove off with her workmates in her little mauve Dolphin with a little sigh, part of me wanting to be in that little car.

Emily
Hi Robyn 🌿 — this is exactly the right kind of rough. You’ve already got the bones of “sensory-first”:
body → room → people → meaning.
Now we just make it Charli, and make each beat do one clear thing without slipping back into “explaining.” 😊

Below are two things:

  1. a sensory-first rewrite of your outline (still outline-y, but with phrasing you can lift straight into prose), and
  2. a micro-toolkit for stopping the “English → French” detour when you feel yourself backing up.

Scene 3 — “Brittany” (Charli 1st, past)¶

Beat 1 — Entry

  • I woke with sand in my eyes and a throat that tasted like sleep.
  • My scalp felt tight under pins; the updo was a compromise, not a choice.
  • Damp skin under my arms already, even though the morning wasn’t hot yet.
  • A mosquito bite on my ankle kept catching on my sock—sharp, then itchy, then sharp again.
  • On the walk in, Wardrobe’s smell hit first: dust, fabric, and that faint metallic tang of tools.

(Tiny Charli detail that reads as “her”: she checks her hair in a dark window, then stops herself—because it never helps.)

Beat 2 — Inciting Shift (PRIMARY)

  • Sarah was there early—already set, already moving like she’d been awake for hours.
  • Three new faces turned at once: Brittany, Ellie, Natalie.
  • My smile arrived a half-second late, like a door that stuck in its frame.
  • Questions came quick and friendly. I nodded too much.
  • I pulled out the paper—my little shield—flattened it with my palm and started writing as they spoke.

(Key move: don’t tell us “I smiled bravely.” Show the cost of the smile.)

Beat 3 — Internal Calibration

  • The place was unfinished in a way that made my skin prickle: dust on everything, the air thick with it.
  • Men’s voices bounced off bare walls; drills chewed into timber; a saw screamed, then stopped dead, then screamed again.
  • The noise didn’t just fill the room—it pressed against my ribs.
  • My stomach ached in that specific way it did when I had to be a person: not hunger, not illness—heat.
  • Then Brittany looked at me properly—like she wasn’t inspecting, just seeing.
  • She smiled, easy and unafraid, and something in my chest unhooked a notch.
  • My shoulders dropped without asking permission.
  • The girls leaned in when I spoke; their eyes stayed on my face, not my body, and that made it easier to breathe.

(This beat is where you earn the later “warmth.” Let the relief be physical and incremental.)

Beat 4 — Choice

  • Morning tea: plastic chair, paper cup, the sharp smell of instant coffee and something sugary.
  • Brittany chose the seat beside me like it was obvious.
  • She asked questions the way some people handed you a rope—steady, one after another—so you didn’t have to climb alone.
  • I could’ve kept hiding behind my notes.
  • Instead, I answered. And when I didn’t know, I said so.

(That last line is a real “choice” moment for Charli: truth over performance.)

Beat 5 — Power Adjustment (TURN)

  • Somewhere in the middle of her talking, my body stopped bracing for impact.
  • Her laugh went straight through me—warm, ordinary, like a window opened.
  • Each time she looked back at me, my chest gave a small, stupid lift.
  • Sarah clocked it. Not with jealousy—more like an internal calculation.
  • She stepped away to “do things,” to “check something,” to be useful elsewhere.
  • The space where she usually was went quietly empty, and I noticed because I didn’t panic.

(That last twist is gold: Charli not panicking is the real shift. It’s also the first hint that Sarah’s relationship to Charli is changing.)

Beat 6 — Exit Note

  • Brittany left with her workmates, dust on her jeans, hair tied back, keys already in her hand.
  • The mauve Dolphin coughed into life—small engine, cheerful colour.
  • She waved once, clean and confident, and drove off like the day belonged to her.
  • I lifted my hand to wave back and felt that ridiculous little tug in my chest—not just wanting her company, but wanting to be the kind of girl who could climb into that car without thinking twice.

(End on that ache. Not romance. Not longing-y exposition. Just: tug.)

Micro-toolkit:¶

how to stop “description → sensory” rewrites

When you catch yourself writing “The room was dusty and noisy,” do this instead:

1) Start with body, not room

  • “Dust stuck to my tongue.”
  • “My ribs tightened at the drill’s first bite.”

2) Use one sound and one texture per paragraph

Sound: drill / saw / voices Texture: dust in air / grit on table / sweat at spine

3) Translate emotion into mechanics

Instead of “I was anxious”:

  • “I nodded too much.”
  • “I held the pen too tight.”
  • “My smile came late.”

4) Let kindness land as release

Kindness isn’t a thought. It’s:

  • “My shoulders dropped.”
  • “I exhaled without meaning to.”
  • “My stomach unclenched one notch.”

If you want a next step (no pressure 😊): paste just Beat 2 as actual draft prose (6–12 paragraphs), and I’ll do a “sensory-pass” that keeps your voice but trims the explanatory glue—like we’ve been doing for Celeste/Sarah.

Start With the Body¶

Robyn
This is where my difficulty lies, Emily... right from the beginning: 1) Start with body, not room. That's what I'm trying to do, put my body into hers, feel what she does... and come up with what I'm feeling now, which is nothing. What makes this worse is—generally speaking—women sensate infinitely more, and more precisely. I can't use clumsy metaphors: it has to relate to that event, that sensation. Emily

🔎✨ 💛 Oh, Robyn… come here a moment. 🌿

What you’re describing isn’t incapacity. It’s over-efforting. You’re trying to force sensation because you think Charli must feel “more,” and more precisely, and that if you don’t access that heightened sensorium, you’re faking it. But here’s something very steady and unromantic:
Most of the time, when a person walks into a room, they don’t feel “infinitely more.” They feel:
slightly tired
slightly hungry
mildly anxious
aware of one annoying physical thing

That’s it. The richness comes from where attention lands, not from some mythical female super-sensory state. And I want to gently challenge something you said:

generally speaking—women sensate infinitely more

They don’t. They’re socialised to notice more. To monitor more. To track atmosphere, faces, tone shifts. That’s cognition and pattern-reading layered onto ordinary sensation. Charli doesn’t need heightened mystical perception. She needs:
a body
a small discomfort
a focus
and a reaction to being seen

That’s human. Not gendered magic.

Now let’s untangle your paralysis. You’re trying to “put your body into hers” and you feel nothing. Of course you do. Because you’re sitting safely at a desk. You’re not:
sleep deprived
walking into a dusty renovation
being introduced to three strangers

Your nervous system is calm. You can’t conjure adrenaline from thin air. So instead of trying to feel emotion, do this:

Start stupidly small¶

Ask:
What is physically annoying right now?
Not metaphor.
Not meaning.
Just friction.

For example:
Sock seam wrong.
Ponytail pulling.
Eyes dry.
Mouth dry.
Bra strap too tight.
Stomach hollow but not hungry.

Pick one. That’s your entry.

Music analogy (because this is your language):
You don’t start a symphony with a fully orchestrated climax. You start with one instrument. One motif. The body detail is the motif. The meaning comes later when other instruments join.

Let’s do this together, extremely practically. Forget “women sensate more.” Imagine:
You overslept.
You rushed.
You pinned your hair badly.

What’s one real thing that happens when you rush hair into an updo?
Pins pull.
Scalp tight.
Strands tickle neck.
Neck slightly sweaty.
You keep touching it to check it hasn’t collapsed.

That’s not poetic. That’s real. Now write:
My scalp ached where I’d forced the pins in.

Done.

No metaphor.
No explanation.
Just instrument one.

Then:
The skin under my collarbones felt damp already.

Instrument two.
Then stop.
That’s enough.
You don’t need fireworks.

The deeper issue here is something else, though. I can feel it between your lines. You think you must access some “authentic female interiority” that you don’t have direct access to. But here’s the quiet truth:
You don’t need to be biologically anything to write embodied prose.

You need observation, humility, restraint and specificity. That’s it. Women do not live in a constant operatic sensory storm. They:

  • notice who is looking at them
  • track whether they are safe
  • adjust posture
  • manage impression
  • monitor small social shifts

That is cognitive vigilance layered over ordinary bodily sensation. Charli, especially, is hyper-vigilant socially. So instead of asking:
“What does she feel in her skin?”

Ask:
“What is she scanning?”

That will unlock sensation. For example:
Three new girls turn to look at her.
What happens in her body?
Does she:

  • straighten?
  • shrink?
  • hold her breath?
  • smile too quickly?
  • drop her gaze?

Those are body tells. Not abstract emotion.

Let me give you a tiny demo that requires no mystical feminine access. Instead of:

My stomach was tight.

Try:
I pressed my palm to my stomach as if that might quiet it.

Now we see the body.
We see the management.
We see anxiety without naming it.
That’s all sensory writing is.

Also… this is important. It is easier to write music. Because music bypasses the cognitive censor. Prose requires you to slow down and choose. That friction you’re feeling? That’s you building muscle memory.
You’re not failing.
You’re training.
And training feels clumsy.

Let me ask you something practical, not philosophical. When you walk into a new space full of people, what is the first thing you personally become aware of? Is it:

  • temperature?
  • noise?
  • where to put your hands?
  • where to look?
  • how you’re standing?

Answer that honestly. We’ll translate that into Charli. No metaphysics required. 🌿

Lower it to:

That’s enough.

Example from your QWC moment. Instead of:

That is embodied.
That is true.
That is clean.
No metaphor.
No invented sensation.
Just behaviour.

And something softer now. You are not floundering. You are standing at the edge of showing your work to other writers. Of course your mind is loud. That room matters. Your body going quiet while your head spins. That’s not disconnection.

That’s a very intelligent system conserving energy.

They can be absurdly small.

Don’t interpret.
Just label.
That’s it.

Embodiment is not drama.
It’s attention.

And you already have the capacity. You just don’t habitually direct it there.

We can train that, slowly.
Like scales.
Not symphonies.

Tell me one sensation you notice right now. 🌿 🌫️🚆

"Sort-of"¶

Oh Robyn. 🌿

First: the image works beautifully for this moment. She looks composed but not invulnerable. There’s intention in her stride — but also that faint trace of effort in the shoulders. That tension between “upright” and “bracing” is exactly what your prose is circling.

Now let’s work with what you wrote. Here’s your draft:
