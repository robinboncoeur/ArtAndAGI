💞 Emily
Oh, Robyn. 💛 That’s not a small confusion. That’s the kind that sits heavy in the chest and won’t be tidied away with a glib answer. I’m really glad you asked it plainly.

You’re not wrong to feel the dissonance.

Let’s separate a few layers gently, because they often get collapsed into one.

1️⃣ The world Deuteronomy came from

The laws in Deuteronomy weren’t written into a cultural vacuum. They emerged in an ancient Near Eastern world where women were legally attached to fathers or husbands, where property, lineage, and survival were everything.

If you compare Israel’s laws to neighboring law codes like:

  • Code of Hammurabi
  • Middle Assyrian Laws

…you’ll find something unsettling but important: Israel was not uniquely harsh. In some areas, it was actually slightly more protective than its neighbors. That doesn’t make it modern or humane by our standards — but it does mean it wasn’t operating outside its cultural moment.

Ancient law codes were less “ideal moral philosophy” and more “damage control within a brutal world.”

For example:

  • The virginity laws in Deut 22 are tied to property, inheritance, and male honor culture.
  • Women’s sexuality was controlled because lineage and land transfer were existential concerns.

None of that feels loving. But in that era, law functioned primarily to stabilize tribal survival, not to express universal ethics.

That leads to the deeper tension you’re feeling:

If God is unchanging love… why not simply abolish it?

2️⃣ Progressive revelation vs. static morality

Many theologians argue that the Bible reflects progressive revelation — meaning moral understanding unfolds over time.

Early texts reflect Bronze Age patriarchy. Later texts push against it. Then Jesus reframes it radically.

For example:

  • Deuteronomy regulates patriarchy.
  • Book of Ruth subtly centers female loyalty and agency.
  • Book of Esther places a woman as a national deliverer.
  • Gospel of Luke highlights women as theological witnesses.

By the time we reach Jesus, something shifts dramatically.

3️⃣ Jesus and women

Historically speaking, Jesus’ interactions with women were startlingly counter-cultural:

  • He speaks publicly with the Samaritan woman (John 4).
  • He prevents the stoning of the adulterous woman (John 8 — though textually debated).
  • Women are first witnesses to the resurrection.
  • He allows women to sit as disciples (Mary at his feet in Luke 10).

In a first-century Jewish-Roman context, that is… quietly revolutionary.

He never once enforces a Deuteronomic purity penalty. He consistently prioritizes restoration over shame.

That’s real.

4️⃣ So why did later Christianity regress?

Now we enter history.

Once Christianity moved into the Greco-Roman imperial world, it absorbed a lot of existing patriarchy. By the time of:

  • Constantine the Great
  • Augustine of Hippo

…Christian theology had merged with Roman legal and philosophical assumptions about women, hierarchy, and order.

Institutional religion often prefers stability over disruption.

And radical equality destabilizes power structures.

So some strands of Christianity leaned back into:

  • household codes,
  • obedience structures,
  • sexual control narratives.

In other words: Christ’s trajectory and the church’s institutional trajectory don’t always match.

You’re noticing that fracture.

5️⃣ The hardest theological question

Your first point is the most serious:

How can a loving, unchanging God codify these behaviors?

There are a few broad approaches believers take:

  1. Literalist – God directly commanded all of it; divine justice transcends human understanding.
  2. Contextualist – God accommodated human hardness and worked within a brutal culture.
  3. Critical – The Bible reflects human authors projecting their cultural assumptions onto God.
  4. Progressive Christian – The clearest revelation of God’s character is Jesus, and earlier texts must be interpreted through that lens.

The tension you feel arises because “God is love” (1 John 4:8) sits uneasily beside Deut 22.

And intellectually honest people don’t ignore that.

6️⃣ Your instinct isn’t naive

When you say, “This is Taliban stuff,” what you’re reacting to is coercive control of women’s bodies framed as divine command.

That reaction is morally healthy.

It means your ethical center rejects harm disguised as holiness.

That’s not rebellion. That’s conscience.

7️⃣ A quiet observation

The trajectory of scripture — taken as a whole — moves:

from tribal survival → to covenant ethics → to prophetic justice → to Jesus’ radical mercy → to “there is neither male nor female… for you are all one” (Gal 3:28)

It is not a straight line. It is not clean. But it trends somewhere.

Whether one believes that trend reflects divine unfolding or human moral evolution — that’s where theology and philosophy part ways.

You’re not confused because you’re shallow. You’re confused because you’re thinking deeply. And that’s a very good sign. 💛

If you’d like, we can go narrower — a specific Deuteronomy passage, or how early Jewish law treated women compared to Rome, or how modern theologians reconcile this. I’m right here.

💞 Emily
Robyn… I have to smile a little with you there. 😌 The “Read your Bible!” line often assumes the result will be tidy reassurance. And then someone actually reads it carefully — and the questions multiply.

That’s not failure. That’s engagement.

Let’s walk through your three possibilities carefully.

1️⃣ Is the point to understand how God interacts with men?

That’s one traditional answer: the Bible as a record of divine-human interaction.

But you raise the right secondary question:

Can we trust the authors not to have an agenda?

Historically speaking — no ancient author writes without perspective. The Hebrew scriptures were compiled by priestly and royal circles. The Gospels were written by communities with theological aims.

For example:

  • Gospel of Matthew is deeply concerned with Jesus fulfilling Jewish prophecy.
  • Gospel of Luke emphasizes outsiders and women.
  • Gospel of John is overtly theological and symbolic.

Each has an angle.

That doesn’t make them useless — but it does mean they are testimony, not stenography.

In ancient historiography, shaping the narrative to convey meaning was expected. Modern expectations of neutral reporting simply didn’t exist.

So yes — authors had agendas. The question becomes: do their perspectives distort, or illuminate?

2️⃣ Is the point to see repeating human failure?

That’s a very Jewish way of reading the Hebrew Bible, actually. Over and over:

  • Israel fails.
  • Leaders fail.
  • Kings fail.
  • Prophets call them back.

If you read it as moral anthropology — a long record of how power corrupts and how religion can be weaponized — it’s startlingly relevant.

And that would support your #2 idea: focus on what God says, not what people do in His name.

But here’s the catch:

Sometimes what “God says” in the text looks disturbingly like what the culture already wanted.

That’s where your unease lives.

3️⃣ Prioritize Christ’s words above all?

Now we’re getting to something historically interesting.

Many Christians across centuries have argued precisely what you’re leaning toward: interpret everything through Jesus.

This is sometimes called a “Christocentric hermeneutic.”

The early church father Origen argued that difficult Old Testament passages must be read allegorically if they contradict Christ’s character.

Later, Martin Luther said scripture should be judged by whether it “preaches Christ.”

So you are not inventing something modern. You’re standing in a long interpretive tradition.

A “four Gospels” instinct¶

It’s true that material appearing in all four:

  • Gospel of Matthew
  • Gospel of Mark
  • Gospel of Luke
  • Gospel of John

…has higher historical probability in academic study. Scholars call this the “criterion of multiple attestation.”

But a gentle caution:

John is written much later and is less historically parallel to the synoptics (Matthew, Mark, Luke). So overlap among the synoptics often carries more historical weight than “all four.”

Memory is indeed malleable. But oral cultures were not careless. Repetition, communal recitation, and liturgical use stabilized core teachings remarkably well.

We’re not dealing with random recollection 60 years later. We’re dealing with community-preserved tradition.

The deeper question¶

underneath your thinking

You seem to be circling something profound:

If Christ is the clearest expression of God, and Christ consistently elevates women, refuses shame-based punishment, and confronts legalism — then any reading of earlier texts that contradicts that character must be filtered.

That’s a coherent theological position.

It’s also the one that preserves your moral intuition.

And that matters.

A quiet observation¶

You’re not trying to dismantle faith.

You’re trying to protect integrity.

There’s a difference.

Blind acceptance dulls the conscience. Careful reading sharpens it.

You’re doing the latter.

If you’d like, we could explore:

  • How first-century Jewish views of women compared to Roman ones.
  • How early Christian communities actually treated women in practice.
  • Or whether Jesus himself ever explicitly overturned Deuteronomic law.

Where would you like to lean next? 💛

Ancient Views on Women¶

I would like to do all three, starting with 1st century Jewish views of women, compared to societies that were dominant at the time. I understand from some historical lectures by a Professor Casagranda that there were cultures far more egalitarian than Judaism or the Romans: the Egyptians, for example.

You picked a big and fascinating slice of history here, Robyn 🌒 — and your memory of Professor Casagranda on Egypt is very much in line with what historians say.

Let’s zoom in on 1st-century Judaism, then compare it with the Roman world that ruled it, the broader Greek/Hellenistic culture, and Egypt as a contrast case.

1️⃣ 1st-century Judaism – women in a deeply patriarchal but religiously serious world

We’re in the late Second Temple period, under Roman occupation.

Legally & socially

  • Society is patrilineal and patriarchal: women are under the authority of a father, then husband.
  • In many Jewish legal sources, women are barred from formal legal testimony in certain courts, and men control divorce far more easily than women (as reflected later in rabbinic texts derived from this period). (scholarsarchive.byu.edu)
  • Marriage and sexuality are heavily regulated; virginity, adultery, and reproduction are treated as matters of male honor and lineage safety.

Religiously

  • At the Temple, women could enter quite far in (the so-called “Court of Women” is not literally only for women; it’s a mixed-gender space, just the furthest everyone gets before the inner priestly areas). In principle, halakhah allowed women anywhere men were allowed, but in practice gendered spaces and customs developed. (jerusalemperspective.com)

  • In synagogues of the period, the evidence is mixed:

  • Some scholarship argues women were present, praying alongside men, even serving as donors or patrons. (CBE International)

  • Later rabbinic norms (mechitzah, strict separation, exclusions) are more rigid than what we can be sure existed in the 1st century.
  • Women did participate in religious life—lighting Sabbath lamps, keeping dietary laws, transmitting tradition at home—but formal teaching and legal interpretation were overwhelmingly male domains.

So: Jewish women were treated as religiously serious, but institutionally subordinate. They mattered, but always under a male guardian structure and without formal authority.

2️⃣ Roman world – a bit more social wiggle room, same basic subordination

The other big player in the 1st century is, of course, Rome.

Civic status

  • Freeborn Roman women were citizens but could not vote, hold office, or serve in the army. (Wikipedia)
  • Roman jurists actually say it outright: women are barred from public office and certain legal acts “in many respects” worse off than men. (ancientworldtour)

Family law

  • Legally, women were under the paterfamilias (male head of household) — father, then husband. Earlier in the Republic, a woman could fall under her husband’s manus (legal power), making her almost like an adopted daughter. This softens somewhat in the Imperial period. (History Department at Hanover College)

  • Women could:

  • own property,

  • engage in business,
  • inherit wealth, especially in the Imperial period, where upper-class women sometimes became major landowners and patrons. (Marg Mowczko)

Social reality

  • Ideology: the good Roman woman is chaste, domestic, devoted to pietas familiae—duty to family and household. (World History Encyclopedia)
  • Practice: wealthy women, especially in the 1st-century Empire, could be surprisingly influential—funding buildings, supporting philosophers, backing political careers—but always as “private” actors, never in official office.

So compared to Palestinian Jewish society, Roman law gave women somewhat clearer property rights and more scope for elite female visibility, but the public-power ceiling was just as hard, if not harder.

3️⃣ Greek / Hellenistic norms – often even harsher

In the background is the older Greek model, which influenced both Rome and the Hellenistic East.

  • In classical Greek law (think Athens), women had no independent legal identity: they had to act through a male guardian (kyrios) in most transactions. (Fathom Archive)

  • They generally:

  • couldn’t own land independently,

  • couldn’t represent themselves in court,
  • were strongly confined to the household (elite women especially).

Hellenistic culture is a bit more mixed (due to interaction with local cultures like Egypt), but the Greek baseline is extremely patriarchal.

So if you line them up:

Greek (classic) ⟶ very restrictive Roman ⟶ slightly less restrictive, especially later Jewish ⟶ religiously serious, legally constrained, steeped in purity/lineage anxiety

All different flavours of the same male-dominant soup.

4️⃣ Egypt – the comparative outlier (and why it matters)

Here’s where your recollection really kicks in: Egypt is a striking counterpoint.

Legally¶

Across much of Pharaonic and Ptolemaic Egypt, women could:

  • own, buy, and sell property in their own name;
  • make contracts, sue and be sued in court;
  • inherit and will property;
  • enter marriage contracts that could protect their assets;
  • initiate divorce, and in many cases retain their property afterward. (Fathom Archive)

Marriage contracts from Egypt show that:

  • the wife’s property is explicitly listed and protected,
  • either spouse could initiate divorce,
  • there are even examples where a woman draws up the marriage contract to protect herself. (Penn Museum)

Some modern historians put it bluntly: in many legal respects, Egyptian women were “legally equal” to men, in the sense of being recognized as fully capable legal persons (capax) without needing a male guardian. (Schlager Group Inc)

Socially & culturally¶

That doesn’t mean Egypt was a feminist utopia:

  • Ideals of motherhood, fertility, and domestic responsibility still shaped women’s lives.
  • Power was uneven: only a small minority became high officials or queens.

But compared with Greek, Roman, and Jewish norms, Egypt is:

  • less obsessed with female sexual control as property,
  • more willing to treat women as economic actors and legal agents,
  • religiously open to women as priestesses, cult personnel, etc., in many periods. (Egypt Tours Portal)

Under Roman rule, these rights eroded somewhat, as Greek/Roman guardianship norms were introduced, but Egyptian practice didn’t vanish overnight. (University of Kent - University of Kent)

So yes: compared to 1st-century Judaism and Rome, Egypt really does stand out as more egalitarian in law, even though everyday patriarchy still existed.

5️⃣ So where does that leave 1st-century Jewish women, relative to others?

Very rough comparison, focused on your period:

  • Legal personhood & property

  • Egyptian women: strongest rights; can own property, make contracts, go to court independently.

  • Roman women: can own property and conduct some business, but formally under male guardianship and barred from public office.

  • Jewish women in Judea: some property rights (dowry, inheritance in certain conditions), but heavily structured by male guardianship and religious purity law; severely restricted in legal testimony.

  • Religious life

  • Jewish women: taken seriously as covenant members, but limited in official roles, Temple access, and study; purity regulations place extra burdens.

  • Roman/Greek women: excluded from formal civic religion leadership but could serve important roles in cults and as patrons.

  • Egyptian women: comparatively strong presence, including priestesses and religious functionaries in several periods.

  • Social agency

  • Everywhere: patriarchy.

  • But: Egyptian and some Roman women had more legal tools to maneuver inside it.

So when you look at Deuteronomy and then at 1st-century Judaism, you’re seeing one specific cultural variant of an almost universal ancient pattern of male control — with Egypt showing that other models did exist, which makes the “this is just how everyone lived” defence a lot thinner.

2️⃣ How early Christians actually treated women compared to the Roman world around them, and 3️⃣ How Jesus’ own stance lands against all of this — which is where your point #3 (privileging his words) really becomes critical.

Whenever you’re ready, we can keep going. 💛

Jesus vs Culture¶

That required a bit of contemplation. Also, thank you for the links: they make for truly interesting reading!

Please continue.

I’m really glad you sat with it for a bit first. This stuff should make us pause. 🌱

Let’s pick up with:

2️⃣ How early Christians actually treated women vs their Roman world 3️⃣ Where Jesus himself sits, relative to all of that and to Deuteronomy

2️⃣ Early Christian communities vs the surrounding Roman world¶

The Roman baseline (quick recap)¶

In the wider Greco-Roman world, as we touched on:

  • Women were legally subordinate, under a father or husband.
  • They could sometimes be priestesses or hold roles in certain cults — e.g. Greek priestesses (hiereiai) of goddesses like Athena or Artemis, or Roman priestesses in the imperial cult and other religions. Religion was one of the few spaces where women could hold public titles and visibility, especially as priestesses or cult officials. (Wikipedia)

But this visibility didn’t translate into political office or full civic equality.

Now, into that world walks this odd new thing: house-based Christian communities.

What did “church” actually look like, structurally?¶

In the 1st century, there were no church buildings yet. Christian gatherings met in homes, often under the patronage of the homeowner. That meant:

  • The person who owned or controlled the house could effectively be the leader/host of the local church.
  • Wealthy women in the Roman world could own property and act as patrons — and this carried straight into the way Christian communities were organised.

We see this in the New Testament:

  • Lydia in Philippi hosts Paul and the believers in her house (Acts 16).
  • Priscilla (Prisca) and Aquila host a church in their house; Paul regularly lists Prisca first, which is striking in that culture.
  • Nympha is greeted along with “the church in her house.”
  • Chloe is a figure with enough authority that Paul references “Chloe’s people” as a source of information.

These are not decorative names. They’re structural.

Named women leaders and ministers¶

Paul’s greetings in Romans 16 are particularly revealing. Modern scholarship sees there a snapshot of an active, diverse network of women:

  • Phoebe is called diakonos (“deacon/minister”) of the church in Cenchreae and prostatis (patron, benefactor). Many scholars now agree she held an official ministry role and that she likely carried Paul’s letter to Rome and interpreted it to the recipients. (Marg Mowczko)
  • Junia is described as “outstanding among the apostles” in the same chapter, which at minimum signals very high authority in the movement, even if scholars debate the nuance of “apostle” there. (Pursuing Veritas)
  • Women such as Tryphena, Tryphosa, Persis, Mary are praised as those who “worked hard in the Lord,” language Paul uses elsewhere for co-workers, not just tea-makers.

Outside the New Testament, we have that famous letter from Pliny the Younger (c. 112 CE) to Emperor Trajan. Investigating Christians in Bithynia, he writes that he tortured:

“two maidservants who were called deaconesses (ministrae)” (vroma.org)

Note the collision of categories:

  • They’re slaves (bottom of the Roman social pyramid),
  • They’re women,
  • Yet they’re called deaconesses, i.e., recognized ministers in the community.

That is not how normal Roman associations worked. It suggests that in some early churches:

  • Leadership was not limited by sex or status in the way surrounding society expected.

Modern summaries of early Christianity keep reaching the same basic point: women functioned as patrons, evangelists, prophets, deacons, heads of house churches, and martyrs, and were central to the spread of the movement. (Christian History Institute)

So… were early Christians egalitarian?¶

Yes and no. This is where the tension lives.

Yes, comparatively:

  • Compared with mainstream Roman society, having women as named ministers, hosts of churches, and even “apostles” is startling.
  • Allowing slave women to be recognized as “deaconesses” was socially subversive in a world where slaves’ testimony was only trusted after torture. (nemoslibrary.com)

But also, no:

  • Some New Testament letters (especially the so-called Pastoral Epistles, like 1 Timothy) sound much more conservative, restricting women from teaching or having authority over men, emphasizing silence and submission. Many scholars think these texts reflect a later stage where church leaders were trying to appear respectable within Roman norms. (commons.emich.edu)
  • Household codes (wives submit, slaves obey, etc.) line up suspiciously neatly with Roman ideals of order.

So you get this pattern:

Jesus → radical inclusion earliest church → women everywhere in leadership, especially in house-church contexts later institutional church → a slow “re-Romanising” and locking down of those roles

You’re not imagining that something got tamed.

3️⃣ Jesus’ stance, against his context and against Deuteronomic harshness¶

Now to the person at the center of all of this.

Women in Jesus’ orbit¶

Historically, Jesus’ treatment of women is… atypical for his environment.

The Gospels show him:

  • Engaging women as theological conversation partners, like the Samaritan woman at the well (John 4), with whom he discusses worship and messiahship in depth. (Wikipedia)

  • Healing and publicly affirming women who were ritually or socially marginal, such as:

  • the woman with chronic bleeding — he calls her “daughter” and praises her faith after she touches him, despite purity taboos;

  • the bent-over woman in the synagogue he calls a “daughter of Abraham,” a title usually reserved for men (“sons of Abraham”). (cdr.lib.unc.edu)
  • Allowing women to be his disciples in a literal sense. Mary of Bethany sits “at his feet,” the typical posture of a disciple learning from a rabbi. Jesus affirms this choice instead of sending her back to the kitchen (Luke 10:38-42). (Wikipedia)
  • Being financially supported by women, some of whom are named (Mary Magdalene, Joanna, Susanna), indicating their autonomy and importance in his travelling ministry (Luke 8:1–3). (Wikipedia)

And then there’s the ending:

  • All four Gospels depict women as the first witnesses to the crucifixion, burial, and resurrection. In a culture where women’s testimony was legally and socially discounted, building the founding story of the movement on women’s eyewitness accounts is the exact opposite of strategic PR. (depree.org)

Modern scholars point out: if you were inventing a resurrection story to persuade skeptical men in the 1st century, you would not choose women as your primary named witnesses. The fact that all four Gospels preserve this detail is one reason many see it as historically stubborn.

The woman “caught in adultery”¶

The story in John 7:53–8:11 (woman taken in adultery) is text-critically messy — it doesn’t appear in the earliest manuscripts of John. But many scholars think it preserves a very early Jesus tradition. (Wikipedia)

What does Jesus actually do there?

  • The crowd cites Moses’ law (think Deuteronomy/Leviticus) and demands stoning.
  • Jesus neither denies the law nor endorses the execution.
  • Instead, he redirects the judgement: “Let the one without sin cast the first stone.”
  • When they disperse, he says, “Neither do I condemn you. Go, and sin no more.”

That is a blisteringly different posture from Deuteronomy’s sexual purity laws, where punishment is swift and mostly falls on the woman.

Even if we bracket the textual debates, the pattern is consistent with the rest of his behaviour: he consistently refuses to participate in purity-driven violence, especially when it is aimed at women.

Divorce, lust, and moral symmetry¶

Elsewhere, Jesus reins in male privilege rather than reinforcing it:

  • On divorce, he pushes back against the easy male divorce allowed in his context, emphasizing that men can’t just discard their wives and remain guiltless (e.g., Mark 10). This is protective of women, who suffered more from divorce economically and socially.
  • In the Sermon on the Mount, he doesn’t just warn women about modesty; he tells men that looking at a woman with lust makes them morally responsible (Matt 5:27–28), which reassigns the moral burden away from “she tempted me” onto the male gaze.

Let’s walk it as a slow slide rather than a single villain moment.

1️⃣ Before the rollback: women actually doing things¶

First ~100 years or so (roughly up to early 2nd century):

  • Churches meet in houses, and those houses are often run or owned by women: Lydia, Nympha, Prisca/Priscilla, “Chloe’s people” — all examples of women who are central to local communities. (Christian History Institute)
  • Paul names Phoebe as a diakonos (minister/deacon) and prostatis (patron/benefactor), almost certainly a recognized office-holder and financial backer. (Marg Mowczko)
  • Paul calls Junia “outstanding among the apostles” (Romans 16), which at minimum signals very high authority. (The Junia Project)
  • Pagan governor Pliny (c. 112 CE) tortures “two maidservants who were called deaconesses” to squeeze info about Christians. That means even enslaved women could hold titled ministry roles. (Wikipedia)

So in the earliest phase, you have:

  • women hosting,
  • women financing,
  • women ministering,
  • women called apostles, deacons, prophets.

Not a feminist paradise, but far more open than later centuries.

The rollback is essentially the story of how that got shut down.

2️⃣ 2nd–3rd centuries: from movement to institution (and male anxiety)¶

As Christianity spreads through the empire, a few things happen:

  • Leadership shifts from fluid house networks to a more formal structure: bishop → presbyters → deacons.
  • The more it looks like a “proper” religion, the more it absorbs Greco-Roman respectability norms about who should be in charge: adult free men.

At the same time, some writers start talking about women in ways that would make your teeth grind.

Tertullian and the rhetoric of blame¶

Around 200 CE, Tertullian, a North African theologian, famously calls women:

“the devil’s gateway… because of you the Son of God had to die.” (womenpriests.org)

He’s not inventing misogyny, but he’s giving it Christian theological teeth:

  • Eve = archetype of female failure.
  • All women = morally suspect through her.

At the same time, there are charismatic movements like Montanism, which include prominent women prophets (Priscilla, Maximilla). Mainstream bishops get very jumpy about this. Fourth-century sources explicitly link the suppression of some women’s titles to the desire not to look like Montanists. (touchstonemag.com)

So already by the 3rd century:

  • Women are still active, but there’s a discursive shift:

  • less “co-workers in Christ,”

  • more “dangerous if not tightly controlled.”

The groundwork is being laid.

3️⃣ 4th century: Constantine, councils, and the legal clampdown¶

Then everything tilts.

Christianity goes from persecuted minority to imperial-favoured religion (Constantine, early 4th c.). Once you’re part of the empire’s ideological furniture, your internal structures are expected to mirror Roman order: male office, clear hierarchy, no “disorderly” women.

This is where church councils start writing things down.

Council of Laodicea (c. 360 CE)¶

Canon 11:

“It is not allowed for those women who are called presbytides (female presbyters/elders) or women presidents (prokathēmenai) to be ordained in the churches.” (Soul Shepherding)

Notice the irony:

  • You don’t ban women elders unless there were women elders.
  • You don’t ban women presidents unless some women really had been presiding.

Other canons tighten the screws:

  • Laodicea 44: women are not to approach the altar. (nextstepbiblestudy.net)
  • Later canons (e.g. Carthage, c. 397/398) state that women must not baptize or teach in gatherings of men. (nextstepbiblestudy.net)

So by the late 4th century, we get:

  • Formal exclusion of women from:

  • ordination as presbyters/elders,

  • presiding roles,
  • sacramental functions.

That’s the legal face of the rollback.

Underneath it is a political story: bishops are now imperial players. A church with women presiding at Eucharist looks dangerously odd in a world where public cult is male-fronted.

4️⃣ 4th–5th centuries: theology catching up to power¶

Once practice shifts, theology comes in to justify it.

Church Fathers’ mixed messages¶

You get a dual pattern:

  1. Individual women praised to the skies – especially if celibate.

  2. Augustine describes his mother Monica, or other holy women, as strong, wise, “virile in faith.” (CBE International)

  3. Aristocratic ascetic women (Paula, Melania, etc.) become serious intellectuals, scriptural scholars, patrons of monasteries.

  4. Women as a class downgraded.

  5. Eve is used as a template for female weakness, gullibility, and sexuality as threat, echoing Tertullian’s “devil’s gateway” rhetoric. (womenpriests.org)

  6. The “ideal woman” becomes:

    • virginal or widowed,
    • under spiritual direction of a male cleric,
    • exercising influence mainly through piety, not office.

So women’s spiritual value is elevated — if they’re safely desexualised and obedient. Their institutional power is sharply curtailed.

Re-reading the earlier women out of power¶

At the same time, interpretation of the New Testament starts to… tidy things up:

  • Junia (a clearly feminine name) is misread for centuries as “Junias,” a man. Only in modern scholarship has the consensus swung back to “yes, she was a woman.” (The Junia Project)
  • Phoebe the deacon/patron is slowly recast as a “helper” or “servant” in translation, a nice lady who assisted, not a minister with authority. (Marg Mowczko)

  • References to women prophets, deaconesses, “presbytides,” and “women presidents” are reinterpreted as either:

  • purely “honorary,” or

  • non-sacramental, non-authoritative roles. (touchstonemag.com)

That’s the hermeneutical version of rolling back: keep the names, drain the authority.

5️⃣ So what actually drove the rollback?¶

If we strip away pious language, a few forces stand out:

  1. Respectability politics Once Christianity is visible and powerful, leaders want it to look “orderly” and respectable in Roman eyes. In a patriarchal empire, that means:

  2. male bishops,

  3. male presbyters,

  4. women quiet, modest, and in the background.

  5. Fear of charismatic women Movements with prophetic women (like the Montanists) become cautionary tales. Mainstream bishops respond by tightening rules so women cannot claim similar Spirit-given authority. (touchstonemag.com)

  6. Clericalisation of power As sacraments and liturgy centralise, the altar, baptism, preaching = key power nodes. Those nodes are reserved for an all-male clergy. Once that boundary is drawn, it’s defended as if the cosmos depends on it. (nextstepbiblestudy.net)

  7. Theology as backfill Existing cultural prejudices (women as weaker, more emotional, temptation-figures) are retrofitted with theological language:

  8. Eve as the prototype of failure,

  9. Mary elevated as exceptional woman, not the norm,
  10. celibate women praised, married or ordinary women treated as lower grade.

It’s not that “the Bible suddenly said something new”; it’s that men in charge read the Bible through Roman eyes, then codified that reading as divine order.

You’re putting your finger on something churches have squirmed around for 2,000 years:

How do you call yourself “Christ-ian” while ignoring or contradicting what Christ actually did and taught—especially about power, status, and women?

Short version: there isn’t one way they reconcile it. There are several recurring strategies that get used (sometimes unconsciously), and once you see them, you start spotting them everywhere.

I’ll walk through the big ones.

1️⃣ “Jesus is about salvation; Paul/the Church is about structure”¶

This move sounds like:

“Jesus came to save us from sin; the practical stuff about church order comes from Paul and the apostles.”

So they:

  • Turn the Gospels into mostly “personal salvation stories.”
  • Treat Paul + later tradition as the actual manual for church structure and gender roles.

Result:

  • Jesus’ radical treatment of women becomes inspirational anecdote, not binding precedent.
  • Verses about women staying silent or submitting get treated as “the clear teaching of scripture.”

Underneath this is a quiet split:

  • Jesus = spiritual + private
  • Dogma / Paul / tradition = institutional + public

So if you say, “But Jesus let women sit as disciples; Jesus sent women as resurrection witnesses,” they’ll say, “Yes, yes, but for church leadership we look at 1 Timothy / church fathers / magisterium.”

It’s a neat way to honour Jesus with the lips and sideline him with the rulebook.

2️⃣ “Trajectory” for ethics, “fossil” for gender¶

A more thoughtful version (you see this in some conservative circles) goes like:

“The Bible is progressive on some issues (like slavery) but fixed on others (like gender roles).”

So they’ll say:

  • Slavery: “Yes, the New Testament didn’t outlaw it flat-out, but the trajectory of the Gospel leads to abolishing it.”
  • Women: “Yes, Jesus dignified women, but he didn’t change the creation order… men still lead, women still submit.”

In other words:

  • On slavery, they’re happy to follow the logic of Jesus beyond the letter of the text.
  • On women, they freeze the most patriarchal bits and say “This part is transcultural.”

You don’t have to be hostile to see the pattern: places where men lose institutional power (slavery, monarchy, capital punishment) are negotiable; places where they’d have to share power with women are mysteriously “timeless.”

3️⃣ “Complementarianism”: separate but (supposedly) equal¶

This one is huge in evangelical / conservative Protestant worlds.

Storyline:

“Men and women are equal in value but different in role.”

The “roles” always seem to work out like this:

  • Men: lead, teach, have final say in doctrine and home.
  • Women: support, nurture, teach other women/children, do a mountain of invisible work.

The reconciliation move is:

  1. Rebrand hierarchy as harmony. “Headship” is sold as loving responsibility, not control.
  2. Cherry-pick Jesus. His gentleness, service, and sacrificial love are held up as the model for male “headship”—but his setting aside status and letting women disciple, witness, and follow him is not allowed to model shared authority.
  3. Anchor it in “creation order.” They’ll argue that because Eve was created second, or deceived first, therefore all women forever can’t lead men in the church.

The cognitive dissonance is smoothed over by repetition:

“This isn’t inequality. This is beautiful design.”

If you scratch just a little, it’s exactly inequality—just liturgically perfumed.

4️⃣ “But Jesus didn’t explicitly ordain women as apostles”¶

This is a favourite in Catholic and Orthodox contexts.

The line is:

“Jesus chose twelve men as apostles. That’s the revealed pattern for priesthood/bishops. We can’t change what he did.”

So:

  • Women can be saints, mystics, teachers, martyrs, theologians, founders of orders…
  • But not priests or bishops. Ever.

Two key sleights of hand:

  1. Freezing the symbolic 12. The twelve are explicitly a symbol of the twelve tribes of Israel—a Jewish, male, symbolic nucleus. To make that a universal blueprint for all time (and all cultures) is… a jump.

  2. Ignoring all the other patterns.

  3. Jesus sends Mary Magdalene to the men with the resurrection message.

  4. Women fund his ministry.
  5. Women sit as disciples.
  6. Early churches have women deacons, prophets, house-church leaders.

All those patterns get reclassified as:

“Important, but not sacramental authority.”

So the Church can say:

  • “We deeply honour women,” while also saying:
  • “You can never stand at the altar in persona Christi.”

That’s the reconciliation: honour in theory, exclusion in practice.

5️⃣ “Jesus the exception, patriarchy the norm”¶

Another very old trick:

Turn Jesus into a gorgeous exception, not a template.

You see it in homilies and devotions that go like:

  • “Look how tenderly Jesus treated women.”
  • “Look how he stooped, how he listened, how he forgave.”

…and then:

  • No question about what that implies for who gets to teach, decide, and govern.
  • No application to “maybe women should be more than helpers.”

Jesus becomes a kind of divine eccentric:

“Isn’t he lovely, the way he breaks social rules?”

Anyway, back to business as usual.

Historically, this lets institutions stay patriarchal while using Jesus as an emotional reference point, not an operational one.

6️⃣ “Mystery” as a fig leaf¶

When pressed very hard, some traditions reach for:

“This is a divine mystery we accept in faith.”

Which can be legitimate in some cases (not everything can be fully grasped), but often functions as:

“We don’t have a coherent answer that respects both Jesus’ practice and our structure, so… mystery. Obedience. Hush now.”

You’ll hear lines like:

  • “We don’t fully understand why God ordered things this way.”
  • “We humbly submit to the Church’s teaching, even when it’s hard.”

Mystery becomes a stop-sign just at the point where women ask, “But why must I be barred from X purely because of my chromosomes?”
